On the Theoretical Limitations of Self-Expressive Deep Subspace Clustering

This paper is not trying to invent yet another deep clustering network. It is trying to answer a more uncomfortable question: when we combine an encoder, a self-expression loss, and a clustering head, do we really learn a geometry that helps clustering, or can the optimizer satisfy the objective with a degenerate latent space?

The answer the paper gives is sharp. In several common formulations, the model can reduce the self-expression loss by collapsing the embedding instead of organizing it. That means a method can look like it is learning “cluster structure” while actually learning a trivial arrangement that is bad for downstream spectral clustering.

The whole paper is about what happens when the middle of that pipeline is free to move.

The degenerate embedding illustration

The degenerate embedding illustration is the paper’s main visual argument in one picture.

What to notice is the mismatch between the intended middle panel and the right panel. The method is supposed to turn nonlinear structure into something close to a union of linear subspaces. Instead, the theorem says the optimum can push most points almost to the origin and leave behind only a tiny, useless remnant.

That is the core theme of the paper: self-expression alone does not protect geometry.

The objective the paper is testing

The classical self-expressive subspace clustering loss says that each point should be reconstructed from the other points, while a regularizer keeps the coefficient matrix simple.

Intuition: let each embedded point explain itself through other embedded points, then regularize the coefficient matrix so the solution does not become trivial.

Symbols:

  • \mathbf{Z}: embedded data matrix, with one column per sample
  • \mathbf{C}: self-expression coefficient matrix
  • \lambda: tradeoff between reconstruction and coefficient penalty
  • \theta(\mathbf{C}): coefficient regularizer, often \ell_1, Frobenius, or nuclear-style
  • \|\cdot\|_F: Frobenius norm

Read-aloud meaning: fit coefficients so the embedded points can rebuild one another, but keep the coefficient matrix simple.

Why it matters: this loss works well when the data are already arranged as a union of linear subspaces. The paper asks what happens when we also let the embedding itself move.

Deep subspace clustering turns that fixed-data objective into a learned representation problem. The encoder produces the embedding, and the self-expression loss is added as a regularizer on the latent space.

Intuition: learn an embedding \mathbf{Z} that both reconstructs the input well and admits a clean self-expression graph.

Symbols:

  • \mathbf{X}: input data matrix
  • \Phi_E: encoder network
  • \Phi_D: decoder network
  • \mathcal{W}_e, \mathcal{W}_d: encoder and decoder parameters

  • \mathbf{C}: self-expression coefficients
  • \gamma: weight on the self-expression term
  • \ell(\cdot,\cdot): reconstruction loss, typically squared error

Read-aloud meaning: learn a latent space and a coefficient graph at the same time, while also keeping enough information to reconstruct the original data.

Why it matters: this is where the danger starts. If the encoder and decoder can rescale against each other, the model can shrink the embedding and still keep reconstruction nearly unchanged.

When the autoencoder can cheat

The first failure mode is a scaling loophole.

The paper’s Proposition 1 shows that if the encoder and decoder are flexible enough, and especially if the final encoder nonlinearity is positively homogeneous, then one can scale the encoder down and the decoder up so that reconstruction stays the same while the latent embedding shrinks. ReLU, leaky ReLU, and max-pooling all fit this kind of behavior in the relevant parts of the network.

Once that happens, the self-expression term becomes toothless. If the latent codes get smaller and smaller, the loss F(\mathbf{Z},\mathbf{C}) can also be driven down, so the optimizer can win by shrinking geometry rather than learning geometry.

That is the first big warning in the paper: autoencoder regularization does not automatically prevent collapse. If you want the self-expression term to matter, you need to protect the scale of \mathbf{Z} through weight decay, tied weights, explicit normalization, or another constraint that stops the latent codes from being arbitrarily small.

Once you force a scale, the geometry still collapses

The paper then asks a second question. Suppose we fix the scaling loophole by requiring the latent embedding to keep some minimum norm. What geometry does the self-expression loss prefer then?

The answer is still discouraging. The optimization stops being a clustering problem in the intuitive sense and becomes a singular-value problem in disguise. The paper shows that under dataset-style or channel-style normalization, the best coefficient matrix is the one that makes \mathbf{C}-\mathbf{I} as weak as possible in its smallest singular direction.

Intuition: the optimizer now prefers coefficient matrices that make \mathbf{C}-\mathbf{I} nearly singular in its weakest direction, because that is the easiest way to satisfy self-expression under a norm constraint.

Symbols:

  • \mathbf{C}^*: optimal coefficient matrix
  • \sigma_{\min}(\mathbf{C}-\mathbf{I}): smallest singular value of \mathbf{C}-\mathbf{I}
  • \mathbf{I}: identity matrix
  • \tau: lower bound on the embedding norm imposed by normalization
  • \lambda: regularization weight
  • \theta(\mathbf{C}): coefficient penalty

Read-aloud meaning: pick coefficients that balance a small regularizer with a self-expression residual that is easiest to hide in the weakest singular direction.

Why it matters: this reveals that the objective is no longer looking for rich cluster geometry. It is looking for a degenerate linear-algebraic escape route.

The paper studies three normalization styles:

  • dataset normalization, where the whole embedding must keep a minimum Frobenius norm;
  • channel or batch normalization, where each row of the embedding must keep a minimum norm;
  • instance normalization, where each embedded sample must have a fixed norm.

These are not identical in detail, but the failure patterns they induce are closely related.

What to notice in this figure is that all three regimes are pathological, but in different ways. Dataset and channel-style normalization still let most points fall toward a near-zero cloud, with only a tiny surviving structure left behind. Instance normalization stops the shrink-to-zero behavior, but it replaces it with duplicated or sign-flipped points.

That is the important point: the normalization changes the shape of the collapse, not the fact of the collapse.

Theorem 2 makes the dataset-normalized and channel-normalized story very concrete. When the self-expression penalty is \ell_1-style and the equality \mathbf{Z}=\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{C} is enforced, the optimum essentially keeps only two nonzero latent points, which are copies of each other up to a sign flip, while the rest are driven to zero. Theorem 3 shows that replacing the coefficient regularizer with a Schatten-p norm, including the Frobenius and nuclear norm cases, does not rescue the geometry either. In that case the optimum collapses to a rank-one structure, so every point lies on the same line.

So the regularizer family can change the exact failure mode, but it does not by itself guarantee a clustering-friendly embedding.

Instance normalization fixes one problem and creates another

Instance normalization is the obvious reaction to the near-zero collapse: force every embedded point to have the same length.

That does stop the all-points-near-zero behavior. But Theorem 4 says the optimizer still finds a cheap way out. Because each point is forbidden from using itself directly (diag(\mathbf{C})=\mathbf{0}), the next cheapest reconstruction is to find another point that is exactly the same as itself, or the same up to a sign flip.

So instead of a meaningful cluster geometry, the optimum becomes a graph of copies.

The theorem says two things that are easy to remember:

  • every latent point has a partner that matches it up to sign;
  • a nonzero coefficient can only connect points that are equal up to sign.

That means instance normalization does not really create diverse clusters. It creates duplicate representatives and then lets spectral clustering sort out a graph that may have an arbitrary number of connected components.

In other words, the model has stopped shrinking points, but it has not learned to separate them in a useful way.

The pathology does not require a huge network

One tempting objection is that these degenerate optima only show up in highly expressive networks. The paper addresses that too.

Proposition 2 constructs a very simple two-layer affine/ReLU encoder and decoder and shows that they can still realize nearly the same collapse. By choosing biases so the ReLU behaves like an identity on the finite training set, the authors can make the embeddings arbitrarily close to one another, keep their norms near any chosen value, and still reconstruct the original samples exactly.

That matters because it removes the “too much capacity” excuse. The failure is not only a deep overparameterization story. It can already appear in simple network families as long as the architecture can express identity on the training data.

What the experiments show

The real-data experiments make the theoretical criticism feel less abstract.

Figure 4 tracks the norm of the learned embedding on Extended Yale B and also compares the singular values of the raw data, the autoencoder-only features, and the full SEDSC features.

What to notice is that the embedding norm keeps falling instead of settling into a stable, meaningful scale. That is exactly what the scaling argument predicts. The singular-value plot says something even stronger: the raw data and the autoencoder-only features are closer to a linear-subspace geometry than the full SEDSC embedding.

That is a bad sign for a method whose whole purpose is to learn a better subspace-like latent space.

Clustering accuracy with and without post-processing

The paper also re-runs the original benchmark experiments and checks what happens when the coefficient matrix \mathbf{C} is post-processed or left alone. The post-processing pipeline includes hard thresholding, a shape interaction matrix transform, and an entrywise power operation.

The main lesson is that a large part of the apparent gain can come from that extra post-processing, not from the learned embedding itself. On YaleB, for example, full SEDSC gets 59.09% without post-processing, while autoencoder-only features get 71.96%. With post-processing applied consistently, the numbers jump to 96.79% for full SEDSC and 97.12% for autoencoder-only. Similar patterns show up on COIL100 and ORL: once the same post-processing is applied across methods, the full model is often no better, and sometimes worse, than simpler baselines.

That is why the paper is so critical of evaluation protocol. If the final score depends heavily on an ad hoc transformation of \mathbf{C}, then the reported win is not really proof that the latent objective learned the right geometry.

The synthetic experiments make the same point in a cleaner setting. On a toy dataset of two parabolas, the dataset-normalized model learns almost exactly the degenerate pattern predicted by the theorem: most points are pushed close to the origin, two points remain on a line, and the recovered coefficient matrix is dominated by just a couple of entries.

What to remember

The cleanest takeaway is that self-expression is not enough on its own. If the embedding is free to shrink, the model can win by collapsing scale. If you add normalization, the model can still prefer duplicated points, sign-flipped copies, or rank-one latent lines. If you look only at the final clustering score, post-processing can hide a lot of that failure.

So the paper is best remembered as a warning about objective design. The deep self-expressive recipe sounds like it should preserve cluster geometry, but without explicit geometric protection it can optimize away the very structure clustering needs.
