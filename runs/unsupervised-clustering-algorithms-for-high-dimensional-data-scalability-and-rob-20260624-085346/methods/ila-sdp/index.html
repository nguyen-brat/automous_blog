Likelihood adjusted semidefinite programming for clustering heterogeneous data

This paper is trying to fix a very specific clustering pain point: what if the clusters do not have the same shape?

K-means assumes that every cluster can be treated with the same spherical geometry. EM is more flexible, but it still spends a lot of effort repeatedly estimating centroids, and that can make it brittle when the initialization is bad or the cluster centers sit in an awkward configuration. LA-SDP takes a different route. It says: if the centroids are just nuisance variables, remove them first, then search over cluster memberships using a likelihood objective that still remembers the covariance structure of each cluster.

That is the main idea in one sentence. The method is not really about centroids anymore. It is about finding the partition that makes the observed data most plausible once each cluster’s shape has been taken into account.

Start with the part every method wants to hide

If you already knew which points belonged to a cluster, the best centroid would just be the average of those points. The paper uses that fact to its advantage. Instead of optimizing centroids and labels together, it profiles out the centroids and keeps only the parts of the likelihood that still depend on the partition and the covariance matrices.

That matters because heterogeneous clusters change the scoring rule. A point may be a good fit for one covariance matrix and a bad fit for another, so the objective has to keep track of shape, not just distance. LA-SDP does that by turning the label search into a semidefinite program over soft membership matrices.

Purpose: Turn clustering into a convex search over soft memberships that still respects each cluster’s shape.

Equation:

Intuition: Each Z_k behaves like a relaxed membership block for cluster k. The score matrix A_k rewards points and pairs of points that fit cluster k under its covariance-aware geometry.

Symbols: Z_k is the soft membership matrix for cluster k. A_k is the cluster-specific score matrix built from the current covariance \Sigma_k and the observed data. \operatorname{tr}(Z_k) measures the total mass assigned to cluster k. \mathbf{1} is the all-ones vector. Z_k \ge 0 means entrywise nonnegative. Z_k \succeq 0 means positive semidefinite.

Read-aloud meaning: “Choose a valid relaxed partition that maximizes covariance-aware fit.”

Why it matters: This is the central replacement for centroid chasing. The label step becomes a global convex problem instead of a local responsibility update.

If all clusters shared the same spherical covariance, this construction collapses back to the classic SDP relaxation for K-means. That is a useful sanity check: LA-SDP is not inventing a new clustering universe, it is extending the old SDP picture to settings where cluster shape really matters.

The real object is a membership matrix

The easiest way to read the paper is to stop thinking about a single cluster assignment vector and start thinking in blocks. In the isotropic case, one membership matrix is enough. In the heterogeneous case, the paper lifts the problem to K cluster-specific membership matrices, one per cluster. That extra bookkeeping is what lets the method keep track of different covariance structures without flattening everything into one global similarity score.

The LA-SDP memberships to cluster labels figure makes that concrete. The solver first produces soft membership blocks, and only then does spectral rounding turn the block structure into hard labels.

What to notice is the sequence, not just the picture itself. The optimization does not jump straight to final labels. It builds a relaxed matrix that already has the right block pattern, then rounding reads that pattern and converts it into a partition. That is the same broad idea behind classic SDP clustering, but here each block is cluster-specific because each cluster can have its own covariance.

Oracle LA-SDP: when the geometry is already known

The paper first studies the oracle version, where the covariance matrices are known. This is the cleanest way to see why the method works.

In that setting, the SDP is optimizing the exact likelihood score with the right covariance-aware geometry already plugged in. The theory says exact recovery is possible when two kinds of separation are strong enough.

The first is a covariance-adjusted center gap. The paper does not care only about Euclidean distance between means. It cares about how far apart the means look after each cluster has been whitened by its own covariance. That is the right notion of separation when shapes differ.

The second is a covariance contrast term. If two clusters have very similar covariances, it is easier to confuse them. If their covariance matrices are very different, the method needs more separation to avoid ambiguity. The theorem packages that into a cluster-pairwise covariance contrast measure.

So the recovery story is not “centers are far apart” in the naive sense. It is “centers are far apart in the metric induced by the cluster geometry, and the covariances themselves are distinguishable enough that one cluster cannot impersonate another too easily.” When those conditions hold, the SDP relaxation returns the true block structure.

When the covariances are unknown, the paper alternates

Real data rarely gives you the covariances for free, so the practical algorithm, iLA-SDP, alternates between two steps.

First it solves the LA-SDP label problem using the current covariance estimates. Then it updates each covariance matrix from the current soft memberships. Repeat that loop until the observed-data likelihood stops improving.

Purpose: Re-estimate cluster shape from the soft memberships.

Equation:

Intuition: Once the membership weights tell you how much each point belongs to cluster k, the best covariance is just the weighted spread of those points around their weighted mean.

Symbols: w_{k,i} is the soft weight for point i in cluster k. \hat{\mu}_k is the weighted cluster mean. n_k is the effective sample size of cluster k. In the soft-clustering interpretation used by the paper, the weights can be read from the leading nonzero eigenvector of Z_k when Z_k has rank one.

Read-aloud meaning: “Take the weighted covariance of the points assigned to cluster k.”

Why it matters: This is the iterative piece that lets iLA-SDP adapt its geometry as the memberships sharpen.

The important part is that the covariance update is closed form. The algorithm does not need to search over covariance matrices with a second expensive inner optimization. It uses the current memberships as soft assignments, then recomputes the cluster shapes directly. That is why the method still feels like an alternating maximization scheme even though its label step is not the same as EM.

Why this is not just EM with a new coat of paint

EM and iLA-SDP both alternate. That is the superficial similarity. Under the hood, they are doing different jobs.

EM treats labels as latent variables and optimizes a surrogate lower bound on the observed-data likelihood. The responsibility weights are posterior probabilities under the current parameters. The method then updates the parameters from those probabilities.

iLA-SDP treats labels as optimization variables. The label step is not a posterior calculation; it is a global convex relaxation of the observed-data likelihood after the centroids have been profiled out. That difference is subtle but important. It is why the paper can be more stable under bad initialization and why adversarial centroid configurations do not derail the label update in the same way they can hurt EM.

The covariance update still feels EM-like because both methods end up computing weighted means and weighted covariances. But the source of the weights is different. In EM, the weights come from the posterior. In iLA-SDP, they come from the SDP membership structure.

That is also why the paper emphasizes monotonicity. Each alternating step improves or preserves the observed-data likelihood, even if the paper does not claim a neat closed-form convergence rate.

What the paper assumes, and what it does to scale

The main limitation is simple: the method assumes the cluster covariances are non-singular. If a true covariance is singular, the formulation breaks and the data need to be projected into a suitable lower-dimensional space first.

High-dimensional covariance estimation is the other pressure point. If p is large, estimating a full covariance matrix can get noisy fast. The appendix suggests dimension reduction tricks such as LDA-style projection with feature selection and F-test screening. For large n, the paper also leans on low-rank Burer-Monteiro style approximations so the SDP is less expensive to solve.

So the practical picture is not “solve one giant SDP and be done.” It is “use a likelihood-aware SDP when cluster shape matters, but trim the problem first when dimension or sample size makes the covariance estimates too noisy or the optimization too expensive.”

What to remember

LA-SDP is best remembered as K-means SDP with covariance awareness and no centroid chasing.

The method profiles out the centroids, keeps the cluster shape information, and solves for soft membership matrices globally. The iterative version then alternates that membership step with a covariance update, so the geometry can adapt as the partition improves. If the clusters differ in shape, that is often a better fit than forcing everything through one centroid-driven loop.
