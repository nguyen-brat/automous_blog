Comparative Analysis of Clustering Algorithms for High-Dimensional Data

This paper is not trying to invent a new clustering primitive. It is trying to answer a more practical question: once the data are high-dimensional, which clustering pipeline should you trust, and how much does preprocessing matter before you even choose the algorithm? The paper answers that by comparing K-means, DBSCAN, and Spectral Clustering across three datasets, three dimensionality-reduction options, and three kinds of metrics.

The key idea is simple but easy to miss: in high dimensions, clustering quality is often decided as much by the map as by the rule that cuts the map. UMAP, PCA, and t-SNE reshape the geometry first; only then do the clusterers get a chance to succeed or fail.

That is the real output of the paper. Not a single best algorithm, but a way to choose the right pipeline for the shape of the data and the budget you have.

The quiet first move: put every feature on the same scale

Before any reduction or clustering happens, the paper standardizes the features. This is not decorative preprocessing. Distance-based methods live or die by scale. If one feature has a much larger numeric range than the others, it can dominate the geometry and make the clusterer chase the wrong directions.

Formula Card: Z-score normalization

Purpose: put every feature on a comparable scale before distance-based clustering.

Equation:

z=x−μσz = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}z=σx−μ​

Intuition: recenter each feature around zero and divide by its spread so no coordinate gets to overpower the rest just because of its units.

Symbols:

  • z: standardized feature value
  • x: original feature value
  • \mu: mean of that feature
  • \sigma: standard deviation of that feature

Read-aloud meaning: subtract the average, then divide by the amount of variation.

Why it matters: all three clusterers in the paper rely on geometry, whether through distances, densities, or neighborhood graphs. If the scale is wrong, every downstream comparison is distorted.

The practical effect is easy to picture. Imagine one sensor channel measured in thousands and another measured in decimals. Without standardization, the larger-scale channel would swamp the smaller one, even if the smaller one carries the real cluster signal. Z-score normalization removes that accidental bias before the actual comparison starts.

PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP are not just visual tools here

The paper uses three different reduction methods, and the important thing is not that they all shrink dimensionality. It is that they shrink it in different ways.

PCA is the linear baseline. It keeps directions with large variance, which is useful when the main structure is already roughly aligned with linear axes. It is fast, stable, and easy to interpret, but it cannot untangle nonlinear shapes.

t-SNE is more local. It tries to preserve neighborhood relationships, which makes it attractive when nearby points matter more than global layout. The downside is that it can distort broader geometry, so a plot can look clean even when the global clustering picture is less stable.

UMAP is the most interesting of the three for this paper. It is the method that most consistently improves clustering across the benchmarked datasets. The reason is probably that it preserves local neighborhoods while keeping enough global structure to avoid turning the data into a disconnected sketch. That balance matters when the clusterer downstream is trying to read the shape of the data, not just the closest few neighbors.

This is easiest to understand with a toy picture. Think of the raw data as a tangled bundle of wires. PCA stretches the bundle along the strongest directions, which helps if the tangle is mostly linear. t-SNE pulls nearby wires together so local groups are easier to see. UMAP tries to keep the nearby wires together without shredding the bigger wiring diagram. For clustering, that extra balance is often what makes the difference.

What to notice in this figure is not the exact color palette. Notice the geometry: after UMAP, the clusters are more legible, and Spectral Clustering gets a cleaner partition than it does on raw features. This is a reminder that the pipeline is doing two jobs at once. UMAP makes the map readable, and the clusterer tries to draw boundaries on that map.

The paper’s quantitative results line up with that intuition. UMAP consistently improves ARI over raw features and PCA for all three algorithms and all three datasets. t-SNE helps too, but UMAP is the most reliable overall.

Three clusterers, three assumptions about what a cluster looks like

The most useful way to read the comparison is as three different geometry assumptions.

K-means assumes clusters are roughly spherical and centered around a prototype. That is why it is fast. Once you know the target number of clusters, the update rule is simple and efficient. But the price of that speed is a strong shape bias. If the true groups are not ball-like, K-means has to force them into a ball-shaped answer.

DBSCAN assumes clusters are dense regions separated by sparse space. That makes it especially good when the data contain irregular shapes or noise. It does not need the number of clusters up front, which is a big advantage when the class count is unknown. But it does need a good density threshold, and that threshold can be sensitive to how the data were preprocessed.

Spectral Clustering assumes the most useful structure lives in a similarity graph. Instead of asking for a centroid, it asks for a cut through the graph that separates strongly connected groups. That is why it can capture manifold-like structure better than a centroid method. The downside is compute. The graph machinery is much heavier than K-means, and that cost shows up very clearly in the runtime numbers.

The paper uses standard settings to keep the comparison fair: k=10 for MNIST and Fashion-MNIST, k=6 for HAR, minPts=10 for DBSCAN, and a nearest-neighbors graph for Spectral Clustering. The point is not that these are magic values. The point is that each method gets the kind of input it expects, and then the study asks which one actually handles the benchmark best.

How the paper judges the result from three angles at once

A single metric is not enough for clustering. The paper separates the evaluation into three views.

  • Internal metrics ask whether the clusters look clean from the inside. Silhouette asks whether points are closer to their own cluster than to other clusters. Davies-Bouldin rewards compact clusters that stay far apart. Calinski-Harabasz measures the balance between within-cluster spread and between-cluster separation.
  • External metrics ask whether the predicted clusters match the known labels. ARI adjusts for chance, so it is a stronger agreement score than a raw accuracy intuition would be. NMI measures how much information the clustering shares with the labels.
  • Computational metrics ask whether the method is practical. Runtime and memory matter, especially once the data are large or the pipeline has to run repeatedly.

That split is important because clustering can look good in one lens and bad in another. A method can make compact, well-separated clusters and still disagree with the human label taxonomy. Or it can match labels well while being too slow to use in practice. The paper is trying to expose those tradeoffs instead of hiding them behind one summary number.

The tradeoff becomes very clear in the benchmark figure.

What to notice here is the shape of the tradeoff, not just the bars. Spectral Clustering usually gives the best ARI, but K-means is far cheaper. Across the reported benchmarks, K-means is roughly 15 to 50 times faster than Spectral Clustering. DBSCAN sits between them in runtime, and its main strength is not speed but robustness to irregular cluster shape and noise. This is the kind of plot that turns an abstract “best method” discussion into a real engineering decision.

Why UMAP keeps winning the comparison

The first numerical table makes the main message hard to ignore: every algorithm improves when the raw data are replaced by a more useful representation, and UMAP is the most consistent winner among the reduction choices.

That pattern is especially strong for DBSCAN. Its ARI jumps dramatically once UMAP gives it a better neighborhood geometry, often by roughly a factor of two to three compared with raw features. That makes sense if you think about what DBSCAN needs. Density methods are very sensitive to whether “nearby” really means “same local region.” In high dimensions, raw Euclidean neighborhoods are often too noisy for that to work well. UMAP repairs that geometry enough for DBSCAN to become much more effective.

The same table also shows that Fashion-MNIST is harder than MNIST for every algorithm. That is not surprising once you remember that Fashion-MNIST has more complex class structure and less distinct boundaries. The benchmark is useful precisely because it includes both an easier image dataset and a more ambiguous one, so the geometry-sensitive methods can be compared under different levels of difficulty.

The headline result on MNIST is Spectral Clustering with UMAP, which reaches ARI 0.794. That is the paper’s strongest accuracy point, and it fits the story perfectly: once the data are reshaped into a better neighborhood space, the graph-based method can exploit that structure more effectively than the simpler alternatives.

Stability is not the same thing as accuracy

A method can score well on average and still be annoying to use if it swings around from run to run. The paper checks that too.

For the stability study, the authors repeat the algorithms many times on MNIST and look at how much the ARI moves. That matters because K-means and Spectral Clustering can depend on random initializations, while DBSCAN is deterministic once its parameters are fixed.

What to notice in this figure is the spread. DBSCAN has zero variation under fixed settings, so it is perfectly stable in this experiment. K-means has the most variability when paired with t-SNE, which suggests that a nonlinear embedding with more local distortion can make the random restarts more sensitive. Spectral Clustering with UMAP is the nicest compromise in the paper: it keeps the strongest ARI while also staying relatively stable.

That is an important practical point. A method that is slightly worse on the average score can still be the better choice if it is more repeatable. The paper does not reduce stability to a footnote; it treats it as part of the choice of pipeline.

When the shape of the data changes, the winner changes too

The synthetic experiments are the clearest way to understand why the methods differ. The paper varies cluster separability, shape, density, noise, and balance, then checks which algorithm handles each case best.

This figure is really a taxonomy of assumptions.

K-means does best when clusters are spherical, well-separated, and balanced. That is exactly the world its centroid model is built for. Once the data violate those assumptions, performance drops quickly.

DBSCAN is strongest on irregular shapes and noisy data. The classic moon-shaped example is the cleanest demonstration: density separation can recover structure that centroid methods flatten. It also handles varying densities and noise better than K-means, which is why the paper says it is the best fit when the clusters are non-spherical or contaminated.

Spectral Clustering remains strong across most cases, especially when the data have manifold-like structure. It does not need clusters to be balls, so it can stay competitive when the geometry bends. That is why it often comes out near the top on complex data, even if it cannot match K-means on speed.

This section is where the paper’s practical message becomes clearest. The right method is not the one with the highest theoretical prestige. It is the one whose shape assumption matches the actual data.

What the paper is really recommending

If you step back, the paper is making a pipeline argument.

First, normalize the features so scale does not distort geometry. Then reduce dimensionality before clustering, because the raw space is often too noisy to read directly. In this benchmark, UMAP is the safest default because it consistently improves clustering quality across methods and datasets. After that, choose the clusterer based on what you want the pipeline to emphasize:

  • If speed matters most, K-means is the practical winner.
  • If the data have irregular shapes, noise, or unknown cluster count, DBSCAN is the best fit.
  • If you want the strongest label agreement on complex structure and can afford the compute cost, Spectral Clustering is the top performer.

That advice is not universal gospel. It is the lesson of these datasets under these preprocessing choices. But it is a useful lesson because it shifts the question from “Which clustering algorithm is best?” to “Which combination of representation and clustering assumption matches this problem?”

The limits of the study

The authors are careful not to oversell the framework.

The comparison covers only three clustering algorithms, so it does not settle the full field. Parameter tuning is also limited rather than exhaustive, which means a different tuning strategy could move some numbers. The scalability story is also incomplete: the benchmark does not resolve what happens well beyond roughly 100,000 samples, especially for Spectral Clustering, where graph costs become the obvious bottleneck.

So the study should be read as a strong comparative map, not a final universal ranking. It tells you how these methods behave under a controlled set of choices, and that is already valuable. But it does not replace a full search over every modern clustering variant.

What to remember

The easiest way to remember the paper is this:

standardize -> reduce -> cluster -> score -> repeat

The deeper lesson is that clustering performance in high dimensions is often decided before the clusterer even runs. UMAP, PCA, and t-SNE change the geometry that the algorithm sees. K-means, DBSCAN, and Spectral Clustering then react to that geometry in different ways: centroid, density, or graph structure. The best result comes from matching the reduction and the clusterer to the data shape, not from treating clustering as a one-step black box.

If you keep that in mind, the paper becomes more than a benchmark. It becomes a practical guide for choosing a clustering pipeline when the data are too high-dimensional to trust raw distances on their own.
