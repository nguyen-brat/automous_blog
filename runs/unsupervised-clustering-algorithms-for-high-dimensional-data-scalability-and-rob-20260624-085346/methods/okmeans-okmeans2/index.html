Simple KNN Heuristics for Robust k-Means with Outliers

Robust k-means has a very plain-sounding difficulty that becomes surprisingly stubborn in practice: the algorithm must decide both where the k centers are and which z points should be ignored. This paper answers that problem with a method that is almost offensively simple. Score every point by how far it has to reach into its local neighborhood before it finds enough company, remove the z most suspicious points, and then run an ordinary k-means solver on what remains.

The first version, OKMeans, uses a single K-th-neighbor distance as the suspicion score. The second version, OKMeans2, widens that view into a neighborhood shell so that outliers cannot hide by clustering tightly together. The main contribution of the paper is not a fancy new optimizer. It is the idea that a local-density heuristic can serve as a reduction layer from robust k-means to standard k-means, with a real approximation guarantee and a runtime story that still scales.

Start from the objective

The paper begins with the robust clustering target itself. That is worth making explicit, because the whole method is trying to approximate this objective without directly solving it from scratch.

The target the paper is trying to optimize

Purpose: define robust k-means with exactly z removable outliers.

Equation:

min⁡Z⊆X, ∣Z∣=zmin⁡C: ∣C∣=k∑x∈X∖Zd(x,C)2\min_{Z\subseteq X,\ |Z|=z}\min_{C:\ |C|=k}\sum_{x\in X\setminus Z} d(x,C)^2Z⊆X, ∣Z∣=zmin​C: ∣C∣=kmin​x∈X∖Z∑​d(x,C)2

Intuition: choose which z points to ignore, then place k centers so the remaining points are as close as possible to their nearest center.

Symbols:

  • X: the input data set.
  • Z: the set of points removed as outliers.
  • C: the set of k cluster centers.
  • d(x,C): the distance from point x to its nearest center in C.
  • z: the outlier budget.
  • k: the number of centers.

Read-aloud meaning: remove z points, then minimize the total squared distance from every remaining point to its nearest of k centers.

Why it matters: OKMeans is useful because it turns this robust objective into an easier standard k-means problem on a filtered set.

The key design choice is also stricter than many earlier robust methods. The paper keeps exactly k centers and removes exactly z points. It does not rely on bicriteria tricks such as using extra centers or throwing away extra outliers. That matters because it keeps the reduction clean and makes the final output easier to interpret.

The theoretical story also rests on a practical assumption: every cluster in the optimal solution should have size at least c z for some constant c > 1. In plain language, the true clusters must be large enough that they do not get confused with a tiny pocket of bad points. The paper argues that this is a realistic assumption in robust clustering, because if a cluster is very small, it is genuinely hard to tell whether it is a real inlier group or just a weird arrangement of outliers.

OKMeans: use local crowding as an outlier score

The first heuristic is the cleanest to remember. A point is suspicious if it has to travel unusually far to find enough nearby neighbors.

Vanilla KNN score

Purpose: rank points by how isolated they look in the local neighborhood.

Equation:

rx=d(x,NNX(x,2z))r_x = d\bigl(x, \mathrm{NN}_X(x, 2z)\bigr)rx​=d(x,NNX​(x,2z))

Intuition: if a point belongs to a dense true cluster, then even its 2z-th nearest neighbor should still be relatively close. If it is an outlier, the neighborhood has to stretch much farther before it fills up.

Symbols:

  • r_x: the outlier score of point x.
  • \mathrm{NN}_X(x, 2z): the 2z-th nearest neighbor of x inside X.
  • d(\cdot,\cdot): the metric distance.
  • z: the outlier budget.

Read-aloud meaning: a point gets a high score when its 2z-th nearest neighbor is far away.

Why it matters: at the paper’s anchor setting c = 3, this is the exact score used by OKMeans. More generally, the theorem scales the neighborhood size linearly with z, and the approximation constant improves as the true cluster-size lower bound c grows.

The filtering step is then straightforward: compute this score for every point, remove the z points with the largest scores, and hand the remaining set to any standard k-means routine. If that downstream routine is a β-approximation, the whole pipeline inherits the same kind of guarantee, multiplied by the reduction factor from the KNN filter.

That is the main conceptual move in the paper. OKMeans is not trying to solve robust clustering directly. It is trying to make the robust problem look like an ordinary clustering problem after the right points are removed.

Why the proof idea is believable

The theorem looks simple because the intuition really is simple, even though the formal proof has to work through several cases.

The proof compares two kinds of mistakes the filter can make:

  • It can accidentally remove a true inlier.
  • It can accidentally keep a true outlier.

The large-cluster assumption is what makes both mistakes expensive. If a true inlier gets a large KNN score, then either it is genuinely far from the cluster center, or the neighborhood around it is already contaminated enough that many nearby points are also costly. If a true outlier gets a small score, then it must be sitting close to many inliers, which means those inliers would also contribute significant cost if the outlier were kept.

That is the hidden reason KNN works here. The score is not just a generic density heuristic. It is a way of forcing a contradiction when the wrong type of point looks too attractive. The paper’s theorem says that under the large-cluster assumption, the z-point filtering step can only distort the robust objective by a constant factor, and for c = 3 that factor is 9.

The most important detail is that the neighborhood scale has to grow with z. A fixed tiny K would only inspect a very small pocket around each point, which is not enough to separate a genuine sparse corner of a real cluster from a small pocket of outliers. By tying the score to z, the method makes the neighborhood big enough to “feel” the outlier budget.

OKMeans2: stop outliers from helping each other

The second method starts from a subtle failure mode of the first one. If you simply sum the distances to the K nearest neighbors, a bunch of outliers can sit close together and make each other look deceptively dense. In other words, the bad points can help one another.

OKMeans2 fixes that by ignoring the nearest z neighbors and only scoring the broader neighborhood shell. That way, the points nearest to x do not get to dominate the score. The method is still KNN-based, but it is less gullible.

Neighborhood-shell score

Purpose: rank points while ignoring the closest z neighbors that might be colluding.

Equation:

sx=∑i=z+13zd(x,NNX(x,i))s_x = \sum_{i=z+1}^{3z} d\bigl(x, \mathrm{NN}_X(x,i)\bigr)sx​=i=z+1∑3z​d(x,NNX​(x,i))

Intuition: the first z neighbors are too easy to game if outliers cluster together. The wider shell from z+1 to 3z is harder to fake because it forces the score to look beyond the local pocket.

Symbols:

  • s_x: the outlier score of point x.
  • \mathrm{NN}_X(x,i): the i-th nearest neighbor of x in X.
  • d(\cdot,\cdot): the metric distance.
  • z: the outlier budget.

Read-aloud meaning: add the distances from the neighbors just outside the nearest z points, up to the 3z-th neighbor.

Why it matters: this small change strengthens the approximation guarantee. At c = 3, the paper improves the reduction factor from 9 for OKMeans to about 5.98 for OKMeans2.

This is the nicest technical trick in the paper. It does not change the overall pipeline at all. It only changes what counts as evidence of local density. That is enough to make the analysis tighter, because the score is now harder for adversarial outliers to manipulate.

If OKMeans says, “I trust the K-th neighbor distance,” OKMeans2 says, “I trust the neighborhood, but not the closest few points that might be part of the problem itself.” That is a small change in wording, but a meaningful change in behavior.

Where the idea is fragile

The method is intentionally simple, so its assumptions matter.

The strongest limitation is the cluster-size assumption. The theory wants each optimal cluster to have at least c z points. If the data contain a real but tiny cluster, then the KNN score can blur that cluster together with outliers. The method has no way to know whether a sparse region is a genuine minority group or an anomaly pocket.

The approximation bounds are also not tight. The paper is honest about that. The constants are good enough to show that the heuristic is principled, but they are not presented as the final word on the best possible analysis.

There is also a scalability caveat. If you compute KNN distances directly on the full data set, the cost is quadratic in n. That would defeat the point of the heuristic on large data, so the paper uses a coreset first.

Why the coreset changes everything

The coreset is the practical bridge between the elegant theory and the large-data runtime.

The idea is to compress the data into a much smaller representative set, then run the KNN filter and k-means on that reduced set. The paper cites an O(1)-coreset of size O(k log n) that can be built in O(d k n log n) time. Once the data are that small, the KNN computation is cheap enough that it no longer dominates the runtime.

So the full pipeline becomes:

  1. Build a small coreset.
  2. Compute the KNN-based outlier score on that coreset.
  3. Remove the z points with the highest scores.
  4. Run a standard k-means solver on the filtered set.

That is why the paper can claim near-linear scaling in n rather than a raw quadratic neighbor search. The heavy work is moved into the coreset construction, and the rest of the method runs on a compact summary.

What the experiments say

The empirical results support the same story. The paper compares OKMeans and OKMeans2 against several robust clustering baselines and also checks how sensitive the method is to the choice of K.

On large data sets, the simple KNN filter is not just competitive. It is often faster than much more elaborate robust methods while keeping clustering cost in the same range or better. For example, on SUSY-10, OKMeans reports a mean cost of 4.873e7 versus 5.272e7 for TIKMeans, while running in about 12.9 seconds instead of about 89.2 seconds. On KDDFULL, OKMeans runs in about 9.51 seconds versus about 49.85 seconds for TIKMeans.

The parameter-sensitivity results are especially revealing. Fixed small K values can look reasonable on some sets, but they are not consistently strong on the larger ones. On KDDFULL, the constant-K variants have noticeably worse costs than the scaled-K choice used by OKMeans, even though the runtime barely changes. That is a nice empirical confirmation of the theory: the outlier budget should set the neighborhood scale.

The paper also reports that moderate values of c are usually enough. Performance is fairly stable across a reasonable range, which means the method is not extremely fragile once the neighborhood scale is in the right ballpark.

What to remember

  • OKMeans is a reduction: score points by KNN distance, remove the top z suspected outliers, and then run ordinary k-means.
  • The neighborhood size should grow with z. A tiny fixed K is often too local to separate real clusters from outliers.
  • OKMeans2 improves the score by ignoring the nearest z neighbors, which prevents outliers from helping one another.
  • The large-cluster assumption is the price of the guarantee. If true clusters are too small, the heuristic can confuse them with anomalies.
  • Coresets are what make the method practical at scale.

If you want one mental image to keep, use this: a point that has to travel far before it can find enough neighbors is probably not sitting in the real inlier mass. The whole paper is a careful argument for turning that intuition into a usable robust clustering algorithm.
