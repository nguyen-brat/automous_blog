Two-stage Ensemble Random Projection Clustering for Functional Data

TERP and TERPM are easiest to remember as a projection pipeline that keeps changing the lens. The paper does not try to force functional data into one carefully chosen basis. Instead, it throws many Gaussian-process directions at each curve, turns those projections into a high-dimensional vector, clusters that representation with MADD, and then comes back for a second pass using directions learned from the first partition. TERP is the regular-grid version of that idea; TERPM is the version adapted to irregular and fragmented curves.

The whole flow is simple enough to sketch as text:

What matters is not any single lens. What matters is that the method keeps asking the same question from many directions: do these curves look like they belong together, and do they keep looking that way after the first rough partition?

Why random projections help curves

Functional data are awkward because the raw objects are not vectors in the usual sense. A curve can be observed on a common dense grid, on a sparse grid, or on a curve-specific grid with missing stretches. So the first problem is representation, not clustering. TERP solves that by replacing “one basis that should explain everything” with “many random directions that each reveal one slice of the curve.”

The theoretical motivation is reassuringly strong. Under a mild moment condition, different functional distributions almost surely stay different after projection onto a Gaussian random direction. In plain language: if two populations of curves really differ, a random Gaussian lens is very unlikely to hide that difference forever. Sampling many such lenses makes it more likely that at least some of them expose the separation.

Formula Card: Random projection vector

Purpose: Turn each curve into a finite vector of many random measurements.

Equation:

Xi∗=(⟨Xi,Z1⟩,⟨Xi,Z2⟩,…,⟨Xi,ZM⟩).\mathbf{X}_i^*=(\langle X_i,Z_1\rangle,\langle X_i,Z_2\rangle,\dots,\langle X_i,Z_M\rangle).Xi∗​=(⟨Xi​,Z1​⟩,⟨Xi​,Z2​⟩,…,⟨Xi​,ZM​⟩).

Intuition: Each Gaussian-process direction is one lens on the same curve. A single lens can miss structure, but many lenses can accumulate it.

Symbols:

Read-aloud meaning: Represent curve XiX_iXi​ by its inner products with MMM random Gaussian-process directions.

Why it matters: This is the bridge from an infinite-dimensional curve space to a finite representation that can still preserve cluster-separating information.

The paper uses several projection families, not because it wants extra complexity for its own sake, but because different families reveal different kinds of structure. Some directions are smoother, some are more localized, and some are better at global oscillation. That diversity is exactly what you want when you do not know in advance whether the cluster signal lives in mean shape, roughness, or covariance structure.

Why MADD instead of Euclidean distance

Once the curves are projected into a large vector, ordinary Euclidean distance is no longer the right instinct. High-dimensional coordinates tend to blur together, and raw distances can become too similar to separate clusters reliably. TERP avoids that trap with MADD, which compares distance profiles rather than raw coordinates.

The idea is intuitive: two points are similar if they “see” the rest of the dataset in the same way. If their distances to all other observations look alike, then they probably belong to the same cluster, even if the coordinate system itself is large and awkward.

Formula Card: MADD dissimilarity

Purpose: Compare two projected observations by asking whether they see the rest of the dataset in the same way.

Equation:

Intuition: Two points are close when their distances to the other observations have similar patterns.

Symbols:

  • ρ(v,w)\rho(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w})ρ(v,w): MADD dissimilarity between two projected observations.
  • d(v,u)d(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u})d(v,u): base distance between projected vectors.
  • nnn: number of observations in the dataset.
  • u\mathbf{u}u: another observation used to compare the distance profiles of v\mathbf{v}v and w\mathbf{w}w.

Read-aloud meaning: Compare two curves by checking whether they relate to the rest of the dataset in the same way.

Why it matters: This avoids relying on raw Euclidean separation, which becomes unreliable in the high-dimensional projected space.

The base distance inside MADD is Laplace-style and coordinatewise, so a few large coordinate gaps do not dominate the whole comparison. That choice matters because the projected vectors are intentionally large. TERP wants many coordinates, but it does not want any single coordinate to bully the rest of the geometry.

TERP two-stage ensemble pipeline

The method has two passes for a reason. Stage I gets a coarse partition from generic random projections. Stage II uses that coarse partition to estimate a better covariance structure and then generates new projection directions from that estimate. The second pass is not a cosmetic refinement. It is the part that can recover structure that generic random directions did not emphasize strongly enough.

Stage I: the first pass

In the first pass, the paper forms a partition S={S1,…,SK}\mathcal{S}=\{S_1,\dots,S_K\}S={S1​,…,SK​} and minimizes a within-cluster MADD objective on the projected vectors. The important move is that the cost is normalized by total dispersion, so two different projection families or two different projection counts can be compared on the same scale. Without that normalization, a solution produced with one covariance family might look artificially better or worse simply because the projected data have a different magnitude.

Formula Card: Scale-invariant clustering objective

Purpose: Score a candidate partition in a way that can be compared across different projection settings.

Equation:

Φ~(i)(S)=Φ(i)(S)T(i),i∈{1,2}\widetilde{\Phi}^{(i)}(\mathcal S)=\frac{\Phi^{(i)}(\mathcal S)}{T^{(i)}},\qquad i\in\{1,2\}Φ(i)(S)=T(i)Φ(i)(S)​,i∈{1,2}

Intuition: A partition should be judged relative to the total spread of the projected data, not by raw cost alone.

Symbols:

  • S\mathcal SS: a partition of the data indices into KKK non-empty clusters.
  • Φ(i)(S)\Phi^{(i)}(\mathcal S)Φ(i)(S): the within-cluster squared MADD cost at stage iii.
  • T(i)T^{(i)}T(i): total MADD dispersion of the projected data at stage iii.
  • iii: stage index, with 111 for the first pass and 222 for the second pass.

Read-aloud meaning: Within-cluster cost divided by total dispersion, at either stage one or stage two.

Why it matters: This is what makes the ensemble selection meaningful. Different Gaussian-process families and different values of MMM can now be judged on a common scale.

So Stage I is basically the broad sweep: project, measure with MADD, and cluster. It gives the method a plausible initial partition without making any claim that the first answer is already the best answer.

Stage II: let the first partition choose better directions

Stage II begins by taking the first-stage labels and computing cluster-wise mean functions. The curves are then centered within those provisional clusters, and a pooled covariance operator is estimated from the centered sample. From that estimated covariance, the method draws new Gaussian random directions and projects the original curves again.

That is the key twist. The second set of lenses is not generic anymore. It is shaped by what the first pass thought the clusters looked like. If the first pass already found a rough separation, the covariance estimate can expose directions tied to cluster-specific spread or smoothness. That is exactly why the second stage helps most when the classes differ in scale or covariance structure rather than just in mean shape.

After the new projections are formed, the same MADD-based clustering objective is run again. The output is a refined partition that can either improve on the first pass or, if the first pass was already better, leave the coarse structure mostly intact.

This is also where TERPM earns its name in the irregular-data setting. When the observation grid is not common across curves, the method still keeps the first-stage projection idea, but the second-stage covariance estimate has to be built with a missing-data functional estimator such as PACE. The workflow stays the same; only the covariance recovery step changes.

What makes the ensemble part useful

The paper does not commit to one Gaussian-process family or one value of MMM. It tries several choices and keeps the best normalized solution. That matters because no single projection family is guaranteed to be best for every dataset. Some datasets reward smoother directions, others reward more local structure, and the right number of projections also varies.

The ensemble is therefore not an extra flourish. It is the method’s way of admitting that the right lens is data-dependent. TERP/TERPM still makes a concrete choice at the end, but it does so after testing a small family of plausible projection settings rather than trusting a single guess.

Why the irregular-data version is important

This is the part that makes TERPM more than a regular-grid clustering trick. Real functional data are often observed on different time grids, and some curves have missing contiguous segments. Standard smoothing-first methods can struggle there, because the smoothing step itself may bias the geometry before clustering even starts.

TERPM avoids that by working directly with the observed points. In the first stage, inner products are approximated with numerical quadrature on each curve’s own grid. In the second stage, the covariance estimate is rebuilt from the incomplete sample using a functional missing-data estimator. The method is still trying to do the same thing as TERP: preserve relative geometry while avoiding a brittle single-basis representation.

What the experiments suggest

The empirical message is consistent with the design. On regular functional data, TERP is usually best or second-best across the simulated and real examples. On irregular real datasets, TERPM is especially strong, which makes sense because the covariance-informed second pass is doing real work there. The paper also reports that the second stage is particularly helpful when the classes differ mainly in scale or smoothness.

The more general lesson is that the benefit does not come from projection alone. It comes from the combination of many projections, a distance built for high-dimensional projected vectors, and a refinement pass that learns where the next projections should look.

Where it is fragile

The paper is clear about the limits. The number of clusters KKK has to be preset. Performance drops when trajectories are very sparse or heavily fragmented, because the projection step has less reliable inner-product information to work with. And the ensemble over many projection choices adds runtime, even though the underlying method is still computationally simple compared with heavier functional clustering pipelines.

There is also an open design question about the MADD kernel choice. The paper uses a Laplace-style kernel, but it is natural to ask whether another characteristic kernel could sharpen the distance-profile signal further. A related open problem is how to choose KKK jointly with the projection settings instead of treating KKK as fixed input.

What to remember

TERP/TERPM is a “many views, then refine the views” method. First, it projects each curve onto many Gaussian-process directions so the infinite-dimensional problem becomes a high-dimensional one. Then it clusters that projected data with MADD, which compares distance profiles instead of raw coordinates. Finally, it uses the first partition to build better covariance-informed projections and chooses the best normalized solution across several projection families and projection counts.

If you want the one-line memory hook, use this:

many Gaussian-process lenses -> MADD on projected vectors -> covariance-informed second pass -> normalized ensemble winner

That is the whole method in one sentence, and it explains why the paper can stay effective across regular, irregular, and partially observed functional data.
