High-dimensional Clustering onto Hamiltonian Cycle

HCHC is trying to solve a gap that shows up in a lot of clustering work: the model may be able to assign labels, but the output still does not explain which clusters are related, which points are ambiguous, and which samples are best thought of as outliers. The paper’s answer is a two-stage pipeline. First, GLDC learns soft cluster probabilities with a deep objective that keeps local neighborhoods together while separating unrelated points. Then HCHC turns those probabilities into a circle layout, using an optimal Hamiltonian cycle so similar clusters sit next to each other around the ring.

A good mental image is a wheel of magnets. Each cluster is one magnet on the rim. A confident sample is pulled toward one magnet, a mixed sample lands between neighboring magnets, and a point with no strong allegiance drifts toward the center.

The pipeline at a glance

HCHC starts from high-dimensional samples and their Gaussian-noise augmentations. GLDC compresses the data into latent codes, learns probability vectors over clusters, and then the Hamiltonian-cycle stage converts those probability vectors into 2D anchor positions.

What to notice here is the split of labor. The left half learns cluster probabilities; the right half turns those probabilities into geometry. That separation matters because the visualization is not trying to rediscover clusters from scratch. It is trying to expose what the clustering model already believes.

GLDC learns soft clusters instead of hard labels

The first step is a standard but important one: pretrain an autoencoder so the latent space still remembers the salient structure of the input. If the encoder-decoder pair can reconstruct the input well, the clustering head starts from a representation that is not already scrambled by noise.

Formula card: reconstruction warm start

Purpose: train the autoencoder to keep the important information in the latent space.

Lr=∑i=1n∥xi−Gθ′(Gθ(xi))∥22L_r = \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left\|\mathbf{x}_i - G_{\theta'}(G_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i))\right\|_2^2Lr​=i=1∑n​∥xi​−Gθ′​(Gθ​(xi​))∥22​

Intuition: if the decoder can rebuild the input from the latent code, the latent code is carrying something useful rather than noise.

Symbols:

  • L_r: reconstruction loss
  • \mathbf{x}_i: input sample i
  • G_\theta: encoder
  • G_{\theta'}: decoder
  • G_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i): latent code for sample i
  • \|\cdot\|_2^2: squared Euclidean reconstruction error

Read-aloud meaning: encode each sample, decode it back, and penalize the difference from the original input.

Why it matters: GLDC does not want to learn cluster assignments from a representation that has already forgotten the structure of the data.

Once the latent space exists, GLDC builds a weighted kNN graph in that space. Connected neighbors should behave alike; points with no graph connection should not collapse into the same probability pattern. That is the paper’s local-plus-global idea in one sentence.

Formula card: graph consistency in probability space

Purpose: make nearby latent neighbors share similar cluster probabilities while pushing unrelated points apart.

Lw=−1B2∑i,j=1B[wijlog⁡(pi⊤pj)+(1−wij)log⁡ ⁣(1−pi⊤pj)]L_w = -\frac{1}{B^2}\sum_{i,j=1}^{B}\left[w_{ij}\log(\mathbf{p}_i^\top \mathbf{p}_j) + (1-w_{ij})\log\!\left(1-\mathbf{p}_i^\top \mathbf{p}_j\right)\right]Lw​=−B21​i,j=1∑B​[wij​log(pi⊤​pj​)+(1−wij​)log(1−pi⊤​pj​)]

Intuition: if two samples are connected in the latent graph, their probability vectors should align; if they are not connected, the model should avoid making them look the same.

Symbols:

  • L_w: graph learning loss
  • B: mini-batch size
  • w_{ij}: weighted adjacency between samples i and j
  • \mathbf{p}_i: soft cluster probability vector for sample i
  • \mathbf{p}_i^\top \mathbf{p}_j: probability that the two samples belong to the same cluster

Read-aloud meaning: connected points should have similar cluster distributions, and disconnected points should not.

Why it matters: this is how GLDC keeps local structure inside clusters while still learning relationships between different clusters.

The graph term is the part that makes the method feel different from a plain clustering head. It is not only saying “put this sample in a cluster.” It is saying “make the whole probability vector behave sensibly relative to the neighborhood graph.” That is why the output can later be used to talk about similarities between clusters, not just final labels.

GLDC also adds a simple stability check: the same sample and a lightly noised version of that sample should land on nearly the same probability vector. In the paper, the augmentation is Gaussian noise, so the model is not being asked to survive a dramatic transformation. It is being asked not to overreact to small perturbations.

Formula card: augmentation stability

Purpose: keep the cluster probabilities stable under small Gaussian perturbations.

La=∑i∥pi−p~i∥22L_a = \sum_i \left\|\mathbf{p}_i - \widetilde{\mathbf{p}}_i\right\|_2^2La​=i∑​∥pi​−p​i​∥22​

Intuition: if a tiny bit of noise changes the cluster assignment a lot, the assignment is probably not trustworthy.

Symbols:

  • L_a: augmentation consistency loss
  • \mathbf{p}_i: probability vector for the original sample
  • \widetilde{\mathbf{p}}_i: probability vector for the augmented sample

Read-aloud meaning: the original point and its noised copy should produce almost the same cluster probabilities.

Why it matters: this makes the assignments more stable and less sensitive to small input fluctuations.

The three pieces come together in one objective.

Formula card: the full GLDC objective

Purpose: combine reconstruction, local/global graph learning, and augmentation consistency into one training objective.

Lclu=Lr+β1Lw+β2LaL_{\text{clu}} = L_r + \beta_1 L_w + \beta_2 L_aLclu​=Lr​+β1​Lw​+β2​La​

Intuition: reconstruction keeps the representation grounded, graph learning encodes structure, and augmentation keeps the probabilities stable.

Symbols:

  • L_{\text{clu}}: total clustering loss
  • L_r: reconstruction loss
  • L_w: graph learning loss
  • L_a: augmentation consistency loss
  • \beta_1, \beta_2: weights that balance the three terms

Read-aloud meaning: train the model by adding together the reconstruction, graph, and augmentation losses with learned importance weights.

Why it matters: this is the part of the method that makes the probability vectors rich enough to support both clustering and visualization.

At the end of this stage, the model can still produce a hard label with c_i = \arg\max_j p_{i,j}, but the soft vector is the real payload. The hard label is just the final decision; the probability vector is what HCHC later uses to draw the map.

From probabilities to a circle

Once GLDC has produced the probability matrix, the paper looks at the columns rather than the rows. Each column is the membership pattern for one cluster across all samples. If two columns rise and fall together, those clusters are similar. If they move in opposite directions, they are less alike.

This is where the circle becomes meaningful. HCHC does not place cluster anchors randomly around a ring. It orders them so similar clusters become neighbors on a Hamiltonian cycle. Then the cycle is wrapped around a circle, and the circular distance becomes a readable version of inter-cluster similarity.

What to notice in the figure is that the anchor order is doing most of the work. The circle itself is just the canvas. The real information is in the ordering of the anchors and the fact that the distances between them reflect how similar the clusters are.

Formula card: cycle score for similar clusters

Purpose: choose a cluster order that keeps similar clusters adjacent on the ring.

Ssam=∑i=1c−1s(ρi,ρi+1)+s(ρ1,ρc)S_{\text{sam}} = \sum_{i=1}^{c-1} s(\rho^i,\rho^{i+1}) + s(\rho^1,\rho^c)Ssam​=i=1∑c−1​s(ρi,ρi+1)+s(ρ1,ρc)

Intuition: if the neighboring clusters along the cycle are very similar, the ring is doing a better job of preserving inter-cluster relationships.

Symbols:

  • S_{\text{sam}}: total similarity collected along the cycle
  • c: number of clusters
  • \rho^i: the ith cluster-probability column
  • s(\rho^i,\rho^j): Pearson correlation between two cluster-probability columns

Read-aloud meaning: the best cycle is the one whose neighboring clusters have the largest total similarity.

Why it matters: this is what turns the ordering problem into a similarity-preserving layout instead of a random arrangement.

The paper solves that ordering as an optimal Hamiltonian cycle. The exact search is NP-hard, so this is the part that can become expensive as the number of clusters grows. In practice, the point is not that the method has a magical exact solver for every case. The point is that it uses the strongest available ordering rule to keep similar clusters close on the circle.

Once the anchors are fixed, each sample is mapped to a weighted average of anchor positions. This is the bridge between soft probabilities and 2D geometry.

Formula card: mapping a sample to the ring

Purpose: turn a sample’s cluster-probability vector into a 2D location.

μxi=∑j=1cpi,j μρj\mu_{\mathbf{x}_i} = \sum_{j=1}^{c} p_{i,j}\,\mu_{\rho^j}μxi​​=j=1∑c​pi,j​μρj​

Intuition: a sample sits near the anchors of the clusters it belongs to most strongly, and it moves toward the center when its membership is spread out.

Symbols:

  • \mu_{\mathbf{x}_i}: 2D position of sample i
  • p_{i,j}: probability that sample i belongs to cluster j
  • \mu_{\rho^j}: 2D position of cluster anchor j
  • c: number of clusters

Read-aloud meaning: place each sample at the probability-weighted average of the cluster anchors.

Why it matters: this is the step that turns clustering into a picture that can show both confidence and ambiguity.

A useful analogy is to think of the anchors as magnets on the rim of a wheel. A sample with one dominant cluster gets pulled near one magnet. A sample split across two similar clusters lands between them. A sample with weak support everywhere ends up near the center. That center is not just empty space; it is the method telling you that the point is ambiguous or outlying.

This is also the main difference from RadViz Deluxe. RadViz orders feature anchors and uses raw feature values as the weights. HCHC orders cluster anchors and uses cluster probabilities as the weights. That swap is what makes the picture explain clustering rather than just data geometry.

How to read the map

Once you understand the wheel, the map becomes easy to read:

  • tight blobs near an anchor are confident clusters,
  • nearby anchors are similar clusters,
  • points between anchors are mixed or borderline samples,
  • points near the center are low-confidence or outlier-like samples.

The paper’s examples make this concrete. On MNIST, similar digits such as 4 and 9 are placed closer together because their probability columns are similar. On Fashion, visually related categories are grouped near one another, which makes the map useful for diagnosis, not just for display.

That diagnostic role is the real payoff. HCHC is not only asking “what cluster does this sample belong to?” It is also asking “how close are the clusters to each other, and which samples do not fit cleanly anywhere?” Those are exactly the questions that ordinary hard labels hide.

What the experiments support

The reported numbers are best read as support for the mechanism rather than as a leaderboard chase. GLDC reaches 0.979 ACC on MNIST, 0.715 ACC on Fashion, and 0.834 ACC on Reuters10k. The ablation shows that removing the augmentation term or the reconstruction term hurts clustering quality, so both pieces matter. The paper also argues that HCHC makes similar clusters and central outliers easier to see than generic embedding methods such as t-SNE or UMAP in its examples.

Where it bends

The main tradeoff is visual and combinatorial.

  • The optimal Hamiltonian-cycle step is NP-hard, so large cluster counts may need approximations.
  • A 2D ring can crowd when too many clusters have to fit on one circle.
  • The layout depends on the chosen similarity measure, and the paper uses Pearson correlation between cluster-probability columns.

So the method is strongest when you want an interpretable map of a moderate number of clusters, not an arbitrary number of perfectly separated groups.

What to remember

HCHC is easiest to remember as “soft clustering first, circular explanation second.”

GLDC learns probability vectors that preserve local neighborhood consistency, global separation, and stability under small perturbations. HCHC then turns those probabilities into a ring ordered by cluster similarity, so neighboring anchors mean similar clusters and the center means ambiguity.
