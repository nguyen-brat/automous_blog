Extending TCLUST to higher dimensions

tHDDC is easiest to understand as a compromise between two things that usually pull clustering in opposite directions. TCLUST gives you trimming and robust likelihood fitting, but it starts to struggle when the covariance matrices become too large to estimate reliably. RLG gives you a subspace view of the world, but it is too blunt when clusters intersect or when the location of the projected point inside the subspace matters. tHDDC keeps the robust trimming idea from TCLUST, keeps the low-dimensional geometry idea from RLG, and then adds a more careful covariance model so each cluster can live near its own thin affine sheet instead of pretending to fill the whole ambient space.

If you want the method in one memory hook, use this:

each cluster lives near its own low-dimensional sheet, bad points get trimmed, and the variances are kept in check separately along and across that sheet

That is the whole paper in miniature.

Why TCLUST alone stops being enough

TCLUST is robust because it does not try to explain every point. It trims a fixed fraction of the least plausible observations, then fits Gaussian clusters under eigenvalue constraints so the solution does not collapse onto a few points. In low dimensions, that works well.

The problem is what happens when the ambient dimension grows. A full covariance matrix has too many degrees of freedom, initialization becomes fragile, and the algorithm spends a lot of effort trying to estimate shape that may not even be meaningful in the raw coordinate system. The paper’s motivation is practical: high-dimensional data are often not spread uniformly through the full space. They are usually concentrated near lower-dimensional structures.

That is why the authors do not try to make TCLUST a little more robust. They change the geometry it fits.

Why RLG is not enough either

RLG goes in the opposite direction. Instead of fitting full covariances, it assumes each cluster lies near a lower-dimensional affine subspace and scores points by their orthogonal distance to that subspace. That is much closer to how high-dimensional structure often behaves.

But RLG throws away too much information. If two subspaces intersect, or if a point projects near the wrong subspace, the method can absorb the point into the wrong cluster. It also treats the orthogonal noise too simply, with an isotropic error model that does not let the residual spread differ across clusters.

The paper’s Figure 1 is the clearest visual warning sign.

What to notice here is that distance to the subspace is not enough. When subspaces meet or nearly meet, points from one cluster can look close to another cluster’s geometry even when they belong elsewhere. The black trimmed points show that some observations are being discarded, but the deeper issue is that plain orthogonal distance does not tell the whole story.

tHDDC keeps the subspace idea, but it makes the score richer.

A cluster is a thin affine sheet, not a blob

The model behind tHDDC is a Gaussian cluster whose covariance has a very specific shape. In the eigenbasis of cluster g, the first q_g directions get their own variances, while the remaining p - q_g directions share a single residual variance. So each cluster has:

  • a center \mu_g,
  • an intrinsic dimension q_g,
  • a cluster-specific affine subspace \mathcal{B}_g,
  • principal variances \lambda_{g1}, \ldots, \lambda_{gq_g} inside that subspace,
  • one residual variance \lambda_g outside it.

That structure is the whole trick. It says: do not estimate a fully free covariance matrix when most of the signal lives in a thin direction set. Keep the informative directions separate from the orthogonal noise, and let each cluster have its own version of both.

The geometry then becomes easy to picture. Each cluster is not a ball and not a full slab. It is a low-dimensional affine sheet with some spread along the sheet and a smaller, controlled spread away from it.

Trimming is the robustness layer

The paper uses trimmed likelihoods in the same spirit as TCLUST. A fixed fraction \alpha of the least plausible observations is dropped, so the model does not have to explain the worst points. In the classification version, each non-trimmed point is assigned to the most likely cluster. In the mixture version, the same trimming idea is used with soft responsibilities.

That distinction matters mathematically, but the practical story is the same. First score every point under the current cluster models. Then discard the weakest fraction. Then refit the subspaces, means, and variances on the remaining mass.

Trimming is not just an outlier filter here. It is what makes the subspace model robust enough to survive contamination, because the worst points are allowed to leave instead of pulling the sheet away from the real structure.

Why the eigenvalue constraints are split in two

If the model were left unconstrained, the likelihood could still cheat. A component could try to collapse onto a tiny set of points, or a variance could become too small or too large in one part of the model. tHDDC prevents that with two separate ratio constraints: one for the informative eigenvalues inside the subspace, and one for the residual variance outside it.

The split is useful because the two variance regimes mean different things. The principal eigenvalues control the spread along the informative directions. The residual variance controls how thick the cluster is around its sheet. You may want some freedom in the first part and much tighter control in the second part. The paper explicitly allows that tradeoff by using separate constants c_1 and c_2. It also notes that c_2 must stay finite for the theoretical existence and consistency results.

How the algorithm actually moves

The implementation is a multi-start trimmed C-step routine. That sounds heavy, but the flow is straightforward once you read it as a loop over the same four operations.

The details are worth following because they show how the geometry, the trimming, and the constraints interact.

First, each start uses a small random subset. The paper emphasizes that this is much cheaper than initializing full covariances in TCLUST. For a cluster with intrinsic dimension q_g, only q_g + 2 points in general position are needed to seed the mean and the subspace directions. That is a much more realistic requirement than selecting G(p+1) points for full covariance initialization when p is large.

Then the method scores every observation under the current cluster models. The points with the smallest scores are trimmed. After that, the cluster weights, means, subspace eigenvectors, and eigenvalues are updated from the weighted sample statistics of the non-trimmed points.

The update is not just a standard eigen-decomposition. Only the top q_g eigenvectors are needed, and the paper notes that this can be accelerated with methods such as Arnoldi-type eigensolvers. Once the raw eigenvalues are computed, they are truncated so they satisfy the ratio constraints. If a truncated value still violates the ordering constraints, the algorithm merges the conflicting values into a common level and repeats the truncation until the constraints are satisfied.

That last step is subtle but important. It is the mechanism that turns a nice idea on paper into a feasible constrained optimizer instead of a free-form covariance fit.

The method then repeats these C-steps from many random starts and keeps the run with the best trimmed likelihood.

Choosing the intrinsic dimensions

The intrinsic dimensions q_g are not treated as a fixed truth. The paper lets them be re-estimated from the eigenvalues, using a Cattell-style idea: look at the drops between consecutive sorted eigenvalues and keep the directions where the drop is still large enough to look meaningful.

The practical reason is simple. If you let the model keep adding directions, the trimmed likelihood will often improve just because the model gets more flexible. That is not the same as learning a better cluster. So the paper adds a complexity penalty, in the spirit of a modified BIC, to avoid choosing a larger q_g just because it fits the training data more tightly.

This is one of the few places where the method asks the user to make a judgment call. The paper is honest about that. Picking G, \alpha, q_g, c_1, and c_2 is still difficult, and large initial values of q_g can make initialization worse because the method then needs more points to seed each cluster reliably.

What the theory gives you

The paper does not just propose a heuristic. It also shows that the constrained optimization problem is well defined: under mild regularity conditions and a positive trimming level, a maximizer exists, and the empirical solution converges to the population solution under the usual uniqueness assumption.

The practical meaning is not that the optimizer magically finds the true data-generating parameters. It is that the objective itself is mathematically stable. The trimming and eigenvalue constraints are doing real work here: they keep the likelihood from running off to degenerate covariance solutions and make the trimmed subspace model a legitimate estimation problem rather than an unstable trick.

What the experiments show

The simulations make the design story look justified. Across three 200-dimensional scenarios, tHDDC achieved near-perfect classification over the tested separation values, while TCLUST stayed poor and RLG only looked strong when the subspaces were more favorable. The main message is not just that tHDDC wins. It is that trimming plus subspace geometry solves the two problems the baselines each handle only halfway: contamination and high-dimensional structure.

The runtime story is also important. In the reported timing comparison, tHDDC was roughly 2.5 to 3 times faster than TCLUST. That is a strong sign that the parsimonious subspace model is not just statistically helpful; it also cuts the computational burden that comes from full covariance estimation.

The USPS358 digit example makes the robustness point even clearer. With added outliers, tHDDC reached a classification error slightly above 7% on the non-trimmed observations, while TCLUST stayed above 38% error and non-robust HDDC stayed above 39%. The trimmed points were the obvious synthetic outliers first, and then the more atypical digit images. That is exactly the behavior you would want from a robust clustering method.

What to remember

tHDDC is a robust clustering method for high-dimensional data that replaces full covariance fitting with cluster-specific low-dimensional subspaces.

The method keeps the parts that matter:

  • trim the worst points,
  • fit each cluster near its own affine subspace,
  • score points by both within-subspace and orthogonal fit,
  • keep principal and residual variances separately constrained,
  • repeat from many starts and keep the best solution.

The durable lesson is simple. If high-dimensional clusters are really thin, do not force them to look like full blobs. Fit the thin geometry directly, trim the points that do not belong, and regularize the remaining variances so the model cannot cheat.
