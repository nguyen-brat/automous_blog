Stabilizing High-Dimensional Clustering with Compression, Projections, and Pretrained Features

High-dimensional clustering usually fails before the clustering algorithm itself gets a fair chance. Distances flatten, small initialization changes produce different splits, discrete coordinates explode combinatorially, and even strong pretrained embeddings are often shaped for recognition rather than unsupervised separation. The common theme here is not a single algorithm. It is a shared interface problem: how to hand downstream clustering a representation whose geometry is stable enough to trust.

That is why this subtopic matters. In practice, many unsupervised pipelines spend more effort on preparing the space than on the final partitioning step. Compression can remove redundant detail before fitting densities. Random projections can turn awkward objects into many usable views. Pretrained encoders can supply semantics, but then still need reshaping so clusters occupy cleaner regions. The point is not to replace clustering with a new magic rule. The point is to make clustering behave sensibly when raw geometry does not.

The recurring pipeline: repair the representation before trusting the split

Across these methods, the recurring flow is:

That exact pipeline is most explicit in Probabilistic Partitive Partitioning, but the broader lesson carries across the family: first reshape or compress the data, then apply a clustering step, then use some extra criterion to decide whether the resulting groups are meaningful.

Other members occupy different points in that flow. CPP starts with a frozen CLIP encoder and refines the feature geometry before cluster selection and semantic labeling. TERP / TERPM changes the representation and distance definition together by projecting functional data into many random views and comparing distance profiles instead of raw Euclidean gaps. Scaled base-10 compression pushes even earlier in the pipeline by deterministically compressing huge discrete vectors into a small continuous space before ordinary clustering begins.

Why raw high-dimensional geometry is hard to trust

There are four different failure modes in the input, and each one breaks clustering for a different reason.

Unstable cluster splits are the classic divisive-clustering problem. A top-down split can look crisp in one run and dissolve in another because high-dimensional partitions are sensitive to initialization and local noise. If you recurse on every binary split you see, you build a tree of accidents.

Feature geometry quality is a different issue. A powerful pretrained encoder may preserve semantics well enough for classification, retrieval, or zero-shot labeling, but unsupervised clustering needs more than semantic relevance. It needs within-cluster cohesion and between-cluster separation in the specific unlabeled dataset at hand.

Distance collapse in high dimension is more structural. When objects live in large spaces, raw pairwise distances often become too similar to separate groups reliably. Functional data makes this worse because each curve is already an infinite-dimensional object observed on imperfect grids.

High-dimensional discrete embedding creates another failure mode: sparsity, ties, and enormous coordinate spaces make many generic embedding tools slow or brittle. Here the problem is less about semantic abstraction and more about turning unwieldy discrete vectors into something numerical methods can handle efficiently.

The main design axes

Because the bottlenecks differ, the family is best understood through design choices rather than through a single shared mechanism.

One axis is how the data is compressed or initialized. PPP uses self-organizing-map vector quantization to reduce the instance space before validation. CPP starts from a frozen CLIP backbone instead of learning visual features from scratch. TERP / TERPM uses many Gaussian-process projection directions rather than one fixed basis. Scaled base-10 compression uses a deterministic weighted aggregation that preserves uniqueness while shrinking dimensionality.

Another axis is how a candidate partition is made trustworthy. PPP validates binary splits with Gaussian-mixture posteriors and only keeps branches with positive overlap quality. CPP refines embeddings with a rate-reduction objective so the feature space itself becomes more cluster-friendly before spectral partitioning. TERP / TERPM changes the notion of similarity by comparing how each point relates to the rest of the dataset. Scaled base-10 compression accepts some loss of fine-grained structure in exchange for a stable, cheap downstream space.

A third axis is how stopping or model selection happens. PPP stops weak branches with a positive Φ\PhiΦ criterion. CPP handles unknown cluster count after training with coding-length selection rather than fixing kkk up front. TERP / TERPM ensembles across projection families and projection counts, then keeps the best normalized solution. Scaled base-10 compression leaves the final cluster-count choice to downstream criteria such as silhouette or BIC after the representation has been simplified.

When a split is worth keeping

PPP is a good example of the first bottleneck: the split itself may be the unstable object, not just the features underneath it. Its strategy is simple in spirit: compress first, split second, validate third. A binary partition is accepted only if both children preserve meaningful support from the parent instead of merely reflecting a lucky initialization.

Purpose: define whether a binary split is strong enough to keep during recursive tree growth.

Equation:

Intuition: a split should survive only when both children inherit convincing support from the parent. If one side is weak, the combined score stays weak.

Symbols:

  • Φ\PhiΦ: overall split quality score.
  • ϕ1\phi_1ϕ1​: normalized overlap between the parent’s high-posterior support and child 1 support.

Why it matters: this turns “should I trust this branch?” into an explicit validation decision instead of a visual judgment on a noisy partition.

This kind of validation matters because divisive clustering otherwise compounds early mistakes. The compact evidence here is narrow but supportive: PPP reportedly separated cancer subtypes with shallow binary trees on two gene-expression datasets, and the paper claims convergence to the same settings despite different k-means initializations. Those are exactly the kinds of outcomes you want when the bottleneck is unstable recursion rather than raw capacity.

When the embedding is semantically good but cluster-geometrically bad

Pretrained models changed clustering because they gave practitioners a strong starting point, but they did not remove the geometry problem. CPP illustrates this well. CLIP features already know a lot about visual semantics, yet off-the-shelf features plus k-means can still leave clusters too mixed, too elongated, or too dependent on a guessed cluster count.

CPP’s lesson is that pretrained features often need structure-aware refinement, not wholesale retraining. The representation should spread out globally while making each cluster occupy a tighter local subspace.

The evidence anchor is again conceptual rather than exhaustive. CPP reportedly recovers the expected cluster counts for CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 without retraining, and it improves ImageNet-1k clustering over raw CLIP plus k-means. That supports the claim that representation refinement and post-hoc cluster-count handling can matter more than swapping one final clustering rule for another.

When distance itself stops being informative

For functional data, the problem is not only that features are large. It is that raw observations may be irregular, fragmented, and poorly served by standard distances. TERP / TERPM responds by asking a better question: instead of measuring whether two projected curves are directly close, ask whether they relate to the rest of the dataset in similar ways.

Purpose: define a distance that stays informative when raw Euclidean separation becomes unreliable in high dimension.

Intuition: two observations are similar when they have similar distance profiles to all the other observations, even if their direct raw distance is not especially informative.

Read-aloud meaning: compare two points by averaging how differently they see every other point in the dataset.

Why it matters: this shifts the pipeline away from fragile pairwise geometry and toward relational structure, which is often more stable in high dimension.

The tutorial lesson is broader than the specific method: random projections are useful not because one projection is perfect, but because many cheap views can reveal cluster-separating structure that a single basis or raw distance misses. The compact input gives only limited direct evidence, but it does say TERP is usually best or second-best across many simulated settings and that TERPM performs strongly on irregular real datasets. That is consistent with the idea that better relational geometry can rescue difficult functional clustering problems.

When discrete dimensions need a cheaper interface

Not every high-dimensional pipeline needs a sophisticated learned embedding. Sometimes the bottleneck is simply that the data type is too large and too discrete for expensive generic reductions to be a good first move.

Scaled base-10 compression teaches a useful counterpoint: if cluster structure is mostly about broad location patterns, a deterministic weighted aggregation can preserve identity and neighborhood structure well enough to make downstream clustering practical. The value here is not expressiveness for its own sake. It is a numerically stable interface between huge discrete vectors and standard clustering tools such as k-means or Gaussian mixtures.

This design is attractive when runtime and robustness matter more than fine feature-level interpretability. The strongest support in the compact input is operational: the method is reported to be much faster than heavier reductions such as MDS, PCA, and t-SNE while staying effective on sparse, noisy, and correlated discrete settings. The open caveat is equally important: if clusters differ mainly by covariance or dependence patterns rather than by mean location, aggressive deterministic compression may hide the very signal you need.

What to remember

Compression, projection, and pretrained-feature pipelines are best seen as ways of repairing the geometry that clustering inherits. They do not share one algorithmic core, and they should not be forced into one. Some methods stabilize recursive splits, some reshape pretrained embeddings, some replace brittle distances with relational ones, and some build a cheaper numeric interface for discrete data.

The durable takeaway is practical: when clustering fails in high dimension, first ask which interface is broken. Is the split unstable? Is the embedding semantically strong but geometrically weak? Has distance collapsed? Is the raw data type too discrete and too large for direct use? Once that bottleneck is identified, the right pipeline often becomes less about inventing a new clusterer and more about making the space clusterable.

Open questions remain. How should these interfaces adapt to streaming data, stronger distribution shift, or modern distributed-scale workloads? How often can deterministic compression preserve the right signal when clusters differ in more than mean location? And can cluster-count selection be made reliable across pipelines without leaning on fragile heuristics? Those questions matter because the hardest part of clustering is still deciding what structure the representation is allowed to reveal.
