Found in the Middle: Calibrating Positional Attention Bias Improves Long Context Utilization

Found-in-the-middle is easiest to understand as an inference-time attention calibration step for long-context RAG. The model is not asked to learn a new retriever or a new attention architecture. Instead, the paper treats raw document attention as a mixture of two things: how useful a document really is, and how much the model prefers that position in the prompt. The method estimates the position-only part, subtracts it, and then feeds the corrected score back into attention during generation.

That is the central shift in the paper. Long-context models do not merely miss middle documents because the documents are absent. Often the evidence is there, but the model’s routing system gives the edges a standing advantage. So the question becomes: how much of the lost-in-the-middle problem is really a bias in where attention goes?

What to notice in the figure is that the attention curve stays U-shaped even after the documents are shuffled. That is the giveaway: the model is not simply following relevance. It has a slot preference, with the beginning and end of the prompt getting more attention than the middle. The paper also shows that documents receiving more attention are much more likely to be the ones the model actually uses when it writes the answer, so this is not just a pretty visualization. It is already shaping generation.

Why the middle gets ignored

The paper starts from a standard multi-document QA setup: a question, a set of retrieved documents, and one gold document hidden among distractors. To make the query visible on both sides of the context, the prompt repeats the question before and after the documents.

The hard case is when the gold document lands in the middle. The model still has access to the answer, but its output often leans on the first document anyway. When the documents are shuffled, the U-shape remains. That tells us the bias is positional rather than tied to one particular document.

The useful mental model is simple:

  • relevance says which document should matter,
  • position bias says which slot the model prefers,
  • observed attention mixes both.

Formula card: attention as relevance plus position

Purpose. Describe raw document attention as a combination of content usefulness and a position effect.

Equation.

Attn(xkdoc,k)=rel(xkdoc)+bias(k)+ϵ\mathrm{Attn}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_k, k)=\mathrm{rel}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_k)+\mathrm{bias}(k)+\epsilonAttn(xkdoc​,k)=rel(xkdoc​)+bias(k)+ϵ

Intuition. A document gets attention because it is relevant, but the slot it occupies can add or subtract from that attention on its own.

Symbols.

  • \mathrm{Attn}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_k, k): average attention assigned to the kth document in the prompt
  • x^{\mathrm{doc}}_k: the kth document
  • \mathrm{rel}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_k): position-independent relevance of that document
  • \mathrm{bias}(k): position-dependent attention bias for slot k
  • \epsilon: leftover variation not explained by the two main terms

Read-aloud meaning. Observed attention equals document relevance plus a position bonus or penalty plus noise.

Why it matters. If attention really decomposes this way, then the position part can be estimated and removed instead of being treated as an unavoidable property of the model.

The paper does not stop at intuition. It checks that the two factors behave like separate forces: when relevance is held fixed, attention still follows position; when position is held fixed, more relevant documents still get more attention. A simple linear fit is good enough to capture the pattern, with rank correlation around 0.76, slightly above a log-linear variant.

That is enough to justify the next step. If raw attention is a mixture, measure the position-only part first, then subtract it.

Subtract the position baseline

The calibration trick is very direct. For each slot, the paper measures attention on a dummy document placed in that same position. Since the dummy and the real document share the same slot, they share the same positional bias. Subtracting the dummy reading removes the bias term.

Formula card: dummy-document baseline subtraction

Purpose. Estimate the document’s position-free relevance by subtracting a baseline measured at the same location.

Equation.

rel(xkdoc)=Attn(xkdoc,k)−Attn(xdum,k)+rel(xdum)\mathrm{rel}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_k) = \mathrm{Attn}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_k, k) - \mathrm{Attn}(x^{\mathrm{dum}}, k) + \mathrm{rel}(x^{\mathrm{dum}})rel(xkdoc​)=Attn(xkdoc​,k)−Attn(xdum,k)+rel(xdum)

Intuition. The dummy document acts like a calibration probe. Since it sits in the same slot, the shared position bias cancels when we take the difference.

Symbols.

  • x^{\mathrm{dum}}: the dummy document used to measure the position baseline
  • \mathrm{Attn}(x^{\mathrm{dum}}, k): attention measured on the dummy document at slot k
  • \mathrm{rel}(x^{\mathrm{dum}}): constant relevance assigned to the dummy document
  • other symbols are as defined above

Read-aloud meaning. The real document’s score at a given slot, minus the dummy document’s score at that same slot, leaves behind the document’s calibrated relevance.

Why it matters. This is the core of found-in-the-middle: it turns attention from a biased observation into a position-corrected ranking signal.

If you only care about ranking documents, the constant \mathrm{rel}(x^{\mathrm{dum}}) is just an offset. The important part is the difference between documents at the same slot, which is now much closer to true relevance than to the model’s positional habit.

What to notice here is not a single headline score. It is the shape of the curves. Calibration sits above vanilla attention in almost every setting, and the paper reports the strongest gains in the hardest middle-document cases. On NaturalQuestions with 20 retrieved documents, Recall@3 jumps from 0.2052 for vanilla attention to 0.6832 for calibrated attention.

Put the cleaned score back into generation

Once the paper has a position-free document score, it turns that score back into an actual attention budget. More relevant documents get more total mass. Then that mass is pushed down to the token level inside each document, so the model keeps the internal structure of the document while changing how much global attention it gets.

Formula card: attention redistribution

Purpose. Convert calibrated document relevance into token-level attention during generation.

Equation.

αk=Softmax(rel(xkdoc),t)\alpha_k = \mathrm{Softmax}(\mathrm{rel}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_k), t)αk​=Softmax(rel(xkdoc​),t) attncalibrated(xk,idoc)=αkAttnoriginal(xkdoc)⋅attnoriginal(xk,idoc)⋅C\mathrm{attn}_{\mathrm{calibrated}}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_{k,i}) = \frac{\alpha_k}{\mathrm{Attn}_{\mathrm{original}}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_k)} \cdot \mathrm{attn}_{\mathrm{original}}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_{k,i}) \cdot Cattncalibrated​(xk,idoc​)=Attnoriginal​(xkdoc​)αk​​⋅attnoriginal​(xk,idoc​)⋅C

Intuition. First decide how much total attention each document deserves. Then scale the tokens inside that document so the document keeps its internal pattern while receiving a new overall budget.

Symbols.

  • \alpha_k: softmax weight assigned to document k
  • \mathrm{Softmax}(\mathrm{rel}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_k), t): softmax over calibrated relevance scores
  • t: temperature that controls how sharp the redistribution is
  • \mathrm{Attn}_{\mathrm{original}}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_k): original total attention on document k
  • \mathrm{attn}_{\mathrm{original}}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_{k,i}): original attention on token i inside document k
  • \mathrm{attn}_{\mathrm{calibrated}}(x^{\mathrm{doc}}_{k,i}): adjusted token-level attention
  • C: normalization constant that keeps the total attention mass unchanged
  • x^{\mathrm{doc}}_{k,i}: the ith token inside document k

Read-aloud meaning. Give each document a new share of the attention budget based on calibrated relevance, then scale its tokens proportionally.

Why it matters. This is the step that turns a ranking trick into an actual generation-time intervention.

In practice, the authors only apply the intervention in later decoder layers. Earlier layers were more unstable, so the method avoids touching them. That is a useful implementation detail because it shows the paper is not trying to rewrite the whole model. It is nudging the attention signal where it matters most.

Why this helps beyond reranking

A lot of long-context RAG fixes work by moving the evidence around: put the best document first, put it last, or rerank the retrieved set before the model sees it. Those tricks help, but they leave the model’s internal bias untouched. Found-in-the-middle is different because it corrects the routing signal itself.

That is why it can stack on top of reordering methods. If a reranker already improves the prompt order, calibration can still help because it acts on the model’s own attention after the order has been chosen. The paper shows that attention calibration improves existing RAG pipelines rather than replacing them.

The empirical picture matches that story. The calibrated curve lies above vanilla attention in almost all settings, with the strongest gains when the gold document is in the middle. The paper also reports that the method works on both NaturalQuestions and SynthWiki, and that it improves over prompt reordering, query-generation reranking, relevance prompting, and attention-sorting style baselines.

What to keep in mind

The easiest way to remember found-in-the-middle is this:

the model’s attention is not just a measure of relevance; it is relevance plus a positional habit.

The paper estimates that habit with a dummy document, subtracts it, and then uses the cleaned-up score to rebalance attention during generation. In other words, it does not ask the model to become a different model. It asks the model to stop overcounting the edges.

There are also real limits:

  • the calibration needs extra O(K) forward passes,
  • the additive bias model is a simplification rather than a full theory,
  • removing positional bias may hurt tasks where edge-focused attention is actually useful,
  • the true root cause of the bias is still unclear.

So the method is best read as a mechanism-level fix for a specific failure mode: the answer is present, but the model keeps sending too much of its attention budget to the wrong positions.
