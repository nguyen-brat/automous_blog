An Efficient Recipe for Long Context Extension via Middle-Focused Positional Encoding

CREAM is a training recipe for RoPE-based language models that want longer context without being retrained on full-length examples. Instead of asking the model to process a 32K or 256K sequence directly during fine-tuning, CREAM keeps the training window at the pre-trained length and remaps a longer document into that window in a more deliberate way.

The name is a good clue. CREAM stands for Continuity-Relativity indexing with Gaussian Middle. Those three words are the whole method:

  • continuity keeps local token neighborhoods familiar,
  • relativity keeps long-range distance information visible,
  • Gaussian Middle makes the model spend more effort on the part of the prompt that long-context models usually handle worst.

The paper is trying to solve a very specific failure mode. Many long-context methods can make the model read farther, but they still leave the middle of the prompt fragile. The model remembers the beginning and the end more easily than the middle. CREAM is built around that weakness.

  • The long target sequence is not trained as one giant block.
  • The head and tail act like anchors at the edges.
  • The middle span is the part that gets moved around and reindexed.
  • All of that still fits inside the original pre-trained window.

That is why CREAM can train at 4K and still target 32K or even 256K later. The model never needs to see the full long sequence during fine-tuning. It sees a carefully stitched version of it.

That is the recipe in one line. CREAM does not add a new attention block or a memory module. It changes which positions the model gets to practice on.

Why the middle is the hard part

The paper starts from two practical observations.

First, the beginning tokens of a sequence often carry a lot of stable information. The model can lean on them even when the position handling is imperfect. Second, the ending tokens also tend to be easy to localize because they sit near the boundary of the window. The middle has neither advantage.

That is the lost-in-the-middle problem in plain language. When the prompt gets long, the important fact buried in the center is the one most likely to disappear.

CREAM treats the head and tail as landmarks. Once the model has those landmarks, the middle span can be understood relative to them. This is a good fit for RoPE-style positional encoding, because RoPE is built around relative distances rather than absolute IDs alone.

So the method is not just “stretch the positions.” It is “teach the model long-range structure by showing it anchored fragments whose relative placement still feels like a real long document.”

Two ways to carve the window

CREAM uses two segmentation strategies, and the balance between them is the point.

Continuity mode

In the continuity setting, the head and tail are kept very small. The paper uses k = 32 in the experiments.

Why do this? Because it makes the middle segment look as close as possible to the pre-trained context window. The model still sees mostly contiguous text. That helps preserve the smooth local behavior it already knows from pre-training.

This is the conservative mode. It says: keep the example as familiar as possible, but still let the positions imply a much longer document.

Relativity mode

In the relativity setting, the head and tail are each set to about one third of the pre-trained window.

Why do this? Because now the model sees many more relative distances between the beginning, middle, and end of the long sequence. That gives RoPE more position pairs to learn from.

This is the exploratory mode. It says: widen the span of distances the model experiences, even if the training example looks less like a tiny contiguous slice.

Why both are needed

Either choice on its own is incomplete.

If you only chase continuity, the model stays close to the pre-trained pattern but does not learn enough long-range distance diversity. If you only chase relativity, the example can drift too far from the familiar pre-training shape. CREAM samples both strategies with equal probability so the model gets the best of both worlds.

That balance is one of the paper’s main ideas. Continuity protects stability. Relativity teaches scale. CREAM wants both, not one or the other.

Why the middle is sampled with a Gaussian bias

The other half of the recipe is the sampling distribution for the middle span.

Uniform sampling would treat every valid middle location as equally interesting. The paper argues that is not ideal, because the real pain point is not every position. It is the center of the long context, where retrieval is most likely to fail.

So CREAM uses a truncated Gaussian to bias the middle span toward the center of the sequence. “Truncated” just means the distribution is clipped to the valid range of positions. The result is still a legal sample, but one that is more likely to land where the model needs extra help.

This matters for two reasons:

  • It makes the model practice on the hard part more often.
  • It reduces wasted samples on positions the model already handles reasonably well.

The ablation is straightforward: uniform sampling works, but not as well as the Gaussian-biased version. That is a nice sign that the distribution is doing real work, not just adding complexity.

If you want a memory hook, think of it this way: the model already likes the edges. CREAM spends more of its training budget on the middle because that is where the model is weakest.

How CREAM fits with Linear, NTK, and YaRN

This is an important point: CREAM is not a replacement for interpolation schemes like Linear, NTK, or YaRN.

Those methods decide how positional information is stretched or reshaped. CREAM decides what kind of long-document slices the model sees during fine-tuning.

So the stack looks like this:

  • Linear, NTK, or YaRN handle the positional encoding interpolation itself.
  • CREAM shapes the training examples so the model learns from anchored long-range structure.
  • The two parts work together instead of competing.

That is why the paper reports CREAM working across all three interpolation choices. The recipe is not brittle to one specific scaling trick. It sits on top of them.

What the experiments are really showing

The results are best read as evidence that the recipe changes behavior, not just benchmark numbers.

On LongChat-Lines and Lost-in-the-Middle, CREAM consistently beats PoSE across Linear, NTK, and YaRN. The headline number is CREAM-YaRN’s 73.4 average on the 75-key Lost-in-the-Middle setting versus 50.0 for PoSE-YaRN. That is the clearest sign that the middle-focused sampling is doing what it is supposed to do.

On LongBench, the 7B CREAM model reaches 35.9 average, ahead of LongChat-v1.5-7B-32k at 34.3. On Needle-in-a-Haystack, CREAM-Chat stays near-perfect until about 29K after only 100 tuning steps. And the 256K perplexity results show that the method stays stable even when the target length becomes extreme.

The common pattern is simple: the model does better when the training recipe deliberately preserves anchors and spends more effort on the center.

The ablations make the design choices feel less arbitrary

The ablation section is useful because it tells you which parts are structural and which parts are just knobs.

The paper shows three things clearly:

  • Gaussian sampling is better than uniform sampling.
  • Fixing both the head and the tail matters a lot. Removing both is worst.
  • Balancing continuity and relativity beats using either one alone.

Those results are exactly what the method story predicts. If the head and tail disappear, the middle loses its landmarks. If the sampling is uniform, the model does not get enough emphasis on the hard region. If the recipe leans too far toward only one segmentation style, it loses either stability or coverage.

That is what makes the method feel coherent. Each design choice supports the others.

Where the method stops short

CREAM is strong, but it does not claim to fully solve long-context retrieval.

There are three limits worth keeping in mind:

  • Position interpolation beyond the pre-trained length still loses some positional information.
  • Lost-in-the-middle is mitigated, not eliminated.
  • The results are centered on RoPE-based decoder LLMs, so transfer to other settings is still an open question.

The paper is also honest that the best continuity-relativity balance may depend on scale. What works at 32K may not be the final answer at much larger target lengths.

So the right way to read CREAM is not as a universal fix. It is a practical and surprisingly effective recipe for teaching long-context behavior without paying for full target-length fine-tuning.

What to remember

CREAM is easiest to remember as a three-part move:

  • keep short anchors at the head and tail,
  • bias the middle span toward the hard center cases,
  • combine that recipe with any RoPE interpolation scheme you already trust.

The intuition is more important than the acronym. CREAM teaches a short-window model to behave like it has seen a much longer document by remapping positions instead of rewriting the architecture.

If you remember one sentence, make it this:

CREAM extends context by showing the model anchored glimpses of long documents, with the middle sampled more aggressively than the edges.
