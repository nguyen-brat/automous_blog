Multi-scale Positional Encoding for Better Context Utilization in LLMs

Ms-PoE is a very small intervention with a very specific target: long-context LLMs often miss the useful information sitting in the middle of the prompt. The paper does not try to redesign attention or retrain the model from scratch. Instead, it changes how RoPE counts position, and it does that differently for different attention heads. Heads that already behave sensibly with position stay close to the original geometry. Heads that are more prone to edge bias get a stronger positional squeeze so distant tokens become easier to fuse.

The easiest way to picture it is as several zoom levels running in parallel. One head keeps almost the native RoPE scale. Another head sees a more compressed version of the same prompt. The model is not asked to choose one universal ruler. It is given a family of rulers, then told to let the more position-aware heads stay conservative and let the weaker heads do the heavy lifting.

What to notice in the figure is the parallelism. The same token sequence flows through multiple heads, but each head uses a different positional scale. The first matrix is the unmodified RoPE case with r_i = 1. The later matrices squeeze positions more aggressively. That is the whole method in one image: same model, same weights, different positional coordinate systems.

Why one global scale is too blunt

A uniform RoPE rescaling can help, but it is a blunt instrument. If every head gets the same compression, the model may improve one part of the context while damaging another. The paper’s core observation is that heads are not interchangeable. Some heads already track where the relevant document moved. Other heads mostly cling to beginning and end tokens. Those two kinds of heads should not receive the same positional treatment.

Formula card: rescaled RoPE

Purpose: show the one place where Ms-PoE changes the rotary coordinate system.

Equation:

f(x,m;r)=xei(m/r)θf(\mathbf{x},m;r)=\mathbf{x}e^{i(m/r)\theta}f(x,m;r)=xei(m/r)θ

Intuition: divide the token position by r before RoPE rotates the feature, so the same token looks closer in positional space.

Symbols:

  • \mathbf{x}: the query or key feature block being rotated
  • m: the original token position
  • r: the scaling ratio for that head
  • \theta: the fixed RoPE frequency schedule across feature pairs
  • i: the imaginary unit used in the complex representation

Read-aloud meaning: apply the usual rotary transform, but first squeeze the position index by a head-specific factor.

Why it matters: this is the only operation Ms-PoE needs to change. It keeps the model weights intact and attacks the long-term decay effect by changing how far away positions look to attention.

That small change is powerful, but only when it stays moderate. If the ratio is too extreme, the model starts living in positional coordinates it never really practiced during pretraining. The paper’s ablations show that the sweet spot is a careful range, not a giant universal compression.

Finding the heads that already know where to look

The second key observation is that heads have different positional habits. Some heads follow the important document as it moves from the beginning to the middle to the end. Other heads barely move at all and keep staring at the edges. Figure 4 makes that difference obvious.

What to notice is the moving peak. In the position-aware head, the attention bump shifts when the crucial document shifts. In the weaker head, the mass stays stuck near the beginning or the end. Ms-PoE uses exactly that gap to decide which heads should stay close to the original RoPE scale and which heads can tolerate stronger compression.

Formula card: position-awareness score

Purpose: quantify how much a head gives meaningful attention beyond a tiny set of edge tokens.

Equation:

Aˉ=1l∑i=1lAi,SP=1l∑i=1l1(Ai≥αAˉ)\bar A = \frac{1}{l}\sum_{i=1}^{l} A_i,\qquad \mathcal{S}_P = \frac{1}{l}\sum_{i=1}^{l} \mathbf{1}(A_i \ge \alpha \bar A)Aˉ=l1​i=1∑l​Ai​,SP​=l1​i=1∑l​1(Ai​≥αAˉ)

Intuition: compute the head’s average attention, then count how many entries rise above a threshold set by that average.

Symbols:

  • A_i: the attention score at position i for the most recent query
  • \bar A: the mean attention score in that head
  • l: the number of positions in the score vector
  • \alpha: the threshold multiplier, set to 3 in the paper’s experiments
  • \mathcal{S}_P: the position-awareness score for that head
  • \mathbf{1}(\cdot): indicator function, equal to 1 when the condition is true and 0 otherwise

Read-aloud meaning: a head scores higher when more of its attention weights stay meaningfully above the average baseline.

Why it matters: this score gives the paper a way to sort heads from position-aware to position-unaware without retraining anything.

The score is not trying to tell you whether a head is “good” in an absolute sense. It is trying to tell you whether that head already tracks shifting evidence. That distinction matters because the most position-aware heads should be perturbed as little as possible, while the less reliable heads can absorb more compression.

What inference actually does

The actual runtime loop is short. During prefilling, the model looks at the prompt once and measures the most recent query’s attention pattern in each head. It sorts the heads by position-awareness, maps the sorted heads onto a fixed ratio schedule from 1.2 to 1.8, and then keeps those ratios fixed during decoding.

Formula card: head-wise ratio schedule

Purpose: assign a different compression strength to each head after sorting by position awareness.

Equation:

ri=1.2+(i−1)(1.8−1.2)nh−1r_i = 1.2 + \frac{(i-1)(1.8-1.2)}{n_h-1}ri​=1.2+nh​−1(i−1)(1.8−1.2)​

Intuition: after sorting heads from most position-aware to least position-aware, let the scale increase smoothly from mild compression to stronger compression.

Symbols:

  • r_i: the ratio assigned to the ith sorted head
  • i: the rank of the head after sorting
  • n_h: the number of attention heads
  • 1.2 and 1.8: the default lower and upper bounds for the scale range

Read-aloud meaning: earlier heads keep a gentler scale, later heads get a stronger positional squeeze.

Why it matters: this is where Ms-PoE becomes multi-scale instead of merely rescaled. The method preserves heads that already know how to track position and uses the less sensitive heads to absorb more aggressive compression.

Once those ratios are chosen, decoding proceeds normally. There is no second forward pass, no extra inference branch, and no fine-tuning step. The method is plug-and-play because all of the adaptation happens in the way positions are numbered, not in the model’s learned weights.

Why the numbers improve

The results match the story. On Vicuna-7B, the ZeroSCROLLS average rises from 15.0 to 18.8. On MDQA, the average goes from 61.12 to 65.04. On key-value retrieval, the jump is much larger, from 36.24 to 79.96. The paper also reports that the gap between the best and worst placements shrinks by roughly 2 to 4 points, which is exactly what you would hope to see if the method is really helping the middle of the prompt instead of just one lucky position.

The ablations are the most convincing part. A uniform scaling ratio of 1.5 helps, but the head-wise 1.2 -> 1.8 schedule does better on MDQA, reaching 65.3 versus 63.1. That difference is the whole point of the paper in miniature: the heads are not interchangeable, so the scaling policy should not be interchangeable either.

Compared with methods like Positional Interpolation and Self-Extend, Ms-PoE is more selective. Those methods treat the positional remapping more uniformly. Ms-PoE keeps the position-aware heads close to what they already know and pushes the less reliable heads harder. That is why it can improve context utilization without asking the entire model to relearn positional geometry.

Where the method is fragile

Ms-PoE is simple, but it still has limits. The scaling range has to be tuned. Extreme ratios can hurt because the model starts living in a positional geometry it never really learned. The method is also designed around RoPE, so it does not transfer cleanly to every other positional scheme. And it reduces positional bias rather than eliminating it. Middle-context failures get smaller, but they do not disappear.

The open question the paper leaves behind is whether the head-awareness score can be estimated more robustly across prompts, and whether the same idea can be adapted to larger closed models or non-RoPE architectures. That is a healthy kind of unfinished work, because it points to the real boundary of the idea instead of pretending the boundary is gone.

What to remember

Ms-PoE is head-wise RoPE compression. Keep the heads that already understand position close to the original RoPE geometry, squeeze the weaker heads more aggressively, and freeze the chosen ratios during decoding. That gives the model a multi-scale view of the prompt without fine-tuning or extra inference branches.
