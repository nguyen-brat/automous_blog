Extending Context Window of Large Language Models from a Distributional Perspective

Most RoPE extension methods ask a blunt question: how do we stretch the context window from 4k to 8k or 16k? This paper asks a stricter one: after extension, do the rotary angles still look statistically like the ones the model saw during pretraining?

That shift is the whole method. The paper treats every RoPE dimension as a tiny angle distribution, compares candidate extension strategies against the pretrained distribution, and keeps the lower-disturbance choice for that dimension. In other words, it does not just stretch the position range. It tries to preserve the angle pattern the model already knows.

You can think of the pipeline like this:

pretrained RoPE angles -> bucket each dimension's angles -> compare interpolation vs extrapolation -> choose the lower-disturbance option per dimension -> extend the context window

The result is a very simple deployment story: no new runtime module, no extra inference cost, and no change to attention itself. Only the RoPE frequencies change.

Why one global stretch is not enough

The paper’s first important observation is that interpolation and extrapolation are not universally good or bad. They can behave oppositely on different RoPE dimensions.

In one dimension, interpolating positions makes the extended angle histogram look closer to pretraining. In another dimension, interpolation introduces too many unseen angles and extrapolation is the safer choice. The paper’s own examples make that contrast obvious: the 6th dimension and the 22nd dimension prefer different strategies.

That is why heuristics like “stretch everything equally” feel too blunt. Each RoPE band has its own rhythm. If you only look at the context length, you miss the fact that some bands stay familiar under stretching while others drift away from the pretrained pattern.

Turning rotary angles into a histogram

To make that idea concrete, the paper treats the rotary angles produced during pretraining as samples from an empirical distribution.

The angle at position m in dimension i is \Theta_m^i = (m\theta_i) \bmod 2\pi. The paper then divides the circle [0, 2\pi) into b equal bins, counts how often each sampled angle falls into each bin, and turns those counts into a probability distribution. With b = 360, each bin is one degree wide, which is a reasonable compromise between resolution and noise.

Why it matters: once you have a histogram, you can compare extension strategies dimension by dimension instead of relying on a hand-built scaling rule.

The key thing to notice is that this histogram is only an approximation. That is fine, because the paper does not need a perfect analytic density. It needs a stable way to compare the pretrained pattern with the pattern introduced by extension.

Measuring disturbance

Once the pretrained histogram is available, the paper asks which extension strategy changes it least.

For each dimension, it builds two candidate distributions at the new context length L': one from interpolation and one from extrapolation. Then it measures the difference from the pretrained histogram with KL divergence. If interpolation creates lots of mass in bins that were empty or rare during pretraining, the disturbance becomes large. If extrapolation stays closer to the old histogram, its disturbance score stays lower.

Why it matters: this turns “that scaling looks nicer” into a concrete selection criterion.

Disturbance versus LongBench-E performance

What to notice in the plot:

  • As disturbance rises, LongBench-E performance falls.
  • The trend is close to monotonic, so the score is not just a mathematical convenience.
  • The paper uses this to justify the whole distributional view: if the extension perturbs the angle histogram less, the model usually behaves better on long-context tasks.

This is the paper’s strongest sanity check. It says the metric is not arbitrary. It tracks the same failure mode users care about.

The per-dimension strategy rule

Once the paper can score both candidates, the final decision becomes local and simple.

If interpolation is clearly less disturbing than extrapolation on a dimension, use interpolation there. Otherwise keep the original frequency and let that dimension extrapolate. The threshold t prevents the method from switching on near-ties, where the score difference is probably too small to trust. That matters because the histograms are only approximate.

Purpose: choose interpolation or extrapolation separately for each RoPE dimension.

Equation:

Intuition: for each rotary band, ask which candidate stays closer to the pretrained histogram. If interpolation wins by a clear margin, stretch that band. If not, leave it alone.

Symbols:

  • \hat{\theta}_{i}: the chosen extended angular frequency for dimension i.
  • \theta_i: the original RoPE frequency.
  • s = L'/L: the context-extension ratio.
  • P^{\mathcal{E}}_{L'}: the candidate distribution produced by extrapolation.
  • P^{\mathcal{I}}_{L'}: the candidate distribution produced by interpolation.
  • t: the margin threshold that avoids switching on small score differences.

Read-aloud meaning: interpolate a dimension only when interpolation is clearly less disturbing than extrapolation; otherwise keep the original frequency.

Why it matters: this converts a global heuristic into a per-band decision rule, which is exactly what the angle histograms were telling the authors to do.

The nice part is that this choice happens before inference. Once the frequencies are chosen, the model runs as usual. There is no extra module to execute and no extra cost at test time. The paper also notes that methods like FlashAttention remain compatible because the attention mechanism itself is untouched.

What the method buys you

The experiments line up with the metric story.

  • On LongBench-E, the method beats PI, YaRN, and CLEX on average.
  • For LLaMA2-7B at 16k, it reaches 30.12 average, versus 28.87 for PI and 28.65 for YaRN.
  • Disturbance drops by up to 72% at 8k and 32% at 16k.
  • Passkey retrieval reaches 100% accuracy within the tested range.
  • Short-context performance stays close to the original model, with average fluctuation roughly between -0.12 and +0.22.

The important takeaway is not the exact leaderboard ordering. It is the pattern: lower disturbance usually means better long-context behavior, while short-context quality barely moves because the model is not forced into a completely new runtime mechanism.

Where the idea stops

The method is clean, but it is not a universal solution.

  • It only applies to RoPE-based models.
  • It does not remove the transformer’s quadratic long-context cost.
  • Its distribution estimates are approximate and depend on the pretraining length.
  • Very fine binning can make the histogram noisier instead of more accurate.
  • The rule does not make disturbance zero; it just tries to minimize it in a practical way.

That is also why the paper frames this as a distributional perspective rather than a final theorem. It gives a better selection principle, not a magic long-context architecture.

What to remember

The easiest way to remember the paper is this: each RoPE dimension has its own rhythm, so stretch only the dimensions whose new angle histogram still looks familiar.

That is the whole method in one sentence. Estimate the pretrained angle distribution, compare interpolation and extrapolation by KL disturbance, and keep the lower-disturbance choice per dimension.
