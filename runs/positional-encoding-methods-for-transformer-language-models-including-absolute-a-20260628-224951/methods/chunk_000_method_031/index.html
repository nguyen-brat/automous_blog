Position Bias Mitigates Position Bias:Mitigate Position Bias Through Inter-Position Knowledge Distillation

Pos2Distill is easiest to understand as a teacher-student trick built on top of the model’s own position bias. The paper notices that some context locations are naturally strong: for retrieval, the sink position where the gold document lands first; for reasoning, the recent position where the relevant documents sit at the tail. Instead of treating that unevenness as a bug to hide, Pos2Distill uses the strong positions as supervision and copies that behavior into weaker ones.

That is the whole philosophy in one sentence: if the model already knows how to behave when the evidence is easy to see, let that behavior teach the hard cases.

The method is then split into two variants because retrieval and reasoning fail in different ways. Retrieval usually breaks at a few decisive tokens, while reasoning breaks along the whole chain of thought. The paper’s answer is not one generic objective. It is two position-aware recipes that both start from the same principle.

What to notice in the figure is the reframing. Position bias is not only a failure mode. It is also a free source of teacher signals. The best-position output becomes a target that weaker positions can imitate, so the method turns an existing asymmetry into training data.

Two kinds of bias, two kinds of supervision

The paper separates position bias into two different behaviors.

In retrieval, the problem is token shifting. The answer is mostly there, but a few decisive tokens flip when the gold document sits in a weaker slot. That makes the final output fail even though the model is close.

In reasoning, the problem is thought shifting. Retrieval and reasoning interact, so the model can drift onto the wrong chain of thought and then keep compounding the mistake. Here, the issue is not just a bad token. It is the wrong reasoning path.

That is why Pos2Distill-R1 and Pos2Distill-R2 are not the same recipe with a different dataset. R1 is a token-distribution distillation method for retrieval. R2 is a trajectory distillation method for multi-hop reasoning.

The teacher is not a larger model. It is the same backbone reading the same evidence in a better position. That matters because the target behavior is already inside the model; the task is to transfer it across positions.

Retrieval: let the sink position teach the weaker ones

For retrieval, the paper defines the advantageous position as the sink position, meaning the gold document is placed first. The other positions are treated as trivial positions. In that setup, the model often has the cleanest attention pattern and the most reliable answer generation.

The paper samples a response from the advantageous position and then asks the model to reproduce that response when the same answer-bearing document is moved to a weaker position. The important choice is the loss: rather than hard-label supervision, it uses KL divergence over the teacher response distribution. That gives token-level correction where the model actually drifts.

Formula card: retrieval activation loss

Purpose. Align a weak position with the output distribution produced by the same model at a strong position.

Equation.

LActPni=E[KL(PΘ(Radv∣Padv)  ∥  PΘ^(Radv∣Pni))]\mathcal{L}_{\text{Act}}^{\mathcal{P}^{n_i}}=\mathbb{E}\left[\mathrm{KL}\left(P_{\Theta}(\mathcal{R}^{adv}\mid\mathcal{P}^{adv})\;\|\;P_{\widehat{\Theta}}(\mathcal{R}^{adv}\mid\mathcal{P}^{n_i})\right)\right]LActPni​​=E[KL(PΘ​(Radv∣Padv)∥PΘ​(Radv∣Pni​))]

Intuition. The model is scored against the whole teacher answer sequence, not against one rigid gold label. If a weak position puts probability mass on the wrong token at a decisive step, KL exposes that mismatch directly.

Symbols.

  • \mathcal{L}_{\text{Act}}^{\mathcal{P}^{n_i}}: activation loss for weak position n_i
  • P_{\Theta}(\mathcal{R}^{adv}\mid\mathcal{P}^{adv}): teacher distribution from the advantageous position
  • P_{\widehat{\Theta}}(\mathcal{R}^{adv}\mid\mathcal{P}^{n_i}): student distribution at the weak position
  • \mathcal{R}^{adv}: response sampled from the advantageous position
  • \mathcal{P}^{adv}: advantageous prompt
  • \mathcal{P}^{n_i}: prompt with the gold document at weak position n_i
  • \Theta: teacher parameters used for sampling
  • \widehat{\Theta}: current student parameters being updated

Read-aloud meaning. Make the weak position behave like the strong position on the same teacher-generated answer.

Why it matters. Retrieval failures often live in just a few shifted tokens, so distribution matching is a much better corrective signal than hard labels.

That would still be too crude if every weak position were weighted the same way. Some positions are harder than others, and even within one position some examples are harder than others. The paper handles that with position-aware alignment.

Intuition. First, compare whole position bins and give more weight to the bins with larger average mismatch. Then, inside one bin, give more weight to the hardest examples in that bin.

  • \alpha_{ij}: final weight for example j at weak position i

Read-aloud meaning. Harder positions get more gradient budget, and harder cases within those positions get even more budget.

Why it matters. This keeps the method from wasting effort on the easy weak positions while still covering the whole positional range.

The other safety rail is anchoring. If the model only learns to imitate weak positions, the sink position can start to lose its special advantage. The paper avoids that by explicitly preserving the strong-position behavior.

Formula card: anchoring and the final objective

Purpose. Preserve the strong-position capability while weak positions are being taught.

Equation.

LAnc=E[KL(PΘ(Radv∣Padv)  ∥  PΘ^(Radv∣Padv))]\mathcal{L}_{\text{Anc}}=\mathbb{E}\left[\mathrm{KL}\left(P_{\Theta}(\mathcal{R}^{\text{adv}}\mid\mathcal{P}^{\text{adv}})\;\|\;P_{\widehat{\Theta}}(\mathcal{R}^{\text{adv}}\mid\mathcal{P}^{\text{adv}})\right)\right]LAnc​=E[KL(PΘ​(Radv∣Padv)∥PΘ​(Radv∣Padv))] L=LAct+λLAnc\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{\text{Act}}+\lambda\mathcal{L}_{\text{Anc}}L=LAct​+λLAnc​

Intuition. Keep the advantageous position anchored while the weak positions are being aligned. The anchor stops the model from flattening the very position that provided the teacher signal.

Symbols.

  • \mathcal{L}_{\text{Anc}}: anchoring loss on the advantageous position
  • \mathcal{L}_{\text{Act}}: total activation loss over the weak positions
  • \mathcal{L}: final retrieval training objective
  • \lambda: weight that controls how strongly the model protects the anchor

Read-aloud meaning. Teach weak positions to match the strong one, but also penalize the strong one if it starts drifting.

Why it matters. Without the anchor, the model could learn that every position should be averaged together, which would erase the sink-position advantage that makes the whole distillation loop work.

Reasoning: copy the whole trajectory, not just the answer string

Reasoning is more than retrieval plus a final answer. The model has to retrieve the relevant pieces and then choose a reasoning path that stays consistent across steps. The paper treats the best arrangement for that process as the recent position, specifically the last two slots \mathcal{P}^{(n-1,n)} for the two-hop setup.

The teacher data here is not a single answer token. It is a chain-of-thought trajectory sampled from the advantageous arrangement. The student is then trained on weak-position pairs to reproduce that trajectory with cross-entropy. In other words, the model is learning a better reasoning path, not just a better answer format.

Formula card: reasoning trajectory distillation

Purpose. Train weak-position prompts to reproduce the teacher’s reasoning trajectory from the advantageous position.

Equation.

L=−∑k=1Klog⁡M(Cadv∣P(nkpre,nkpost))\mathcal{L}=-\sum_{k=1}^{\mathcal{K}}\log\mathcal{M}\left(\mathcal{C}^{\text{adv}}\mid\mathcal{P}^{\left(n_{k}^{\text{pre}},n_{k}^{\text{post}}\right)}\right)L=−k=1∑K​logM(Cadv∣P(nkpre​,nkpost​))

Intuition. The right reasoning path matters more than any single output token. The model should learn to produce the same CoT trajectory even when the relevant documents are moved to weaker positions.

Symbols.

  • \mathcal{L}: reasoning training loss
  • \mathcal{K}: number of weak prompt pairs sampled for one example
  • \mathcal{M}: model being trained
  • \mathcal{C}^{\text{adv}}: teacher chain-of-thought trajectory from the advantageous position
  • \mathcal{P}^{\left(n_{k}^{\text{pre}},n_{k}^{\text{post}}\right)}: prompt with the two hop documents placed at positions n_k^{pre} and n_k^{post}
  • n_{k}^{\text{pre}}, n_{k}^{\text{post}}: positions for the first-hop and second-hop documents

Read-aloud meaning. Given a weaker arrangement of the same evidence, train the model to reproduce the strong-position reasoning path.

Why it matters. Reasoning bias is less like a shifted token and more like a drifting trajectory, so the repair has to move the whole path rather than only a few output probabilities.

That difference is the reason the paper says token shifting is not the right assumption for reasoning. The failure lives earlier in the process, when the model chooses the wrong thought path, so the supervision needs to target the path itself.

What the attention map looks like after training

The paper’s analysis figure gives a useful internal check. It tracks how attention over the documents changes as the gold document moves through the context, and it shows that Pos2Distill makes the model focus on the relevant document more consistently.

What to notice is that the model is not merely memorizing a better answer string. Its attention becomes more position-stable. That is the mechanistic version of the external result: the model is learning to route evidence more reliably even when the gold content is not sitting in an easy location.

What the paper shows in practice

The results line up with the mechanism. On retrieval, the weak positions move much closer to the sink-position behavior. For example, on WebQ, Llama-3-8B reaches a 56.7 average score, very close to its 57.9 sink-position score. On reasoning, the gains are stronger when the task needs a full trajectory: on MusiQue, Pos2Distill-R2 reaches 42.8 EM on Llama-3.1-8B, ahead of the strongest listed baseline at 39.9.

The data-efficiency story also fits the design. The model is not learning from scratch; it is learning to copy behavior it already knows how to produce in a strong position. That is why the paper can get useful gains with modest training sets, including the reported 250-example Mistral setting on NQ.

Where it still bends

The method is effective, but it is not position-agnostic magic.

  • It depends on being able to identify positions that are reliably advantageous.
  • The reasoning variant is less fine-grained than the retrieval variant, because whole CoT trajectories are harder to calibrate than token recovery.
  • The evaluation is centered on retrieval and multi-hop QA, so broader long-context behaviors still need more testing.

Those limits also point to the open questions the paper leaves behind. Could advantageous positions be discovered dynamically instead of picked by heuristic? Could longer reasoning chains use a more unified objective that repairs both token recovery and trajectory choice? The paper suggests those directions, but it does not solve them yet.

What to remember

Pos2Distill is easiest to remember as “let the best position teach the weak positions.”

  • Retrieval uses KL distillation from the sink position, plus position-aware weighting and anchoring.
  • Reasoning uses cross-entropy distillation from the recent position pair, because the whole chain of thought has to be copied.
  • The backbone stays the same; only the positional arrangement of the teacher signal changes.

The durable lesson is that position bias does not have to be fought only with architecture changes. Sometimes the simplest repair is to treat the model’s strongest positions as built-in teachers and then transfer that behavior to the positions that need help.
