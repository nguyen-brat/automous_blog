Length Generalization in Decoder-only Transformers: Does a Good Embedding Matter?

The paper asks a question that sounds simple but is not: if a decoder-only Transformer is trained on short sequences, which positional scheme still helps when the test sequence gets longer than anything seen in training?

The answer is not the one most people would expect. On these downstream reasoning-style tasks, the model without any explicit positional encoding is the strongest overall. T5-style relative bias is the best explicit method, ALiBi sits in the middle, and Rotary behaves more like the absolute baseline than the relative one. The headline is not just that NoPE works. It is that NoPE works while adding no positional computation at all.

You can think about the paper as a small pipeline:

That pipeline is important because the usual in-distribution benchmark is too forgiving. Inside the training window, almost every method looks fine. The real separation appears only when the model has to extrapolate to unseen longer lengths.

The first thing the benchmark reveals

The paper uses the same decoder-only architecture, the same hyperparameters, and training from scratch for every positional setup. That keeps the comparison clean. The tasks are also deliberately structured: copying and reversing, addition, polynomial evaluation, sorting, summation, parity, LEGO, SCAN, and PCFG. They cover simple sequence manipulation as well as compositional reasoning.

The evaluation target is exact-match accuracy, not perplexity alone. That choice matters. A model can look fluent on a language-modeling metric and still miss the right answer on a structured downstream task. Here, the question is whether the model actually solves the task at longer lengths.

On seen lengths, the models are nearly perfect or close to it. That is why the paper does not stop there. The interesting part is the unseen region, where the test lengths exceed the training support.

What to notice in the ranking is the broad ordering, not the tiny gaps. NoPE is best overall. T5 relative bias is the strongest explicit positional method. ALiBi helps less than people often assume, and Rotary is much closer to APE than to the relative-bias family in this benchmark.

That is a surprisingly strong claim because Rotary is often marketed as a relative scheme. In this paper’s downstream setting, the algebraic similarity is not enough to guarantee the same extrapolation behavior.

Why NoPE can still know where it is

The paper does not just report the result. It asks why NoPE can work at all without an explicit position channel.

The short answer is that a causal decoder-only Transformer can infer useful position structure from the sequence geometry and the causal mask. It does not need a printed position ID on every token to recover order information.

Absolute position first

The constructive proof for absolute positions is a bit artificial, but the intuition is clean. The model can use the beginning-of-sequence token as an anchor. Because the causal mask only lets each token look backward, the size of the visible prefix already carries counting information. The proof shows that with a suitable parameterization, the first layer can turn that prefix-length signal into a hidden feature that behaves like an absolute position code.

The important thing is not the exact handcrafted weights. It is the existence claim. NoPE is not trapped in a bag-of-words regime. A decoder-only Transformer can build a counter-like signal internally.

Then relative distance

Once an internal absolute signal exists somewhere in the hidden state, later layers can combine it with token content to produce relative-distance behavior. The paper shows that the attention score can be decomposed into a content term plus a term that depends only on the offset between two positions.

The paper is careful here: this does not mean SGD must learn the same exact construction. It means the model class has enough capacity to do it, so the observed behavior is plausible rather than magical.

What the attention maps say in practice

The theory becomes more convincing when the attention patterns line up with it.

To compare NoPE with explicit positional encodings, the paper measures how close the attention distributions are, layer by layer, and then keeps the closest pair of heads between two models. This is a nice way to ask a practical question: if NoPE has learned a useful position strategy, which explicit scheme does it look like?

Why it matters. This is how the paper supports the claim that NoPE is not just working by accident. Its attention looks most like T5 relative bias and least like APE or Rotary.

What to notice in the figure is the consistent ordering. Across layers, NoPE stays closest to T5-style relative bias. It is farther from APE and Rotary. The same-seed NoPE baseline is a useful sanity check: the distance is not just random noise from optimization.

That aligns with the ranking figure above. The same method that wins the extrapolation benchmark also learns attention behavior that looks relative rather than absolute.

Why scratchpad does not make position irrelevant

The paper also asks whether scratchpad or chain-of-thought style outputs make the positional choice matter less. The answer is no.

Scratchpad creates intermediate computation steps in the output, so the model gets explicit room to carry out partial reasoning. That can help, but it is not a universal fix. The authors vary five scratchpad components in each step:

  • input
  • computation
  • output
  • variable_update
  • remaining_input

That design lets them turn parts of the scratchpad on and off to see what the model actually uses. Because scratchpad makes sequences much longer, they lower the length threshold to L = 8 for those experiments to avoid memory issues.

The result is narrow but important. Across positional methods and scratchpad formats, scratchpad helps mainly on addition. On the other tasks, its effect is weaker or inconsistent. So the presence of intermediate reasoning text does not make positional encoding a solved problem.

The more interesting mechanistic result comes from the attention-distance analysis on the addition task. NoPE and T5 relative bias show a bimodal pattern: they attend both to nearby scratchpad steps and to far-away input tokens. ALiBi, with its recency bias, leans heavily toward short-range attention. Rotary looks much flatter, closer to APE.

That bimodal pattern is a good mental model for why the best methods win. The model needs to keep two things visible at once: the current scratchpad state and the original problem statement.

Why the paper matters, and where it stops

The paper’s strongest practical message is that explicit positional encoding is not always necessary for decoder-only Transformers to generalize well to longer downstream sequences. In this setting, NoPE is not just competitive. It is the best aggregate choice, and it does so without the extra cost of computing a positional term in attention.

The paper also shows why perplexity is an incomplete guide. In the 1B-scale discussion, the in-distribution perplexities are similar across variants, but the out-of-distribution behavior separates sharply. Rotary fails badly, NoPE and ALiBi extend farther, and ALiBi becomes relatively more stable at the longest contexts tested. That is exactly the kind of mismatch that downstream exact-match evaluation is meant to reveal.

There are still clear limits:

  • The study focuses on decoder-only models trained from scratch.
  • The tasks are synthetic or structured, not open-ended natural language.
  • Large-scale pretraining and instruction tuning could change the picture.
  • The paper’s own fine-tuning probe at 1.3B scale is only preliminary.

So the safe conclusion is not “position does not matter.” The safer conclusion is more precise: in this causal decoder setting, a model can learn useful relative-style position behavior without an explicit positional encoding, and that behavior can generalize better than several common explicit schemes.

What to remember

If you only keep one idea from the paper, keep this:

NoPE can still learn position-like behavior because causal attention gives the model enough structure to build an internal counter and then turn that counter into relative-distance attention.

That is why the paper matters. It challenges the assumption that explicit position features are the default path to good length generalization. In this benchmark, the model with no explicit position signal is not a weak baseline. It is the strongest aggregate choice.
