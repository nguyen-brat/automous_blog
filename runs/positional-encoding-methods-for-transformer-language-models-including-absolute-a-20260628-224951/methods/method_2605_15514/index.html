RoPE Distinguishes Neither Positions Nor Tokens in Long Contexts, Provably

This paper is a warning shot more than a new recipe. It asks what happens when the rotary positional signal that works so well at normal lengths has to keep running across much longer contexts. The answer is unsettling: the score stops behaving like a clean position ruler and starts behaving like a noisy random variable.

You can keep the whole story in one flow:

The key claim is not just that RoPE decays with distance. It is that, once context gets long enough, RoPE can no longer reliably preserve two things Transformers need at the same time:

  • where a token is located,
  • and how relevant one token is compared with another.

That is why the paper treats longer context as a tradeoff, not a free extension.

How RoPE actually makes a score

The paper starts with the raw RoPE product, which is the unnormalized dot product after rotary position encoding has been applied to query and key vectors. The cleanest way to think about it is as a stack of little rotating waves, one per rotary frequency.

Formula card: RoPE product as summed rotating waves

Purpose: show the raw attention score that RoPE produces before softmax.

Equation:

S(m)=∑n=0h−1ancos⁡(mθn+ϕn)S(m)=\sum_{n=0}^{h-1} a_n \cos(m\theta^n+\phi_n)S(m)=n=0∑h−1​an​cos(mθn+ϕn​)

Intuition: each rotary pair contributes one cosine wave. The attention score is their superposition, so long-range behavior comes from many waves interacting at once.

Symbols:

  • S(m): the RoPE score at relative distance m
  • m: the query-key distance
  • h: the number of rotary pairs in the head, equal to half the head dimension
  • a_n: the amplitude of the nth rotary component
  • \theta^n: the rotation speed of the nth component, set by the RoPE base
  • \phi_n: the phase offset for that component

Read-aloud meaning: the RoPE score is a weighted sum of many rotating cosine waves.

Why it matters: this is the mechanism behind both RoPE’s useful locality bias and its long-context failure modes. Once the score is a waveform, distance can create oscillation, decay, or accidental collisions.

The important point is where this score sits in attention. It is the number softmax sees. So if the raw score itself becomes ambiguous, softmax is already starting from a damaged signal.

RoPE is usually described as having two useful behaviors:

  • fast rotary terms create local contrast between nearby positions,
  • slow rotary terms create a broad downward trend that favors recency.

That sounds good, but it also hides a trap. The same spectrum that gives RoPE its locality bias can become unstable when the context grows. The paper is especially interested in that transition.

Why long context turns the score into a bell curve

The paper’s main theoretical move is to stop treating the score as a deterministic waveform and start treating it like a random variable over long ranges of positions. Over a large interval of distances, the many oscillating terms can be approximated as a normal distribution.

Formula card: long-range RoPE score looks approximately normal

Purpose: capture the long-context distribution of the RoPE score.

Equation:

S~∼N(μM,σM2)\widetilde{S}\sim N(\mu_M,\sigma_M^2)S∼N(μM​,σM2​)

Intuition: as the distance range grows, the high-frequency terms behave like oscillatory noise while the low-frequency terms set the average level. The whole score begins to look bell-shaped.

Symbols:

  • \widetilde{S}: the approximate RoPE score over a long interval of distances
  • N(\mu_M,\sigma_M^2): a normal distribution with mean \mu_M and variance \sigma_M^2
  • \mu_M: the average score over the interval
  • \sigma_M: the spread of the score over the interval
  • M: the context length limit

Read-aloud meaning: over long contexts, the RoPE score behaves like a normal random variable with a context-dependent center and spread.

Why it matters: this is the bridge from rotating waves to probabilistic failure. Once the score is approximately bell-shaped, questions about position order and token order become probability questions.

Think of the spectrum this way:

  • high-frequency terms mostly add oscillation, so they inflate the spread,
  • low-frequency terms mostly add the broad trend, so they set the mean.

So the score is pulled in two directions at once. It wants to decay with distance, but it also keeps oscillating. The paper’s point is that, as context grows, those oscillations do not stay harmless. They begin to look like noise.

That is why the paper’s failure-mode summary is so blunt: larger M makes every failure more likely, while larger RoPE base B shifts the tradeoff rather than eliminating it. A bigger base slows the rotations down, which helps token separation, but it also weakens position separation. That is the core design tension.

Failure-mode summary

The paper names four ways this tension shows up:

  • position inversion: a farther key gets a higher score than a nearer one,
  • position aliasing: two different positions collapse to the same score,
  • token inversion: a key ranking that was correct at one distance flips at another,
  • token aliasing: two different tokens become indistinguishable at a distance.

The shortest way to remember the table in the paper is this:

  • more context M makes all four failures more likely,
  • a larger RoPE base B hurts position discrimination,
  • a larger RoPE base B helps token discrimination.

So the paper is not saying “RoPE is bad.” It is saying that RoPE cannot preserve both near-vs-far order and token-ranking stability forever just by stretching the rotary clock.

When positions stop being trustworthy

The position side is the easier place to see the problem. If a farther token can beat a nearer token, RoPE has lost the locality bias that made it useful in the first place.

Position inversion

Position inversion means exactly what it sounds like: the closer key should have scored higher, but the farther key wins instead. The paper proves that this failure gets more likely as the context grows, and the probability heads toward 0.5, which is basically a coin flip.

That is a strong statement. It says that long-range comparisons stop being predictably local. The model is no longer reliably favoring the nearer token just because it is nearer.

The paper’s empirical case study makes this visible in a single head of Llama 3.1-8B. As the key is moved farther away, the score stops following a clean downward pattern. The trend bottoms out and then starts climbing again, with oscillations layered on top. The paper reports that this reversal begins to show up around the 50K-token scale in that head.

Position aliasing

Position aliasing is even harsher. Instead of the score flipping order, two different positions become numerically indistinguishable. In other words, the score no longer tells those distances apart.

This is where finite precision matters. The paper does not only care about exact mathematical equality. It cares about whether the computed score difference survives the datatype’s resolution. In BF16 or FP16, a tiny real-valued difference can be rounded away and become a tie.

In the case study, this is not a rare corner case. Within an 8K BF16 context, the paper reports more than 75K position aliasing pairs. That means position collisions are already abundant at lengths that do not sound extreme for modern LLMs.

The important intuition is that aliasing is not just a theoretical curiosity. If two positions collapse to the same score, the model cannot use RoPE to separate them cleanly anymore. The score has stopped being a reliable coordinate system.

When token ranking stops being trustworthy

The token side is a little subtler. Here the question is not “which position is closer?” but “which key is more relevant?”

Token inversion

Token inversion happens when the relative ordering of two keys flips as distance changes. One key is more relevant at zero distance, but at some nonzero distance the other key overtakes it.

The reason this matters is that a model usually needs the same key to stay more relevant across a range of placements. If that ranking keeps flipping, then relevance is no longer stable under position changes.

The paper proves the same kind of long-context deterioration here too: as context grows, the probability of token inversion also approaches 0.5. So this is not a small perturbation. It is a stability failure.

The RoPE base tradeoff shows up clearly here. Slowing the rotations down helps keep token relevance ordering intact, which is why larger bases can improve token discrimination. But that same slowdown weakens the position signal. The paper’s message is that you can ease one side of the problem, but you do not get both for free.

Token aliasing

Token aliasing is the strongest version of the failure. Instead of swapping order, two different tokens produce the same score at some distance. The model then cannot distinguish them through the RoPE product at that point.

This is where the paper’s Token aliasing probability example is useful.

What to notice in the figure is not the exact curve shape but the existence of many distances where two different token choices become too close to tell apart. That is the practical meaning of aliasing: a score gap that matters in real arithmetic can disappear after rounding.

The paper reports that token aliasing is already visible in the 8K case study, with around 150 token aliasing positions. It also notes that the count rises with context length. So token collisions are not a fringe artifact of huge windows; they are part of the same long-context drift that affects the position signal.

Why the paper thinks this is intrinsic, not just an engineering bug

The big conceptual move in the paper is to separate the mechanism from the implementation. It does not argue that some kernel bug or one model’s training setup caused the problem. It argues that the RoPE mechanism itself degrades under long contexts in a predictable way.

That is why the analysis is single-head and context-length centric. The authors strip away the content of the prompt and ask what happens to the rotary score as the distance range grows. Then they check the same idea in a real model and in a simple indexing task.

The indexing task is a good choice because it isolates position identification. The model sees a Python list of tiny integers and must return the value at a requested index. Since the list values are only 0 through 3, this is almost a pure bookkeeping test. If the model cannot locate the right position, it is effectively guessing.

Across six open long-context LLMs, the paper finds that the models start strong and then fall close to random by only a few thousand tokens. That result matters because it shows the theoretical failure is not limited to a toy head. The full model still loses track of positions surprisingly early.

The paper also uses Llama 3.1-8B as a case study because it is a representative open long-context model. The point of that case study is not to claim the whole network is literally one head. It is to show that the same kind of score instability is visible in a real model’s attention head and that the broader model does not fully wash the problem away.

The practical lesson

The most useful lesson is also the most uncomfortable one: longer advertised context windows do not automatically mean reliable long-context reasoning.

If RoPE is the positional mechanism, then context extension is only a tradeoff. A larger base can make token discrimination better, but it comes at the cost of weaker position discrimination. The paper’s conclusion is that future long-context systems probably need something beyond standard rotary signals if they want to preserve both properties at once.

What to remember

If you want one compact memory hook, use this:

RoPE is a bank of rotating waves. At short range, the waves give useful position bias. At long range, the same waves become noisy enough that positions can invert, positions can alias, tokens can invert, and tokens can alias.

That is the whole paper in one sentence. The failure-mode summary is not a side result; it is the main message.

The caveats are worth keeping in mind:

  • the high-versus-low frequency split is only approximate,
  • the regular-amplitude assumption is a simplification,
  • full multihead and multilayer behavior is checked empirically rather than fully derived.

Even with those limits, the argument is strong enough to change how you think about RoPE scaling. It is not a complete fix. It is a tradeoff knob.
