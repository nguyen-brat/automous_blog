Make Your LLM Fully Utilize the Context

The paper starts from a problem that looks simple but is surprisingly stubborn: a long-context model can accept more tokens without actually using them well. If the useful clue lands near the middle, the model may drift toward the beginning or the end instead of reading the whole window evenly. In2 training is the paper’s answer to that failure mode. Instead of changing the attention mechanism first, it changes the practice set. The model is repeatedly trained on synthetic long-context questions where the answer-bearing evidence can appear anywhere in the window.

That makes the method feel less like a new architecture and more like a disciplined scavenger hunt. The model is shown a long context, the decisive clue is hidden inside it, and the loss only rewards the answer. Over many examples, the model learns a new habit: important information is not always near the edges.

The core idea in one sentence

Make long-context supervision position-robust by hiding answer-bearing segments at random places inside 4K to 32K contexts, then fine-tune on both single-segment retrieval and multi-segment reasoning.

That sentence is the whole method. Everything else in the paper is either the machinery for building those examples or the evaluation that checks whether the habit really changed.

Figure 2 is the best way to picture the recipe

Figure 2 is the clearest overview of the data construction pipeline. It shows how the paper turns a short source segment into a long synthetic context, then uses that context to train the model on targeted questions.

What to notice in Figure 2:

  • The upper path is about fine-grained awareness: one short segment contains the answer, but that segment is buried inside a much longer context.
  • The lower path is about integration: the answer only appears when the model combines information from multiple short segments.
  • Random distractor segments are not noise for its own sake. They are what force the model to stop assuming that the useful clue will always be sitting in the first or last place it looks.

The visual trick is simple, but the training effect is not. The model is no longer learning “find the obvious sentence near the edge.” It is learning “search the whole context, then decide what matters.”

How one synthetic example is built

The paper treats about 128 tokens as the smallest useful unit of information. That choice matters because it gives the training recipe a concrete granularity. The model is not told to reason over a whole document blob. It is told to recover a specific short segment or to combine a few such segments when the answer requires it.

The construction loop is straightforward:

  1. sample a short source segment from a general text corpus;
  2. generate a question-answer pair that is tightly tied to that segment or segment set;
  3. build a longer context by mixing the answer-bearing material with random distractor segments;
  4. shuffle the segments so the useful evidence can land anywhere in the window;
  5. fine-tune on the answer tokens.

That loop is the heart of In2. The long context is not just long for the sake of length. It is long because the model needs repeated practice with evidence that may appear at any position.

Formula card: single-segment clue retrieval

Purpose. Teach the model to answer a question from one short evidence segment, even when that segment is buried inside a longer context.

Equation.

(qi,ai)∼Prompting(si,If;LLM),Li=⊕{Shuffle(si,[rj])}(q_i, a_i) \sim \mathrm{Prompting}(s_i, I_f; \mathrm{LLM}), \qquad \mathcal{L}_i = \oplus \{ \mathrm{Shuffle}(s_i, [r_j]) \}(qi​,ai​)∼Prompting(si​,If​;LLM),Li​=⊕{Shuffle(si​,[rj​])}

Intuition. Start with one short answer-bearing segment s_i, ask a strong generator model to create a highly specific question and answer from that segment, then build a long context by concatenating the clue segment with randomly sampled distractor segments.

Symbols.

  • q_i: the generated question for example i
  • a_i: the answer to q_i
  • s_i: the short source segment that contains the key evidence
  • I_f: the instruction that tells the generator to make the question highly specific to s_i
  • \mathrm{LLM}: the model used to generate the synthetic question-answer pair
  • \mathcal{L}_i: the assembled long context for training example i
  • \oplus: concatenation
  • \mathrm{Shuffle}: the operation that mixes the evidence segment with distractor segments before concatenation
  • [r_j]: randomly sampled distractor segments from the corpus

Read-aloud meaning. Generate a segment-specific question and answer from one clue segment, then shuffle that clue together with unrelated segments to make the final long context.

Why it matters. This is the simplest form of position-robust supervision. The model has to recover one precise piece of evidence no matter where the segment lands in the context window.

The important part here is not just the question generation. It is the random placement. If the answer-bearing segment always appeared in the same place, the model could fall back on positional habit. Random shuffling removes that shortcut.

Formula card: multi-segment reasoning

Purpose. Teach the model to combine information from two or more short segments, not just retrieve one isolated clue.

Equation.

(qi,ai)∼Prompting({si},Ir;LLM),Li=⊕{Shuffle({si},[rj])}(q_i, a_i) \sim \mathrm{Prompting}(\{s_i\}, I_r; \mathrm{LLM}), \qquad \mathcal{L}_i = \oplus \{ \mathrm{Shuffle}(\{s_i\}, [r_j]) \}(qi​,ai​)∼Prompting({si​},Ir​;LLM),Li​=⊕{Shuffle({si​},[rj​])}

Intuition. Instead of asking for a fact from one segment, ask a question that only makes sense after the model joins multiple relevant segments together, then hide those segments among distractors in a long context.

Symbols.

  • q_i: the generated multi-hop question
  • a_i: the answer that depends on several source segments
  • \{s_i\}: the collection of answer-bearing short segments
  • I_r: the instruction that asks for multi-segment reasoning
  • \mathrm{LLM}: the model used to draft the synthetic question-answer pair
  • \mathcal{L}_i: the final long context used for training
  • \oplus: concatenation
  • \mathrm{Shuffle}: the operation that jointly shuffles the answer-bearing segments and distractors
  • [r_j]: randomly sampled distractor segments

Read-aloud meaning. Generate a question that requires several evidence fragments, then mix those fragments with distractors and train the model to recover the combined answer.

Why it matters. This is what turns the recipe from retrieval practice into actual long-context reasoning practice. The model must learn to gather evidence across the window, not just find one sentence.

Together, these two training modes cover the two behaviors the paper cares about most: finding a single clue no matter where it appears, and integrating multiple clues when no single segment is enough.

Why the context lengths are balanced

The paper does not train on one fixed synthetic length and call it a day. It spreads the long contexts across roughly 4K to 32K tokens so the model sees a range of long-distance layouts instead of overfitting to one size.

That balance matters because a model can otherwise learn a narrow trick. If every training example has the same length, the model may become good at one positional scale and less flexible elsewhere. By distributing lengths more evenly, the paper encourages a weaker dependence on any one location inside the window.

The mixture also protects short-context ability. The recipe keeps a small slice of ordinary short-context QA and some general instruction-tuning data in the mix. That way the model does not spend all of its adaptation budget learning synthetic long documents and then forget how to answer normal short prompts.

This is one of the paper’s quiet strengths. It is not only trying to improve long-context retrieval. It is trying to do so without wrecking everyday instruction following.

What the model is actually trained to do

The fine-tuning step is ordinary instruction tuning in spirit, but with a very specific target. The long context and the question are fed in as instructions, and the loss is applied to the answer tokens. That keeps the supervision focused on the actual retrieval or reasoning outcome instead of encouraging the model to copy the entire synthetic prompt.

In plain language, the model is being taught to read, think, and answer from the answer-bearing part of the context. It is not being asked to memorize the surface form of the long document.

That distinction matters because the whole point of In2 is position-robust evidence use. If the model only learns to reproduce patterns from the prompt, it has not really learned to search the window.

Why the probe is harder than a needle test

The paper argues that Needle-in-the-Haystack can be too easy. It uses a natural-language document style and a forward retrieval pattern, which is close to a simple continuation task. A model can look good there while still being shaky on other retrieval shapes.

VaL Probing is the paper’s answer to that blind spot. It tests three context styles and three retrieval patterns:

  • document sentence retrieval, which is bi-directional because the needed span can include words before and after the query fragment;
  • code function retrieval, which is backward because the function name comes before the body;
  • database entity retrieval, which is forward because the label and description follow the ID.

That mix is useful because it forces the model to retrieve in more than one direction and from more than one kind of structure. A model that only learned one retrieval habit can pass a simple needle test and still fail here.

The headline result is that FilM-7B averages 85.9 overall on VaL Probing with a 13.9 min-max gap. That gap is the telling number. It means performance is much flatter across positions, which is exactly what you want if the training goal is to make the model use the whole context instead of only the easy parts.

The paper also reports that the model is comparable to a proprietary baseline on the database task and more robust on the document and code tasks. Read that as evidence that the synthetic supervision is not just helping on one toy retrieval shape. It is changing the model’s long-context behavior in a broader way.

What the ablations teach

Two analysis results are especially useful if you want to remember the method.

First, a 4K sliding window still breaks retrieval once the evidence falls beyond the visible range. That is an important boundary condition. In2 can teach the model to use long contexts, but it cannot recover information that the attention mask has already cut away.

Second, increasing the RoPE base above the default improves VaL Probing during In2 training. The paper’s practical interpretation is that higher information intensity needs more room in position space. In other words, the data recipe and the position encoding still need to agree with each other.

Those two findings give the method its limits and its tuning advice at the same time. The supervision helps a lot, but architectural constraints still matter.

What to remember

The easiest way to remember In2 is this: hide the clue anywhere, not just near the edges, and train the model until it stops assuming the middle is unimportant.

That is why the method works. It changes the training distribution so that useful evidence can appear at any position, and it makes the model practice both one-segment retrieval and multi-segment reasoning under that condition. The result is better long-context utilization without a clear short-context collapse.

The tradeoff is real: the method depends on synthetic long-context data and fine-tuning, and the paper’s evidence is strongest around 32K. But within that setting, the lesson is clear. Better long-context behavior can come from targeted supervision, not only from a bigger window.
