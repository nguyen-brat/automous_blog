Base of RoPE Bounds Context Length

RoPE base is often treated like a harmless scaling knob. This paper argues it is really a capacity parameter: if the base is too small, the model can still sound fluent, but the distance signal stops being strong enough to prefer the right distant token over nearby decoys.

The easiest way to keep the idea straight is this:

That is the paper’s shift. It does not just ask whether rotary angles look out of distribution. It asks whether the attention mechanism still ranks the right evidence above noise when the context gets long.

Why low perplexity is not enough

A language model can keep next-token prediction smooth and still be terrible at retrieval. If the local syntax around the current token is enough to predict the next word, the model can look healthy on perplexity even after its positional signal has become too weak to identify a distant fact.

What to notice in this figure:

  • perplexity can stay low even when retrieval accuracy falls off a cliff,
  • the failure shows up much earlier on Long-eval or needle-style retrieval than on next-token prediction,
  • a small base can make the model feel long-context aware without actually recovering the right far-away evidence.

That is why the paper pushes beyond perplexity. Perplexity tells you whether the model can keep modeling text smoothly; it does not tell you whether it can still tell a relevant old sentence from a pile of decoys.

The older decay story only tells half the truth

The paper first revisits a familiar RoPE fact: as relative distance grows, the upper bound on attention score decays. That is the classic long-term decay view, and it already tells us that nearby tokens are easier to prefer than far ones.

The paper’s Upper bound of attention score vs distance plot makes that envelope easy to see.

What to notice here:

  • the envelope falls as distance increases,
  • this explains why far-away interactions are fragile,
  • but it still does not answer the more important question: does the right far token still beat a random one?

That missing piece is where the paper’s new analysis begins.

The sharper question: does a similar token still outrank a random token?

The paper defines a more task-relevant diagnostic. Suppose the current token has query vector q. A similar token has key vector k^* = q + \epsilon, where \epsilon is small zero-mean noise. A random token has key vector k.

Now the question is not just “how big is attention?” but “how much more attention does the model give to the similar token than to the random one?”

Formula Card: Discrimination gap under RoPE

Purpose: measure whether RoPE still lets a relevant distant token outrank a random token.

Equation:

12σ2(Eq,k∗[qTRm,θk∗]−Eq,k[qTRm,θk])=∑i=0d/2−1cos⁡(mθi)\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \left( \mathbb{E}_{q,k^*}[q^T R_{m,\theta} k^*] - \mathbb{E}_{q,k}[q^T R_{m,\theta} k] \right) = \sum_{i=0}^{d/2-1}\cos(m\theta_i)2σ21​(Eq,k∗​[qTRm,θ​k∗]−Eq,k​[qTRm,θ​k])=i=0∑d/2−1​cos(mθi​)

Intuition: after stripping away scale, the positional part is just a cosine sum over rotary bands. As m grows, that sum can shrink, oscillate, and eventually flip sign.

Symbols:

  • q: query vector for the current token.
  • k^*: key vector of a similar token, modeled as q + \epsilon.
  • k: key vector of a random token.
  • R_{m,\theta}: RoPE rotation at relative distance m with frequency schedule \theta.
  • m: relative token distance.
  • \theta_i: frequency for the i-th rotary pair.
  • \sigma: shared standard deviation of the query and key components.
  • d: hidden size, split into d/2 rotary pairs.

Read-aloud meaning: the expected scoring advantage of a similar token over a random token equals the sum of cosine responses across rotary bands.

Why it matters: this turns long-context ability into a discrimination problem, not just a score-magnitude problem.

The paper names the cosine sum on the right side B_{m,\theta}. That is the distance-sensitive relevance budget the method cares about. When B_{m,\theta} stays nonnegative, the model still has a reason to prefer the similar token over a random one. When it drops below zero, the geometry has become so distorted that the random token can score higher.

That is the decisive insight. A model does not need to fail everywhere for long context to fail in practice. It only needs to lose the ability to rank the right far-away evidence above the wrong far-away evidence.

How the base turns into a lower bound

RoPE uses a simple frequency schedule:

Formula Card: RoPE frequency schedule

Purpose: show how the base parameter sets the rotation frequency of each rotary pair.

Equation:

θi=base−2i/d\theta_i = base^{-2i/d}θi​=base−2i/d

Intuition: the base controls how quickly the rotary clocks spin. A larger base makes the frequencies smaller, so the rotation changes more slowly with distance.

Symbols:

  • \theta_i: frequency for rotary pair i.
  • base: the RoPE base hyperparameter.
  • i: the pair index.
  • d: hidden dimension.

Read-aloud meaning: the i-th RoPE frequency is the base raised to a negative power that depends on the dimension index.

Why it matters: once base sets all the frequencies, it also sets where the cosine-sum signal starts to lose discriminative power.

The paper turns that signal into a concrete notion of usable range:

Formula Card: Effective context length

Purpose: define how far the model can go before the similar-vs-random advantage disappears.

Equation:

Lθ=sup⁡{L∣Bm,θ≥0, ∀m∈{0,1,…,L}}L_\theta = \sup\{L \mid B_{m,\theta} \ge 0,\ \forall m \in \{0,1,\dots,L\}\}Lθ​=sup{L∣Bm,θ​≥0, ∀m∈{0,1,…,L}}

Intuition: the effective context is the longest distance range over which the model can still keep the discrimination signal at least nonnegative.

Symbols:

  • L_\theta: effective context length for a given RoPE frequency schedule.
  • B_{m,\theta}: the cosine-sum discrimination signal at distance m.
  • L: a candidate context length.
  • m: relative distance, ranging from 0 up to L.

Read-aloud meaning: the effective context length is the largest range of distances for which the similar-token advantage never goes below zero.

Why it matters: this replaces vague “long-context support” with a concrete criterion tied to token ranking.

From there the paper defines the design rule directly:

Formula Card: Lower bound of RoPE base by target context length

Purpose: turn the context-length goal into a lower bound on the RoPE base.

Equation:

baseL=inf⁡{base∣Bm,θ≥0, ∀m∈{0,1,…,L}}base_L = \inf\{base \mid B_{m,\theta} \ge 0,\ \forall m \in \{0,1,\dots,L\}\}baseL​=inf{base∣Bm,θ​≥0, ∀m∈{0,1,…,L}}

Intuition: if you want a longer usable context, you need a base large enough that the cosine-sum signal stays nonnegative over that whole range.

Symbols:

  • base_L: the minimum RoPE base needed for target context length L.
  • base: a candidate RoPE base.
  • B_{m,\theta}: the discrimination signal.
  • L: target context length.
  • m: relative distance.

Read-aloud meaning: the smallest safe base is the one that keeps the similar-vs-random signal nonnegative for every distance up to L.

Why it matters: this is the paper’s practical rule. It says RoPE base is not just a stylistic choice; it sets a lower bound on usable context length.

Because B_{m,\theta} is a sum of many cosine terms, the paper does not solve the bound in closed form. It computes the boundary numerically instead. That gives the memorable anchor result: for a 32k target context, the paper’s bound is about 6.4e5. That is far above the common 1e4 default and helps explain why some long-context setups look plausible at first but collapse on retrieval.

What the fine-tuning experiments show

The paper checks the theory by fine-tuning Llama2-7B and Baichuan2-7B on 32k context while sweeping the base value. The striking pattern is that perplexity barely changes, but retrieval quality changes a lot.

The paper’s Lower bound of RoPE base by target context length line is exactly what the fine-tuning plot is testing.

What to notice here:

  • perplexity differences are small across bases,
  • Long-eval drops sharply once the base falls below the derived bound,
  • the theoretical lower bound lines up with the retrieval collapse much better than perplexity does.

That mismatch is the whole point. If you only looked at perplexity, you might conclude that many bases are fine. But Long-eval says otherwise: once the base falls below the theoretical lower bound, retrieval accuracy drops sharply.

The practical lesson is straightforward. A base like 2e5 can even look good on perplexity while still failing retrieval badly. That is exactly what “superficial long-context capability” means here: the model can keep writing smoothly without really remembering what matters far away.

The pre-training experiment makes the same point stronger

The paper then checks whether the same lower-bound logic applies before fine-tuning, not just after it. The answer is yes.

What to notice in this figure:

  • a model trained from scratch with base 1e2 does not really use the full 4,096-token training window,
  • retrieval works only for the most recent few hundred tokens,
  • increasing the base during later fine-tuning increases effective context, but the gain is still limited by how large the base is.

This is the most concrete evidence that the base is doing more than helping with extrapolation. It determines how much of the training window the model can actually exploit.

The paper also observes that a 7B model with the same base and the same 32k fine-tuning regime ends up with nearly the same effective context as the 2B model. That is a useful sanity check: the limit is coming from the RoPE base, not mainly from model size.

Why OOD theory is not enough

The paper’s final empirical argument is a stress test against the older “avoid OOD angles” story.

It constructs two context-extension methods that are both free of angle OOD. Under the OOD story, they should both look good. In practice, they do not.

One method preserves the discrimination signal over the relevant range; the other lets B_{m,\theta} go negative across a large stretch of distances. Long-eval follows the discrimination signal, not the OOD label.

That is the paper’s strongest critique of the standard explanation. Angle OOD is a useful clue, but it is not the thing users actually care about. Users care whether the model can still retrieve the right evidence from far away.

What this paper is really saying

The cleanest way to remember the paper is this:

RoPE base is a relevance budget, not just a scaling knob.

If the base is too small, the model may still look good on local language modeling, but the long-range similarity signal collapses and distant relevant tokens stop outranking random decoys. If the base is large enough, the cosine-sum signal stays nonnegative farther out, and the model has a real chance to use that context.

The paper’s limitations are worth keeping in mind:

  • the bound is numerical rather than closed form,
  • layer stacking can sometimes push effective context beyond the single-layer bound, so the rule is not perfectly strict,
  • there may also be an upper bound on useful base values, but the paper does not settle that,
  • long-context benchmarks are still imperfect measures of real-world context use.

Even with those caveats, the design lesson is strong: do not judge RoPE extension by perplexity alone, and do not treat OOD avoidance as sufficient. Ask whether the model still prefers the right distant token over the wrong one.
