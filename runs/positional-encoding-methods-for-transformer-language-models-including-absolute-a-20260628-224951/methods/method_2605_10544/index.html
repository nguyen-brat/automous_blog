EXACT: Effective-Context Allocation for Context Training

EXACT is not trying to make the model architecture smarter. It is trying to make the training signal smarter.

The paper starts from a mismatch that is easy to miss: a packed causal-LM run can use a long window, but each supervised token only sees the same-document prefix in front of it. If most targets still have short effective left context, then “long-context training” can quietly behave like mostly short-context training. EXACT fixes that by measuring each token’s effective left context, grouping tokens into context buckets, and giving the long tail extra loss weight.

That is the whole method in one line. The stream stays the same. The model stays the same. The supervision is what changes.

What to notice in the figure:

  • the method is a control layer around training, not a new architecture;
  • the same packed stream flows through the same language-model objective;
  • the only new decision is where the extra supervision weight goes.

The real unit is effective left context

The paper’s key idea is that the right unit of analysis is not the packed sequence length. It is the target token’s effective left context: the amount of same-document text it can actually see when predicting the next token.

A token near the start of a document segment can sit inside a 16K packed sequence and still have only a few dozen visible tokens from its own document. That is why long windows alone do not guarantee long-context supervision.

Formula card: effective left context

Purpose: define the supervision variable EXACT measures for each target token.

Equation:

ℓi=i−s(i)\ell_i = i - s(i)ℓi​=i−s(i)

Intuition: count how far the current token is from the start of its own document segment, not from the start of the packed sequence.

Symbols:

  • \ell_i: effective left-context length for token i
  • i: target token position in the packed sequence
  • s(i): start position of the current document segment
  • document-boundary masking: the rule that excludes tokens from earlier documents

Read-aloud meaning: the usable context for token i is the number of same-document tokens before it.

Why it matters: a long packed window can still produce many short-context targets, so the training signal is much less “long” than the raw sequence length suggests.

A toy way to think about it: if a document segment starts at packed position 4200 and a supervised token sits at 4310, then its effective left context is only 110 tokens. The 4K or 16K window is real, but that token did not actually receive 4K or 16K worth of same-document history.

Why packed position is only a weak proxy

Before introducing the full weighting rule, the paper makes the mismatch visible with a histogram. The important point is not that the packed window is short; it is that supervision mass is still concentrated in short and medium effective-context regimes.

What to notice here:

  • the nominal window can be 16K, but most of the loss still sits below 4K effective context;
  • EXACT keeps the packed stream fixed, yet shifts more loss mass into the 4K+ tail;
  • within that tail, the farther buckets get more of the bonus.

This is also why packed-position weighting only helps a little in the ablation. Absolute position inside the packed sequence is a rough hint, but it ignores document boundaries. A token that appears late in the packed stream may still have short same-document context if a new document just started.

Turning exposures into bucket weights

EXACT does not give every long-context token the same extra weight. It first groups effective contexts into logarithmic buckets such as [0,7], [8,15], [16,31], and so on. That keeps the histogram manageable and makes the long tail easy to measure.

Inside the tail, the paper turns bucket counts into rarity scores, then turns rarity scores into weights. The rarer the bucket, the larger its share of the extra supervision.

Purpose: turn tail bucket counts into a rarity signal.

Equation:

Intuition: measure how much of the tail each bucket owns, flip that frequency so rare buckets get larger scores, then normalize the scores so the whole tail stays controlled.

Symbols:

  • b: an effective-context bucket
  • c_b: the number of supervised tokens in bucket b
  • q_b: the tail-local fraction of supervision in bucket b
  • \mathcal{T}: the set of long-context tail buckets
  • \epsilon: a tiny stabilizer to avoid division by zero
  • \gamma: the rarity sharpness knob
  • r_b: the inverse-frequency score for bucket b
  • \bar r: the tail-average rarity score used to normalize the bonus

Read-aloud meaning: buckets with less tail mass get larger rarity scores, but the overall tail bonus remains under control.

Why it matters: this is how the method turns “rare long-context case” into a concrete training weight instead of just a vague intuition.

Purpose: assign ordinary loss to common buckets and extra loss to the long-context tail.

Equation:

Intuition: common buckets keep the baseline weight of 1. Tail buckets get a bonus, and the rarest tail buckets get the largest share of that bonus.

Symbols:

  • w_b: the loss weight for bucket b
  • \mathcal{T}: the long-context tail
  • \alpha: the average extra tail weight
  • r_b: the bucket’s inverse-frequency score
  • \bar r: the tail normalizer

Read-aloud meaning: long-context buckets matter more, and the rarest long-context buckets matter most.

Why it matters: the average tail boost is fixed, so the method does not simply inflate all long-context tokens equally. It directs the extra supervision toward the least common long-context situations.

In the reported runs, the authors set alpha = 0.15 and gamma = 0.5, with the tail threshold at 1K, 2K, or 4K depending on the CPT stage. So the method nudges the tail rather than overwhelming the base language-model objective.

Intuition: keep next-token prediction exactly the same, but multiply each token’s loss by the weight for its effective-context bucket.

Symbols:

  • \mathcal{L}_{\text{EXACT}}: the final training loss
  • i: target token index
  • m_i: the standard loss mask
  • w_{b(i)}: the bucket weight for token i
  • b(i): the bucket that token i belongs to
  • \mathrm{CE}(x_i): the usual next-token cross-entropy at position i

Read-aloud meaning: the model still learns next-token prediction, but tokens that saw rarer long context count more.

Why it matters: this is why EXACT can change long-context behavior without changing the data stream or the model architecture.

What the method is really saying

The method is making a strong claim about where supervision should go. If the model is going to be tested on distant evidence, then training has to reward the rare predictions made under distant same-document context.

That sounds obvious, but it is easy to miss in packed pretraining. Long documents increase the ceiling on context length, yet the loss still lands mostly on short-context targets because document boundaries reset the visible history. EXACT is a way to pay attention to that hidden imbalance.

The result is a clean separation:

  • the packed stream decides what the model could see;
  • effective left context decides what each token actually saw;
  • the loss weights decide which tokens should matter more.

What changes in practice

The empirical pattern follows the mechanism closely. Across seven Qwen and LLaMA continued-pretraining settings, EXACT improves all 28 trained and extrapolated NoLiMa and RULER comparisons in the main table. The strongest anchor is LLaMA-3.2-3B staged 4K to 8K CPT, where RULER improves by +17.91 points in trained lengths and +16.11 points in extrapolated lengths. On Qwen2.5-0.5B 4K CPT, NoLiMa improves by +10.09 trained and +5.34 extrapolated.

Two things matter more than the raw scores:

  • the gains appear in the long-context evaluations that actually depend on distant evidence;
  • ordinary QA and reasoning stay stable, with only a +0.24 macro change across six benchmarks.

That combination is the main reason the paper is convincing. It is not just making the long-context benchmarks go up by making the model worse at everything else.

Why the ablations are the real proof

The weighting ablation is especially informative because it holds the checkpoint, stream, token budget, optimizer schedule, QA-SFT data, and evaluation protocol fixed. Only the weighting rule changes.

The ordering is the key takeaway:

  • random same-mass weighting hurts, so generic reweighting is not enough;
  • packed-position weighting helps only a little, so absolute packed position is a weak proxy;
  • a uniform effective-context boost helps much more, so the exposure variable is the right axis;
  • normalized EXACT is very close to the best result;
  • full EXACT is strongest on every aggregate.

That ordering says the method is not exploiting some accidental quirk of the loss. It is using the real bottleneck: how often the model is trained on tokens that actually have long same-document context.

Where the gains show up

The paper’s distance-resolved probe closes the loop. The improvement is not uniform across all positions. It appears mainly when relevant evidence is placed thousands of tokens away from the query. Short-distance cases stay close to standard long-window CPT, while the far-evidence regime opens up.

That matters because it matches the training story. EXACT is not trying to make every prediction harder or every token more important. It is specifically trying to make distant evidence more usable when the model is asked to rely on it.

In other words, the method teaches the model to treat long-context supervision as a scarce resource and to spend more of it on the cases that ordinary packing would under-teach.

What the method does not solve

The paper is careful about the limits. EXACT is evaluated in paired long-context continued pretraining, not from-scratch pretraining. It also depends on document boundaries and packed-stream exposure estimates, so noisy boundaries or weak statistics would weaken the signal. And it is a selective fix, not a universal repair for every long-context failure mode.

The open questions are natural:

  • would the same weighting strategy scale cleanly in full pretraining?
  • can the tail threshold adapt online instead of being set per stage?
  • how does this interact with positional-scaling methods such as RoPE variants or ALiBi?

Those are the right questions because they ask whether the supervision-allocation view generalizes, not just whether this one recipe happens to work in continued pretraining.

What to remember

EXACT is easiest to remember as a three-step idea:

  1. measure each token’s same-document left context;
  2. bucket the exposure histogram so the long tail is visible;
  3. give the rare long-context buckets more loss weight.

The deeper lesson is simpler than the math: long windows create capacity, but they do not automatically create supervision in the right places. EXACT makes the rare far-context targets count.
