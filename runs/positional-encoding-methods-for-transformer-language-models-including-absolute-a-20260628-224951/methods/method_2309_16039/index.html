Llama 2 Long: Long-Context Large Language Models from Open Source Foundations

A short-context transformer does not fail on long inputs because it forgot how to tokenize. It fails because distant tokens get harder to distinguish, and the training recipe never forces the model to care enough about them. Llama 2 Long attacks that exact problem.

The paper keeps the base model mostly intact. It slows down RoPE by raising the base frequency, continues pretraining on longer sequences, and then adds a cheap long-context instruction-tuning recipe. The result is a model that still feels like Llama 2 on normal prompts, but can actually use 16k to 32k tokens when the task needs them.

The first fix is positional, not architectural

The simplest way to read the paper is to start with the bottleneck it is trying to remove. Long inputs are not automatically useful just because the model can accept them. If the positional encoding makes far-away tokens look washed out, the attention module still struggles to tell them apart.

That is why the authors keep the dense attention stack and change the positional geometry instead of swapping in sparse attention. For the larger models, dense attention only becomes a true compute bottleneck at much longer lengths than the ones they train for, so the paper chooses the simpler route: preserve the backbone and make positions easier to read.

The paper’s visualization makes the failure mode easy to see.

What to notice in the figure is the long-distance tail. The baseline RoPE curve falls off faster as positions move apart, which means distant tokens become harder to separate. Raising the base frequency flattens that decay. Position interpolation also reduces decay, but it compresses the position space more aggressively. xPos smooths oscillation, yet once the base is adjusted it does not buy much more. The paper’s conclusion is practical: the core issue is long-range decay, and the simplest fix is to slow the rotary clocks down.

Intuition: keep the same rotary encoding, but increase the effective base so the phase rotates more slowly across positions. Far-apart tokens stop washing out so quickly.

Symbols:

  • x: token representation before positional encoding
  • t: token position
  • b: the original RoPE base frequency
  • \beta: the factor that increases the base frequency
  • d: embedding dimension
  • j: rotary pair index
  • i: imaginary unit

Read-aloud meaning: each token still gets a rotary position code, but the rotations advance more slowly because the base frequency is larger.

Why it matters: this is the core mechanism behind the paper’s long-context gain. It keeps the model’s geometry close to Llama 2 while reducing the decay that hides distant evidence.

The paper uses b = 10000 and raises it to an effective base of 500000 in the main setting. That small-looking change is the difference between a model that loses track after a few thousand tokens and one that can keep first-sentence retrieval alive across the full 32,768-token window.

Why they continue pretraining instead of starting over

Once the positional geometry is fixed, the rest of the recipe is surprisingly ordinary. They continue pretraining from Llama 2 checkpoints on longer sequences instead of training a fresh 32k model from scratch. That matters because full long-sequence pretraining is expensive: attention still scales quadratically, and redoing all of training just to reach a longer window is wasteful.

The sequence curriculum is easy to describe. Smaller models are trained on 32,768-token sequences, while the larger 34B and 70B models use 16,384-token sequences. They keep the number of tokens per batch fixed as sequence length increases, train for 400B additional tokens over 100,000 steps, and use FlashAttention so the longer window does not explode memory use.

The paper’s most useful efficiency result is that this continual-pretraining route saves about 40% FLOPs compared with 32k from-scratch training and loses almost no performance. That is the real reason the method matters. It is not just a long-context trick; it is a cheaper long-context trick.

The part people often miss: data mix matters less than data quality

The authors also test whether the gains come from simply adding more long documents. The answer is mostly no. They try removing long texts from the data mix and they also try upsampling the existing long texts more heavily. The model still keeps much of the benefit, and there is no clear win from pushing long-text ratio even higher.

That is a valuable lesson. Long-context capability is not just “feed the model more long documents.” A better corpus, plus the right positional fix and continual pretraining, matters more than the raw long-text fraction.

This also explains why the model does not fall apart on ordinary tasks. The long-context training even improves coding, math, and knowledge benchmarks in many cases, so the recipe does not simply trade short-context competence for longer memory.

How they teach long-context chat without long human labels

The alignment phase is intentionally lightweight. The paper does not ask annotators to label dense long documents by hand. Instead, it starts with the short RLHF data used in Llama 2 Chat and mixes in synthetic long instruction data generated by Llama 2 Chat itself.

Here is the trick in plain language. Take a long pretraining document, sample a chunk from it, and ask Llama 2 Chat to write question-answer pairs grounded in that chunk. The paper collects both short and long answers, then adds a self-critique step where the model checks its own answer. That gives the authors a lot of long-context supervision without paying for human long-document annotation.

During fine-tuning, short instruction examples are packed into 16,384-token sequences. Long instruction examples are padded on the right so each long sample is processed whole instead of being chopped off. That detail matters because long-context fine-tuning can quietly fail if the model never sees the full prompt shape it needs at inference time.

The biggest training tweak is also the simplest: they apply language-model loss to the long input prompts, not just the output answers. Standard instruction tuning only scores the answer tokens, which means the model can treat a long prompt as a passive prefix. By also training on the prompt itself, the model has a reason to model the input carefully, not just respond at the end.

That is why the chat version works with so little extra supervision. Short RLHF data teaches the assistant behavior. Synthetic long QA teaches the model how to use long documents. LM loss on the prompt keeps the whole long context alive during fine-tuning.

What the long-context experiments are really showing

The headline evaluation pattern is simple: the longer the usable context, the better the long-task score. On real long-context QA and summarization tasks, performance improves monotonically as the available context length grows. That is the kind of result you want to see if the model is actually using more evidence instead of just accepting a larger input budget.

The cleanest probe is first-sentence retrieval. The model is asked to return the first sentence of a long input. With unchanged RoPE, the model stalls after a few thousand tokens. With adjusted-base RoPE, it keeps working all the way to 32,768 tokens. That is much more informative than a plain perplexity drop, because it directly tests whether distant tokens remain individually recognizable.

The paper also compares the adjusted-base RoPE route with position interpolation and xPos. The message is not that the alternatives are useless. It is that the simplest base-frequency change gives the strongest usable separation between positions in this setting. The appendix argues that ABF preserves more granularity between neighboring positions than PI, which makes positional identities easier for the model to tell apart.

That same logic carries over to instruction tuning. Without any human-labeled long-context data, the 70B chat model beats gpt-3.5-turbo-16k on 7 of the 10 ZeroSCROLLS tasks. The result does not mean the model is perfect. It does mean the long-context recipe is strong enough to transfer from pretraining into real downstream use.

What this recipe does not solve

The paper is careful about the limits.

It is not a universal long-form generator. The authors explicitly note that the model is not yet tuned for every long-context application, such as creative writing where the output itself has to stay long and coherent.

Tokenizer efficiency is another practical limit. Llama’s tokenizer uses a relatively small vocabulary and often produces about 10% more tokens than GPT-3.5’s tokenizer on average. It also handles whitespace poorly, which makes code and whitespace-heavy inputs less efficient than the nominal 32k window might suggest.

Hallucination is still present too. Dense long contexts can actually make the issue more visible, because the model is juggling more information than it can reliably ground. So the longer window is not a cure for alignment problems. It just gives the model more room to make them.

What to remember

The easiest way to remember Llama 2 Long is this:

Keep the base model mostly intact. Slow down RoPE so distant tokens stay distinguishable. Continue pretraining on longer sequences instead of restarting from scratch. Then teach the chat model with short RLHF data plus synthetic long QA, and score the long prompts themselves during fine-tuning.

The deeper lesson is even simpler. Long-context scaling is not only about feeding more long documents. It is about preserving token identity at long distance so the model can still tell where information came from and why it matters.
