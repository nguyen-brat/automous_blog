Length Generalization of Transformers Without Position Encoding

NoPE looks simple, but the paper is making a sharper claim than “position encoding is unnecessary.” The real story is that a causal Transformer without explicit positional features can keep working surprisingly far beyond its training length, until its attention stops staying focused. The failure mode is not a mysterious loss of order awareness. It is attention becoming too diffuse as the sequence gets longer.

Keep the pipeline in mind like this:

That is the main contribution of the paper. It reinterprets length extension as an attention-focus problem, then fixes it by increasing the softmax scale. The first pass uses one global scale. The better version gives each head its own scale.

When longer context makes NoPE blur

The paper starts with a simple observation: inside the 2K training window, NoPE behaves roughly like a RoPE baseline. The gap shows up only when the test sequence gets longer than what the model saw in pretraining. Even then, NoPE still stretches farther than the explicit-position baseline before it collapses. But it still collapses.

Figure 1 is the paper’s diagnostic picture.

What to notice is the timing. As the context gets longer, the entropy curve bends upward right where perplexity begins to rise. That is the clue the paper follows: the model is not just “bad at long sequences.” Its attention is losing selectivity.

Formula card: attention entropy as the failure signal

Purpose: measure whether a head is concentrating on a few useful past tokens or spreading its weight too evenly.

Equation:

Hi(h)(x)=−∑j=1iαij(h)(x)log⁡αij(h)(x)\mathcal{H}_i^{(h)}(x) = - \sum_{j=1}^{i} \alpha_{ij}^{(h)}(x)\log \alpha_{ij}^{(h)}(x)Hi(h)​(x)=−j=1∑i​αij(h)​(x)logαij(h)​(x)

Intuition: a sharp attention pattern has low entropy, while a flatter pattern has higher entropy.

Symbols:

  • x: one validation sample.
  • i: the current token position being measured.
  • j: an earlier token position that token i can attend to.
  • h: the attention head index.
  • \alpha_{ij}^{(h)}(x): the attention weight from token i to token j in head h.
  • \mathcal{H}_i^{(h)}(x): the entropy of that head’s attention distribution at position i.

Read-aloud meaning: add up p log p over the attention weights so you get one number that grows when the head becomes less selective.

Why it matters: the paper uses this quantity to show that the long-context failure of NoPE tracks attention diffusion rather than some unrelated collapse.

The plotted curve in Figure 1 is the average of this per-head entropy across heads and validation samples. That average rises when the model starts distributing attention too evenly across a longer prefix.

The first fix is just a sharper softmax

Once the failure looks like diffusion, the simplest question is obvious: what happens if we just make attention sharper?

The paper tests that directly. It keeps the NoPE weights frozen and increases the scale before the softmax. In the paper’s notation, \lambda is the attention scale. Larger \lambda means sharper attention, even though the paper sometimes describes it as a temperature hyperparameter. Mathematically, it is the quantity multiplying the logits, so increasing it makes the distribution peakier.

What to notice in Figure 2 is the asymmetry between NoPE and RoPE. For NoPE, a modest increase in scale shifts the entropy inflection point to the right and pushes the model farther out. For RoPE, changing the scale alone does not move the failure point in the same way, because RoPE’s long-context problem is not just that attention is too soft. Its position signal itself is out of distribution.

Formula card: scaled self-attention

Purpose: sharpen attention by multiplying the query-key score before softmax.

Equation:

Intuition: a larger scale makes each head prefer a few strong matches instead of averaging many weak ones.

Symbols:

  • \alpha_{ij}^{(h)}: attention weight from token i to token j in head h.
  • q_i^{(h)}: query vector for token i in head h.
  • k_j^{(h)}: key vector for token j in head h.
  • \lambda^{(h)}: attention scale for head h.
  • k: index over all tokens that token i can attend to.

Read-aloud meaning: multiply the query-key dot product by a head-specific sharpness factor, then normalize with softmax.

Why it matters: this is the smallest intervention that can refocus NoPE without changing any model weights.

Uniform scaling uses the same \lambda for every head. That is enough to show the basic idea. In the paper’s example, a modest increase is enough to push NoPE past 4K tokens. But pushing the same knob harder creates a new problem: it can make short-range perplexity worse, because one global sharpness setting is not right for all heads.

Why one global scale is not enough

The reason uniform scaling stalls becomes clearer once you look head by head. The paper’s Figure 3 shows that different heads settle into very different entropy regimes. Some heads are already quite concentrated. Others stay broad and diffuse. A single global scale has to compromise between them.

What to notice is that the heads are not interchangeable. Some layers already know how to focus; some do not. If you crank one global scale high enough to help the diffuse heads, you can over-sharpen the focused heads and hurt nearby-token behavior. That is why the right target is not “minimum entropy everywhere.” The target is “the right amount of focus for each head.”

This is the key conceptual jump in the paper: long-context failure is not one uniform problem, so one uniform fix is not ideal either.

HeadScale: one scale per head

The head-wise version keeps the same softmax form, but replaces the shared scale with a per-head scale \lambda^{(h)}. That means each head gets its own sharpening dial.

Formula card: head-wise attention scaling

Purpose: give each head its own softmax sharpness so the model can refocus long-context attention without forcing all heads to behave the same way.

Equation:

αij(h)=eλ(h)qi(h)⋅kj(h)∑keλ(h)qi(h)⋅kk(h)\alpha_{ij}^{(h)}=\frac{e^{\lambda^{(h)} q_i^{(h)}\cdot k_j^{(h)}}}{\sum_k e^{\lambda^{(h)} q_i^{(h)}\cdot k_k^{(h)}}}αij(h)​=∑k​eλ(h)qi(h)​⋅kk(h)​eλ(h)qi(h)​⋅kj(h)​​

Intuition: different heads need different amounts of sharpening, so the model should not share one global scale across all of them.

Symbols:

  • \alpha_{ij}^{(h)}: attention weight from token i to token j in head h.
  • q_i^{(h)}: query vector for token i in head h.
  • k_j^{(h)}: key vector for token j in head h.
  • \lambda^{(h)}: learned sharpness scale for head h.
  • k: index over all candidate tokens in the causal prefix.

Read-aloud meaning: each head computes its attention scores as usual, but with its own scale factor before the softmax.

Why it matters: this is the minimal change that lets some heads stay broad while others become much sharper.

The method is called HeadScale for a reason. It is not a new attention architecture. It is parameter-efficient tuning of the attention scale alone. The base model stays frozen. The model just learns a different sharpness for each head.

The paper initializes those head-wise scales from the best uniform scale rather than from the default pretrained value. That matters more than it sounds. Starting from the default scale can make the search unstable, because the best per-head values can sit far away from the original setting. The better uniform value gives the head-wise search a much better starting point.

The paper’s target is not a full retrain. It is a tiny adaptation step. Only the attention scales move, so the number of trainable parameters is tiny compared with the base model. That is why the method feels more like a control knob than a new model family.

Formula card: keep the search focused

Purpose: prevent the learned scales from becoming softer than the pretrained attention pattern.

Equation:

λ(h)≥1d\lambda^{(h)} \ge \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}}λ(h)≥d​1​

Intuition: if long-context failure comes from attention getting too diffuse, do not let the tuned head become even more diffuse than the original model.

Symbols:

  • \lambda^{(h)}: learned attention scale for head h.
  • d: the head dimension used in the usual Transformer scaling rule.

Read-aloud meaning: each head’s scale must stay at least as sharp as the pretrained baseline.

Why it matters: the ablations show that removing this guardrail can look acceptable on perplexity while hurting retrieval-style tasks and LongBench.

This is also where the paper’s design philosophy becomes visible. It is not trying to make every head as sharp as possible. It is trying to preserve the model’s useful short-context behavior while preventing the long-context regime from becoming too blurry.

What changes in practice

Figure 4 shows the main payoff. Uniform scaling helps, but HeadScale reaches farther and does a better job of keeping short-range quality intact.

What to notice here is not just that HeadScale is better at the far end. It is that the model keeps more of its near-range behavior while extending the usable window. That is the real tradeoff the paper cares about. One global scale can drag short-context perplexity down because it has to serve all heads at once. HeadScale keeps the heads separate, so the compromise is less destructive.

The paper pushes the extended NoPE model out to around 18K tokens in its best setting. That is the anchor result to remember. The exact numbers are less important than the shape of the curve: the head-wise version bends more gracefully than the uniform one.

The ablation study makes the design choices feel justified:

  • Without the focus constraint, some language-model numbers can look okay, but retrieval and LongBench suffer.
  • Without the initialization trick, the search becomes unstable and downstream behavior degrades.
  • With both pieces in place, the head-wise scales generalize better than the uniform scale.

That matters because it shows the paper is not just adding parameters and hoping for the best. The guardrails are part of the method.

The broader evaluation tells the same story. On synthetic passkey retrieval and real long-context tasks, NoPE plus HeadScale stays much more usable than the frozen base model at long ranges. It still does not beat the strongest RoPE-based long-context methods overall, and the paper is honest about that. But it does show that explicit positional encoding is not the only path to longer context, and that attention focus is a very real control knob.

What to remember

The cleanest way to remember the paper is this:

NoPE fails at long context because attention becomes too diffuse, and the fix is to sharpen attention just enough, with one scale per head instead of one scale for the whole model.

That is why the method is easy to summarize but still interesting. It turns length generalization into a question of attention concentration, not position engineering. And once you see that, the whole paper becomes a story about refocusing the model rather than rewriting its positional machinery.

It is a useful idea, with clear limits. The base NoPE model still trails the best RoPE systems, very aggressive sharpening hurts nearby tokens, and the theory for why each head wants its own scale is still thin. But as a mechanism, it is memorable: diagnose diffusion, sharpen the logits, then let each head keep its own focus setting.
