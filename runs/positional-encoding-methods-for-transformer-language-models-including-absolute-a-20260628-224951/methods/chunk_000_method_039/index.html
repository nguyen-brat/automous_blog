CoPE: Complex Positional Encoding

CoPE starts from a very clean idea: do not cram content and position into the same real-valued vector. Keep token meaning in the real part, put position into the imaginary part, and let one complex-valued attention layer read both before the model goes back to ordinary real-valued layers.

That sounds small, but it changes where the positional signal lives. Additive encodings mix the two roles together up front. CoPE keeps them separated long enough for attention to inspect the relationship explicitly.

You can remember the flow like this:

The whole method is really about that first conversion step. CoPE does not turn the entire Transformer into a complex network. It uses a complex front-end as a lightweight adapter, then hands a normal real representation to the rest of the model.

Two tracks instead of one crowded vector

The first thing CoPE changes is the input representation. The paper first states the general split between content and position, then chooses a sinusoidal imaginary signal so the model can extrapolate beyond the training length.

Formula card: complex embedding

Purpose. Separate token semantics from position by storing them in different complex channels.

Equation.

Ecomplex(x,pos)=Evocab(x)+i γ sin⁡(ω pos)E_{\text{complex}}(x,\text{pos}) = E_{\text{vocab}}(x) + i\,\gamma\,\sin(\omega\,\text{pos})Ecomplex​(x,pos)=Evocab​(x)+iγsin(ωpos)

Intuition. The token embedding stays in the real channel, while position becomes a separate oscillating signal in the imaginary channel. The two signals travel together, but they are not smashed into one vector.

Symbols.

  • E_{\text{complex}}(x,\text{pos}): the complex token-position representation
  • E_{\text{vocab}}(x): the learned token embedding for token x
  • i: the imaginary unit
  • \gamma: a scalar that controls the positional amplitude
  • \omega: the base angular frequency of the sinusoid
  • \text{pos}: the token position index

Read-aloud meaning. Start with the token embedding, then add a purely imaginary sinusoidal position signal.

Why it matters. This is the paper’s separation principle in one line. Content does not have to fight position inside the same real vector.

That separation is the main conceptual advantage over additive positional encodings. When content and position are summed together, the model has to recover both roles from one blended representation. CoPE avoids that interference by giving position its own orthogonal track.

The sinusoid also matters. It gives the positional channel a smooth phase pattern instead of a learned lookup table. That is what makes the method feel closer to a signal-processing trick than a standard embedding layer.

The first layer reads phase on purpose

Once the embedding is complex, CoPE uses a complex projection in the first attention layer. The paper’s point is not that every layer should become complex. The point is that the first layer is the best place to decode the relationship between content and position, because the raw embedding still carries both signals in a clean form.

The layer learns separate real and imaginary projections for queries and keys, combines them into complex queries and keys, and then compares those complex vectors with a conjugate product.

Formula card: complex attention score

Purpose. Compare query and key vectors after they have been lifted into the complex domain.

Equation.

Acomplex=Qcomplex Kcomplex∗A_{\text{complex}} = Q_{\text{complex}}\,K_{\text{complex}}^{*}Acomplex​=Qcomplex​Kcomplex∗​

Intuition. The conjugate product makes the score sensitive to both size and phase agreement. If the two signals line up in phase, they reinforce each other. If they are misaligned, the score changes accordingly.

Symbols.

  • A_{\text{complex}}: the complex attention score
  • Q_{\text{complex}}: the complex query vector
  • K_{\text{complex}}: the complex key vector
  • *: complex conjugate

Read-aloud meaning. Form the attention score by multiplying the complex query with the conjugate of the complex key.

Why it matters. This is where CoPE actually “reads” position. The score is not just content similarity anymore; it is content similarity filtered through phase structure.

After that score is formed, the paper converts it back into a real-valued attention score before softmax. That keeps the output interface standard. The value vectors stay in real space too, so the rest of the Transformer never has to understand complex arithmetic.

This design choice is important. CoPE is not trying to replace the whole model. It is trying to intercept the positional signal once, extract what matters, and then get out of the way.

Why the paper looks at magnitude, phase, and real parts

The complex score is rich enough that the paper tries several ways to turn it into a real number before softmax:

  • Magnitude asks how strong the interaction is, regardless of phase direction.
  • Phase uses cos of the phase angle so smaller phase gaps mean higher similarity.
  • Real keeps the in-phase component only.
  • Hybrid mixes magnitude and phase with a tunable coefficient.
  • Hybrid-norm normalizes magnitude before mixing so scale does not dominate the phase term.

This is not just a formatting choice. The readout rule changes what the model treats as “similar.” A magnitude readout favors strong interactions. A phase readout favors phase alignment. A hybrid readout tries to balance both.

The experimental table makes that tradeoff very clear. CoPE phase is the strongest variant on SST2 and MRPC, while CoPE magnitude is the most competitive variant on QNLI. The more elaborate hybrids are not automatically better. In other words, the complex representation gives the model options, but the best option depends on the task.

That is a useful lesson in itself. Once the model has both magnitude and phase available, the remaining question is not “can it encode position?” but “which aspect of the complex score should count most?”

Why CoPE says it does not force long-term decay

The paper’s theoretical claim is that the imaginary sinusoid does not build in a decay factor that shrinks with distance. Instead, the positional interaction remains oscillatory.

Formula card: oscillatory positional interaction

Purpose. Show why the sinusoidal positional channel does not automatically impose exponential long-range decay.

Equation.

sin⁡(ωp)sin⁡(ωq)=12[cos⁡(ω(p−q))−cos⁡(ω(p+q))]\sin(\omega p)\sin(\omega q) = \frac{1}{2}\left[\cos(\omega(p-q)) - \cos(\omega(p+q))\right]sin(ωp)sin(ωq)=21​[cos(ω(p−q))−cos(ω(p+q))]

Intuition. The relative-position term becomes a cosine wave in the distance gap. That is oscillation, not exponential fading.

Symbols.

  • p: the position of the query token
  • q: the position of the key token
  • \omega: the base angular frequency

Read-aloud meaning. The interaction between two sinusoidal positions turns into cosine terms over their distance and sum.

Why it matters. The paper uses this identity to argue that CoPE does not hard-code a multiplicative long-term decay bias like e^{-\alpha|p-q|}.

That is the subtle but important point. CoPE still carries absolute position because the embedding uses the absolute index pos, but the phase-aware score also exposes relative position through the complex product. So the method keeps both views at once:

  • absolute placement is still present in the input signal,
  • relative offset appears naturally in the phase comparison.

This is why the paper can claim both “absolute” and “relative” behavior without introducing two separate position systems.

Why only the first layer is complex

The paper limits the complex machinery to the first layer. After that, the model returns to standard attention layers.

That keeps the method lightweight for two reasons. First, the first layer is where the original token and position tracks are still easiest to separate. Second, once the early positional interaction has been extracted, later layers can focus on semantic composition instead of repeatedly re-reading the same position signal.

This is the right mental model:

  • layer 1 decodes the complex position code,
  • later layers work with a normal real representation.

The paper also argues that CoPE is compatible with linear attention. The key idea is that the complex kernel can be decomposed into real inner products after splitting real and imaginary parts. So the complex layer is not a dead end for efficient attention variants. It can still fit into an O(N)-style attention workflow once the feature maps are lifted appropriately.

That compatibility matters because it keeps the method practical. CoPE is trying to improve how position is represented, not force every attention implementation to become a bespoke complex-valued system.

What the results suggest

The GLUE results are not uniform, which is actually informative.

  • SST2: CoPE phase reaches 82.57 and matches the best score in the table, beating RoPE.
  • MRPC: CoPE phase reaches 81.71 F1, again the best result in the table.
  • QNLI: CoPE magnitude reaches 61.63, above RoPE and learned encodings, but still below sinusoidal.

The pattern says two things at once. First, the complex split is strong enough to compete with established positional schemes. Second, the best readout rule depends on the task. Phase is not universally best, magnitude is not universally best, and the hybrids are not a guaranteed improvement.

That unevenness is worth keeping in mind. It suggests CoPE is a useful mechanism, but not a finished recipe. The complex channel gives the model more structure to work with; the readout choice decides how that structure is used.

The training curves in the paper tell the same story at a softer level. CoPE is often comparable to or better than RoPE during optimization, but the advantage is not identical across tasks.

Where the paper is still narrow

The limitations are straightforward:

  • The evaluation is on relatively small models trained from scratch, not large pretraining and finetuning setups.
  • The paper motivates longer-context extrapolation, but it does not include a direct ALiBi comparison.
  • The gains are uneven across tasks and across CoPE readout variants.

So the safest way to read the paper is as a strong architectural proof-of-concept. It shows that separating semantics and position across real and imaginary channels can work, can be lightweight, and can produce competitive results. It does not yet prove that this is the final positional encoding story for very large language models.

What to remember

CoPE is easiest to remember as a two-track positional encoding:

  • real channel for token content,
  • imaginary channel for position,
  • one complex first layer to read the interaction,
  • standard real Transformer layers after that.

The big idea is not that complex numbers are fashionable. The big idea is that positional information does not have to be mixed into the same channel as semantics. CoPE isolates the two, lets attention inspect their phase relationship once, and then passes a normal representation forward.
