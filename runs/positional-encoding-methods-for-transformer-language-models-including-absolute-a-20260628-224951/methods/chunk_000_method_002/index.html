Positional LSH for ALiBi-biased Attention

This paper takes a positional bias that already works well in transformers, ALiBi, and asks a sharper question: can that smooth distance rule be rewritten as an average of simple local masks? If the answer is yes, then long-context ALiBi does not have to rely on one dense all-pairs bias matrix. It can instead be approximated by repeated short-window attention passes whose mean behaves like the original bias.

That is the core move here. The method does not invent a new positional prior. It gives ALiBi a different execution model: sample contiguous blocks from positions, run ordinary attention inside those blocks, and average the sampled masks until the blocky approximation looks like the original exponential decay.

The whole pipeline is easier to remember as:

What Figure 1 is really showing

Figure 1 is the visual bridge in the paper. On one side is the smooth ALiBi decay over positions. On the other side are sampled block-diagonal masks. The point is not that one sampled mask is already a good approximation. The point is that many such masks, drawn from the right positional distribution, line up so that their average behaves like the bias we wanted in the first place.

What to notice in the figure:

  • each sample is made of contiguous blocks, not scrambled collisions;
  • the blocks are local windows, so they can be used with ordinary attention machinery;
  • the smooth distance preference is recovered only after averaging many samples.

That last point matters. The paper is not saying “replace ALiBi with one hard mask.” It is saying “replace ALiBi with a randomized family of local masks whose expectation is ALiBi.”

The bias they are approximating

Formula card: the ALiBi kernel

Purpose. Define the positional decay curve that the method tries to reproduce with sampled masks.

Equation.

Lij⋆=e−∣i−j∣/σL^\star_{ij}=e^{-|i-j|/\sigma}Lij⋆​=e−∣i−j∣/σ

Intuition. Two positions that are close together should have a strong positional affinity. As the distance grows, that affinity should fade smoothly and exponentially.

Symbols.

  • Lij⋆L^\star_{ij}Lij⋆​: positional bias between positions iii and jjj.
  • i,ji, ji,j: token positions in the sequence.
  • σ\sigmaσ: distance-decay bandwidth that controls how fast the bias weakens.

Read-aloud meaning. The positional bonus between two tokens shrinks exponentially as they move farther apart.

Why it matters. This simple distance rule is the thing the paper reinterprets as a randomized mixture of local binary masks.

That kernel view is important because it makes ALiBi feel less like a special-case attention tweak and more like a structured similarity function over positions. Nearby tokens should collide often. Far tokens should collide rarely. The method is built to make that statement precise.

How positional LSH turns positions into masks

The hashing is applied to positions, not token content. That is a big conceptual difference from the better-known LSH attention family, where query and key vectors are hashed based on representation similarity. Here the goal is not semantic retrieval. The goal is to approximate a positional prior.

The specific scheme used in the paper is random binning for the Laplacian kernel. In plain terms, it works like this:

  1. Pick a random bin width from a Gamma distribution tuned by the bandwidth parameter.
  2. Pick a random offset so the bins do not always line up with the same token boundaries.
  3. Partition the number line into equal-width intervals using that width and offset.
  4. Hash each position to the interval it falls into.
  5. Turn each bin into a binary block on the attention matrix.

Because the bins are intervals on the line of positions, the resulting mask is contiguous. That is the crucial property. Without contiguity, the mask would still be blocky after a permutation, but it would be awkward to execute as regular local attention. With contiguity, each sample becomes an ordinary sequence of local blocks.

The paper also uses the fact that the random binning law is matched to the Laplacian kernel. That is why the collision probability between two positions is exactly the right exponential decay, rather than a loose heuristic approximation.

Formula card: the mask-mixture view

Purpose. Show the exact structural statement behind Positional LSH.

Equation.

L⋆=EM∼M[M]L^\star=\mathbb{E}_{M\sim\mathcal{M}}[M]L⋆=EM∼M​[M]

Intuition. Each sampled mask is one noisy local view of position structure. Averaging many such views recovers the smooth bias matrix.

Symbols.

  • L⋆L^\starL⋆: the ALiBi bias matrix over positions.
  • MMM: one sampled contiguous block-diagonal binary mask.
  • M\mathcal{M}M: the distribution over masks induced by positional locality-sensitive hashing.

Read-aloud meaning. The ALiBi bias is the average of random local masks.

Why it matters. This is the bridge that lets the paper translate a dense positional bias into a randomized collection of local attention windows.

That identity is the main conceptual payoff of the paper. It says the bias is not just similar to local attention. It can literally be viewed as the expectation of local attention patterns drawn from the right positional hash.

From one sample to the real approximation

One sample gives one block-diagonal mask. One mask is too noisy to use on its own, but it already tells you which regions of the sequence are allowed to interact in that draw. The approximation is built from many such draws. The empirical average of the sampled masks smooths out the hard block boundaries and moves the result toward the original ALiBi matrix.

Formula card: the empirical mask average

Purpose. Define the Monte Carlo estimator that the algorithm actually computes.

Equation.

M~=1s∑t=1sMt\widetilde{M}=\frac{1}{s}\sum_{t=1}^{s} M_tM=s1​t=1∑s​Mt​

Intuition. More samples mean more averaging, and more averaging means the hard edges of the random blocks fade into the intended positional decay.

Symbols.

  • M~\widetilde{M}M: the empirical mean mask used as the approximation.
  • MtM_tMt​: the ttt-th sampled mask.
  • sss: the number of sampled masks.

Read-aloud meaning. Average sss random masks to get one smoother approximation.

Why it matters. This is the object that replaces the dense ALiBi matrix in the approximate attention computation.

Once you have M~\widetilde{M}M, the attention computation is straightforward. Replace the ALiBi bias with the sampled approximation, keep the usual query, key, and value tensors, and run ordinary attention inside the sampled blocks. The only special thing is the mask generation. The attention math inside each block is standard.

That is why the method is appealing from a systems perspective. It does not ask the model to learn a new attention primitive. It asks the model to execute many small local primitives instead of one global dense one.

Why the theorem is stronger than “it kind of works”

The paper does not stop at a visual intuition. It proves that the sampled masks concentrate around ALiBi in useful matrix norms. That distinction matters.

An entrywise approximation only says each individual bias entry is close on average. That is not enough for attention, because attention normalizes rows and then mixes values. A bad global shape can still cause trouble even if many individual entries look fine.

The spectral norm bound captures the overall matrix shape. The max-norm bound captures the worst individual entry. Together, they give the paper enough control to move from “the mask matrix is close” to “the attention output is close” in a way that holds uniformly with high probability.

The output guarantee is also sensitive to the attention pattern itself. If the underlying attention rows are balanced, the approximation is easier. If a row is extremely peaky, the same positional perturbation can be more fragile. So the theorem is not claiming every input is equally easy. It is claiming that the approximation behaves predictably across inputs once the mask sampling is fixed.

Another useful detail is the block-size control. The random bin widths come from a Gamma law, and the paper uses that tail behavior to show that the largest block stays controlled with high probability. That is what keeps the blockwise computation from collapsing back into full dense attention.

So the theorem is really doing three jobs at once:

  • matching the ALiBi matrix in expectation,
  • showing the approximation concentrates as the sample count grows,
  • keeping the local blocks small enough to preserve the computational benefit.

What the algorithm actually changes

The attention values themselves are not exotic. Queries, keys, and values stay where they are. The approximation only changes the positional routing rule.

That gives the method a clean input-output story:

  • input: queries, keys, values, and an ALiBi-style positional kernel over token indices;
  • intermediate step: sample several contiguous block masks from positional hashes;
  • output: approximate ALiBi-biased attention computed from local unbiased attention blocks.

This also explains why the method is more than a fancy mask generator. It is a way to separate the geometry of position from the mechanics of attention execution. The geometry says which positions should be near each other. The execution says how to spend compute on those neighborhoods.

What the experiments are checking

The experiments are there to make sure the theory is not just abstract algebra. On Llama-4-Scout-17B-16E and Mistral-7B heads, the paper reports that spectral error, max-norm error, and output error all shrink as more masks are averaged. That is the expected Monte Carlo story, and it is a good sanity check that the approximation behaves the way the theorem predicts.

The training experiments push the same idea into downstream behavior. On Qwen3-0.6B, Positional LSH moves closer to ALiBi as the sample count increases and beats a fixed-block baseline during length extrapolation. On Mistral-7B, the results are more mixed, which is a useful reminder that better positional structure does not automatically dominate every model and every extrapolation regime.

The authors are also careful about what these experiments do not show. The implementation is a prototype, not a production-optimized kernel. So the paper supports the structural claim and the approximation claim, but not a universal wall-clock speedup claim over highly tuned exact ALiBi kernels.

What to remember

The easiest way to remember this paper is:

ALiBi is a smooth positional bias over distances. Positional LSH turns that smooth bias into an expectation over contiguous local masks. Averaging enough masks gives a controllable approximation, and the contiguity of the blocks makes the approximation executable as ordinary short-window attention.

That is the paper’s real contribution. It does not simply say “use hashing for attention.” It shows that a positional bias can be rewritten as a randomized local routing scheme with explicit error control. That gives you a clean conceptual bridge between bias, masks, and positional embeddings, even if the systems payoff still depends on the hardware and implementation regime.
