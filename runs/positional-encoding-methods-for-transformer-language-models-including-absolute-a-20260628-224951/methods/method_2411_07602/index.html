Circuit Complexity of RoPE-based Transformers

This paper asks a very specific question: does RoPE give a constant-depth Transformer a fundamentally stronger kind of computation, or does it just make positional information behave better in practice?

The answer is the second one. Under the paper’s setting - poly(n)-precision arithmetic, O(1) layers, and hidden dimension d <= O(n) - the RoPE-based Transformer still sits inside uniform TC^0. So RoPE changes the geometry of attention, but it does not magically move the whole model into a deeper complexity class.

You can keep the whole proof in one mental picture:

That is the real contribution of the paper. It does not claim RoPE is useless. It says that, from the circuit-complexity point of view, RoPE is still a shallow mechanism.

Where RoPE actually enters

RoPE changes attention by inserting a relative rotation between the query side and the key side. Instead of comparing two token vectors directly, the model rotates the query coordinates according to the distance between the two positions, then performs the score computation.

That is why the paper’s first step is not about Transformers in general. It is about the tiny trigonometric pieces inside the rotation matrix. Once sine and cosine are under control, the rest of the proof becomes a stack of ordinary arithmetic blocks.

Formula card: RoPE attention score

Purpose: show the exact place where relative rotation enters the attention logit.

Equation:

Ai,j=exp⁡(Xi,∗WQRj−iWK⊤Xj,∗⊤)A_{i,j} = \exp\left(X_{i,*} W_Q R_{j-i} W_K^{\top} X_{j,*}^{\top}\right)Ai,j​=exp(Xi,∗​WQ​Rj−i​WK⊤​Xj,∗⊤​)

Intuition: project the token vectors into query and key space, rotate the query side by the relative offset, compare the two sides, and turn the score into a positive weight.

Symbols:

  • X_{i,*}, X_{j,*}: token representations at positions i and j.
  • W_Q, W_K: learned query and key projection matrices.
  • R_{j-i}: the RoPE rotation matrix determined by the relative position gap.
  • A_{i,j}: the unnormalized attention weight for the token pair (i, j).
  • \exp: the exponential that turns the raw score into a positive quantity before normalization.

Read-aloud meaning: take a query vector, rotate it according to the token gap, compare it with the key side, then exponentiate the score.

Why it matters: this is the minimal equation that distinguishes RoPE attention from plain dot-product attention. It is also the first point where the proof has to reason about trigonometric functions.

Why the trig part does not break the circuit bound

At first glance, rotation looks like the scary part. RoPE uses sine and cosine, and those sound more analytic than discrete. The paper’s key trick is to show that they are still shallow enough to fit in TC^0 when precision is bounded by poly(n).

The idea is familiar if you think like a numerical analyst. First, reduce the angle to a small range. Then approximate sine or cosine with a truncated Taylor series. The paper’s model already knows how to do the bookkeeping needed for that: floor, comparison, addition, multiplication, division, iterated addition, and iterated multiplication are all available at constant depth in uniform threshold circuits.

So the trigonometric part is not a separate computational universe. It is just the first block in a pipeline of shallow arithmetic operations.

This is why the precision assumption matters. If the numbers had unbounded precision, one number could hide far more information than the paper wants to allow. By working with p-bit floating-point numbers where p <= poly(n), the simulation stays in the same size regime as ordinary circuit complexity.

From one score to one attention layer

Once the pairwise RoPE scores are computed, the rest of attention looks familiar. The raw score matrix is row-normalized and then used to mix the value vectors.

The paper is careful here because it wants to separate the pieces that matter for the circuit proof:

  • exponentiation is a TC^0-friendly primitive,
  • row sums are just iterated addition,
  • normalization is division by a diagonal matrix,
  • value mixing is matrix multiplication.

Nothing in that list adds unbounded depth on its own.

Formula card: single attention layer

Purpose: turn the raw RoPE scores into the actual attention output.

Equation:

Attni(X)=D−1AXWV,D=diag⁡(A1n)\mathsf{Attn}_i(X) = D^{-1} A X W_V, \qquad D = \operatorname{diag}(A \mathbf{1}_n)Attni​(X)=D−1AXWV​,D=diag(A1n​)

Intuition: normalize each row of the score matrix, then use the normalized weights to combine the value projections.

Symbols:

  • \mathsf{Attn}_i(X): output of the i-th attention layer.
  • A: the matrix of exponentiated pairwise RoPE scores.
  • D: diagonal matrix of row sums, so D^{-1} performs row-wise normalization.
  • \mathbf{1}_n: the length-n all-ones vector.
  • X: the input token-representation matrix.
  • W_V: the value projection matrix.

Read-aloud meaning: compute raw scores, normalize each row, and use those weights to mix the values.

Why it matters: this is where RoPE attention becomes a full Transformer block. The proof can now invoke the TC^0 bounds for matrix multiplication, exponentiation, and division instead of treating attention as a single opaque object.

The rest of the Transformer is still shallow

The paper then checks the other common blocks one by one.

The MLP block is just a linear map plus a bias. That is easy to simulate with constant-depth threshold circuits because it reduces to matrix multiplication and addition.

Layer normalization is a little more delicate, but not by much. You need to compute the mean, the variance, and a square root. Those operations are still available in uniform TC^0 under the paper’s floating-point model. So normalization does not create a new depth barrier either.

This is the main pattern in the proof: every block looks complicated if you think in Transformer terms, but simple if you think in terms of arithmetic primitives. The paper keeps decomposing until the whole architecture is only a composition of operations that threshold circuits already know how to parallelize.

Formula card: full RoPE Transformer composition

Purpose: show that the whole model is just a constant stack of attention and non-attention blocks.

Equation:

TF(X)=gm∘Attnm∘⋯∘g1∘Attn1∘g0(X)\mathsf{TF}(X) = g_m \circ \mathsf{Attn}_m \circ \cdots \circ g_1 \circ \mathsf{Attn}_1 \circ g_0(X)TF(X)=gm​∘Attnm​∘⋯∘g1​∘Attn1​∘g0​(X)

Intuition: alternate attention with ordinary per-layer blocks a fixed number of times.

Symbols:

  • \mathsf{TF}: the full RoPE-based Transformer.
  • X: the input representation matrix.
  • \mathsf{Attn}_i: the attention module in layer i.
  • g_i: the other block in layer i, such as MLP or layer normalization.
  • m: the number of layers.
  • \circ: function composition.

Read-aloud meaning: apply a preprocessing block, then attention, then another block, and repeat this only a constant number of times.

Why it matters: if each block is in TC^0 and the number of layers is O(1), the whole network remains in TC^0. The final theorem is basically this observation made formal.

What the main theorem is really saying

Theorem 4.8 is the paper’s main simulation result. In plain language, it says that a RoPE-based Transformer with poly(n)-precision, constant depth, and width d <= O(n) can be simulated by a DLOGTIME-uniform TC^0 circuit family.

The point of the uniformity condition is to prevent hidden advice from sneaking into the circuit description. This is not just “some circuit exists”; it is a circuit family that can be generated efficiently from the input size.

The theorem is therefore a ceiling result. RoPE can improve how attention uses position, but under these assumptions it does not give the model an escape hatch from constant-depth threshold computation.

Why this becomes a hardness barrier

Once the model is inside uniform TC^0, the lower-bound argument is standard complexity theory.

Arithmetic formula evaluation is NC^1-complete. Boolean formula value is also NC^1-complete. So if a RoPE-based Transformer of the kind studied in the paper could solve either of those problems, then NC^1 would collapse into TC^0.

That is why the paper phrases the hardness claims conditionally: unless TC^0 = NC^1, these Transformers cannot solve arithmetic formula evaluation or Boolean formula value.

This is the right level of statement for the theory. It does not say the model fails on every practical reasoning task. It says that, in the constant-depth setting, RoPE does not buy enough computational power to cross a standard NC^1 boundary.

What the paper does not claim

The limits are important, but so are the assumptions.

The argument is about forward computation, not training dynamics.

It is about constant-depth architectures, not Transformers whose depth grows with sequence length.

It uses a bounded-precision floating-point model, not arbitrary real numbers.

And it does not explain every empirical long-context gain that RoPE-based models show in practice.

So the safest way to read the paper is this: RoPE is a strong positional tool, but positional cleverness alone does not automatically raise the circuit class of the whole model.

What to remember

The shortest memory hook is:

RoPE rotates the query-key geometry, but the resulting computation is still shallow enough to fit in uniform TC^0.

That is the paper’s message in one sentence. The relative-position trick is real and useful, but it does not magically turn a constant-depth Transformer into a deeper reasoning machine.
