CLEX: Continuous Length Extrapolation for Large Language Models

CLEX is easiest to understand as a change in how RoPE is allowed to move with context length. Instead of picking one fixed stretch factor and committing the model to it, the paper treats length as a continuous variable and learns a smooth path for the RoPE frequency basis.

The practical story is simple:

That is why CLEX feels like a positional-encoding method rather than a new attention architecture. It keeps the RoPE-based Transformer intact, but it stops treating long-context adaptation as one hard-coded jump.

The main visual idea

The paper’s central move is easier to see than to state. On the left, discrete PE scaling jumps from one preset length to the next. On the right, the same idea becomes a smooth trajectory over the length factor.

What to notice here:

  • the discrete version makes separate moves at 4k, 8k, 12k, and 16k;
  • the continuous version lets the frequency state travel along a single curve as t increases;
  • the point is not just “more scales,” but “a better way to move between scales.”

That is the paper’s core claim in picture form. Existing PE scaling methods are usually designed around a chosen target length. CLEX says the real object to learn is the whole path between lengths, not one destination.

All PE scaling becomes frequency scaling

The first useful trick in the paper is a unifying view: changing positions and changing RoPE frequencies are two sides of the same operation. Once you see that, the design space becomes much cleaner.

Formula card: RoPE scaling as frequency scaling

Purpose: show that index scaling and frequency scaling are equivalent ways to extend RoPE.

Equation:

ft(x,m,θ)=f(x,m,α(t)⊙θ)f_t(\mathbf{x}, m, \theta) = f(\mathbf{x}, m, \alpha(t) \odot \theta)ft​(x,m,θ)=f(x,m,α(t)⊙θ)

Intuition: instead of thinking “we changed the position index,” think “we changed the rotary clock speeds.” Each length factor t corresponds to one frequency-basis transform \alpha(t).

Symbols:

  • f is the RoPE transformation.
  • \mathbf{x} is the token vector being rotated.
  • m is the position index.
  • \theta is the original RoPE frequency basis.
  • \alpha(t) is the per-dimension scaling rule at length factor t.
  • \odot means elementwise multiplication.
  • t is the context-length scale relative to the base window.

Read-aloud meaning: scaling positions or scaling frequencies produces the same RoPE effect if the scale is translated correctly.

Why it matters: this is the step that turns a pile of PE scaling heuristics into one continuous object. Once the method is phrased as \alpha(t), the paper can learn that function instead of hand-writing it.

This unification is also what separates CLEX from fixed recipes like PI and Yarn. Those methods each choose one concrete scaling law. CLEX says the law itself should be a learned trajectory over t.

Turning the scale factor into a path

The next step is to stop treating the scaling rule as a hand-designed formula and let it evolve continuously. The paper works in log-space, so multiplicative frequency changes become additive motion.

Formula card: Learned frequency dynamics

Purpose: model how the RoPE frequency basis should move as the length factor grows.

Equation:

dz(t)dt=gϕ(z(t),t)\frac{d\mathbf{z}(t)}{dt} = g_{\phi}(\mathbf{z}(t), t)dtdz(t)​=gϕ​(z(t),t)

Intuition: as the requested context gets longer, the log frequency state moves along a smooth learned path instead of snapping to one preset scale.

Symbols:

  • \mathbf{z}(t) is the log-scaled RoPE frequency basis at scale t.
  • t is the length scaling factor relative to the base context window.
  • g_{\phi} is the learned dynamics function.
  • \phi are the parameters inside that function.

Read-aloud meaning: the rate at which the frequency basis changes depends on both the current basis and the target length scale.

Why it matters: this is the real engine of CLEX. The method is not just sampling more t values; it is learning a continuous rule for how frequencies should bend as t grows.

The implementation is intentionally small. CLEX parameterizes g_{\phi} with an up-and-down projection and a SiLU nonlinearity, then adds a Yarn-derived constant dynamic as a bias term. That bias does not replace the learned dynamics. It gives the ODE a sensible starting slope so optimization is easier and the loss curve is steadier.

There is also a useful visual consequence. The learned basis is not a uniform stretch. Different dimensions move differently, and the same qualitative pattern shows up across several scales.

What to notice here:

  • the curves are not copies of one another;
  • early dimensions are adjusted more aggressively than later ones;
  • the learned basis keeps a coherent shape as t changes from 4 to 8 to 16.

That is exactly what you would want from a continuous trajectory. The model is not trying to preserve one fixed positional map forever. It is learning how the map should bend as the target window grows.

How the model trains on a whole range of lengths

CLEX only works if the model sees more than one target scale during training. The paper does not fine-tune on a single chosen t. Instead, it samples a fresh scale for each batch.

That alignment step matters. If you change the frequency basis but leave the positions frozen at 1..L_train, the model sees a mismatched pair: the angles have moved, but the coordinates have not. CLEX fixes that with position extrapolation.

In practice, the paper either rescales the position indices into the larger range or randomly samples L_train positions from that range. The random version works slightly better and becomes the default. The important point is not the exact sampling trick. It is that the model is trained on a consistent geometry at each sampled scale.

This is also where CLEX earns the phrase “train on short, test on long.” The model learns from short sequences, but the positional geometry it sees during training already lives on a broader scale range. That is what lets the learned trajectory generalize beyond the training window without collapsing the short-context behavior.

Why inference stays cheap

The ideal inference story would be to solve the ODE freshly for every sequence length. That would make the method feel truly continuous, but it would also add unnecessary overhead.

CLEX takes a cheaper route. It caches a small set of discrete length buckets in advance and, for a given prompt, uses the nearest upper bucket. In the paper’s LongBench setup, the cache covers lengths such as 4k, 8k, 12k, and 16k.

That choice keeps the runtime story practical:

  • no new attention block;
  • no extra per-token positional solver at generation time;
  • negligible overhead compared with the base RoPE model.

The tradeoff is worth keeping in mind, though. CLEX learns a continuous trajectory during training, but inference still uses cached buckets rather than exact per-sequence dynamics. So the method is continuous where learning happens, and discretized where speed matters.

What the numbers actually say

The paper’s headline result is not just that CLEX extends context. It extends context without wrecking the short window.

The cleanest anchor is CLEX-4k: its perplexity is essentially unchanged when moving from 4k to 16k, with 5.86 versus 5.87. That is the behavior you want if the method is really preserving native-length quality while extending outward.

The longer-run result is even stronger. A model trained at 16k stays stable at 32k and 64k, with perplexities of 5.52, 5.55, and 5.64. That is the pattern the paper is after: the curve stays flat far beyond the training window instead of blowing up once the model leaves familiar territory.

On LongBench, the 4k-trained CLEX model is competitive with open-source systems trained up to 32k. That matters because LongBench is closer to real use than perplexity alone. The paper also reports that CLEX can hold its own across summarization, few-shot learning, and code completion, while ALiBi-style extrapolation tends to look better in pure length extension than in practical long-context use.

The ablations make the story believable.

  • If you keep random scale sampling but remove the learned continuous dynamics, performance drops. So the ODE is doing real work, not just adding noise.
  • If you skip position extrapolation, the model loses extrapolation ability. So aligning positions with the sampled frequency scale is not optional.
  • If you change the ODE parameter size \lambda, the final performance stays similar across small settings, which is why the paper keeps the ODE lightweight.

There is also a nice scaling observation. Bigger base models extend farther. Training-data size matters less for the extrapolation curve itself, while a larger parameter budget gives the model more room to carry the longer-range geometry. The paper’s rule of thumb is simple: for moderate jumps, increase the training length proportionally; for very large jumps, scale the base model too.

Where CLEX fits in the family

CLEX sits between two familiar ideas in the RoPE family.

On one side are fixed scaling rules like PI and Yarn. They are easy to use, but they commit to one hand-picked geometry. On the other side are pure length-extrapolation methods like ALiBi, which can generalize beyond training length but often tilt too hard toward locality and lose useful long-range information in practical tasks.

CLEX tries to take the useful part of both sides:

  • like PI and Yarn, it is a drop-in RoPE extension;
  • like length-extrapolation methods, it can train on short and test on long;
  • unlike fixed-scale rules, it learns a continuous path instead of locking the model to one target length.

That is why the method feels less like a one-off heuristic and more like a general principle: positional adaptation should be smooth across scales, not snapped to a single operating point.

Where it stops

CLEX is strong, but it is not universal.

  • It is demonstrated mainly on RoPE-based architectures such as LLaMA and GPT-NeoX.
  • It still depends on training or fine-tuning; it is not a free inference-only fix.
  • Inference uses cached discrete buckets, not exact per-sequence ODE solves.
  • Very large extrapolation ranges may need a larger base model, not just a better scaling rule.

The more general limitation is the same one shared by most RoPE extension methods: they improve how the existing context window is used, but they do not remove the quadratic cost of attention itself. CLEX makes the coordinate system better; it does not replace the coordinate system.

What to remember

The easiest way to remember CLEX is this: it learns how the positional clock should bend as context grows.

Instead of choosing one fixed stretch factor, CLEX learns a continuous trajectory over the length factor, trains on many sampled scales, and uses a tiny cache of length buckets at inference. That is why it can preserve short-context quality while still extrapolating far beyond the training window.

If you want the shortest possible summary, it is this:

CLEX turns RoPE scaling from a hand-set jump into a learned curve.
