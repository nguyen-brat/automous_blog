A Length-Extrapolatable Transformer

LeX Transformer is a practical answer to a simple but nasty question: if a transformer is trained on short sequences, how can it still read position correctly when the sequence becomes much longer? The paper’s answer is not to throw away rotary position encoding. It keeps the rotary geometry, adds distance-dependent decay so the long-range signal does not oscillate itself into confusion, and then uses blockwise causal attention at inference so each token only compares itself with a neighborhood it can still resolve.

The paper is really about making two things line up: the position signal itself and the attention window that consumes it. If either one is too brittle, long context becomes a liability instead of a help.

Why RoPE starts to wobble

RoPE already gives you a relative notion of position. The problem is that the higher-frequency rotary bands flip too fast once the token distance gets large. A distance that should feel “farther away” can start to look like a different phase instead of a steadily changing offset. That is the kind of aliasing the paper is trying to prevent.

Figure 1 makes the failure mode obvious.

What to notice is the shape, not the exact values. Plain RoPE keeps oscillating as distance grows, while xPos smooths the curve so long-range positions stay easier to tell apart. The paper is not trying to remove rotary phase. It is trying to stop the fast phase bands from dominating the story at long range.

The compact way to think about xPos is that it keeps RoPE’s relative geometry but adds a damping envelope. Query and key rotations still depend on position, so the model stays translation-invariant in the relative sense. The extra decay just makes the long-distance signal less fragile.

Formula card: relative translation invariance

Purpose: show that shifting both tokens together should not change the attention score.

Equation:

⟨fq(q,n+r),fk(k,n)⟩=⟨fq(q,r),fk(k,0)⟩\langle f_q(q,n+r), f_k(k,n)\rangle = \langle f_q(q,r), f_k(k,0)\rangle⟨fq​(q,n+r),fk​(k,n)⟩=⟨fq​(q,r),fk​(k,0)⟩

Intuition: only the gap between the tokens should matter. If both tokens slide to a new absolute location, the score should stay the same.

Symbols:

  • f_q, f_k: position-aware transformations applied to the query and key
  • q, k: the underlying query and key vectors
  • n: an anchor position
  • r: the relative offset between the tokens

Read-aloud meaning: moving the whole pair along the sequence does not change the score as long as the distance between them stays fixed.

Why it matters: xPos preserves the relative-position behavior that RoPE is good at, instead of falling back to an absolute position code.

Intuition: each rotary pair votes on the distance between two tokens, but the unstable high-frequency votes fade faster as the distance grows.

Symbols:

  • n: relative token distance
  • \theta_i: rotary frequency for dimension pair i
  • \zeta_i: decay factor for dimension pair i, typically between 0 and 1
  • d: model dimension, so d/2 is the number of rotary pairs

Read-aloud meaning: add several oscillating distance signals together, but damp each one according to how far apart the tokens are.

Why it matters: this is the mathematical reason xPos stays smooth at long range instead of turning into a noisy oscillation.

The important design choice is that the decay is not uniform. Fast rotary bands get smaller zeta_i values because they are the first ones to alias. Slow bands keep most of their strength, so xPos still fits short-range patterns well. The authors also note that direct numerical optimization of the decay schedule was brittle and fp16-sensitive, so they settled on a hand-shaped schedule that stays stable at long lengths.

Compared with ALiBi, this is a different tradeoff. ALiBi pushes distance into an explicit attention bias. xPos keeps the rotary mechanism itself, but adds a decay envelope inside the query/key rotations. That is why xPos can preserve more of RoPE’s short-context behavior while still behaving more monotonically at long range.

Attention resolution is the paper’s compass

The paper does not just say “xPos looks smoother.” It defines a metric for whether the position signal is actually readable. That metric is attention resolution: a way to check whether attention scores decrease in a clean order as distance grows.

Formula card: attention resolution

Purpose: measure whether the attention score curve remains monotonic enough to distinguish different token distances.

Equation:

R(s)=∑i=0Nes[i](es[i]−es[i+1])(∑j=0Nes[j])2R(s)=\sum_{i=0}^{N}\frac{e^{s[i]}\bigl(e^{s[i]}-e^{s[i+1]}\bigr)}{\left(\sum_{j=0}^{N}e^{s[j]}\right)^2}R(s)=i=0∑N​(∑j=0N​es[j])2es[i](es[i]−es[i+1])​

Intuition: if the score at a shorter distance is larger than the score at the next distance, that contributes to the resolution score. The softmax-style weighting keeps the metric from being dominated by tiny tail values.

Symbols:

  • s[i]: expected attention score at relative distance i
  • N: the maximum distance being evaluated
  • e^{s[i]}: the softmax-style weight attached to distance i

Read-aloud meaning: reward distance curves that fall cleanly as tokens get farther apart.

Why it matters: the whole paper is built around the idea that long-context extrapolation fails when the model can no longer resolve distance. This metric turns that intuition into something you can compare across methods.

This is not a training loss. It is a diagnostic. The point is to ask whether the score curve stays ordered enough that the model can still tell “nearer” from “farther” without the phases folding back on themselves.

In the experiments, xPos raises resolution to 0.98 at the training length and 1.08 at 2048 tokens. RoPE drops to 0.08 at 2048, which is a sign that the distance signal is no longer easy to read. ALiBi stays steadier at 0.88 because it has an explicit decay, but xPos still ends up cleaner because it keeps the rotary geometry and the decay in the same representation.

BCA makes long sequences readable again

The second half of LeX is about inference, not encoding. Training still uses ordinary causal attention on the short pretraining length. At test time, though, the paper switches to blockwise causal attention so the model does not have to compare every new token against a huge unreadable history.

What to notice here is the overlap. If the pretraining length is l, the paper splits a long sequence into query blocks of length l/2. Each block attends to itself and the immediately previous block, so the key/value cache can be reused instead of recomputed from scratch. That makes the method cache-friendly and keeps the active attention neighborhood small enough to resolve.

This is the part that explains why xPos alone is not the whole answer. A stable position signal helps, but long inference still becomes ambiguous if the attention window is too wide. BCA keeps the comparisons local enough that the model can actually use the signal xPos is giving it.

A sliding window would also work in principle. The paper chooses blockwise causal attention because it is simple and fits the cache reuse pattern of autoregressive decoding.

What the numbers say

The experiments line up with the story.

  • With BCA, LeX is the only reported method whose perplexity keeps dropping as evaluation length grows from 1024 to 4096.
  • At 4096 tokens, LeX + BCA reaches 20.73 perplexity, better than ALiBi’s 24.26 and RoPE + BCA’s 25.65.
  • Removing rotation is disastrous: xPos without rotation jumps to 33.68 validation perplexity, so decay by itself is not enough.
  • Removing BCA also hurts: xPos without BCA rises to 28.43 at 4096, while xPos + BCA stays at 20.73.
  • The resolution table matches the perplexity story: LeX reaches 1.08 at 2048, while ALiBi reaches 0.88 and RoPE only 0.08.

The clean takeaway is that xPos and BCA solve different problems. xPos makes the distance signal monotonic enough to trust. BCA makes the attention neighborhood small enough to read. Put them together and longer context becomes useful instead of merely tolerated.

What to remember

The method is easiest to remember as two dials:

  • xPos keeps RoPE, but damps the fast rotary bands before they start aliasing.
  • BCA keeps inference local, so each token compares itself against a neighborhood it can still distinguish.
  • LeX works because the position signal stays readable and the attention mask stays resolvable.

That is also where the limitations sit. The paper is focused on causal language modeling, not bidirectional encoders. The best long-context gains depend on switching to BCA at inference, so the method is not encoding alone. And it adds a modest inference cost, roughly 6% versus absolute embeddings.

The useful design lesson is narrower than “longer is always better”: long-context transformers work when the model can keep its distance signal monotonic and keep its comparisons local enough to interpret.
