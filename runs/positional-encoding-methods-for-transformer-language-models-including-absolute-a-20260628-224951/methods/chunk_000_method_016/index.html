KV-Fold: One-Step KV-Cache Recurrence for Long-Context Inference

Long-context inference usually breaks for one of two reasons. Either you try to let the model see everything at once, and the attention matrix becomes too large to be practical, or you keep only a sliding window and lose the exact fact you needed three chapters ago. KV-Fold takes a different route. It keeps the transformer frozen, keeps the KV cache intact, and changes only the way the sequence is fed through the model.

The intuition is simple: do not treat the KV cache as a throwaway serving detail. Treat it as the model’s state.

Instead of one giant forward pass, KV-Fold splits the sequence into manageable chunks. The model processes the first chunk normally, stores its keys and values, then feeds the next chunk while letting that new chunk attend to the accumulated cache from everything that came before. The cache is appended, not compressed. Nothing is evicted. Nothing is retrained. The model just keeps carrying its own notes forward.

That is why the paper describes KV-Fold as a left fold over chunks. Each new chunk applies the same update rule to the state left behind by the previous chunk. If you like a mental picture, imagine reading a long book one chapter at a time while keeping a growing notebook of all the important facts you have already seen. You never rewrite the notebook from scratch; you just keep adding to it.

The basic move: carry the cache, do not restart it

The point of the pipeline is not speed by itself. The point is that every chunk sees the earlier chunks through the same content-addressable attention mechanism the model already knows how to use. The only thing that changes is the schedule.

One detail matters a lot here: the positions stay continuous across chunk boundaries. The paper does not reset the model to position zero at every new chunk. It continues the absolute position IDs from the original sequence, so RoPE rotations stay aligned with the document’s true order. If you restarted positions at every boundary, the model would keep thinking it was back at the start of a fresh document, which would distort the geometry of attention.

KV recurrence update

Purpose. Show the chunk-to-chunk state transition.

Equation.

(K(t),V(t))=Fθ((K(t−1),V(t−1)),xt)(\mathcal{K}^{(t)}, \mathcal{V}^{(t)}) = \mathcal{F}_{\theta}\big((\mathcal{K}^{(t-1)}, \mathcal{V}^{(t-1)}), \mathbf{x}_t\big)(K(t),V(t))=Fθ​((K(t−1),V(t−1)),xt​)

Intuition. Each chunk reads the cache from the previous chunk, processes the new tokens, and writes back a larger cache for the next step.

Symbols.

  • \mathcal{K}^{(t)} and \mathcal{V}^{(t)} are the accumulated keys and values after chunk t.
  • \mathcal{F}_{\theta} is the frozen transformer forward pass with parameters \theta.
  • \mathbf{x}_t is the current chunk of tokens.

Read-aloud meaning. The next cache is what you get when the transformer processes the current chunk while conditioning on the cache from the previous chunk.

Why it matters. This is the whole trick: long-context inference stops being one monolithic pass and becomes a recurrence over chunks. The cache is not just a speed optimization anymore. It is the recurrent state.

Seen this way, KV-Fold is very close to foldl in functional programming. The same update is applied again and again, and the cache is the accumulator. The difference from a classical RNN is that the state is not squashed into a fixed-size vector. It grows with the sequence and keeps structured per-token information, which is exactly why the model can still do content-based retrieval later on.

Why the drift settles instead of blowing up

If each chunk boundary simply injected fresh error, the difference from full attention should grow more or less without bound as the chain gets deeper. That is not what the paper finds. Instead, the drift rises quickly at the first few boundaries and then flattens into a plateau.

The paper measures this with negative log-likelihood on three views of the same data:

  • full attention over the whole sequence, which serves as the ceiling,
  • isolated chunks with no prefix, which serve as the floor,
  • KV-Fold, which sits between the two.

The drift score is the gap between KV-Fold and full attention. The recurrence advantage is the improvement KV-Fold gets over isolated chunk processing. The important point is not the exact formula; it is the behavior. KV-Fold quickly moves into a slightly shifted operating mode, and after that the shift stops accumulating.

That plateau is the part worth remembering. On one Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct run, the drift saturates after only a handful of chunk transitions and then stays essentially flat through deep chains. The paper also shows that increasing numerical precision from bf16 to fp32 barely changes the plateau, which is a strong clue that the effect is structural rather than just rounding noise. If the drift were mostly numerical, a 10,000x precision jump would have erased much more of it.

The paper is careful here. It does not claim a formal convergence proof. What it shows is a repeatable empirical pattern: a short transient, then a stable plateau. The simplest interpretation is that the chunked recurrence nudges the model into a nearby self-consistent attention regime. Once it is there, repeating the same update does not keep pushing it farther away.

That is a subtle but important result. KV-Fold is not trying to make the model behave exactly like full attention forever. It is showing that a frozen transformer can settle into a stable recurrence that remains useful, rather than slowly degrading into nonsense.

Retrieval at scale

This is where the paper stops being about loss curves and starts being about actual memory.

To test whether the cache still holds usable facts, the paper uses a needle-in-a-haystack setup. A fact is planted far back in the sequence, then the model is asked to recover it after many more chunks have passed. That is the kind of thing sliding-window streaming methods usually fail at, because the relevant token eventually falls out of the window.

KV-Fold behaves differently because it never throws old cache entries away. The old tokens stay available for content-based attention, so distant facts remain reachable even after many chunk boundaries. Across 152 retrieval trials spanning contexts from 16K to 128K, the method reaches exact match every time. The deepest runs go all the way to chain depth 511, which is far beyond what a simple one-pass full-attention setup would be practical to run on the same hardware.

The phrase to keep in mind here is retrieval at scale. The method is not just numerically stable; it is still able to find exact facts buried deep in the past. That matters because the whole point of long-context inference is not merely to lower perplexity. It is to preserve the specific information a user might ask for later.

A toy way to think about it: if the first chunk says, “The magic number for amaranth is 48291,” KV-Fold keeps that entry in the notebook. When the final chunk asks for the magic number, the model can still look back and point to the same fact, because the relevant keys and values were never compressed away.

What the method costs

KV-Fold is not magic compression. It is a reorganization of computation.

The tradeoff is straightforward: exact retrieval is preserved, but memory still grows linearly with total context, and later chunks take longer because they must attend over a larger cache. The paper is explicit about this. At 128K on Llama-3.1-8B, KV-Fold uses about 35.57 GB of peak GPU memory and finishes the chain in 171.3 seconds on the reported setup. That is operationally feasible on a 40 GB GPU, but it is still much heavier than a bounded-window streaming method.

That is also why the comparison with StreamingLLM is so clean. StreamingLLM is faster and uses far less memory because it keeps only a small fixed window plus sink tokens. But once the target fact falls outside that window, retrieval drops to zero. KV-Fold pays more memory to keep the full history, and that memory buys exact recall.

So the right way to remember the method is not “better streaming.” It is “recurrent full-history inference.” If bounded memory is your top priority, KV-Fold is the wrong tool. If exact long-range retrieval matters more, and linear cache growth is acceptable, KV-Fold gives you a frozen-transformer way to keep going.

What to remember

KV-Fold turns long-context inference into a stateful chunk-by-chunk recurrence.

The model reads a chunk, appends its keys and values to the existing cache, keeps absolute positions continuous, and passes the enlarged cache forward unchanged. That simple operational shift is enough to produce a stable plateau instead of runaway drift, and it preserves exact retrieval deep into long contexts.

The big lesson is not that transformers suddenly became recurrent in the classical sense. The lesson is narrower and more practical: the KV cache already contains enough structured information to act like memory, and if you stop resetting it, a frozen transformer can reuse that memory across arbitrarily many manageable chunks.

What KV-Fold gives you is fidelity. What it does not give you is bounded memory. That tradeoff is the whole story.
