Long Context Pre-training with Restoration Distillation

LongReD is a repair recipe for context-window extension. The paper starts from a simple problem: if you stretch a Transformer with RoPE scaling and keep training on long texts, the model can learn longer sequences, but it often stops behaving quite like the original model on ordinary short tasks. LongReD treats that as a distribution problem, not just a capacity problem. So the model is not only trained on long text. It is also taught to keep its short-text hidden states and output behavior close to the original model.

You can keep the whole method in one mental picture:

Why extending the window hurts the old behavior

The paper argues that two things go wrong when a short-context model is stretched.

The first is distribution drift. Once the RoPE setup changes, the model does not just see different positions. Its internal geometry changes. Hidden states move, attention patterns shift, and the extended model starts looking less like the original model even on the same short input. The paper measures that drift with hidden-state similarity and attention KL divergence, then shows that better similarity lines up with better short-task retention.

What to notice here is the trend, not the exact dots. Models that stay closer to the original internal representation keep more of their MMLU performance after extension. That does not prove causality by itself, but it strongly supports the paper’s core diagnosis: short-task regression is tied to internal drift, not just to longer sequences being hard.

The second problem is catastrophic forgetting. Even when short-task scores recover early during continual pretraining, they can fall again as long-only training keeps pushing the model toward long-text statistics. The paper shows this by tracking short-task performance over training steps. The pattern is familiar: a brief rebound, then erosion.

What to notice here is that short replay helps, but it does not fully stop the decline. A longer training run on long text alone still pulls the model away from its earlier short-text behavior. That is why the paper does not stop at replaying short data. It adds explicit restoration terms.

LongReD as a three-part repair

The whole method is easiest to understand as three pressures acting on one model at the same time.

The first pressure is ordinary long-text continual pretraining. That teaches the model to use the new window.

The second pressure is short-text distillation. That pulls selected hidden states of the extended model back toward the original model on short inputs.

The third pressure is short-to-long distillation. That takes the restored short behavior and makes it survive when the same content is placed into a simulated long-position frame.

The overview figure shows those three paths together.

What to notice is that this is not just “train longer on longer text.” The method adds two restoration routes beside long-text training. One route repairs internal representations on short examples. The other route teaches those repaired representations to stay valid when positions are stretched.

Formula card: the three losses work together

Purpose: balance long-context learning with two restoration paths.

Equation:

Lfinal=Llong+α1Lshort+α2Ls2l\mathcal{L}_{final}=\mathcal{L}_{long}+\alpha_1\mathcal{L}_{short}+\alpha_2\mathcal{L}_{s2l}Lfinal​=Llong​+α1​Lshort​+α2​Ls2l​

Intuition: one term teaches the model to handle longer sequences, one term pulls it back toward the original short-text behavior, and one term teaches that behavior to survive when positions are stretched.

Symbols:

  • \mathcal{L}_{final}: total training loss.
  • \mathcal{L}_{long}: ordinary long-text language-model loss.
  • \mathcal{L}_{short}: short-text hidden-state distillation loss.
  • \mathcal{L}_{s2l}: short-to-long distillation loss.
  • \alpha_1, \alpha_2: weights that decide how strongly the two restoration terms matter.

Read-aloud meaning: train on long text, but keep paying extra penalties whenever the extended model drifts from the original model on short text or in simulated long positions.

Why it matters: this is the paper’s main design choice in one line. It extends the window, then explicitly restores the behavior that extension would otherwise erase.

1. Stretch the window, but keep the training simple

LongReD still begins with the familiar long-context recipe. The model’s positional encoding is extended first, using either ABF or PI, and then it continues pretraining on long text with a standard next-token objective. That part is important because the model still has to learn to live in the longer window. Without it, the restoration terms would only preserve the old behavior and never teach new long-range behavior.

The paper’s point is not that this first step is wrong. It is that this step is incomplete on its own. Long-only pretraining gives the model more room, but it also shifts the model away from the original short-text distribution.

2. Restore the short-text internals

Short-text distillation is the first explicit repair term. The idea is simple: on short examples, make the extended model’s selected hidden states look like the original model’s hidden states.

Formula card: short-text distillation loss

Purpose: pull the selected layers of the extended model back toward the original model on short texts.

Equation:

Lshort=−∑x∈D2∑i=1MSim⁡ ⁣(Hli;p,Θe,Hli;p,Θo)\mathcal{L}_{short}=-\sum_{\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{D}_2}\sum_{i=1}^{M}\operatorname{Sim}\!\left(\mathbf{H}_{l_i;\mathbf{p},\Theta_e},\mathbf{H}_{l_i;\mathbf{p},\Theta_o}\right)Lshort​=−x∈D2​∑​i=1∑M​Sim(Hli​;p,Θe​​,Hli​;p,Θo​​)

Intuition: if the extended model’s internal states look like the original model’s states at the layers that drifted most, short-context behavior is less likely to disappear.

Symbols:

  • \mathbf{x}: a short-text training example.
  • \mathcal{D}_2: the short-text distillation dataset.
  • M: the number of layers chosen for distillation.
  • l_i: the i-th selected layer.
  • \mathbf{p}: the original positional indices for the short example.
  • \Theta_e: the extended model.
  • \Theta_o: the original model.
  • \mathbf{H}_{l_i;\mathbf{p},\Theta_e}: the selected-layer hidden states from the extended model.
  • \mathbf{H}_{l_i;\mathbf{p},\Theta_o}: the matching hidden states from the original model.
  • \operatorname{Sim}: cosine similarity between corresponding hidden states.

Read-aloud meaning: on short examples, make the extended model’s chosen layer outputs resemble the original model’s layer outputs.

Why it matters: this is the main mechanism for undoing drift and forgetting without forcing every layer to copy the teacher.

The paper does not distill every layer. It first looks for the layers whose attention distributions changed the most and selects those layers for repair, plus the last layer. That is a selective fix, not a blanket copy. It matters because distilling the whole stack can overconstrain the model and hurt long-context learning, while distilling only the last layer leaves too much drift in the middle. The selected-layer strategy is the compromise that keeps the repair focused where the drift is strongest.

Another useful detail is that the short-text replay is not just arbitrary short text. The paper uses a higher-quality short-text mix so the restoration signal is cleaner. That makes sense: if the goal is to restore the original model’s short-text behavior, the replay examples should themselves be good short-text examples.

3. Make the restored behavior survive long positions

The second repair term is the bridge from short text to long text. This is where LongReD becomes more than just distillation on familiar inputs.

The idea is to take a short example and place it inside a simulated long-position frame. The content stays short, but the coordinates change. That is the point. The model must now produce the right behavior while reading positions that look like the long-context regime it will see later.

Think of the position layout like this:

The paper builds that bridge by splitting the position indices into a head, a middle region, and a tail. The head stays anchored. The tail is moved to the far end of the target long window. The middle region is re-sampled with either CREAM or uniform skipped positions so the model sees a plausible long-range layout without needing a truly long input.

CREAM biases the middle positions more toward continuity and relativity. Uniform sampling spreads them out more evenly. In the experiments, that choice mattered. CREAM could be better when the extension factor was modest, while uniform sampling was more stable at 128K because it did not overfocus the middle at the expense of other positions.

LongReD only distills the last layer in this bridge. That is deliberate. The middle layers still carry latent positional structure, so forcing them to match too tightly can disturb the very geometry the model needs to handle long positions. The last layer is enough to pull the final output distribution into alignment without flattening the internal position machinery too aggressively.

What the experiments say

The headline result is easy to remember. On Llama-3-8B extended to 32K with ABF, LongReD-C keeps 99.4% of the original short-task average, compared with 92.5% for naive long-only continual pretraining. That is a large difference for a method that still learns the longer window. It means the restoration terms are not cosmetic.

The long-context story also holds up. LongReD stays competitive on RULER, so the short-task fix does not simply collapse the new window. In other words, the method actually trades between the two goals in a controlled way instead of choosing one and sacrificing the other.

The ablations line up with the mechanism.

  • Remove short-text distillation and short-task performance drops.
  • Remove short-to-long distillation and long-context performance drops.
  • Push either restoration weight too far and the long window suffers.

The skipped-position choice matters too. LongReD-C and LongReD-U usually preserve short tasks similarly, but their long-context behavior differs. CREAM helps when the extension is moderate because it concentrates more heavily on the middle, but uniform sampling can win at larger scales because it spreads the training signal more evenly across the simulated long positions.

The positional-scaling choice underneath the method matters as well. In these experiments, ABF was easier for LongReD to recover than PI. That fits the paper’s broader diagnosis: some scaling schemes create a larger distribution gap, so the restoration problem becomes harder before the distillation even starts.

The paper also compares against model merging and parameter-efficient tuning. Those methods can help forgetting, but they do not directly address the internal distribution drift created by positional scaling. LongReD does, which is why it ends up stronger on the short-context side.

Limits and what to remember

LongReD is useful, but it is not a one-knob fix. It adds several moving parts: a long-text loss, a short-text distillation loss, a short-to-long bridge, layer selection, and a choice of skipped-position sampler. That makes it more finicky than naive long-only pretraining.

The method also has real limits.

  • Very long distillation can hurt long-context behavior.
  • Too much weight on either restoration term can overconstrain the model.
  • The method does not solve the distribution disruption caused by the positional scaling method itself.
  • Better scaling schemes would still help, because LongReD is repairing drift rather than eliminating it at the source.

The easiest way to remember LongReD is to think of it as three steps:

  • train on long text so the model can use the new window,
  • distill short-text behavior so the original model is not forgotten,
  • replay that behavior through skipped positions so it still works in a long-position frame.

That is the paper’s core idea. LongReD extends the context window, but it also treats the original short-context model as something worth preserving, not something to overwrite.
