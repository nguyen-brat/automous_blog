Flow-GRPO: Online Policy Gradient Reinforcement Learning for Flow Matching

Flow-GRPO starts from a deceptively simple question: if a flow-matching image model already knows how to sample, why is it so awkward to train with online reinforcement learning?

The answer is that the default flow sampler is deterministic and expensive. Deterministic sampling is good for clean generation, but it is a bad fit for exploration. Online RL needs the model to try different trajectories, compare them, and learn from the differences. It also needs to do that repeatedly without spending too much time on each rollout.

Flow-GRPO solves both problems at once. During training, it wraps the pretrained flow model in a stochastic sampler that preserves the original marginals, then it runs GRPO on grouped rollouts. At the same time, it shortens the training denoising schedule so those rollouts are cheaper. At inference time, it keeps the full denoising schedule for quality.

What to notice in this figure is the split between training and inference. The paper is not replacing the whole generator. It is changing the sampling interface just enough to make online RL practical, then restoring the high-quality sampler when the model is used normally.

Why the usual flow sampler is a problem

Flow matching models usually generate images by following a deterministic ODE path from noise to data. That is elegant, but it creates two bottlenecks for online RL.

First, a deterministic sampler does not explore well. If every prompt follows the same denoising path unless the random seed changes, the model has very little chance to compare alternative trajectories for the same prompt. GRPO wants variation inside a group so it can decide which samples are better. A rigid sampler makes that signal weak.

Second, rollout collection is expensive. Flow models often need many denoising steps to produce a high-quality image. If online RL has to spend the full sampling budget on every exploratory rollout, training becomes slow enough to be impractical.

That is the core tension the paper is addressing. The model is already strong at generation, but the default generation loop is not a good learning loop.

Turning a deterministic sampler into a trainable policy

The most important idea in Flow-GRPO is the ODE-to-SDE conversion.

The paper does not simply inject arbitrary noise. It constructs a stochastic sampler whose marginals match the original ODE at every timestep. That matters because the goal is not to change what the pretrained model represents. The goal is to add exploration during training without leaving the model family behind.

You can think of it this way: the ODE gives you one clean path through latent space, while the SDE gives you a small family of nearby paths that still live in the same distributional world. That extra randomness is what online RL needs to compare alternatives.

This also makes the policy update tractable. Once the one-step transition is Gaussian, the KL term against the reference policy becomes easy to compute in closed form. That is a big practical win. Without that, the RL objective would be much harder to stabilize.

So the sampler now plays two roles at once:

  • it keeps the pretrained flow model’s behavior intact at the distribution level,
  • and it introduces just enough randomness for exploration and policy optimization.

That is the bridge from a deterministic generator to a policy that can actually learn online.

How GRPO fits into the denoising chain

Once the sampler is stochastic, the rest of the RL setup becomes much more natural.

For each prompt, Flow-GRPO samples a group of images and their denoising trajectories. Each image gets a final reward, and the rewards are compared within the group. The model does not need a separate value network to estimate returns. Instead, it uses group-relative normalization to turn raw rewards into advantages.

That design choice is important for two reasons. It keeps the method lighter than PPO-style systems, and it matches the structure of image generation better. The model is not solving a long interactive task with many intermediate rewards. It is generating a final image, then being told how well that image satisfied the prompt or preference signal.

The policy update itself is the familiar clipped objective: improve samples that beat their group, but do not let the policy jump too far in one step. The KL penalty to the reference model is the other stabilizer. It keeps the aligned model close to the pretrained generator, which is exactly what you want when reward hacking is a risk.

The practical picture is:

  1. sample several images for the same prompt,
  2. score them with the task reward,
  3. compute group-relative advantages,
  4. update the denoising policy with clipped policy gradients,
  5. keep the policy near the reference with KL regularization.

The group size matters here. Too small, and the advantage estimates become noisy enough that training can destabilize. The paper finds that reasonably large groups are part of what keeps the online loop stable.

Why shorter training rollouts still help

This is the other piece that makes Flow-GRPO usable in practice.

The paper observes that online training does not need the full high-fidelity denoising schedule. During training, it uses fewer denoising steps than inference. For SD3.5-M, the training schedule is reduced to 10 steps while inference still uses the original 40-step sampler.

At first glance that sounds risky. The short rollouts are visibly worse, with more artifacts and blur. But that is exactly why they are useful for RL. They are cheaper, and they still contain enough variation for the reward model to tell better samples from worse samples. The training loop does not need perfect images. It needs informative images.

The paper’s ablations show that this is a real sweet spot. Ten steps speed training a lot, while five steps are too aggressive and do not consistently help. So the denoising reduction is not “make everything faster no matter what.” It is “find the smallest rollout schedule that still produces useful learning signals.”

That is a subtle but important systems lesson. In online RL, rollout quality and rollout usefulness are not the same thing.

What the method changes in practice

The cleanest way to remember Flow-GRPO is to separate training-time behavior from inference-time behavior.

During training, the model is allowed to be noisy, slightly messy, and much cheaper. That noise is not a bug; it is the exploration mechanism. The shorter schedule is not a compromise; it is what makes the online loop affordable.

During inference, the model goes back to the full-step sampler. That is how the paper avoids the usual tradeoff where training shortcuts permanently hurt generation quality.

The evidence in the paper supports that story. On compositional generation, GenEval accuracy rises from 63% for SD3.5-M to 95% with Flow-GRPO. On visual text rendering, accuracy rises from 59% to 92%. The same recipe also works with human preference rewards, which suggests the method is not tied to one reward type.

The KL term is especially important in those results. Without it, the model can chase reward in ways that damage quality or diversity. With it, quality and preference scores stay strong while reward hacking stays low. In other words, KL is not just a regularizer in the abstract. It is the thing that keeps the aligned policy from drifting into a reward-shaped shortcut.

The remaining rough edges

Flow-GRPO is effective, but it is not free.

The method is tuned for text-to-image generation rather than video, so its transfer to longer and more expensive rollout settings is still open. The noise level and KL coefficient are also sensitive hyperparameters. Too little stochasticity weakens exploration; too much noise wrecks sample quality. And even with the shorter training schedule, online RL still relies on a large number of sampled rollouts.

That makes the method feel like a strong but carefully engineered bridge, not a universal shortcut. It works because it respects the shape of the pretrained generator instead of trying to replace it.

What to remember

If you want the one-sentence version, it is this:

Flow-GRPO makes a deterministic flow sampler trainable for online RL by wrapping it in a marginal-preserving SDE, then makes the online loop practical by shortening training-time denoising while keeping full inference quality.

Or, more casually:

  • add controlled randomness so the model can explore,
  • compare samples within groups instead of using a value network,
  • keep a KL leash on the policy,
  • and make training rollouts cheaper than final inference.

That is the core idea. The paper’s contribution is not just better image scores. It is a usable recipe for post-training flow matching models without changing what makes them good generators in the first place.
