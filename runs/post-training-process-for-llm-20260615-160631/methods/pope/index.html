POPE: Polling-based Object Probing Evaluation for Object Hallucination in Large Vision-Language Models

Large vision-language models can sound fluent and confident while quietly inventing objects that are not in the image. That is the core problem this paper is chasing. The authors are not trying to build another captioning model. They are trying to answer a more basic question: when an LVLM talks about an image, how often is it making up objects?

The key move is simple once you see it. Instead of asking the model to generate a full caption and then trying to parse that caption for hallucinated objects, POPE turns hallucination evaluation into a series of small yes/no checks. Ask about one object at a time. Keep the answers balanced. Make the absent objects harder in controlled ways. Then score the model like a classifier.

Why captions made the problem harder than it needed to be

The paper starts from a very practical complaint: open-ended caption evaluation is brittle. If a model says, “A man is standing near a car,” how do you decide exactly which words count as object mentions, which words map to objects, and which ones should be treated as hallucinations? CHAIR tries to do that, but it depends on caption parsing rules, and those rules are sensitive to prompt wording and generation style.

That instability shows up immediately in the paper’s experiments. On LLaVA, CHAIR changes a lot when the instruction changes from “Generate a short caption of the image” to “Provide a brief description of the image.” The score shift is not small noise. It is the kind of change that makes you wonder whether you are measuring the model or the prompt.

What to notice here is that hallucination is not just a rare typo. It is a systematic tendency to fill in likely objects, especially when the model is forced to talk about the image in free form. That is why the paper does not just ask, “How do we score captions better?” It asks, “Can we ask a simpler question?”

The intuition behind POPE

POPE stands for Polling-based Object Probing Evaluation, and the name is doing real work. The protocol treats hallucination as a binary probing problem. For each image, it builds questions like:
