Rubric-ARM: Alternating RL for Rubric-Based Alignment

Rubric-ARM is a method for a problem that shows up whenever you try to judge open-ended LLM answers: the thing you want to evaluate is too nuanced to fit into a single scalar score, but the criteria are also too prompt-specific to freeze into one static rubric forever.

The paper’s answer is to learn the rubric itself.

That sounds small, but it changes the whole reward-modeling loop. Instead of treating the rubric as a fixed handoff artifact and the judge as a separate scorer, Rubric-ARM makes the rubric generator and the judge co-evolve. The generator writes criteria for a prompt, the judge uses those criteria to compare two responses, and then the training loop updates the two modules in alternation so they stop chasing each other’s noise.

The intuition is easy to keep in mind:

  • the rubric generator decides what matters for this prompt
  • the judge decides which response better satisfies those criteria
  • alternating updates keep one side steady while the other learns

That is the core of the paper. It is not trying to replace preference modeling with a new scoring trick. It is trying to make preference modeling stable when the criteria are latent, prompt-dependent, and messy.

The basic pipeline

You can think of Rubric-ARM as a small system with one job: turn a prompt and two candidate answers into a more reliable preference label.

The flow is:

prompt + response pair -> rubric generator -> rubric-conditioned judge -> preference label

The clever part is that the rubric is not a fixed template. It is learned. So the model is not just asking, “Which answer is better?” It is also asking, “What does better even mean for this prompt?”

That matters most in non-verifiable domains like creative writing or open-ended instruction following. There is no exact answer key. A good evaluation needs to preserve prompt intent, cover the relevant criteria, and still make a robust pairwise decision. A single scalar reward tends to blur those pieces together.

What to notice in the figure is the feedback loop. The rubric generator is on one side, the judge is on the other, and the alternating RL schedule keeps the loop from collapsing into a moving-target problem. The paper is intentionally not training both pieces in one uncontrolled step.

Why static rubrics are not enough

The paper starts from a familiar failure mode. If the rubric is frozen, then the judge is forced to adapt to whatever criteria it was given, even if those criteria are incomplete or slightly misaligned with the prompt. That is tolerable for a narrow benchmark, but it is fragile once prompts become more diverse.

The other failure mode is the opposite one: if you let the rubric writer and the judge change at the same time, they can reinforce each other’s mistakes. A weak judge can reward bad rubrics. A drifting rubric generator can feed the judge unstable criteria. The result is noisy gradients and biased preferences.

Rubric-ARM is built to avoid that loop.

The paper treats rubrics as latent actions. That phrase is important. It means the rubric is not just text the model happens to emit; it is part of the policy behavior that the system is learning because it affects the final preference label. If the rubric improves, the judge can recover the right preference more often. If the judge improves, it gives the rubric generator a clearer target for what kind of criteria actually help.

So the central question becomes: how do you let both parts learn without letting both parts drift at once?

Start with a warm-up, not a cold start

Rubric-ARM does not begin RL from scratch. First, both modules get a supervised fine-tuning warmup.

That warm start gives the rubric generator basic rubric-writing ability and gives the judge a basic judging format before the alternating RL stage begins. The paper uses synthetic rubrics and judge traces built from open-source data to do this bootstrapping.

This is not a cosmetic step. It is what keeps the later RL loop from exploring in the dark. Without some initial structure, the judge would be too noisy and the rubric generator would have nothing reliable to exploit.

You can think of the warmup as teaching the two modules the local grammar of the task:

  • what a usable rubric looks like
  • how a judging trace should be formatted
  • how a pairwise decision is expressed

Only after that does the paper ask them to improve each other.

First half of the alternation: train the judge with rubrics fixed

The first RL phase freezes the rubric generator and updates the judge.

This choice is easy to understand if you imagine learning to grade essays. If the grading rubric keeps changing while you are trying to learn how to apply it, you never know whether a failure came from your judgment or from the rubric itself. Rubric-ARM removes that ambiguity by holding the rubric fixed for the judge update.

Even better, the paper caches rubrics during this phase. Each training example gets a sampled rubric once, and that same rubric is reused for multiple judge updates. That does two useful things at once:

  • it lowers sampling cost
  • it stops the judge from chasing a moving target

The judge is trained with a shaped reward. The final correctness signal still matters, but the paper also adds a format reward. That format reward encourages valid judging traces: the judge should address the rubric criteria, explain the comparison, give an overall justification, and end with a clear decision.

This is a practical detail, but it matters. A reward model can become degenerate if it only learns to emit the final label. The format reward pushes it to actually use the rubric, which makes the whole system more interpretable and less brittle.

So the judge phase is really about this:

  1. keep the rubric fixed
  2. train the judge to use that rubric faithfully
  3. reward both correctness and valid reasoning structure

Then train the rubric generator with the judge fixed

Once the judge has stabilized, the paper flips the roles.

Now the judge is frozen, and the rubric generator is updated to produce rubrics that help the current judge recover the right preference label. In plain language, the generator is learning to write criteria that are actually useful for discrimination, not just plausible-sounding checklist items.

This is the part that makes the rubrics less generic. A rubric that sounds sophisticated is not enough. It has to be the kind of rubric that leads the judge to the correct answer on that prompt.

The training signal here is simpler than the judge phase: the generator gets rewarded when the judge predicts the correct preference under the generated rubric. The paper uses a single rollout for this step, which keeps the update lightweight.

So the rubric generator is not being trained to mimic a static rubric library. It is being trained to search for criteria that are informative for the current prompt and the current judge.

That is why the paper describes the rubric as a latent action. The generator is choosing the action that makes downstream judgment easier.

Why the order matters

The paper is explicit about update order: judge first, then rubric generator.

That order matters because the judge is the more fragile piece early in training. If you let the rubric generator lead too soon, it can exploit weaknesses in the judge and amplify noise. If you first improve the judge against fixed rubrics, you get a steadier target for the rubric generator to learn from.

The paper’s theoretical section frames this as a variance problem. You do not need the full proof to get the intuition. The rough idea is:

  • fixed rubrics reduce one source of randomness during judge learning
  • a drifting rubric generator adds extra variance and makes gradients wobblier
  • alternating updates lower that instability compared with simultaneous co-training

The paper even connects the procedure to a generalized EM-like view. The rubric generator plays the role of a latent-variable approximation, and the judge update acts like improving the conditional model under the current rubrics. The important thing is not the label; it is the structure: hold one side still while the other side learns, then switch.

That is the main stability trick in Rubric-ARM.

How the trained model is used downstream

Rubric-ARM is not only a reward model for its own sake. The trained rubric generator and judge become supervision for downstream policy training.

For offline alignment, the system samples two policy rollouts for a prompt, labels the pair with Rubric-ARM, and then uses those labels in DPO or iterative DPO. The paper keeps only examples where the preference is consistent across both orders of the pair, which helps reduce position bias.

For online RL, the same trained judge supplies rewards for GRPO-style optimization. The paper also evaluates both response orders under the same rubric so the reward signal is less sensitive to which answer was shown first.

That downstream use is a good way to remember the method. Rubric-ARM is not just a judge. It is a learned rubric-conditioned labeling system that can feed either offline or online policy post-training.

What the paper shows in practice

The headline result is that the alternating schedule beats the simpler rubric-based baseline.

On the white-box reward-model benchmarks, Rubric-ARM raises the average score from 70.1 to 74.8, and voting@5 reaches 76.2. The improvement is not just on one metric family; it shows up across the instruction-following and preference-style benchmarks the paper reports.

The paper also checks out-of-distribution writing evaluation, where Rubric-ARM reaches 63.2 on WritingPreferenceBench, the best among the compared white-box reward models.

Two ablations reinforce the main story:

  • reversing the update order hurts, which supports the “judge first” schedule
  • removing the format reward also hurts, especially on structure-sensitive metrics

Those results fit the method intuition. If the judge is weak, the generator learns from noisy feedback. If the judge is trained without a format constraint, it can miss the discipline needed to actually apply the rubric.

What this method is really buying you

Rubric-ARM is more complicated than a single reward model. That is the tradeoff.

You get more moving parts, more sampling, and a warm-start requirement. You also depend on the quality of the generated rubrics and on the judge traces being reasonable. If either side is bad, the alternation can still propagate that weakness.

But the upside is strong in exactly the regime that motivated the paper: preference-only, non-verifiable tasks where the quality criteria are latent and prompt-specific. In that setting, a fixed rubric is too rigid and a jointly drifting rubric-and-judge pair is too unstable. Alternating RL sits in the middle. It gives each module a stable partner while it learns.

What to remember

The easiest way to remember Rubric-ARM is this:

  1. warm up both modules with supervised data
  2. freeze rubrics and train the judge first
  3. freeze the judge and train the rubric generator
  4. use the trained pair as a better reward signal for policy post-training

The paper’s main lesson is broader than this one method. When supervision is preference-only and the criteria themselves are latent, alternating updates are often safer than simultaneous co-training.

Rubric-ARM is that idea made concrete for rubric-based alignment.
