Understanding On-Policy Distillation: a Perspective from Thinking Pattern Alignment

On-policy distillation, or OPD, looks simple from far away. The student generates its own rollout, the teacher scores each prefix, and the student gets updated from that dense token-level signal. In practice, though, the method behaves like a gate that sometimes opens wide and sometimes barely moves at all.

This paper is useful because it explains that sharp difference. The main message is not that OPD needs a stronger teacher in the abstract. It needs a teacher whose reasoning pattern already sits close enough to the student’s own trajectory, and it needs a teacher that actually knows something new. When those two conditions hold, OPD becomes a kind of progressive sharpening process on the student’s own high-probability tokens. When they do not, the gradients are weak, the overlap stays flat, and the student can even drift backward.

That is the idea to keep in mind:

student rollout -> teacher scoring on visited states -> alignment on shared high-probability tokens -> updated student policy

The part that decides everything before training even starts

The paper begins with a surprisingly practical question: why does one teacher help while another, sometimes stronger teacher, does not?

The answer is that benchmark score alone is not a reliable guide. What matters first is whether the student and teacher have compatible thinking patterns. Think of this as the shape of their next-token preferences on the states the student actually visits. If the student and teacher are already looking at almost the same region of the vocabulary, OPD has a shared surface to work with. If they are looking at different regions, the teacher’s advice lands in the wrong place.

The paper measures this with overlap-related diagnostics. The overlap ratio is the cleanest one: it checks how much of the student’s top-k set also appears in the teacher’s top-k set. A low value is a warning sign for mode mismatch. A rising value means the student is finding the teacher’s support region.

The important intuition is not just “more overlap is better.” It is that OPD can only steer the student where the two distributions nearly touch. If the student never visits a place the teacher can meaningfully interpret, the teacher’s log-probability signal is dense but not useful.

The paper’s first empirical surprise is that a teacher with slightly weaker benchmark performance can still distill better if its reasoning style is closer to the student’s. A stronger teacher with a different pattern can fail outright.

What successful OPD is actually doing

Once the student and teacher are compatible enough, OPD stops looking like a broad search and starts looking like a local refinement process.

The student rolls out its own continuation. At each prefix, the teacher scores the same state. The update then focuses on the tokens that already live in the student’s high-probability region, especially the ones the teacher also prefers. The paper argues that the useful learning signal is concentrated there, not in the low-probability tail.

That is why the paper watches three dynamic signals during training:

  • overlap ratio, which says whether student and teacher are moving toward the same support;
  • overlap-token advantage, which checks whether the student is becoming properly calibrated inside the shared support;
  • entropy gap, which checks whether the student and teacher are converging in confidence on the same state.

The key pattern is progressive alignment. In successful runs, the overlap ratio rises from about 72% to above 91%, the overlap-token advantage moves toward zero, and the entropy gap shrinks. That combination says the student is not merely matching the teacher on random tokens. It is learning to place probability mass on the same high-value tokens the teacher uses when reasoning from the student’s own rollout.

What to notice in this figure is the shape of the training curve, not just the final number. The successful run does not jump magically to the end state. It steadily discovers the teacher’s preferred region and then keeps tightening the fit inside that region. That is why the paper describes the behavior as progressive alignment.

The overlap tokens are doing most of the work here. The paper reports that they carry roughly 97% to 99% of the total probability mass. So the overlap is not a cosmetic set-theoretic coincidence. It is where almost all of the probability actually lives.

Why stronger can still mean worse

The reverse-distillation experiment is the cleanest way to see the paper’s point about novelty.

Here the student is first improved with RL, and then the paper distills it backward toward an earlier checkpoint from the same family. That older checkpoint is weaker, but it shares the student’s family traits. The striking result is that this can drag the student back toward its pre-RL behavior. Replacing the teacher with a larger same-family teacher does not change the story much if that teacher induces nearly the same local target distribution on the student’s own trajectories.

This is important because it separates “teacher is big” from “teacher has something new to say.”

The paper’s conclusion is subtle but sharp:

  • OPD learns thinking patterns, not just scores.
  • A higher benchmark score does not automatically mean the teacher has transferable knowledge.
  • If the teacher only looks stronger because it is a scaled version of the same training recipe, the student may get little useful signal.

So the second condition is not just that the teacher should be compatible. The teacher should also bring genuinely new capabilities beyond what the student has already absorbed during training. Otherwise there is not much to transfer.

Why the overlap region matters more than the rest

The paper does more than observe alignment. It tests whether the shared high-probability region is actually the part that drives the update.

The answer is yes. Training only on the overlap tokens recovers nearly the same benefit as training on the student’s whole top-k support. Training on the non-overlap tokens is much weaker. That tells you where the gradient signal really comes from.

The simplest way to read this is:

shared high-probability tokens -> useful gradient non-overlap tokens -> mostly noise or weak correction

There is also a self-reinforcing effect. Once a token enters the shared high-probability region and the teacher prefers it, reverse-KL updates make that token even more likely. That pushes competing tokens out of the student’s top-k set, which raises the overlap further. So the method does not merely observe alignment. It amplifies it.

That feedback loop is why successful OPD feels stable once it gets going. The student is not trying to learn the entire vocabulary from scratch. It is progressively sharpening a shared support set.

When the run is failing, how do you rescue it?

The paper’s practical section is valuable because it treats failure as a training-design problem, not as a mystery.

The first recovery recipe is cold-start distillation. If the student and teacher are too far apart at the beginning, pure OPD has very little to exploit. The fix is to warm-start the student with supervised fine-tuning on teacher-generated rollouts before switching to OPD. That off-policy bridge raises the initial overlap and makes the teacher’s token-level signal immediately usable.

This figure is the repair story in one picture. The important thing to notice is that the cold-start student enters OPD with a much healthier alignment profile. The overlap starts higher, the entropy gap is smaller, and the later OPD phase becomes smoother. In other words, the warm-start does not merely make the first few steps nicer. It changes the whole downstream trajectory.

The second recovery recipe is teacher-aligned prompt selection. Here the idea is to improve alignment from the data side instead of the initialization side.

If the prompts look more like the teacher’s post-training prompts, the generated states are more compatible with the teacher’s distribution. That can mean a better prompt template, or prompts drawn from the teacher’s own post-training data. The paper shows that both variants can help.

There is a tradeoff, though. Teacher-aligned prompts can also reduce the student’s entropy too much. That can make the student more concentrated on the shared tokens, which is good for alignment, but too much of it can hurt exploration. The paper’s practical warning is straightforward: if you use teacher-aligned prompts, it may be safer to mix them with out-of-distribution prompts rather than relying on them exclusively.

Where the method starts to strain

The paper is very clear that OPD is not free. Dense token-level supervision is attractive, but it becomes less reliable as the trajectory gets longer.

The reason is intuitive if you think about student-generated prefixes. As the student keeps writing, the prefix can drift farther from states the teacher would naturally produce. That means later positions are scored under increasingly unfamiliar conditions. The teacher’s signal becomes noisier, and the update quality drops.

What to notice here is the depth effect. Shorter and moderate trajectories support cleaner learning, but very long trajectories become unstable. The paper’s discussion suggests a practical ceiling: the reward may still be globally informative, yet be hard to exploit locally.

That distinction matters. In one controlled test, both a successful teacher and a failing teacher could still assign higher sequence-level reward to correct rollouts than incorrect ones. So the problem is not that the reward is useless in the global sense. The problem is that the local geometry of the update can make that reward difficult to use.

This is the paper’s most important caution for future work: OPD can have a good signal in aggregate and still fail as an optimization problem. The signal is only helpful if it points in a coherent local direction on the student’s own states.

How to remember the recipe

The whole method can be remembered as a sequence of gates.

First, ask whether the teacher and student are already speaking a similar reasoning language. If not, pure OPD is likely to stall. Second, ask whether the teacher is actually bringing in new knowledge rather than just a larger version of the same distribution. If not, the student may regress instead of improving. Third, watch the overlap ratio during training. If it is not rising early, the run probably needs help. Fourth, if the support mismatch is too large, warm-start with teacher rollouts or align the prompt format so the student starts closer to the teacher’s support.

The broader lesson is easy to miss if you only look at final scores. OPD is not simply “more teacher supervision.” It is supervision on the student’s own visited states, and that supervision only works when the teacher can meaningfully shape the student inside the shared high-probability region. That is why overlap growth, entropy tightening, and prompt compatibility are not side details. They are the mechanism.

The open questions are also clear. The paper is mostly about math reasoning, so it is still unknown how well the same recipe transfers to code or open-ended settings. The long-horizon limit is also unresolved, and the authors are careful not to overclaim their local-geometry hypothesis. But as a practical recipe, the paper gives a much more predictable way to think about OPD: start with compatible thinking patterns, verify that the teacher really adds something new, and repair weak runs by increasing overlap before expecting the dense reward to pay off.
