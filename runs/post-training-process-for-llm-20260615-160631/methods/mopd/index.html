MiMo-V2-Flash

MOPD is the paper’s answer to a familiar post-training problem: if you improve one skill at a time, you often break another skill somewhere else.

That tradeoff shows up all the time in large language models. A model gets better at math after specialized RL, but its coding or general instruction following may slip. A code-tuned model becomes sharper on repositories, but it may lose some broad chat quality. If you try to merge several specialist models directly, the result can feel like averaging a team of experts into one person who is slightly confused about everything.

MOPD, short for Multi-Teacher On-Policy Distillation, takes a different route. It says: do not mix the experts too early. First, create specialists for different domains. Then let the student learn from its own rollouts, while the matching specialist gives dense token-level guidance. In other words, separate expert creation from expert integration.

That is the main idea behind the method, and it is easy to keep in mind:

  • build strong domain teachers first,
  • let the student act on its own prompts,
  • route each prompt to the right teacher,
  • reward the student token by token using teacher log-probabilities,
  • optionally add outcome-based reward signals on top.

What to notice in this figure is the separation of labor. The first stage creates a helpful base assistant, the second stage creates domain specialists, and the third stage does not start from scratch. It turns those specialists into training signals for a single student that is still learning on-policy.

Why the paper needs a new post-training recipe

The paper is trying to solve a capability imbalance problem.

In ordinary post-training, each new round of tuning can pull the model in a different direction. If you push too hard toward one domain, you can get a see-saw effect: one benchmark goes up while another drops. Static offline distillation has a similar weakness. It can copy what the teacher said, but it cannot easily teach the student how to behave on its own distribution of rollouts. Parameter merging has a different failure mode: it blends the weights, but it does not really explain how to combine the behaviors.

MOPD is built to avoid those traps. The student is not asked to imitate a frozen dataset of teacher answers. It is asked to explore its own trajectory, and the teacher then shapes that trajectory with token-level rewards. That is much closer to how a human coach works in practice: watch the student solve a problem, then comment on the steps that matter.

The pipeline, in plain language

The method has three stages, and each stage plays a different role.

The first stage is supervised fine-tuning. This gives the base model a usable assistant shape: it learns to follow instructions, answer in the right style, and stay stable enough for later RL. This stage is not the main contribution, but it matters because the rest of the pipeline depends on a student that can already listen.

The second stage builds the teachers. Instead of one general teacher, the paper trains domain specialists for math, coding, agentic tasks, general reasoning, and related areas. These teachers are trained independently, often with RL on their own domains, so each one is strong where it matters most.

The third stage is where MOPD actually happens. The student samples responses from its own current policy. A prompt-domain router picks the matching teacher. The teacher does not just say whether the final answer is good or bad. It provides dense token-level guidance based on how likely each token is under the teacher versus the student.

That is the crucial shift. The student is learning on-policy, but the reward is shaped by specialist knowledge.

What on-policy distillation buys you

On-policy means the student is learning from the kinds of outputs it actually produces right now, not from a fixed offline transcript.

That matters for two reasons. First, it avoids distribution mismatch. If the student changes, the training data would otherwise become stale very quickly. Second, it keeps the learning loop aligned with deployment behavior. The student is not just copying polished traces; it is learning how to behave while exploring its own answer space.

The dense teacher reward is what makes this work. For each generated token, the paper uses a reverse-KL-style signal based on the teacher and student token probabilities. Intuitively, if the specialist teacher assigns high probability to the token and the student does not, the token gets pushed up. If the teacher would strongly avoid the token, the signal pushes it down.

That gives the model a much richer learning signal than a single final score. A math teacher can reward the exact intermediate step that makes the derivation sane. A code teacher can reward the token sequence that keeps the function valid. An agent teacher can reward the move that sends the rollout in the right direction. The student does not just get told “good answer” or “bad answer.” It gets guidance at the level where decisions actually happen.

How the paper keeps the student from drifting

The formal objective is a token-level policy gradient surrogate with an important safety valve.

The paper computes a teacher-vs-student advantage from the log-probability gap, then weights tokens by an importance term that depends on the ratio between the training policy and the sampling policy. Tokens with a big mismatch are dropped. That is a practical stability trick: if the rollout and training policies diverge too far, the update is no longer trustworthy.

You can think of it as the paper saying, “Reward the tokens we sampled from a policy that is still close to the one we are training.” That keeps MOPD from turning into a noisy off-policy imitation loop.

This is also why the method is not just ordinary distillation with a new name. The student is always training on its own trajectories, and the reward is shaped by the teacher’s preferences token by token. That combination is what makes the supervision dense without becoming static.

Where the outcome reward fits

The teacher signal is strong, but the paper does not stop there. It can also mix in outcome rewards, such as those from an outcome reward model.

This is important because teacher logits and outcome correctness are not identical signals. The teacher reward gives dense local guidance. The outcome reward keeps the end goal visible. If the student takes a weird path that still ends in a correct answer, the outcome reward can still approve it. If the student drifts into a path that looks plausible but fails at the end, the outcome reward pushes back.

So the design is a hybrid:

  • teacher logits tell the student how to move locally,
  • ORM signals tell it whether the whole answer is actually good.

That blend is one reason MOPD is more flexible than pure teacher imitation. It can preserve specialist behavior while still optimizing for verifiable success.

Why specialists first is the right ordering

The order matters as much as the components.

If you try to build the student before the specialists are strong, there is nothing clean to distill. If you collapse everything into one training stream too early, the model starts averaging incompatible behaviors. If you rely only on static teacher answers, the student never learns how to behave on its own rollout distribution.

MOPD avoids those problems by staging the work.

First, the base model becomes a general assistant. Then each domain gets its own expert. Only after that does the student absorb the experts through on-policy training.

That ordering lets specialization and integration happen in different phases instead of fighting each other inside the same optimization step. It is a simple idea, but it solves the core tradeoff the paper is targeting.

A useful way to picture the mechanism

Imagine a student working through a coding question.

The student writes an initial attempt on its own. The routing system recognizes that the prompt is code-heavy, so it assigns the code specialist teacher. The teacher does not replace the student’s answer with a fixed demo. Instead, it scores the student’s actual token choices. The tokens that look like something the specialist would have produced get positive signal. The awkward ones get less or no credit. If the response is also judged by an outcome model, that score is folded in too.

Now imagine the same thing for a math prompt or an agentic task. The architecture does not need to change. Only the specialist teacher changes.

That is why the paper treats MOPD as modular. The method is not tied to one teacher type. The teacher can be RL-trained, SFT-trained, or even a student model reused later in the loop. The point is not the exact teacher identity. The point is that the right expertise is routed to the right prompt and delivered on the student’s own trajectory.

What the paper shows in practice

The paper’s evidence is consistent with the story above.

After MOPD, the student reaches 94.1 on AIME 2025 and 73.4 percent on SWE-Bench Verified. More importantly for the method claim, the student matches or beats the strongest teacher on most of the listed tasks. That is the real signal that the integration step is working: the student does not merely inherit a weaker average of all teachers. It becomes a single model that can carry the specialist strengths forward.

The paper also shows that MOPD compares favorably with pure ORM training and with MOPD without outcome rewards. That is a useful reminder that the dense teacher signal and the outcome signal are doing different jobs. One shapes the path; the other checks the destination.

What the method costs

The downside is straightforward.

MOPD needs separate specialist teachers, and that means substantial compute. The routing also has to stay sane as more domains are added. The paper’s own discussion hints at the open question here: how many teachers can you integrate before the switchboard becomes unwieldy?

There is also a systems requirement. On-policy distillation only works well if the RL infrastructure is stable enough to keep student rollouts and teacher scoring aligned. The method depends on careful routing and training stability, not just a clever loss.

Finally, the paper still does not close the gap to the strongest closed models on every benchmark. MOPD is a strong open-weight recipe, but it is not a finished solution to post-training at the frontier.

What to remember

MOPD is easiest to remember as a two-step separation:

  1. make strong specialists first,
  2. then teach one student on its own rollouts with the right specialist watching each domain.

The useful trick is the token-level reverse-KL reward. It turns teacher knowledge into dense local feedback instead of a static answer dump. The optional ORM term keeps the whole answer grounded in outcome quality.

If you want the one-sentence intuition, it is this: let the student explore, route each problem to the right expert, and reward each token according to the specialist that best understands that domain.
