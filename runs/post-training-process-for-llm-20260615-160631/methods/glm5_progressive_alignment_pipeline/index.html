GLM-5

GLM-5 is not a single post-training trick. It is a staged alignment recipe that turns a capable base model into a stronger reasoner, a better coding agent, and a more usable general assistant without letting later stages erase what earlier stages taught.

The paper’s central move is easy to state but hard to execute: separate specialization from retention. First, teach the model to follow instructions and think in the right format. Then sharpen reasoning with RL. Then push it into long-horizon agentic tasks with asynchronous rollout infrastructure. Then widen the objective to general assistant behavior. Finally, distill the gains from each stage back into one policy so the model does not forget how to do the earlier jobs.

What to notice in this figure is the shape of the system, not just the boxes. GLM-5 is built as a progressive alignment stack. The stages are not interchangeable, and they are not trying to solve the same problem. Each stage handles a different bottleneck, and the final distillation step ties the whole thing back together.

Why a staged pipeline matters

The paper is solving a very practical problem: post-training a large model for reasoning, coding, and agentic work usually creates two failures at once.

The first failure is compute. Long-horizon rollouts are expensive, and if generation and learning are tightly synchronized, the GPUs spend too much time waiting.

The second failure is forgetting. If you keep moving the model toward the newest objective, you can quietly damage the earlier capabilities that made it useful in the first place.

GLM-5 treats those as separate issues. It does not hope that one reward model or one RL stage will solve everything. Instead, it lets different stages specialize and then uses cross-stage distillation to preserve the useful parts.

That design is why the paper feels more like a training system than a single algorithm. The method is about the order of operations as much as the objective itself.

SFT is where the model learns how to behave

The first post-training step is supervised fine-tuning, but the paper uses SFT for more than plain imitation. It is where GLM-5 learns the behavioral grammar that later stages will depend on.

The SFT data covers three broad areas:

  • general chat
  • reasoning
  • coding and agent tasks

The important change is not just that the dataset is larger. It is that the model is taught to think and act in a more controlled way. The paper highlights three forms of thinking control:

  • interleaved thinking, where the model thinks before responses and tool calls
  • preserved thinking, where reasoning blocks survive across multi-turn coding-agent sessions
  • turn-level thinking, where the model can switch reasoning on or off depending on task difficulty

That trio matters because the later RL stages assume the model already knows how to expose its reasoning in a usable form. If the model is going to be optimized for long-horizon work, it has to keep track of context, maintain its own reasoning state, and avoid re-deriving the same plan from scratch at every turn.

So SFT is doing a quiet but essential job. It gives the rest of the pipeline something stable to refine instead of starting from a noisy, inconsistent policy.

Reasoning RL sharpens the chain of thought

Once the model can already follow the right format, the next question is whether it can reason better under RL. GLM-5 answers that with a reasoning-focused RL stage built on GRPO, with IcePop-style handling for training-inference mismatch.

The intuition is simple. During RL, the model is not learning from a static answer key. It is sampling candidate solutions, scoring them, and updating from the relative quality of those samples. That only works well if the sampling behavior and the learner’s view of the policy stay close enough together.

The paper makes that explicit by distinguishing two policies:

  • the inference policy, which generates rollouts
  • the training policy, which receives gradients

If those two drift apart too much, the update becomes noisy and the sampled trajectories stop reflecting the policy being optimized. GLM-5 therefore masks tokens whose training-vs-inference mismatch is too large and keeps the update in a controlled trust region.

There is also a practical DSA detail here that matters more than it might first appear. GLM-5 uses DeepSeek Sparse Attention, which relies on an indexer that selects the most relevant keys and values. In RL, nondeterminism in that selection can hurt stability because the rollout engine and learner no longer see the same behavior. The paper’s fix is blunt but effective: use deterministic top-k selection and freeze the indexer by default during RL. That keeps the training signal aligned with the actual rollout behavior.

The domains used in this stage are also telling. The model is trained on mixed reasoning tasks across math, science, code, and tool-integrated reasoning. In other words, the paper is not trying to make the model brilliant in only one narrow reasoning lane. It is training the same reasoning core to work across several kinds of verifiable problems.

Agentic RL is where rollout cost becomes the real enemy

The most important operational jump in GLM-5 happens when the paper moves from reasoning problems to agentic tasks.

Reasoning RL can still be run in a fairly compact loop: sample answers, score them, update the model. Agentic work is different. A coding agent or search agent can take a very long time to finish one trajectory, and different prompts can have wildly different rollout lengths. If training waits for every rollout to finish before it starts learning, GPU utilization collapses.

GLM-5’s answer is to decouple rollout generation from learning.

The rollout engine keeps generating trajectories. The learner keeps updating on completed batches. A central orchestrator routes tasks, balances sampling ratios, and keeps the whole system from stalling on long-tail jobs.

This is the core efficiency idea in the paper. The model is allowed to act for a long time, but the trainer no longer has to sit idle while one difficult trajectory drags on.

The paper then adds three stabilizers to make the asynchronous setup usable.

First is token-in-token-out, or TITO. Instead of re-tokenizing text after the fact, the learner consumes the exact token stream produced by the rollout engine. That sounds like a small interface choice, but it prevents boundary mismatches, whitespace normalization errors, and other subtle bugs that can corrupt long trajectories.

Second is direct double-sided importance sampling. Because the rollout engine may have been updated several times while a long trajectory was being generated, the paper does not try to reconstruct a perfect history of old checkpoints. Instead, it uses the rollout log-probabilities directly as the behavior reference and clips tokens outside a two-sided trust interval. The point is not to be mathematically elegant. The point is to keep the asynchronous system stable without drowning it in checkpoint bookkeeping.

Third is sample hygiene. Very stale trajectories are dropped, and environment failures that come from sandbox crashes rather than model behavior are filtered out. That matters a lot in coding-agent training, where a bad container can look like a bad policy if you are not careful.

The result is a more realistic agentic RL loop. The model learns from long-horizon attempts, but the training system does not pretend those attempts are all equally fresh or equally trustworthy.

General RL broadens the target again

After reasoning and agentic specialization, GLM-5 widens the alignment target.

This stage is about turning a strong specialist into a better general assistant. The paper splits the objective into three dimensions:

  • foundational correctness
  • emotional intelligence
  • task-specific quality

That split is useful because these goals are related but not identical. Correctness is the baseline: follow the instruction, avoid hallucination, do not make obvious logical mistakes. Emotional intelligence is about tone and naturalness, so the model does not sound mechanical or alien. Task-specific quality pushes the answer from merely acceptable to genuinely strong in a particular domain such as writing, translation, or subjective Q&A.

To supervise those dimensions, the paper uses a hybrid reward system:

  • rule-based rewards for deterministic checks
  • outcome reward models for lower-variance signals
  • generative reward models for richer, language-based judgment

The reason to mix them is straightforward. Rule-based signals are precise but narrow. Outcome reward models are efficient but easier to game. Generative reward models can judge more flexibly, but they are noisier. GLM-5 combines them so no single weak spot dominates the training loop.

The paper also keeps human-written responses in the loop as style anchors. That is important because pure model-generated optimization can drift toward polished but artificial-sounding responses. Human exemplars pull the model back toward the kind of answer people actually want to read.

This stage is the broadest one, but its role is still specific: it makes sure the model is not only good at solving tasks, but also pleasant and reliable enough to use as a general assistant.

Distillation is the memory system

The final stage is on-policy cross-stage distillation, and it is the piece that keeps the whole stack from turning into a sequence of overwritten skills.

The problem is obvious once you think about it. If you train a model on reasoning, then agentic work, then broad alignment, each new stage can subtly damage the previous one. GLM-5 solves that by turning earlier stage checkpoints into teachers and distilling their behavior back into the final policy.

The key idea is that the teacher is not an abstract offline model. It is the model’s own earlier self, sampled on the same families of prompts that were used for the earlier stages. The learner then tries to match the teacher’s behavior on-policy.

That matters because distillation here is not just knowledge transfer. It is retention.

The paper even simplifies the RL group setup in this stage. Since the comparison is directly against a teacher model, the group size drops to 1 and the batch size can be much larger. That is a good sign that the stage is doing a different job from the earlier RL passes: it is compressing and preserving, not exploring.

If you want a mental model, think of this stage as the glue that lets the model keep all its previous upgrades at once.

Why the pipeline works

The pipeline works because each stage solves a different failure mode.

SFT gives the model the right interaction style and thinking format. Reasoning RL improves verifiable problem solving. Agentic RL teaches long-horizon action taking without wasting as much rollout time. General RL aligns the assistant toward correctness and human usefulness. Cross-stage distillation stops the final model from forgetting what came before.

That is also why the paper spends so much effort on infrastructure. The asynchronous RL setup is not a side note. It is what makes the agentic stage practical enough to run at scale. Without decoupled rollouts, TITO, stable token clipping, and sample filtering, the pipeline would spend too much time waiting and too much time learning from noisy trajectories.

What to notice here is not just the leaderboard position. The result tells you that the staged pipeline is not narrowly optimizing one benchmark family. It is producing a model that holds up in both general chat and code-heavy public comparison settings, which is exactly what a progressive alignment stack is supposed to do.

The paper’s headline numbers fit the story. GLM-5 reports about a 20% average improvement over GLM-4.7 across eight agentic, reasoning, and coding benchmarks. It reaches 77.8 on SWE-bench Verified, 56.2 on Terminal-Bench 2.0, and 50 on the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index v4.0, becoming the open-weights leader there.

Those results are evidence that the pipeline is pulling in the right direction. They do not prove every piece is essential, but they do show that the combination is doing real work.

The tradeoffs

The paper is also fairly honest about what it costs.

This stack is infrastructure-heavy. It is much easier to describe than to reproduce, because the async rollout machinery, environment orchestration, and multi-stage training loops all have to work together.

Rewards and judges still need careful hand tuning. That is especially true once the tasks stop being clean math problems and start involving coding agents, tool use, or subjective general responses.

And some of the gain may be entangled with architecture and efficiency changes, not just the post-training recipe. The paper itself leaves room for that possibility.

So the right way to read GLM-5 is not “we found one universal alignment trick.” It is “we built a post-training stack that is strong enough to carry multiple forms of capability without collapsing under its own rollout cost.”

What to remember

The cleanest way to remember GLM-5 is this:

  • SFT teaches the model how to think and speak in the right format.
  • Reasoning RL makes the model better at verifiable problem solving.
  • Agentic RL scales long-horizon behavior by separating rollouts from learning.
  • General RL broadens the assistant toward correctness, style, and task quality.
  • On-policy cross-stage distillation keeps the earlier gains from being overwritten.

If you only keep one idea, keep this one: progressive alignment works better than one-shot alignment when the tasks are very different and the rollouts are expensive. GLM-5 is the paper’s proof of that claim.
