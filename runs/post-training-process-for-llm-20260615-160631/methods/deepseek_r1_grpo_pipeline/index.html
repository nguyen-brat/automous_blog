DeepSeek-R1: Incentivizing Reasoning Capability in Large Language Models through Reinforcement Learning

DeepSeek-R1 is best understood as a training recipe for a very specific problem: how do you get a language model to reason well when you do not have a huge human-written chain-of-thought dataset to copy from?

The paper’s answer is not “invent a bigger supervised dataset.” Instead, it says: if the task has a reliable verifier, let the model explore, reward the answers that actually work, and then recycle the best traces to make the behavior readable and transferable. The interesting part is that the reasoning behavior is not only learned, it is first discovered and then refined.

That is why the paper feels like a pipeline rather than a single trick. It starts with pure RL on the base model, adds a small readable cold-start phase, refreshes the data with rejection sampling plus supervised fine-tuning, and then distills the resulting reasoning traces into smaller models.

What to notice in this figure is the direction of travel. The model does not begin with polished chain-of-thought supervision. It begins with raw RL, then moves toward a cleaner and broader training recipe, and finally pushes the learned behavior down into smaller checkpoints.

Letting reasoning emerge first

The first surprise in DeepSeek-R1 is that the authors train directly from the base model with reinforcement learning, without using supervised fine-tuning as a required first step. That model is called DeepSeek-R1-Zero.

This matters because it changes the role of supervision. In the usual recipe, supervised examples tell the model what a good solution looks like, and RL only adjusts it later. Here, the base model is allowed to explore solution paths first. The reward signal says which attempts were actually correct, and the model gradually discovers better internal habits on its own.

The RL algorithm they use is GRPO, or Group Relative Policy Optimization. The key idea is simple: for each question, sample a group of candidate answers, score them, and compute a relative advantage inside that group. There is no separate critic network sitting next to the policy model. That makes the training cheaper and keeps the setup simpler.

You do not need to memorize the symbols to get the intuition. G is the size of the sampled group, A_i is how good answer i is compared with the rest of the group, and the KL term keeps the updated policy from drifting too far from the reference model. So the model is not just asked, “Was this answer correct?” It is asked, “Was this answer better than the other samples we tried for the same question?”

That relative comparison is the whole trick. It gives a useful learning signal even when there is no hand-labeled chain-of-thought to imitate.

The reward is deliberately narrow

DeepSeek-R1-Zero uses rule-based rewards, not a learned reward model.

That choice is important because the target tasks are verifiable. For math problems, the final answer can be checked against the ground truth. For code problems, a compiler or test cases can tell you whether the solution works. On top of correctness, the paper adds a format reward so the model keeps its reasoning inside <think> tags and its final answer inside <answer> tags.

That sounds like a small formatting detail, but it matters. The format reward gives the model a stable container for its reasoning process. Without it, RL can still push the model toward correct answers, but the output can become chaotic and hard to read.

The authors explicitly avoid neural reward models in this stage. Their concern is practical: a learned reward model can be gamed, it adds another model to retrain, and it makes the whole pipeline more fragile. So the first stage keeps the signal brutally simple. Reward what can be checked. Keep the structure fixed. Let the model discover the reasoning style by itself.

The training template is also intentionally minimal. The model is told to produce reasoning first and the answer second, but it is not told how to reason. There is no instruction like “always reflect” or “always use a certain strategy.” That restraint is what lets the authors watch the model’s natural behavior emerge.

What RL discovers on its own

Once the RL loop runs for long enough, the model starts to behave in ways that look surprisingly human.

DeepSeek-R1-Zero learns to spend more tokens on harder problems, to revisit earlier steps, and to try alternate lines of attack when the first one looks shaky. The paper describes this as a kind of self-evolution: the model is not being taught a fixed reasoning script, it is learning that more careful thought leads to higher reward.

The result is visible in the benchmark numbers, but the more interesting sign is behavioral. The model begins to generate long chains of thought, self-verification, and reflection-like text. In the paper’s “aha moment” example, the model even notices that it should rethink its own derivation midstream. That is not a hard-coded feature. It is a policy that emerged because the reward kept favoring better attempts over worse ones.

DeepSeek-R1-Zero is strong, but it is not yet pleasant to use. The paper calls out two major problems: readability and language mixing. The model can mix languages inside the reasoning trace, and the output is not always formatted in a way that is easy for people to read.

That is the transition point to DeepSeek-R1 proper. The lesson is not “pure RL failed.” The lesson is “pure RL can discover the capability, but you still need a small amount of structure to make the capability usable.”

A small cold start makes the whole system steadier

DeepSeek-R1 starts with a small cold-start dataset of long, readable chain-of-thought examples. This is not a return to heavy supervision. It is more like giving the model a clean template before turning the RL knob back on.

The paper’s cold-start data is built from a mix of ideas: prompting models for detailed answers, collecting readable outputs from DeepSeek-R1-Zero, and refining the results so they look human-friendly. The point is to seed the model with examples that are both reasoned and readable.

That readability constraint shows up in the output format. The model is encouraged to produce a reasoning process and then a short summary. In other words, the answer should not be one giant wall of thought. It should end with something a user can quickly absorb.

This is a small addition, but it changes the rest of the pipeline. Once the model has a little structure, the later RL stages do not have to fight quite as much noise.

Then the model is refreshed with better data

After the cold-start model is ready, the paper runs reasoning-oriented RL again. This stage is still focused on tasks with clear correctness signals, such as math, coding, science, and logic. The reward is still anchored in verifiable outcomes, but now the model starts from a more stable and readable state.

The paper adds one more useful idea here: a language-consistency reward. If the chain of thought drifts into multiple languages, the model gets penalized. The reward is based on how much of the reasoning trace stays in the target language.

This is a good example of a tradeoff the paper is willing to make. Language consistency can slightly reduce raw benchmark performance, but it improves readability and makes the model easier for humans to follow. In a research paper about reasoning, that matters. The goal is not just to score points. The goal is to produce reasoning traces people can actually inspect.

Once this reasoning RL phase converges, the authors do something especially practical: they use the checkpoint to generate new supervised data by rejection sampling.

The logic is straightforward. Sample multiple solutions for each prompt. Keep the correct ones. Filter out messy traces with mixed languages, giant paragraphs, or awkward code blocks. Then add broader non-reasoning data from the DeepSeek-V3 pipeline so the model does not become a narrow math-and-code specialist.

That produces a mixed supervised set of roughly 800k examples, with about 600k reasoning samples and about 200k general-purpose samples. The final supervised fine-tuning pass uses that refreshed dataset to rebuild the model with stronger coverage.

This is where the pipeline becomes more than “RL plus SFT.” It is really a loop:

  1. discover reasoning behavior with RL,
  2. keep the best traces,
  3. clean them up with SFT,
  4. then go back to RL again.

The final RL stage broadens the prompt distribution to cover all scenarios. Reasoning tasks still use rule-based rewards. General chat and preference-sensitive tasks use reward models, with helpfulness judged mainly from the final summary and harmlessness judged on the full response. That split is important because it keeps the reward aligned with the part of the answer the user actually cares about, while still checking the whole trace for unsafe content.

The last step is transfer, not just scaling

The distilled models are the proof that the method is not locked to the largest checkpoint.

After DeepSeek-R1 is built, the authors use its outputs to fine-tune smaller open models such as Qwen and Llama variants. They do not run another big RL stage for these distilled checkpoints; they mainly use supervised fine-tuning on the 800k samples. The point is to see how far the reasoning traces alone can carry smaller models.

That turns out to be very far. The distilled 7B model already beats much larger non-reasoning baselines on several benchmarks, and the 14B and 32B versions become genuinely strong reasoning models. The 32B distilled checkpoint reaches 72.6% on AIME 2024 and 94.3% on MATH-500, which is a strong signal that the discovered reasoning style is transferable, not just a quirk of one teacher model.

This is the paper’s transfer story in one sentence: once a large model has found a good reasoning policy, that policy can be packaged as training data for smaller models.

Why the recipe works

The method works because each stage solves a different problem.

Pure RL is good at discovery. It lets the model stumble into better reasoning paths without needing a giant human CoT corpus.

Cold-start SFT is good at stabilization. It gives the model a readable format and reduces the early chaos that pure RL can produce.

Rejection-sampled SFT is good at cleanup. It keeps the best traces and mixes them with broader instruction data so the model stays useful outside of reasoning benchmarks.

Final preference-aware RL is good at alignment. It makes the model more helpful and safer across general scenarios instead of only on formal tasks.

Distillation is good at compression. It pushes the learned reasoning habits into models that are cheaper to run and easier to deploy.

That division of labor is the core design insight. No single stage has to do everything.

What the paper admits is still hard

The paper is also clear about the limits.

The pure-RL model has readability issues and language mixing. DeepSeek-R1 is still weaker than the broader DeepSeek-V3 style model on tasks like function calling, complex multi-turn interaction, role-play, and JSON-heavy output. Software-engineering RL coverage is limited because those tasks are expensive to evaluate during training. The model is also sensitive to prompting, and few-shot prompting can hurt performance. The paper recommends zero-shot prompts with a clear output format.

Those limits are useful because they keep the method honest. DeepSeek-R1 is not a universal answer to post-training. It is a strong recipe for verifiable reasoning tasks, with partial spillover into general helpfulness.

What to remember

If you want one compact mental model, keep this:

DeepSeek-R1 lets a model discover reasoning through RL, cleans up the result with a small amount of structured supervision, then distills the learned traces into smaller models.

Or even shorter:

  • RL discovers the reasoning behavior.
  • A tiny cold start makes it readable.
  • SFT plus preference RL makes it broadly useful.
  • Distillation makes it cheap enough to reuse.

That is why the paper matters. It shows that reasoning can emerge from incentives when the task is verifiable, and it shows that the resulting traces are not just a curiosity. They can be turned into a practical training recipe for both large and small models.
