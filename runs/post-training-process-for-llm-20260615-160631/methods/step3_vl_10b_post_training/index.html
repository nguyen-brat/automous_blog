Step3-VL-10B

Step3-VL-10B is a paper about leverage. The base model is compact, but the post-training stack is where the paper pushes it into frontier territory. Instead of relying on a single giant training trick, the authors split the job into stages: first teach the model to reason in a stable way with supervised fine-tuning, then shape it with large-scale PPO, and finally spend extra test-time compute on parallel visual deliberation through PaCoRe.

That is the big idea in plain language: do not ask a 10B multimodal model to become strong all at once. Teach it in layers, reward what can be checked, and then let it explore several visual hypotheses before it answers.

What matters about this flow is not just that it improves scores. It changes how the model uses its compute budget. During training, the model learns which kinds of thinking are worth expanding. At inference, it learns when to keep searching instead of rushing to the first plausible answer.

Why the post-training stack matters

The paper starts from a simple but important observation: compact multimodal models often lose either reasoning quality, perceptual fidelity, or alignment once the scale gets tight. Step3-VL-10B tries to fix that with post-training rather than by only changing the backbone.

That choice is visible in the way the method is organized. The authors do not jump straight into reinforcement learning. They first run a text-heavy SFT stage to stabilize language and reasoning, then a multimodal SFT stage to rebalance the model toward visual tasks. Only after that do they turn on PPO, first for verifiable tasks and then for preference-aligned tasks.

The effect is easy to remember: first teach the model how to talk, then how to look, then how to improve itself.

Two-stage SFT gives the model a stable starting point

The supervised fine-tuning stage is doing more than just “instruction tuning.” It is setting up the model so RL has something reliable to build on.

The first SFT stage is text-dominant. The mixture is heavily skewed toward text, roughly 9:1 versus multimodal data. That makes sense if you think about the failure mode the authors are trying to avoid. A multimodal model that is weak at reasoning can become visually aware but still structurally sloppy. The first stage teaches the model to keep its logic organized before asking it to coordinate that logic with pixels.

The second SFT stage shifts to a balanced 1:1 mix of text and multimodal samples. This is where the model stops behaving like a text assistant that happens to see images, and starts behaving like a vision-language assistant that can move back and forth between both kinds of input.

The training recipe also uses a long context window, because the instruction data is not just short question-answer pairs. It includes long reasoning traces, OCR-heavy tasks, grounding, document understanding, and GUI-style interactions. In other words, the model is not only learning what to answer. It is learning how to keep track of more structured multimodal context without falling apart.

PPO is the part where the model learns what to trust

After SFT, the paper switches to PPO with the GAE advantage estimator. GAE is important because it smooths the reward signal into a steadier training target. In practice, that helps when the model is generating long trajectories and the useful signal is scattered across many tokens.

The policy sees a multimodal input, generates a rollout, and gets updated from the resulting advantage estimate. The authors keep the encoder frozen during RL and update only the decoder. That is a practical choice: it protects the visual front end from being shaken around while the language side learns the new policy behavior.

The reward design is split on purpose:

  • Verifiable tasks get objective rewards.
  • Open-ended tasks get preference rewards plus behavioral constraints.

That separation is the backbone of the RL stage. The model is not forced into one reward mold for everything. If the answer can be checked, use a checker. If it cannot, use preference modeling and guardrails.

For verifiable tasks, the paper uses perception rewards for grounding-style outputs and model-based verification for more general visual tasks. The perception rewards are straightforward: align the output with ground truth using geometric criteria such as IoU or distance. The model-based verifier is more interesting. It lets the system judge whether an answer is semantically correct even when formatting is messy, LaTeX is imperfect, or the reasoning path is not written in the exact same style as the reference.

That is a good fit for multimodal reasoning, because many correct answers do not look identical on the surface. The verifier has to be more forgiving than string matching, but still strict enough to reject answers that only got lucky at the end.

For non-verifiable tasks, the paper uses a generative reward model and extra behavioral penalties. The reward model compares rollouts against stronger teacher responses, while the penalties keep the model from drifting into easy-to-game behavior like language mixing, fabricated citations, or unjustified certainty. This is the part that keeps preference optimization from becoming reward hacking with nicer wording.

Figure 2 shows the two kinds of scaling pulling in different directions

Once RL starts, the training dynamics become the real story.

What to notice in Figure 2 is that reward keeps rising while rollout length does not simply keep growing forever. At first the model spends more tokens, then the average length comes back down toward the starting level. The paper interprets this as a cancellation effect between two different kinds of tasks.

Reasoning tasks often benefit from longer chains of thought. If the problem is mathematical or requires multi-step synthesis, more tokens can mean more search.

Perception tasks behave differently. Once the model becomes more confident about a visual target, RL can actually reduce waste by pruning redundant exploratory tokens. That is why the length can shrink even as reward improves. The model is not losing capability. It is becoming less noisy.

This is a subtle but useful lesson: longer does not always mean better. Sometimes the model gets stronger by learning which tokens to stop generating.

Figure 3 shows the downstream metrics following the same curve

The paper also tracks actual task metrics during RLVR, not just reward.

Figure 3 matters because it shows the reward is not rising in a vacuum. The downstream benchmarks improve in the same broad shape: a fast jump early in training, then slower but still steady gains later on.

That pattern tells you the RL stage is not simply overfitting to one reward proxy. It is building a policy that is more useful across reasoning, recognition, OCR, and grounding. The paper highlights this especially in tasks like MMMU, OCRBench, and GUI grounding, where the model needs both visual fidelity and task discipline.

The numbers are strong enough to make the point without drowning the explanation: the model reaches 80.11 percent on MMMU, 86.75 percent on OCRBench, 92.2 percent on MMBench, 94.43 percent on AIME2025, and 59.02 percent on OSWorld-G. Those results are not the mechanism, but they are evidence that the mechanism is doing real work.

The missing trace problem, and why PaCoRe exists

The paper’s next move is easy to miss if you only think about sequential chain-of-thought. The authors argue that some perceptual reasoning is hard to learn through ordinary RL because the relevant internal search process is not well represented in training corpora. In plain terms, the model may know how to answer, but not how to show itself the intermediate visual hypotheses that would make the answer more reliable.

That is the reason PaCoRe exists.

PaCoRe stands for Parallel Coordinated Reasoning. Instead of asking the model to follow one line of thought from start to finish, it asks for multiple hypotheses in parallel and then a synthesis step that cross-checks them. It is a little like having several careful assistants inspect the same image from different angles before one of them writes the final answer.

The important shift is that test-time compute becomes part of the method. The model does not have to commit to the first plausible visual story. It can explore a few competing stories, keep the useful ones, and then merge them.

PaCoRe is proposal then refinement

The easiest way to understand PaCoRe is as a proposal-and-synthesis loop.

The training data for this stage comes from the difficulty filtration used in RLVR. The authors reuse rollouts from prompts that were neither too easy nor impossible, then simulate coordinated reasoning by sampling multiple messages from that pool and feeding them back as synthesis context. Only cases that remain solvable in that coordinated setting are kept.

That filtering step matters because it keeps the task from becoming trivial. If the synthesis context made every problem too easy, the reward would stop teaching anything useful. The paper wants hard-but-solvable tasks, because those are the ones that force the model to compare evidence.

The final PaCoRe training uses long contexts, up to 64k tokens, and multiple rollouts per prompt. At inference time the paper also uses a much longer synthesis context when running PaCoRe, because the whole point is to let the model hold several candidate traces in memory at once before it commits.

This is where the method becomes qualitatively different from standard single-pass decoding. Single-pass decoding can miss a subtle visual cue or lock onto one weak hypothesis. PaCoRe gives the model a chance to notice that one path is inconsistent and another path better explains the image.

How the pieces fit together

If you follow one example through the full method, the logic is compact:

  1. SFT first teaches the model basic reasoning habits and multimodal fluency.
  2. RLVR then sharpens behavior on tasks that can be checked.
  3. RLHF adds preference alignment and behavioral constraints where ground truth is not available.
  4. PaCoRe spends extra inference compute on parallel visual exploration and synthesis.

That sequence is the paper’s real contribution. Each stage solves a different problem. SFT stabilizes the model, verifiable RL makes it more accurate, preference RL makes it more usable, and PaCoRe makes it think harder when the input deserves it.

What the method assumes

The pipeline is strong, but it is not cheap.

It relies on strong verifier and preference models, so the quality of the reward stack matters a lot. RL training itself is expensive, and PaCoRe adds long-context inference cost on top of that. The model is also leaning on the idea that multiple sampled rollouts contain useful diversity; if the rollouts are too similar, the parallel synthesis loses value.

There are also open questions the paper leaves on the table. Can the parallel trace be distilled into a single pass? Which reward pieces matter most across tasks? How well does this transfer to embodied or real-world settings where perception is messier than benchmark images?

Those are good questions because they point to the paper’s boundary. Step3-VL-10B shows that post-training can carry a compact multimodal model a long way, but it also shows that the gains are bought with extra compute and stronger infrastructure.

What to remember

The shortest useful memory of Step3-VL-10B is this:

  • teach the model in stages,
  • reward what can be verified,
  • use preference learning where verification is impossible,
  • and spend extra test-time compute on parallel visual hypotheses when a single pass is not enough.

That is why the paper stands out. It is not claiming that a 10B model magically becomes large. It is showing that a compact model can be pushed much farther when post-training is structured as a curriculum of stability, verification, and synthesis.
