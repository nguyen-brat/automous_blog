Search-R1

Search-R1 is trying to fix a very specific weakness in search-augmented language models: a model can be good at reasoning, and a search engine can be good at retrieval, but the combination is still brittle if the model never learns when to search, what to ask, and how to continue reasoning after evidence arrives.

The paper’s answer is to stop treating search as a fixed preprocessing step. Instead, search becomes part of the reinforcement learning environment. The model can think for a while, decide it needs outside knowledge, ask for it, read the returned passages, and keep going. In other words, search is not a static input. It is an action the policy learns.

That simple shift changes the whole training problem. The model is no longer only producing an answer. It is learning a rollout policy over interleaved reasoning and retrieval, where every new search result changes the state of the conversation.

The basic loop

The main thing to notice in the figure is the rhythm of the rollout. The model reasons, searches when needed, receives retrieved passages, and then reasons again with a richer context. The search engine is not outside the loop. It is one of the steps in the loop.

That matters because retrieval changes the future of the trajectory. Once new passages are inserted, the next tokens are conditioned on both the original question and the evidence that just arrived. Search-R1 therefore trains on the full interleaved path, not just on the final answer.

This is a better fit for hard factual and multi-hop questions than ordinary RAG prompting. A single upfront retrieval can help, but it does not teach the model how to recover when the first query is weak, how to ask a second query, or when to stop searching and answer. Search-R1 is built around exactly those decisions.

What the policy is actually learning

Think of the model as learning a pattern with three repeating moves:

  1. reason about what is missing,
  2. issue a search request,
  3. absorb the retrieved text and continue.

The paper keeps the control structure intentionally simple. It does not try to hard-code a sophisticated search strategy or reward every intermediate move. The model only needs to learn the rhythm of “think, search if needed, read, continue.”

That simplicity is important. If the training recipe tries to micromanage the search behavior, the policy can become dependent on hand-written trajectories instead of learning how to adapt on its own. Search-R1 wants the opposite. It wants the model to discover when search is useful by actually interacting with the search engine during rollout.

Why retrieved text is masked out

One of the most important technical choices in the paper is retrieved-token masking.

When the rollout contains both model-generated tokens and retrieved passages, it is tempting to treat the entire sequence as if it were produced by the policy. Search-R1 does not do that. The retrieved text is context, not the model’s action. So the optimization step only applies to the tokens the model actually generated.

This is a small detail with a big effect. If you let the loss “credit” the retrieved passage itself, the update can drift in strange ways. The model may start learning from tool output as if it were part of its own policy, which makes the optimization noisier and less stable. Masking keeps the training signal focused on the model’s decisions: when to search, how to frame the query, and how to reason afterward.

The paper’s ablation makes that point concrete. On the Qwen2.5-7B-base setup, masking raises average exact match from 0.343 to 0.431. That is not a tiny cleanup effect. It is one of the main reasons the method trains reliably.

You can think of it like this: the retrieved passages are reference material, not homework the model wrote itself. The policy should learn from its own actions, not from the pasted evidence block.

PPO or GRPO, the same search loop

Search-R1 is compatible with both PPO and GRPO, and that makes the paper more general than a single optimizer recipe.

PPO is the steadier option. It uses a critic and tends to train more stably in this setting, which is useful when the rollout already contains a moving target in the form of interleaved search. GRPO removes the critic and can converge faster, but the paper finds that it can also become less stable later in training.

The important point is not that one optimizer “wins” universally. It is that both can train the same search-augmented policy, because the real novelty is the environment design, not the choice of policy gradient flavor.

In practice, the paper’s analysis says PPO is the safer default here. GRPO can get moving faster, but PPO is less likely to collapse once the rollout gets complicated.

A reward that stays deliberately plain

Search-R1 does not rely on a fancy process reward. It uses a simple outcome-based signal: did the model end up with the correct answer or not?

That choice fits the rest of the method. If the search engine is already shaping the rollout state, you do not necessarily need a separate reward for every step of the reasoning trace. The paper argues that the final answer is enough to train useful search behavior, as long as the rollout itself is structured and the optimization is stable.

This is another place where the method feels practical rather than overdesigned. A learned reward model would add cost and complexity. A dense process reward would also risk rewarding the wrong intermediate habits. Search-R1 prefers to keep the objective minimal and let the policy learn the search pattern from outcomes.

The training template follows the same philosophy. It asks the model to reason in structured blocks, search when necessary, and then provide the answer. It does not try to force a detailed search script. The template gives shape, but not content bias.

Why the method works better than plain RAG

The strongest mental model is to compare Search-R1 with ordinary retrieval-augmented generation.

RAG usually retrieves first and then asks the model to answer. That works when the first retrieval is good enough. It breaks down when the model needs to refine its query, verify a clue, or combine several pieces of evidence across multiple hops.

Search-R1 learns that missing evidence is itself a decision point. If the first passage is incomplete, the model can search again. If the answer is still unclear, it can reason a bit more and search differently. That is why the method feels more like active problem solving than passive retrieval.

The outcome in the paper reflects that difference. Across seven QA benchmarks, Search-R1 improves average performance by 24 percent on Qwen2.5-7B and 20 percent on Qwen2.5-3B over RAG baselines under the same setup. On Qwen2.5-7B-base, the average exact match reaches 0.431 versus 0.304 for RAG.

That gap is the best way to remember the method. Search is no longer a prompt trick. It becomes a learned skill.

What the training dynamics reveal

The analysis section gives a useful clue about how the policy changes over time.

Early in training, the model starts shedding filler and learning to produce shorter, cleaner reasoning. Later, once it understands the rollout structure better, it starts using search more often and the responses grow longer again because the retrieved passages are now part of the context. The reward rises at the same time, which suggests that the extra length is not just verbosity. It is evidence that the model is actually using the search engine more effectively.

That pattern is worth remembering because it is easy to misread long outputs as inefficient outputs. In Search-R1, longer trajectories can be a sign that the policy has learned to expand the context at the right moment.

The paper also reports that larger models learn the search behavior better. The 7B model shows a bigger gap over baselines than the 3B model. That fits intuition: search planning, evidence selection, and multi-turn reasoning all benefit from more capacity.

Where it still breaks

Search-R1 is not magic, and the paper is clear about that.

If retrieval returns noisy or misleading passages, the model can confidently follow the wrong evidence. If the query decomposition is poor, the search trajectory may never reach the clue that matters. The method also depends on stable search integration and careful rollout handling, because the whole training loop now has to manage tool calls as part of generation.

There is also a deeper limitation in the reward design. Outcome-only rewards are elegant, but they do not directly measure search quality. A model can occasionally stumble onto the right answer while still using poor search behavior. That means the method is strong for final correctness, but it may miss finer-grained distinctions between good and merely lucky retrieval.

So the tradeoff is real: Search-R1 gives you stronger search behavior, but it still lives and dies by retrieval quality and query decomposition.

What to remember

Search-R1 turns search into a trainable action sequence. The model learns to interleave reasoning and retrieval, the retrieved passages are masked out of the policy loss, and a simple outcome reward is enough to improve answer quality.

If you want the shortest possible intuition, it is this: the model is learning not just what to say, but when to go look for more evidence.

That is why the method matters. It turns tool use from a prompting convention into a post-training skill, and it shows that a search engine can be part of the RL environment rather than just a prefilled context window.
