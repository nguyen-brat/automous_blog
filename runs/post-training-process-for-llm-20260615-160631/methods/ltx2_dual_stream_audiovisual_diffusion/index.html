LTX-2: An Open-Source Foundation Model for Interactive General-Domain Audiovisual Generation

LTX-2 is trying to solve a very specific mismatch in generative media: the video might look right, but the sound is either missing, late, or only loosely related to what is on screen. That is the weakness of sequential pipelines. If you make the video first and add audio afterward, the audio can only react to a finished visual story. If you make audio first and add video later, the image has to catch up to a soundscape it never helped shape.

The paper’s answer is not to force audio and video into one shared blob. Instead, it keeps them separate where that helps, and couples them where it matters. Video and audio each get their own latent space, their own compression path, and their own positional encoding. Then a dual-stream diffusion transformer denoises both streams together so the two modalities can keep negotiating with each other while the sample is still forming.

That is the core idea to hold onto:

input text + separate audio/video latents -> joint denoising with bidirectional exchange -> synchronized frames and stereo sound

Start with two different kinds of signal

The simplest way to understand LTX-2 is to forget about the transformer for a moment and look at the data it receives.

Video and audio are not just two copies of the same thing. Video is spatial and temporal. Audio is mostly temporal, with a very different density and rhythm. LTX-2 respects that difference by using separate causal VAEs. Video is compressed into spatiotemporal latent tokens, while audio is converted into its own latent sequence from mel spectrograms.

That separation does a lot of work for the model:

  • it lets each modality use the compression it needs
  • it avoids forcing sound and image into an awkward shared representation
  • it makes it possible to edit or condition one modality without pretending the other one is identical

In the audio path, the model works with stereo from the start. The waveform is treated as two channels, converted into mel spectrograms, and encoded into compact tokens. Those tokens are still temporal, so the model can reason about timing, speech rhythm, and acoustic events without carrying raw waveform complexity through the whole diffusion process.

On the way out, a VAE decoder and vocoder turn the latents back into a waveform. The paper uses a HiFi-GAN-style vocoder adapted for stereo reconstruction, so the final audio is not just a placeholder soundtrack. It is meant to come back as a coherent two-channel signal.

The architecture is asymmetric on purpose

LTX-2 does not split capacity evenly between the two streams. The video side is wider and heavier, while the audio side is smaller and more specialized. That asymmetry is not an accident. It is the paper admitting that video usually carries more structure, more spatial detail, and more compute demand than audio.

What to notice in this architecture figure is the overall rhythm of the system. The model is not one encoder, one backbone, and one decoder. It is a set of modality-specific entry points, a joint denoising core, and modality-specific exits. The point of the middle block is to keep the two streams in conversation while they are still uncertain, rather than letting one modality finish first and asking the other to follow along later.

That choice shows up again in the positional encoding. Video gets 3D RoPE, so the model can keep track of where things happen in space and time. Audio gets 1D temporal RoPE, because only timing matters there. When the modalities interact, the model focuses on temporal alignment instead of pretending audio should carry spatial coordinates. That sounds small, but it is exactly the kind of detail that keeps cross-modal attention from becoming sloppy.

How the two streams talk to each other

Inside each dual-stream block, the model does a predictable sequence of things: self-attention within the modality, text cross-attention, audiovisual cross-attention, and then feed-forward refinement.

The important part is not just that cross-attention exists. It is that it is bidirectional. Video can ask audio what kind of event is happening, and audio can ask video what the scene suggests should be heard. That matters for the cases sequential pipelines routinely get wrong:

  • a moving object that should trigger a sound cue
  • a visible speaker whose mouth motion should guide speech timing
  • a scene whose visual context implies music, ambience, or foley

The paper also adds cross-modality AdaLN. That gives the model a way to modulate one stream using the other stream’s hidden state and timestep. In plain language, the two modalities do not just exchange attention scores; they also influence how strongly the other side should listen at that stage of denoising. Early in generation, the model may need broader coordination. Later, it can narrow in on finer timing and detail.

That is why the architecture feels more like negotiation than fusion. Each stream keeps its own identity, but neither one gets to ignore the other.

The prompt is not just a prompt

Text conditioning is a bigger deal here than it might first seem. The paper is explicit that ordinary final-layer embeddings from a decoder-only LLM are not enough for this job. That is especially true when the model has to produce speech, because speech is not just semantic. It is phonetic, rhythmic, and often multilingual.

The text pipeline has three ideas stacked on top of each other.

First, Gemma3 provides the base language understanding.

Second, the feature extractor does not trust only the last layer. It gathers intermediate representations from across the decoder stack, then normalizes and projects them into a shared embedding space. The intuition is simple: early layers may carry more local linguistic detail, later layers may carry more abstract meaning, and the model benefits from both.

Third, the text connector refines those embeddings with bidirectional attention and a set of learnable thinking tokens. Those extra tokens act like scratch space. They can absorb and summarize context before the result is handed to the diffusion transformer. In effect, the text side is allowed to think a little before it conditions the audiovisual model.

That extra processing is not decorative. It is there because LTX-2 needs the text to do more than describe the scene. It has to support speech content, pronunciation, and prompt adherence at the same time. The video stream and audio stream each get their own connector, so the same prompt can be interpreted in slightly different ways depending on what the modality needs.

This is one of the paper’s quiet but important design choices: the text is not a single universal embedding shared blindly by both modalities. It is refined separately so the audio side can lean into phonetic precision while the video side can focus on scene semantics.

Why modality-aware guidance matters at inference time

Once the model has been trained, generation still needs a way to balance text faithfulness against audiovisual coherence. LTX-2 handles that with a multimodal version of classifier-free guidance.

The key idea in the figure is that guidance is split into two separate controls:

  • text guidance, which pushes the sample toward the prompt
  • cross-modal guidance, which pushes audio and video toward each other

That split is useful because those two goals are related but not identical. A sample can follow the prompt fairly well and still fail to sync. Or it can synchronize nicely and drift away from the wording. With separate scales, the user can tune those two forces independently.

The paper reports different guidance settings for the audio and video streams, which makes sense once you think about what each stream is trying to do. Speech usually benefits from stronger textual control, while visual generation may need a little more room to preserve motion and scene structure. The important thing is that LTX-2 does not collapse these pressures into one guidance knob.

This is the paper’s practical inference trick: keep prompt control and inter-modal alignment separate so you can tune one without breaking the other.

How the output becomes a full audiovisual clip

The denoising core does not emit raw pixels or raw waveform samples. It emits latents. After that, the decoders take over.

Video latents go back through the video VAE to produce frames. Audio latents go back through the audio VAE and then through the vocoder to produce the final stereo waveform. Because the two streams were denoised together, the decoded outputs already agree on timing more than a sequential pipeline would.

That also helps explain the model’s reported efficiency. The paper’s H100 benchmark shows a very large step-time gap compared with Wan 2.2-14B, with LTX-2 reported at 1.22 s/step versus 22.30 s/step on the setup described in the paper. The speed comes from the architecture choices that reduce unnecessary duplication: separate modality paths, but not a full duplicated backbone for each one.

LTX-2 also uses a multi-scale, multi-tile inference strategy to reach high resolution without blowing up memory. It starts with a lower-resolution base latent, upscales that latent, and then refines overlapping tiles in space and time. The intuition is easy to remember:

  1. get the global scene and synchronization right first
  2. increase resolution in latent space
  3. refine local detail in tiles so memory stays manageable

Because the tiles overlap and are blended in latent space, the final result can reach 1080p without obvious seams. This is the kind of systems trick that matters in practice: the model is not only trying to be good, it is trying to be usable.

Why joint modeling beats a sequential pipeline

The reason LTX-2 matters is not that audio-video generation had never been attempted before. It is that the paper takes the coupling seriously.

If a model generates video first, the sound becomes an afterthought. If it generates audio first, the video becomes an illustration of a sound decision that was made too early. LTX-2 avoids both traps by keeping the uncertainty shared until the sample is close to done.

That is why the paper’s main takeaway is stronger than “generate both modalities.” It is really saying:

when two outputs constrain each other, encode them separately, but denoise them together

That sentence is the memory hook for the whole method. The separate latent spaces keep each modality efficient and well-shaped. The joint denoising keeps them synchronized. The bidirectional cross-attention lets them exchange evidence. The modality-aware guidance lets you tune prompt adherence and alignment independently. And the stronger text pipeline gives the model enough semantic and phonetic grounding to make the whole thing work across more than just a narrow speech dataset.

What the paper is honest about

The limitations are worth keeping in view because they match the architecture’s strengths.

Speech quality and alignment weaken for languages or dialects that were underrepresented during training. Multi-speaker scenes can confuse who should speak. And once generations go beyond roughly 20 seconds, coherence and synchronization can drift.

Those limits are not surprising. They are the flip side of a model that works by keeping two streams aligned over time. The longer the clip, the more room there is for drift. The more ambiguous the prompt, the harder it is to decide which sound belongs to which visual source. And the more multilingual the setting, the more the text pipeline has to carry phonetic detail that may not be evenly covered by the data.

The paper is also careful not to pretend this is a world model. It is a diffusion model for audiovisual synthesis, not a system with explicit reasoning or deep narrative understanding. For complex stories, it still depends on the prompt or another system to supply the structure.

What to remember

LTX-2 is easiest to remember as a joint denoising system with asymmetric capacity.

It does four things in a row:

  • compress audio and video separately
  • refine text more deeply than a plain prompt embedding
  • denoise the two modalities together with bidirectional exchange
  • decode them back into synchronized frames and stereo sound

That combination is what makes the paper interesting. It is not a simple audio add-on to a video model, and it is not a fully shared multimodal soup. It is a practical middle ground: separate where the signals are different, coupled where they must stay in sync.

That is the method in one sentence, and it is the reason LTX-2 is a useful example of modern audiovisual generation.
