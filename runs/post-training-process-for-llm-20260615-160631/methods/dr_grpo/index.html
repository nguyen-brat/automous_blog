Understanding R1-Zero-Like Training

This paper is a reality check for R1-Zero-style reasoning RL. Its main point is not that reinforcement learning does nothing, and not that reasoning is fake. The point is more precise: when a model starts looking better, the cause may be split across three different layers at once - the base model, the prompt template, and the RL objective itself.

That matters because a long chain of thought can be easy to overread. A training run can look like it is discovering deeper reasoning when part of the gain is already sitting in the pretrained model, part comes from how the prompt is phrased, and part comes from hidden weights in the optimizer that favor certain responses.

Only after that does it introduce Dr. GRPO, a cleaner version of the objective that removes the hidden normalizations.

Before RL, the model already matters a lot

The first mistake the paper tries to prevent is crediting RL for behavior the base model already knows how to produce. On the surface, the usual R1-Zero story says that reasoning emerges because RL makes the model explore more and think longer. The paper says: slow down. Some of that behavior may already be latent in the pretrained checkpoint.

Figure 3 is the clearest reminder of this.

What to notice here is the three-way comparison. The paper looks at question-answering ability, exploration ability, and self-reflection across different base models and templates. Those are not the same thing. A model can be good at answering only when a certain template is used. Another model can already behave like a chat assistant even with no template at all. And a model can emit self-reflection keywords without that meaning the answer is actually more accurate.

That last point is important. The paper is careful not to treat words like “Aha” or “wait” as proof of deeper reasoning. Self-reflection keywords can appear in base models before RL and do not reliably track correctness after RL. They are a behavioral signal, not a truth oracle.

The strongest example is Qwen2.5-Math-7B. The paper reports that it scores 38.2 average with no template, versus 26.5 with the Qwen template and 0.0 with the R1 template. That is a huge warning sign. If a model does best with no template, then the template is not just formatting. It is part of the algorithmic setup. In this case, the wrong template can actively hide capability instead of revealing it.

So the paper’s first lesson is simple: if you want to understand reasoning RL, you have to check the base model before you celebrate the optimizer.

Where GRPO quietly changes the objective

Once the paper moves from base models to RL, it makes a clean mathematical point. In outcome-reward reasoning tasks, you want the policy update to reflect whether the sampled response was correct, not to quietly reweight answers because they are longer, shorter, or part of an easy or hard question group.

GRPO starts from a sensible idea. For each question, sample a group of responses, score them with a verifier, and center each response against the group mean. That gives a relative advantage signal instead of a raw reward. In plain English, the model learns which answers were better than the other answers it produced for the same question.

The problem is that the paper says GRPO adds two extra normalizations on top of that idea.

The first is response-length normalization. When the loss is divided by the number of generated tokens, shorter responses get larger updates when they are correct, and longer incorrect responses get punished less. That means the optimizer is not only learning “which answer was right,” it is also learning “which answer had the right length.” For reasoning models, that is a dangerous confounder because longer wrong answers can start to look more thoughtful just because the objective makes them cheaper to produce.

The second is question-level standard-deviation normalization. Dividing by the within-question reward spread means some questions get much larger weights than others. Easy questions and impossible questions often have low variance, so they can end up dominating the update. That is not the same as a batch-level normalization trick, where the scale is smoothed across many examples. Here, each question gets its own weight, so difficulty itself becomes part of the bias.

Figure 4 makes that distortion easier to picture.

What to notice is that the effective advantage is no longer just “how much better was this response than its siblings?” It is being reweighted by response length and by the reward spread within the question group. That means the optimization can drift even when the reward signal itself is stable.

The paper also points out that this length bias is not unique to GRPO. It shows up in popular open-source PPO implementations too, because many of them normalize the masked loss by the actual response length. That seems harmless if you are thinking about pretraining, where sequence length is fixed, but it is no longer harmless in RL when response length is a variable the model is actively changing.

Dr. GRPO: the same idea, but without the hidden weights

Dr. GRPO is the paper’s fix for that problem. The change is intentionally small. Remove the response-length normalization. Remove the per-question standard-deviation normalization. Keep the centered baseline, keep the verifier reward, and update the policy with PPO-style gradients.

In other words, the paper is not trying to invent a new worldview for RL. It is trying to recover the objective that the math should have implied in the first place.

The training loop is easy to summarize:

  1. Sample a small group of responses for each question.
  2. Score each response with a verifier reward.
  3. Subtract the group mean to get a centered baseline.
  4. Apply PPO-style clipped policy gradients without dividing by response length or per-question standard deviation.

Because the reward is rule-based - correct final answer gets 1, otherwise 0 - the paper also drops the KL penalty. That makes sense in this setting. A learned reward model is vulnerable to distribution shift, but a verifier gives a direct outcome signal. If you already know whether the final answer is correct, there is less reason to pay the extra cost of a KL regularizer and reference policy during training.

The implementation detail that matters is that the masked loss should be normalized by a fixed generation budget, not by however many tokens the model happened to produce. That is the clean way to keep the per-token gradient aligned with the actual RL objective.

The result is a more faithful policy update. The model is still encouraged to explore and still compared against its own group of samples, but the optimizer is no longer secretly rewarding length or special question groups.

Why the learning curves change

Figure 5 shows why the paper thinks this fix is worth caring about.

The key pattern is that both GRPO and Dr. GRPO can show the familiar R1-Zero-style trend: reward improves and response length grows during training. But the difference is in how far that length growth goes. GRPO keeps pushing responses longer even after the reward improvement starts flattening out. That is exactly the kind of behavior you would expect if the objective is still favoring verbosity behind the scenes.

Dr. GRPO changes that. The response length still grows when the model is learning, but it does not blow up in the same way. In the evaluation plots, incorrect answers also become noticeably shorter. That is a useful sign: the model is spending less effort on wrong overthinking, which is one way to describe token efficiency.

The most concrete anchor result is that the 7B recipe reaches 43.3% on AIME 2024. But the paper is not just claiming a benchmark win. It is making a narrower argument about how to interpret that win. If the objective is biased, then some of the apparent long-CoT growth may be an artifact of the loss, not a pure emergence of better reasoning.

The ablation story supports that reading. Removing either the length normalization or the std normalization already improves token efficiency and outperforms vanilla GRPO. That is what makes the paper persuasive: the improvements are not tied to one fragile trick. They come from removing two separate sources of bias.

Templates and question coverage are part of the method

One of the more useful side findings is that the prompt template is not just a formatting detail. Template choice changes the initial policy, and the wrong template can suppress an already capable base model.

The paper also finds that question-set coverage matters a lot during RL. If the template and base model are mismatched, then the question set has to do more work. Broader coverage helps the policy generalize, but narrower or simpler sets can sometimes work surprisingly well when the template already gives the model a good starting point.

That is why the paper compares different question sets such as MATH, GSM, ASDiv, and a larger mixed set. The lesson is not “bigger is always better.” The lesson is that template choice and training data coverage interact. A strong base model can already solve a lot of math; if you choose a poor template, RL may be spending its effort reconstructing a capability that the model had before training began.

Why math pretraining raises the ceiling

The same idea shows up again when the paper switches base models. A weak base model can improve under RL, but only a little. Once the model has more math-specific pretraining, the ceiling rises sharply.

This is the deeper reason the paper insists on separating base-model effects from RL effects. RL can amplify a useful prior, but it is not magical. If the base model has little math competence, the reward signal has less to build on. If the base model has already seen math-like data or question-answer text, then RL has a much better starting point.

So the paper’s broader picture is not “RL creates reasoning from nothing.” It is “RL can expose, reshape, and sometimes overstate reasoning-like behavior that was already partially present in the base model.”

What this paper is really teaching

Dr. GRPO is useful because it gives a cleaner answer to a messy question. It says that when outcome rewards are available, you should be very careful about any extra normalization that changes how much each response or question counts. If the optimizer rewards long wrong answers or overweights unusual question groups, then you are no longer measuring only reasoning quality.

The limitations are also clear. The main evidence is math-focused, so the story is strongest when the reward is a reliable verifier and the task has a crisp notion of correctness. The method does not prove that the same objective will transfer cleanly to every reasoning domain. It also does not make self-reflection keywords a reliable proxy for accuracy; if anything, the paper argues the opposite.

What to remember is straightforward:

base model readiness + task-sensitive templates + unbiased policy updates

That combination is the real lesson of the paper. Before you celebrate emergent reasoning, check whether the model already had the skill, whether the template was helping or hurting, and whether the optimizer was quietly biasing the result. If you strip those confounders away, you get a much better picture of what RL is actually doing.
