Llama 2: Open Foundation and Fine-Tuned Chat Models

Llama 2-Chat is not just a bigger pretrained model with a chat wrapper on top. It is a post-training recipe for turning a capable base model into a dialogue assistant that is useful, safer, and more stable over multiple turns. The paper’s core lesson is that one mixed reward is too blunt. The better recipe is to start from a stronger pretrained model, collect high-quality supervised and preference data, train separate helpfulness and safety reward models, and then iterate with rejection sampling, PPO, and safety-specific tuning.

The authors also make one operational choice that matters a lot in practice: they keep refreshing preference data as the policy improves. Once the model changes, the reward model has to keep up. Otherwise the judge drifts away from the behavior you are actually trying to shape.

Start from a stronger base

The alignment story only makes sense because the foundation is already better than before. Llama 2 is pretrained on more tokens than Llama 1, uses a longer context window, and adopts grouped-query attention on the larger models. That matters because post-training works best when the base model already has enough capability to be worth steering. Alignment is then mostly about behavior, not about rescuing a weak model.

The paper is also careful not to pretend that pretraining magically makes the model safe. Even a cleaned corpus still contains a toxic tail, and a model trained on the web will absorb some of that structure.

What should you notice here is not the exact fraction but the shape of the problem. A small toxic slice is still enough to justify separate safety tuning later, because the model will happily learn patterns from the long tail.

The recipe at a glance

The whole method is easier to understand as a pipeline:

That pipeline is doing three different jobs. SFT teaches the model how to look like a good assistant. Reward modeling teaches it what humans prefer. Safety tuning then puts extra pressure on the adversarial cases where helpfulness and safety pull in different directions.

Why the supervised stage stays small

A lot of people assume that more instruction data is always better. Llama 2 pushes back on that. The paper argues that quality matters more than raw volume, especially for dialogue-style instructions. Instead of drowning the model in noisy third-party instruction corpora, the authors invest in a smaller, cleaner supervised set and stop once the data is good enough.

That choice does two things. First, it keeps the SFT stage from baking in lots of low-quality habits. Second, it leaves more annotation budget for the thing that really needs human judgment: preference comparisons for RLHF.

The mechanics are simple. Each example is a prompt plus an answer, the model is trained autoregressively, and the loss is applied only to answer tokens. The important part is not the plumbing but the role it plays in the larger recipe: SFT gives the model a stable chat prior before the reward models start reshaping behavior.

Why there are two reward models, not one

This is the heart of the paper.

Llama 2 splits reward modeling into a helpfulness reward model and a safety reward model. That looks like a bookkeeping choice, but it is really the right abstraction. Helpfulness and safety overlap, yet they are not the same objective. A single mixed reward has to learn a fuzzy boundary between “answer the request” and “refuse the unsafe request,” and that is exactly where alignment gets brittle.

The paper makes that tension concrete with adversarial prompts. A request can be responsive in the narrow sense and still violate the safety policy. If one reward model tries to encode both behaviors at once, it has to carry too many conflicting signals. Separate objectives let each reward specialize.

The reward models are trained from pairwise human comparisons: given two answers to the same prompt, learn to score the preferred one higher. The paper also uses rating margins, so stronger preferences push the score gap farther apart. That matters because “significantly better” should not be treated the same as “slightly better.”

Another subtle but important detail is that the reward models are initialized from pretrained chat checkpoints. That way, the judge knows the same world the chat model knows. Without that, the reward could prefer answers that look good only because the judge and the policy disagree on basic facts.

Just as important, the preference data is refreshed in batches. Once the policy improves, older comparisons stop exposing the model’s current failure modes. Keeping the reward signal on-distribution is part of what makes the iterative loop work.

Rejection sampling first, PPO next

Once the reward models exist, Llama 2 does not jump straight to PPO. It starts with rejection sampling. For each prompt, the model generates several candidate answers, the reward model scores them, and the best candidate becomes the new target.

That is a deceptively simple move. It widens the funnel before the policy update even begins. If the model can already produce a few good answers somewhere in its sample set, rejection sampling pulls those answers into the training distribution. The paper’s curves show exactly the expected pattern: the best-of-N score rises as you sample more, while the median stays roughly where it is.

The large 70B model does the rejection sampling, and smaller models are then fine-tuned on those chosen outputs. In effect, the best policy distills into the smaller ones.

PPO then refines the policy on top of that. Here the KL penalty is doing essential work. It keeps the updated policy from drifting too far from the original model and helps prevent reward hacking, where the model learns to maximize the reward model instead of real human preference.

The paper also routes risky prompts through the safety reward and whitens the final score before combining it with the KL term. In plain language, it is trying to keep reward scales sensible while still giving safety the first say on adversarial prompts.

GAtt is the memory trick

Multi-turn alignment has its own failure mode: the model forgets the system message.

Ghost Attention, or GAtt, is the paper’s fix. The idea is almost mischievously simple. Take a multi-turn conversation and synthetically repeat the system instruction across the user turns. Then generate a response and fine-tune the model on that dialogue, but zero out the loss on earlier context tokens so training does not punish the model for the artificial repetition.

The intuition is that the instruction should behave like a thread running through the conversation, not like a note the model saw once and then lost. GAtt is a training-data hack rather than a new architecture, but it works because it makes the instruction hard to forget without requiring a special memory module.

The paper’s qualitative claim is easy to remember: without GAtt, the system message fades after a few turns; with GAtt, the model keeps the instruction alive much longer.

Safety gets its own lane

The safety stack is layered on purpose.

First comes safety SFT. Annotators write adversarial prompts and safe demonstrations so the model learns to refuse or redirect in a controlled way. After that, safety RLHF pushes the model on harder adversarial cases and trains a safety reward model. Finally, context distillation uses a safety preprompt like “You are a safe and responsible assistant” to elicit safer outputs, then fine-tunes the model on those outputs without the preprompt.

That last step is useful, but it has a catch. Safety context distillation can make the model vague or overly cautious if it is applied too broadly. The paper handles that by using it only on adversarial prompts and by keeping the distilled version only when the safety reward model says it is better than the original. In other words, the preprompt is a bootstrap tool, not a universal replacement for the original answer.

This is also where the main tradeoff becomes visible. Stronger safety reduces toxic outputs, but it can also increase false refusals on borderline prompts. The paper acknowledges that tension instead of pretending it disappears.

What the recipe buys you

The anchor results tell the story without needing a benchmark dump. The 70B Llama 2-Chat model was judged competitive with ChatGPT in human helpfulness evaluation. Safety fine-tuning reduced toxic generations to effectively zero across Llama 2-Chat sizes. And the pretrained 70B model’s TruthfulQA score improved from 50.18 to 64.14 after chat tuning.

Those results are impressive because they come from a recipe rather than a single trick. The base model carries the capability. SFT gives the assistant shape. Separate reward models keep helpfulness and safety from collapsing into one muddled objective. Rejection sampling and PPO keep improving the policy without losing the plot. GAtt and safety context distillation clean up the multi-turn and adversarial edges.

The limitations matter just as much. Evaluation is largely English-only. Safety tuning can over-refuse or become vague. And benchmark coverage is not the same as deployment coverage. So this is best read as a strong open recipe, not a universal guarantee.

What to remember

If you want one sentence to carry around, it is this: Llama 2-Chat improves by splitting alignment into pieces, refreshing the preference signal as the policy changes, and treating safety as its own objective rather than forcing one reward to do everything.

A shorter memory hook is:

  • imitate first,
  • rank with fresh human preferences,
  • separate helpfulness from safety,
  • search with rejection sampling,
  • stabilize with PPO,
  • and patch long-turn memory with GAtt.

That is the recipe the paper is really teaching.
