DAPO: Decoupled Clip and Dynamic sAmpling Policy Optimization

DAPO is a repair kit for long-chain reasoning RL.

The paper starts from a simple observation: once a model is trained with verifiable rewards, the reward signal is not the only thing that matters. The update rule itself can quietly break learning. If clipping is too strict, exploration dies. If a batch is full of prompts that already have no useful gradient, training wastes time. If long responses are averaged the wrong way, the model learns to underweight exactly the traces that matter. If truncated answers are punished too harshly, the model gets mixed messages about length.

DAPO is the paper’s answer to that whole bundle of failures. Instead of treating them as one problem, it separates them into separate knobs and fixes each one directly.

The input-output picture is easy to keep in mind:

  • input: a verifiable prompt with a reference answer
  • method: sample several candidate responses, keep the informative ones, update with a looser clip and token-level weighting, and shape length gently
  • output: a policy that learns longer, more reliable reasoning traces

That is the real story of the paper. It is not about inventing a new kind of reward. It is about making the RL loop stop fighting itself.

Why the baseline falls apart

The paper begins from naive GRPO on Qwen2.5-32B, and the starting point is not great. The model does get a reward signal, but the learning dynamics are unstable. Entropy collapses, many sampled prompts become uninformative, and long outputs can dominate the update in unhealthy ways.

That combination is especially bad for long-CoT reasoning. When the model becomes too deterministic too early, it stops exploring alternate solution paths. When a batch contains too many all-correct or all-wrong prompt groups, the advantage signal becomes weak or zero. When the loss treats each sample as equally important regardless of length, long traces get their credit diluted. And when the system punishes truncated outputs too aggressively, it can mistake “long but correct” for “bad.”

The paper’s headline result makes the motivation concrete. DAPO reaches 50 AIME 2024 avg@32 on Qwen2.5-32B, and the paper shows the gain as a stepwise climb over a naive GRPO baseline.

What to notice in the curve is not just the final score. The important thing is that the gains come from a sequence of training-loop fixes, not from one magic change. The recipe gets better because the whole loop becomes healthier.

The loop DAPO actually runs

Algorithm 1 is straightforward once you strip away the notation.

The trainer samples multiple responses for each prompt, computes a rule-based reward for each one, and then filters the batch so it keeps only informative prompt groups. After that, it computes token-level advantages and updates the policy with DAPO’s clipped objective. If outputs are truncated, length shaping pushes back gently instead of turning the reward into noise.

That structure matters because DAPO is not just “GRPO plus one trick.” It is a training loop that tries to preserve four things at once:

  • exploration diversity
  • useful gradients
  • fair credit for long responses
  • a sane length signal

The paper’s main contribution is that it treats those as separate engineering targets.

First fix: stop clipping away exploration

The first problem DAPO attacks is entropy collapse.

In standard clipped policy optimization, the same clip range is used on both sides. That is usually a reasonable safety rail, but in long reasoning RL it can become too blunt. Tokens that are already common are easy to push upward. Rare tokens that might open up a better reasoning path are much harder to rescue, because the upper bound shuts the door too early.

DAPO’s “Clip-Higher” idea is to relax the upper side more than the lower side. The lower bound stays conservative, which protects against destructive updates. But the upper bound gets more room, which makes it easier to increase the probability of low-frequency exploratory tokens.

That distinction is subtle but important. The paper is not saying “clip less everywhere.” It is saying that the two sides of the clip do different jobs.

  • The lower side prevents the policy from crashing.
  • The higher side should not choke off exploration before it has a chance to grow.

This is why the paper argues that symmetric clipping is too blunt for long-CoT reasoning. If you want the model to discover new reasoning patterns, the update rule has to leave more headroom on the optimistic side.

Second fix: keep the batch informative

The next issue is deceptively simple. What happens when a prompt group is already trivial?

If every sampled response for a prompt is correct, the group-relative advantage can collapse to zero. If every sampled response is wrong in the same way, the batch is not much better. Either way, the gradient signal is weak or useless. You are spending compute, but you are not teaching the policy much.

DAPO’s dynamic sampling fixes that by oversampling until the batch is filled with prompt groups that are neither all-correct nor all-wrong. In other words, it actively avoids dead-gradient groups.

This is easy to miss, but it is one of the most practical ideas in the paper. The method is not just “sample more.” It is “sample until the batch contains prompts that can still teach the model something.”

The payoff is twofold:

  • training gets denser, because each batch contains more useful updates
  • optimization gets stabler, because the effective prompt count stays more consistent

The paper also notes that this does not necessarily slow training as much as it sounds. In a synchronized RL system, generation is often dominated by the long-tail samples anyway. So filtering and resampling can make the learning signal better without turning the wall-clock cost into a disaster.

Third fix: make every token count

The sample-level loss used in GRPO is another hidden source of trouble.

At first glance, averaging over samples sounds fair. Every generated response gets one vote. But long reasoning traces are not “one vote” in any useful sense. They contain many more tokens, many more intermediate decisions, and many more chances to either discover a good pattern or drift into nonsense.

If you average first within each sample and then across samples, long responses get diluted. That creates two problems at once:

  1. good long traces do not get enough credit for the useful reasoning they contain
  2. bad long traces are not punished strongly enough for repetitive or gibberish behavior

DAPO switches to token-level policy gradient loss. That means the update is normalized over all tokens across the batch, not over samples as if every sample were the same size. A token in a long response counts like a token in a short one.

This sounds like a small bookkeeping change, but it changes the incentives in exactly the right place. It makes the model care about the quality of the reasoning path itself, not just the fact that one sample happened to be long.

The paper’s interpretation is useful to remember:

  • sample-level averaging hides what is happening inside long traces
  • token-level weighting lets the optimizer see those traces at the right scale

That is also why the paper ties this fix to long-CoT reasoning specifically. If your outputs are short, the difference is smaller. If your outputs are long and structured, the reduction method starts to matter a lot.

Fourth fix: do not punish length in a way that confuses the model

Long reasoning needs room to grow, but generation still has a maximum length. That creates a problem: truncated samples.

If you simply give every truncated output a hard penalty, you create noise. A sound reasoning process can be punished just because it ran out of space. From the model’s point of view, that is a bad teaching signal. It says, “your reasoning might have been good, but the cutoff made it look bad.”

DAPO’s answer is softer. Instead of making truncation a binary disaster, it introduces a gradual length-aware penalty in a buffer zone before the hard limit. The closer the output gets to the cutoff, the stronger the warning becomes. Once it goes past the limit, the penalty becomes severe.

That is the right shape for this kind of task. The model is not being told to always be short. It is being told that excess length is a risk signal, especially when it starts to crowd out a clean finish.

This matters because the paper is trying to balance two competing goals:

  • preserve useful long reasoning
  • avoid letting uncontrolled verbosity dominate training

The soft penalty is a compromise that gives the model a warning before the trapdoor opens.

Why the reward is rule-based and the answers are integers

One of the most pragmatic parts of the paper is the reward setup.

Instead of relying on a reward model, DAPO uses rule-based correctness for verifiable tasks. That keeps the reward signal cleaner and avoids reward-model noise or reward hacking. For math, that is especially attractive because the answer can often be checked directly.

The paper goes one step further and transforms many math problems so the answer becomes an integer. That is not a cosmetic choice. It makes parsing much easier and reduces the chance that the verifier gets confused by a formula, a fraction, or a symbolic expression.

So the reward pipeline is intentionally plain:

  • choose a task where correctness can be verified
  • transform the answer format when needed so checking is robust
  • use the checker directly instead of a learned reward model

That design is what allows the RL recipe to focus on reasoning quality rather than reward-model quirks.

How the pieces fit together

Once you line the pieces up, the method becomes very readable.

First, DAPO samples multiple responses per prompt. Then it throws away prompt groups that are uninformative because they are all correct or all wrong. Next it computes token-level updates so long traces are not undercounted. Then it applies the decoupled clip so rare exploratory tokens still have room to grow. Finally, it shapes overlong outputs gently so length is treated as a useful signal instead of a noisy failure mode.

The best way to remember the method is to think of it as four guardrails around the same RL engine:

  • keep exploration alive
  • keep the batch informative
  • keep token credit fair
  • keep length under control without corrupting the reward

That is why DAPO feels less like a single algorithm and more like a cleaned-up training recipe for long reasoning.

What the paper actually shows

The strongest evidence in the paper is the stepwise improvement on AIME 2024.

Naive GRPO starts around 30. Then the paper adds overlong filtering, Clip-Higher, soft overlong punishment, token-level loss, and dynamic sampling. Each step adds a few points, and the final recipe reaches 50. The paper also says this happens with only half the training steps used by DeepSeek-R1-Zero-Qwen-32B in the comparison it reports.

That pattern matters because it supports the paper’s main claim: reproducible reasoning gains often come from the training recipe, not only from the base model or the reward function.

At the same time, the paper is honest about the tradeoff. The system is more engineered than plain GRPO. It also depends on tasks where a reliable verifier exists. The authors mostly validate it on math reasoning, so the broader transfer story is still open.

What to remember

DAPO’s core lesson is that long-CoT RL fails for several reasons at once, so it needs several fixes at once.

If the policy gets too deterministic, relax the upper clip. If the batch is full of dead prompts, oversample until the batch is informative. If long outputs are being undercounted, switch to token-level loss. If truncation is confusing the reward, use soft length shaping instead of a hard slap.

Put together, those changes make the training loop behave more like a stable search process and less like a brittle optimization trick. That is the real contribution of the paper.

The caveat is just as important: this recipe is strongest when the task has a clean, verifiable reward. The paper shows that this is enough to make a big difference for math reasoning. It does not prove that the same setup will transfer unchanged to code or agentic tasks.
