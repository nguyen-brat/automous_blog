InternVL3.5

InternVL3.5 is a good example of a multimodal model family that treats reasoning, visual token cost, and deployment layout as one optimization problem. The paper does not just say “make the model bigger.” It stages the work: first align and strengthen the base model, then use reinforcement learning in two phases to push reasoning, then learn when visuals can be compressed, and finally serve vision and language on different GPUs so the system can actually keep up.

What to notice is the order. The model is not handed to reinforcement learning too early. It starts with supervised alignment, then moves through an offline warm-up, then an online refinement stage, and only after that does it learn the visual router and the decoupled serving layout. The whole recipe is a staircase: each step makes the next one less fragile.

Why the paper needs a cascade

Open-source multimodal models have a familiar problem. They can see, and they can talk, but hard reasoning still falls apart when the input gets dense or the output needs multiple steps. On top of that, high-resolution images make every answer expensive, because the vision side has to process a lot of visual tokens before the language model can even begin.

InternVL3.5 is designed to attack both bottlenecks together. The reasoning side gets a staged reinforcement-learning recipe. The efficiency side gets adaptive visual compression and a deployment split that removes the vision encoder from the language model’s critical path. The paper’s main lesson is that you do not have to choose between capability and efficiency if you are willing to train and serve the system in stages.

The training flow, from broad alignment to sharper reasoning

The first stage is ordinary but necessary: supervised fine-tuning on higher-quality multimodal and thinking data. This is where the model learns the kind of responses the rest of the pipeline should preserve. The paper keeps the context window at 32K tokens here, which matters because long multimodal prompts and long reasoning traces need room to breathe.

After that comes the part that makes InternVL3.5 stand out: Cascade RL. The name is literal. The model is not thrown straight into a heavy online reinforcement-learning loop. It is warmed up offline first, then refined online.

The offline stage uses mixed preference optimization, or MPO. The useful mental model is that MPO blends three signals: preference, quality, and generation. That combination is stronger than pure preference tuning because it can reward answers that are not only preferred, but also coherent and generative enough to stay useful. Offline RL is attractive here because rollouts can be collected once and reused, which makes the warm-up more stable and less wasteful.

The online stage switches to GSPO. This is the sharper phase. Instead of relying on a reference model constraint, the method updates from rollouts generated by the model itself. For each query, multiple responses are sampled, their rewards are normalized relative to one another, and the policy update is clipped so the new model does not drift too far in one jump. The practical result is that the model keeps refining the output distribution instead of just memorizing a static preference set.

What to notice in the ablation is the staircase again: SFT gives a solid baseline, MPO adds a meaningful bump, and the online phase adds the last push. The paper reports that this cascade improves overall reasoning by up to 16.0% over InternVL3, which is a large jump for a post-training recipe. The important point is not just the final score. It is that the offline warm-up makes the online stage less brittle and lets the stronger policy learn from better rollouts.

Why the offline warm-up matters

It is tempting to think offline RL is just a cheaper version of online RL. That misses the real reason it is useful here. Offline warm-up changes the quality of the data that the online stage sees.

If the model begins online RL from a weak policy, the rollouts are noisy, and the online stage spends a lot of its budget learning to avoid obvious mistakes. If it begins from a better offline policy, the rollouts are already more useful, so the online stage can spend its time pushing the ceiling higher instead of repairing the floor. That is the “cascade” part of the recipe.

This also explains why the paper talks about training efficiency as well as performance. The offline stage amortizes sampling cost, the online stage benefits from stronger rollouts, and the final result is better than using either stage alone. In other words, the stages are not redundant. They are complementary.

Visual compression is not just shrinking tokens

The efficiency side of the paper starts from a very concrete observation: high-resolution images are expensive because they expand into lots of visual tokens. But not every patch of an image is equally important. A blank margin, a smooth background, or a simple object region does not need the same fidelity as dense OCR or a diagram full of tiny marks.

That is the logic behind the Visual Resolution Router, or ViR. InternVL3.5 starts with the usual visual path: each image patch is encoded, then compressed to 256 tokens before it reaches the language model. InternVL3.5-Flash adds an extra route that can compress some patches further, down to 64 tokens, when the patch looks semantically simple.

The useful thing to notice in the architecture diagram is that compression is patch-aware rather than image-wide. The router does not say “this whole image gets the same treatment.” It decides per patch, which is exactly what you want if the input contains both dense and sparse regions. That makes the token budget adaptive instead of fixed.

How ViCO teaches the router safely

A router that compresses aggressively can easily break grounding if it gets overconfident. InternVL3.5 avoids that by adding Visual Consistency Learning, or ViCO, before the router is turned loose.

ViCO has two parts. First is consistency training. The model is shown the same image at different compression rates, and it is trained so that the output distributions stay close. In practice, a frozen reference model runs with the safer 256-token setting, while the policy model sees either the 256-token version or the 64-token version. The training objective is to minimize the divergence between those outputs.

That sounds simple, but it solves a real problem: if the compressed view and the full view lead to very different answers, then compression is unsafe. ViCO makes that mismatch explicit and tries to erase it.

The second part is router training. Here, the main vision-language model is frozen and only the router is updated. The router is trained as a binary classifier: keep the higher-resolution path when compression would hurt too much, or send the patch through the stronger compression path when the loss increase is small. The target labels come from comparing the output loss under full and compressed visual tokens. Rather than using a fixed threshold forever, the paper keeps a sliding history of these loss ratios and sets the threshold from a percentile of that history. That keeps the target roughly balanced as the model evolves.

There is one more detail that matters. During consistency training, patches from the same image are randomly assigned compression rates so the model does not forget how to operate without compression at all. That protects the fallback path. If the router ever makes a bad call, the model still has experience with the high-detail mode.

The payoff is a good one: InternVL3.5-Flash reduces visual tokens by 50% while keeping nearly full performance. The tradeoff is also real: if the image is visually dense and tiny details matter, compression can still hide the cue that changes the answer. The paper is honest about that.

Splitting vision and language at serving time

Even with better compression, the vision encoder and language model still have very different behavior at inference time. The vision side is highly parallel and mostly stateless. The language side is autoregressive and latency-sensitive. If you put both on the same device and force them to run serially, they block each other.

Decoupled Vision-Language Deployment, or DvD, is the systems answer. The vision encoder, the MLP adapter, and the router live on the vision server. The language model lives on a separate server. The vision side processes the image, sends compact BF16 features over the network, and the language side fuses those features with text and continues decoding.

What to notice in the deployment diagram is the overlap. Vision processing, feature transfer, and language decoding are organized as an asynchronous pipeline, so the vision work can run in parallel with the language model’s prefilling and decoding. That is the key to the throughput gains. The paper reports up to a 4.05x speedup at high resolution when DvD and ViR are combined, which is large enough to change whether a system is practical in production.

This part of the paper is easy to underestimate because it sounds like an implementation detail. It is not. Once images get large or multi-image/video workloads enter the picture, serial execution becomes the bottleneck. DvD removes that bottleneck by making the serving topology match the different compute patterns of vision and language.

Where test-time scaling still helps

InternVL3.5 also uses test-time scaling, but only where it helps most: reasoning tasks. The paper does not claim that every benchmark needs extra inference-time search. For perception-heavy tasks, the base model is already strong enough. For reasoning-heavy tasks, though, giving the model more deliberate time can still matter.

The first mode is deep thinking. The model is prompted to reason step by step before answering, which helps on problems that need intermediate checks.

The second mode is parallel thinking. The paper uses Best-of-N with a critic model, VisualPRM-v1.1, to select the best response among several candidates. That is a straightforward way to spend extra inference budget on breadth instead of just depth. In practice, the paper reports additional gains on reasoning benchmarks when this is combined with the trained model.

This is a useful reminder that post-training and test-time scaling are not substitutes. The post-training stack teaches the model to reason better in general. Test-time scaling lets it spend more effort on the hardest questions.

What the results say, without turning the chapter into a leaderboard

The headline results are best read as evidence that the staging worked.

InternVL3.5-241B-A28B reaches 74.1 overall on the general multimodal comparison, essentially matching GPT-5 at 74.0. On reasoning-focused benchmarks, the same family reaches 77.7 on MMMU and 82.7 on MathVista. On the efficiency side, InternVL3.5-Flash cuts visual tokens by 50%, and DvD plus ViR reaches up to 4.05x throughput speedup at high resolution.

Those numbers matter because they line up with the design. The staged RL recipe is about reasoning. ViR and ViCO are about selective visual compression. DvD is about serving. The paper is strongest when you see these as one stack rather than three unrelated tricks.

What remains imperfect

The limitations are also informative.

The hallucination gains are not uniform across scales, so the safety and grounding story is better but not solved. Some understanding scores saturate or even regress slightly versus InternVL3, which suggests the post-training focus on reasoning can trade off against some aspects of raw multimodal understanding. And on visually dense inputs, compression can still miss small but crucial details.

Those are not contradictions. They are the price of pushing capability and efficiency together. The paper is good at showing that the tradeoff can be managed, not eliminated.

What to remember

InternVL3.5 is easiest to remember as a staged system.

First, it uses supervised alignment to get a high-quality base. Then it uses cascade RL to warm up reasoning offline and refine it online. After that, it learns which visual patches deserve full resolution and which can be compressed. Finally, it splits vision and language across devices so the serving stack can run in parallel instead of serially.

If you want the shortest version, keep this sentence in mind: train in stages, compress only where the image can afford it, and decouple vision from language when you serve the model.

That is the real contribution of the paper. It shows that post-training can improve capability and efficiency at the same time, as long as the training recipe and the serving system are designed together.
