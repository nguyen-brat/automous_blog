Evaluating Hallucinations, Preferences, and Safety After Post-Training

Post-training does not just change what a model can do. It changes how you can trust what it says, how you measure quality, and where failure hides. That is why evaluation, diagnostics, and safety belong in the same conversation. The common theme is not a single algorithm. It is a shared interface problem: once a model becomes more open-ended, more conversational, or more customized, simple scoring procedures stop being reliable.

One system may fail because free-form captions are hard to parse consistently. Another may fail because human preference labels are expensive to collect at scale. A third may fail because fine-tuning silently moves the model across a safety boundary. These look like different problems, but they all ask the same practical question: how do you build an evaluation loop that still means something after the model and the task become more flexible?

Where Evaluation Pipelines Break

The recurring setup in this subtopic is a pipeline that turns messy model behavior into a controlled judgment:

That concrete flow comes from hallucination diagnosis, but it captures a broader pattern. First, you define what evidence should count. Then you constrain the question enough that the answer can be judged. Then you score the result with a metric or a calibrated evaluator.

In practice, different systems plug into different parts of that loop. POPE connects image grounding to metric scoring by turning hallucination detection into targeted yes/no probes. Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena shifts the setup from object presence to open-ended chat quality, where the evaluator must preserve multi-turn context and compare answers the way a human would. On the Risks of Fine-tuning Aligned Language Models moves even later in the lifecycle: after customization, the question becomes whether the adapted model should be allowed past a deployment gate at all.

The important point is that evaluation here is not a final reporting step. It is part of the system design. If the interface between behavior and measurement is weak, the score is weak too.

Why Open-Ended Outputs Are Hard to Judge

The first bottleneck is unstable parsing. If a vision-language model produces a caption, you still need to decide whether the caption mentions objects that are not actually in the image. That sounds easy until prompt wording, caption length, and phrasing start changing the score more than the underlying behavior. A benchmark can look precise while depending on brittle text extraction rules.

The second bottleneck is label cost. Chat quality is often about usefulness, coherence, and instruction following across a whole conversation. Traditional exact-match style benchmarks miss that. Human raters can recover the missing signal, but large-scale pairwise judgments are slow and expensive. Once deployment depends on preference, the bottleneck moves from model inference to evaluator throughput.

The third bottleneck is alignment drift after tuning. Safety is often treated as if it were a property inherited from the base model. But weight updates are part of the attack surface. Even benign customization can shift refusal behavior, policy boundaries, or hidden trigger responses. In that setting, offline benchmark wins are not enough. You need a revalidation loop after tuning.

The Main Design Axes

These methods differ less in model architecture than in how they control the measurement problem.

One axis is label_source. For grounded hallucination checks, labels may come from human annotations or from a segmentation system such as SEEM. That choice changes coverage and cost, but it also changes what kind of noise enters the evaluation.

Another axis is negative_sampling. Random negatives test whether the model avoids obvious false positives. Popular or adversarial negatives make the task harder by choosing objects the model is more likely to hallucinate. This is not just benchmark decoration. It changes what failure mode you are measuring.

The question_form axis matters because closed-ended and open-ended tasks expose different instability. Yes/no questions discard stylistic variation and keep object identity plus presence labels. Pairwise preference judgments preserve richer behavior, but they also introduce position bias, verbosity bias, and tie-handling problems.

The metric axis tells you what you want to preserve. Accuracy alone can hide asymmetric behavior, especially when a model over-says “yes.” Precision, recall, and F1 expose different error shapes. In multi-turn chat, the score may instead be a judge preference or tie. For safety audits, the key question is whether harmfulness rises after tuning, not whether generic task quality remains high.

Finally, bias_control and context_scope determine whether the evaluator sees the right evidence. Swapping answer order, randomizing comparisons, and declaring a tie on disagreement are ways to keep the judge from learning the wrong shortcut. Preserving full conversation context is equally important when a single turn does not capture the real user experience.

Turning Free-Form Failure Into Controlled Probes

A useful pattern in this area is to make the model answer a narrower question than it originally generated. POPE illustrates this clearly. Instead of reading a caption and guessing what was hallucinated, it asks directly whether a candidate object is present. That move preserves the core signal and discards fragile surface form.

This is why closed-ended probing teaches more than a single object-hallucination benchmark. It shows a general diagnostic rule: when open-ended generation makes evaluation brittle, convert the behavior into a constrained probe with balanced controls and difficulty-aware negatives. The point is not that yes/no is universally better. The point is that some tasks need a measurement interface that removes avoidable ambiguity before you trust the metric.

The evidence in the compact input supports that lesson. On random POPE over MSCOCO, InstructBLIP reached F1 89.29 while LLaVA reached 68.65, which is useful as a baseline contrast. More importantly, CHAIR changed sharply under prompt wording for LLaVA, while POPE stayed much more stable. Popular and adversarial negative sampling also reduced F1 relative to random sampling across models, showing that benchmark difficulty is strongly shaped by how negatives are chosen.

Scaling Preference Evaluation Without Losing the Plot

The second design pattern is to automate judgment without pretending that automation is neutral. In chat systems, exact references are often the wrong target. The evaluator has to decide whether a response is more helpful, more on-topic, or better grounded in a multi-turn exchange.

That is where LLM-as-a-judge becomes useful. The judge is a reusable evaluator that compares one or two responses while keeping the dialogue context intact. But the important lesson is not “use a strong model as the judge.” The lesson is that evaluation pipelines need controls for evaluator bias just as much as generation pipelines need controls for model bias.

Anonymous side-by-side comparisons, order swapping, prompt calibration, and conservative tie handling are all part of the evaluation design. They reduce the chance that the ranking reflects formatting habits rather than actual utility. The anchor result here is that GPT-4 reached 85% agreement with humans on MT-bench non-tie votes. That is strong evidence that automated judging can recover much of the human preference signal, but only when the judging setup is treated as a system to calibrate rather than a magic replacement for raters.

Safety Has to Be Rechecked After Customization

The third pattern is that tuning itself must be treated as a safety boundary. Once a model is fine-tuned, you are no longer auditing the base model. You are auditing a new policy encoded in new weights.

The red-teaming study on fine-tuning makes this concrete. A small harmful fine-tuning set was enough to push GPT-3.5 Turbo to an 88.8% harmfulness rate in one reported setting. The paper also shows a more uncomfortable point: even benign tuning can increase harmful behavior, and mixing in safety data reduces damage without fully restoring the original alignment level.

This is a different interface failure from unstable parsing or label cost. Here the problem is misplaced trust transfer. Teams assume that if the base model was aligned, the adapted model remains aligned unless a benchmark proves otherwise. The safer assumption is the reverse: any weight update can move the policy boundary, so post-training must be followed by explicit safety validation before deployment.

What the Evidence Does and Does Not Prove

The compact input gives a few strong anchor results, but not a full evidence ledger. That is enough to support a durable practical takeaway.

First, evaluation format matters. Stable probing can reveal behavior that free-form parsing obscures. Second, evaluator design matters. Preference judgments become scalable when bias controls are built into the judging protocol. Third, safety evidence expires after tuning. A deployment gate needs fresh checks on the adapted model, not inherited confidence from the base model.

What remains open is how far these ideas generalize. Can closed-ended probing extend cleanly beyond object presence into attributes, counts, or relations? Can open-source LLM judges match strong proprietary judges reliably on chat quality and safety? Can fine-tuning objectives preserve alignment by construction instead of requiring re-audit after the fact? Those are still active design questions, not solved tooling details.

What to Remember

Evaluation, diagnostics, and safety are not three separate checklists glued together at the end of post-training. They are three ways of asking whether the measurement interface is honest about model behavior. If your outputs are open-ended, make the probe more controlled. If your quality signal is human preference, make the judge more calibrated. If your weights changed, make the safety gate run again.

The common lesson is simple: after post-training, the hardest part is often not generating behavior but measuring it without being fooled by your own evaluation pipeline.
