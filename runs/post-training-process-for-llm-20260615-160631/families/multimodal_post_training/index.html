Multimodal Post-Training and Reasoning

Multimodal models do not fail only because they lack parameters or data. They often fail at the interface between perception and reasoning: the model sees an image, a chart, or a document, but it does not reliably preserve the right visual facts long enough to think with them. That is why multimodal post-training matters. It is the stage where a vision-language model learns how to align modalities, how to spend extra compute on hard reasoning problems, and how to do that without making inference too slow to be practical.

The common theme here is not a single algorithm. It is a shared interface problem. Some methods try to reduce alignment glue by learning text and vision together from the start. Others treat post-training as a staged reasoning curriculum. Others focus on how to preserve perception quality while expanding test-time reasoning. Across all of them, the question is the same: how do you turn visual input into reasoning-ready context without losing grounding or blowing up latency?

InternVL3.5 focuses on the handoff from supervised tuning to reinforcement learning and then to serving. Step3-VL-10B shows how a smaller model can rely heavily on staged post-training and test-time synthesis to recover reasoning strength.

Where the Interface Fails

The first bottleneck is modality alignment. If vision is bolted onto a text model too late, the system may need extra freezes, adapters, or alignment stages, and each extra bridge is a chance to lose either language quality or visual grounding. The model may know how to talk fluently and still fail to reason from what it actually saw.

The second bottleneck is reasoning versus latency. Multimodal reasoning often improves when the model explores more candidate traces or preserves more visual detail, but both moves are expensive. High-resolution images create many visual tokens, and long reasoning traces increase decode cost. A model that reasons better in the lab can still be unusable in deployment if every hard prompt becomes slow.

The third bottleneck is reasoning versus perception. Strong reward optimization can improve verifiable reasoning while degrading subtle visual understanding, and aggressive compression can save compute while hiding exactly the small cue that matters. The hard part is not merely adding more optimization. It is deciding what information can be compressed, delayed, reranked, or discarded without breaking grounded answers.

The Main Design Axes

One major axis is when alignment happens. A joint text-plus-multimodal pretraining strategy treats visual tokens as first-class context from the beginning. A later-stage strategy accepts a more modular base model, then uses supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning to reshape reasoning after pretraining.

Another axis is what the model is trained to predict. The compact input highlights text-token-only loss as a deliberate choice: generation stays language-centered even when the context contains images or video. That choice can preserve a strong language interface, but it also increases pressure on the visual encoder and tokenization path to deliver the right facts into text-side reasoning.

A third axis is how extra compute is spent. Fractional V2PE extends multimodal context handling, Best-of-N reranking spends compute on selection at inference, and staged SFT plus MPO spends compute during post-training to improve reasoning before deployment. The broader pattern is that multimodal quality does not come from one knob. It comes from choosing where the budget goes: longer context, better post-training, more candidates, or cheaper serving.

Learning the Interface Early

One answer to the alignment problem is to make multimodality native instead of retrofitted. InternVL3 illustrates that idea by mixing text-only and multimodal corpora during pretraining, updating all parameters jointly, and keeping the autoregressive objective focused on text tokens. Conceptually, this reduces the amount of alignment glue the later pipeline has to repair. If the model already treats visual context as normal context, then supervised tuning and preference optimization can focus more on reasoning quality and less on basic cross-modal translation.

This matters because post-training is more effective when the base interface is stable. InternVL3 also layers in longer-context handling through fractional V2PE, then adds SFT, MPO, and Best-of-N selection at inference. The teaching point is not the exact recipe. It is that a cleaner interface at pretraining time can simplify everything that follows.

The evidence in the compact input is consistent with that story. InternVL3-78B reaches 72.2 on MMMU, and the same stack reports 906 on OCRBench with 96.0 and 98.6 on VCR. MPO also improves overall reasoning from 50.5 to 54.6. Those numbers do not prove a universal recipe, but they do support the claim that native multimodal learning gives post-training a stronger base to build on.

Spending Compute Without Wasting It

Once the interface is good enough to trust, the next question is how to buy more reasoning without letting cost explode. This is where staged post-training becomes important. Instead of treating reinforcement learning as one monolithic step, a staged recipe can use supervised fine-tuning for initial alignment, an offline warm-up to stabilize reward learning, and an online phase to keep improving difficult reasoning behavior.

InternVL3.5 is a clean example of that idea. Its lesson is not just “use RL.” The lesson is that reasoning, token budget, and deployment should be optimized together. If the model explores more deeply but still processes every image at full visual cost, latency stays high. If it compresses visual tokens too aggressively, perception fails. If it optimizes only for reward, other capabilities can regress.

That is why the paper combines cascade RL with adaptive visual token routing and decoupled vision-language serving. The conceptual move is to keep full detail only where it matters, compress redundant detail where it does not, and avoid serial execution when vision and language can be separated operationally. The reported results anchor the tradeoff: cascade RL improves overall reasoning by up to 16.0% over InternVL3, and the system stack reaches up to 4.05x throughput speedup at high resolution. The important point is that faster serving is not treated as an afterthought; it is part of the post-training design.

When Post-Training Does Most of the Heavy Lifting

A different lesson appears when the base model is relatively compact. In that setting, post-training is not just refinement. It may be the main source of capability. Step3-VL-10B shows this through a sequence of text-heavy SFT, multimodal SFT, PPO with verifiable rewards, PPO with preference rewards, and then parallel coordinated reasoning at inference.

The key concept is that reasoning and perception can be improved in stages rather than all at once. First teach the model the task format. Then reward what can be checked. Then let the model consider multiple hypotheses before committing to one answer. That last step matters in multimodal settings because single-pass decoding can lock onto a weak visual interpretation too early.

What Still Looks Unsettled

Several open questions remain. It is still unclear how far native multimodal pretraining scales before grounding quality plateaus, or how to choose the best text-versus-multimodal data mixture across domains. On the post-training side, staged RL helps, but the field still lacks a clean rule for how much reward optimization can be applied before hallucination, perception, or other capabilities start to drift.

There is also no settled answer to the latency problem. Best-of-N reranking, parallel synthesis, and adaptive visual compression all spend compute in different places. The open systems question is not simply which one is best. It is how to allocate compute across context length, candidate generation, routing, and serving topology for different deployment budgets.

What to Remember

Multimodal post-training is really about building a reliable reasoning interface on top of perceptual input. The family does not share one algorithm, and it should not be described that way. What it shares is a set of bottlenecks: getting modalities aligned early enough, preserving the right visual facts through reasoning, and spending extra compute where it improves answers rather than just increasing latency.

If there is one durable takeaway, it is this: better multimodal models do not come only from seeing more images or adding more RL. They come from carefully choosing where alignment happens, what the loss emphasizes, and how post-training and inference divide the work of reasoning.
