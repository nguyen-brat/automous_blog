CLIP-KD: Distilling CLIP Models with Knowledge Distillation

CLIP-KD is easiest to understand as a way to shrink CLIP without asking the student to forget what makes CLIP useful in the first place. The student still learns the normal image-text contrastive task. The teacher simply adds extra pressure on top: make the student’s final embedding space look more like the teacher’s space.

That choice matters because CLIP-KD does not depend on same-architecture weight transfer. The paper is willing to distill across different model families, so it avoids the fragile idea that a student must inherit the teacher’s internal layers. Instead, it focuses on the output geometry where image and text alignment actually shows up.

Think of the teacher as a map of semantic neighborhoods. If the teacher puts a dog photo near captions about pets and far from captions about machinery, the student should land in the same region. CLIP-KD asks several different questions about that map: how close are the final embeddings, how similar is the batch-level ranking structure, how do the gradients move, and what happens if the student and teacher compare each other directly during contrastive training?

Here is the training flow in plain language:

The Overall CLIP-KD objective

The outer recipe is simple: keep the original CLIP objective, then add one weighted distillation loss. The CLIP part keeps the student honest about the image-text task. The distillation part nudges the student toward the teacher’s representation geometry. That balance is important because the student should not become a copy machine that forgets the base contrastive objective.

This is also where the paper’s main design philosophy becomes visible. The method does not try to replace CLIP training with something entirely new. It preserves the original training loop and uses the teacher as a guide for how the final embeddings should organize themselves.

Matching the shape of the space

One branch of the paper is relation-based rather than vector-based. Contrastive Relational Distillation asks the student to preserve the teacher’s similarity structure inside a batch: which texts look closest to a given image, which images look closest to a given text, and how sharply the teacher separates them from the rest.

What to notice in the image is that the student is not matching one output vector to one output vector. It is matching a whole pattern of relationships. If the teacher ranks one caption high and the others low for a given image, the student is pushed to keep the same ranking. That is why the method is called relational: it preserves the structure around the embeddings, not just the embeddings themselves.

This matters because CLIP is not only about getting a single pair close together. It is about shaping a joint space where the right image-text pairs are consistently nearer to each other than the wrong ones. CRD tries to preserve that shape directly.

Why feature distillation is the strongest simple baseline

The simplest option is Feature Distillation. Here the student directly mimics the teacher’s final image and text embeddings with mean-squared error. If the embedding widths differ, the student passes through a small projection head first so the vectors live in the same dimensional space.

That sounds almost too plain to be the main result, but the paper’s ablation says otherwise: direct feature matching is the strongest single distillation loss. The reason is intuitive. If the student’s final representation sits very close to the teacher’s representation, then downstream retrieval and zero-shot behavior have a better chance of lining up too.

This is the most important mental shift in the paper. The student does not need to reproduce the teacher’s internal wiring perfectly. It mainly needs to land in the right place at the end of the forward pass.

Interactive contrastive learning: let the student learn inside the teacher’s space

Interactive Contrastive Learning goes one step beyond imitation. Instead of using the student only as the thing being corrected, it makes the student act as the anchor in a contrastive game against the teacher’s embeddings.

In one direction, the student image embedding is compared against the teacher’s text embeddings. In the other direction, the student text embedding is compared against the teacher’s image embeddings. So the student is not only trying to be close to the teacher; it is trying to discriminate correctly inside the teacher’s embedding world.

You can think of this as a cross-model version of CLIP training:

The appendix gives this a more formal interpretation by connecting the loss to a lower bound on mutual information. The plain-English reading is simpler: ICL encourages the student to keep more of the informative structure that the teacher already knows.

This is why ICL is a strong companion to feature distillation. Feature MSE says, “move to the teacher’s current location.” ICL says, “also learn how the teacher separates the right pairs from the wrong ones.”

The other variants the paper tries

The paper explores a few more output-level signals, but they all play supporting roles rather than stealing the show.

  • Masked Feature Distillation gives the student a masked image and still asks it to match the teacher’s final features. The intuition is borrowed from masked image modeling: if the student can recover semantics from partial input, the representation may become more robust. In practice, the paper finds it behaves very similarly to plain feature distillation.

  • Gradient Distillation tries to match how the CLIP loss changes with respect to the embeddings. Instead of copying the teacher’s current output, it copies the teacher’s update direction. That is a more indirect signal, and the paper reports that it is weaker than direct feature alignment.

  • Augmented Feature Distillation fuses teacher and student embeddings through a small projection layer before applying the standard CLIP loss. This is a neat hybrid idea because it lets the teacher and student interact before the contrastive step, but it still does not beat the simpler feature-plus-ICL recipe.

Taken together, these variants test the same core question from different angles: what kind of teacher signal best preserves the final joint embedding space?

Why the paper ends up favoring simple feature alignment

The experiments answer that question pretty clearly. Feature Distillation is the strongest individual method. Interactive Contrastive Learning is the next most useful ingredient. When the paper combines them, the student improves further, and adding Contrastive Relational Distillation gives a small additional gain in many settings.

That pattern says something important about CLIP compression. The best gains come from directly narrowing the teacher-student feature gap, then adding one more signal that preserves contrastive structure. More elaborate losses can help, but they are not the main source of improvement.

The paper’s best-known anchor result makes the point concrete: a ViT-B/16 student distilled from a LAION-400M teacher reaches 57.5% zero-shot ImageNet top-1. The same general recipe also transfers across heterogeneous pairs, including ViT-to-CNN cases, because the losses act on final embeddings instead of hidden-layer alignment.

Where the method gets harder

CLIP-KD is flexible, but it is not frictionless.

  • Some gains depend on choosing a sensible weight for the distillation term. Too little guidance and the student stays far from the teacher; too much and the student can overfit the teacher’s quirks.
  • A much larger teacher is not always easier to distill. The paper notes that very large teacher-student capacity gaps can make alignment harder.
  • Intermediate-feature distillation is less robust when the teacher and student have different architectures, which is exactly why the paper leans on final-output matching instead.

So the method is architecture-agnostic, but not capacity-gap-proof. That is the right boundary to remember.

What to remember

The cleanest mental model is this:

  • The teacher is a map of the final CLIP embedding space.
  • Feature Distillation is the simplest way to copy that map.
  • Interactive Contrastive Learning adds a second signal by making the student compare itself against teacher embeddings.
  • Contrastive Relational Distillation preserves the batch-level shape of similarity, not just individual vectors.

If you remember one sentence, make it this one: CLIP-KD keeps the original CLIP task, then compresses the teacher’s embedding geometry into a smaller student without requiring the same architecture.
