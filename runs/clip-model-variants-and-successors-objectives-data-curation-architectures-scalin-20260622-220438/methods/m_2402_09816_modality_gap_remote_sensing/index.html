Mind the Modality Gap: Aligning CLIP with Remote Sensing Modalities

CLIP is strong when the world looks like internet photos and the label can be expressed in natural language. Remote sensing breaks both assumptions. Satellite scenes often come with a different visual distribution, and the useful signal may live in multispectral bands rather than RGB alone. This paper’s answer is not to retrain a new model from scratch. It is to repair CLIP in two steps: first patch its vision side so it stops being miscalibrated on satellite imagery, then teach a new satellite encoder to land in that repaired space.

That order is the whole method. If you try to align a satellite encoder to the original CLIP space too early, you inherit the same domain mismatch that caused CLIP to struggle in the first place. The paper treats that as the real bug: the teacher itself needs calibration before it can teach the student.

Why the paper starts with RGB, even though the final goal is multispectral

The method begins with labeled remote sensing RGB composites, not because RGB is the end goal, but because RGB is the bridge that lets CLIP adapt without losing its general geometry.

Here is the first stage in one picture.

What to notice is the logic of the patch. The model does not learn a brand-new classifier head. Instead, the CLIP text tower acts as a frozen label space, so the image encoder is nudged toward the right satellite classes without abandoning CLIP’s semantic vocabulary. Then the paper does something even more important: it does not keep only the fine-tuned weights. It blends the original zero-shot weights with the adapted weights.

That interpolation is the safety valve. Pure fine-tuning would move CLIP too far toward the satellite proxy task and risk damaging the broad behavior that made it useful in the first place. Pure zero-shot CLIP is too weak on remote sensing. The blend keeps both goals in play.

In practice, the paper uses BigEarthNet as the patching proxy because it is the most effective of the tested choices. The broader lesson is easy to remember: a good proxy is not just “satellite data.” It is a proxy large and diverse enough to move the model, but still close enough to the target domain to expose the right mismatch.

The result is a repaired CLIP image encoder that is still recognizably CLIP, but less surprised by satellite imagery. That repaired encoder becomes the teacher in stage two.

The second stage is teacher-student transfer across modalities

Once CLIP has been patched, the paper turns to the real target: multispectral remote sensing. The goal is to make a satellite modality encoder live in the same embedding space as the patched CLIP image encoder and the CLIP text encoder.

This is the second key picture.

The student here is a pre-trained satellite encoder. The teacher is the patched CLIP visual pathway, with the text tower still serving as the frozen class anchor. If the student and teacher produce vectors of different sizes, the paper adds a small linear projection so they can be compared in the same space.

The training signal has two jobs.

One job is geometric: make the satellite encoder’s embedding look like the patched CLIP embedding for the same scene. This keeps the student tied to the repaired representation instead of drifting into its own private space.

The other job is semantic: keep the class prediction anchored to the CLIP text tower. This is what stops the model from becoming a pure mimic of the teacher geometry with no class awareness.

The paper’s ablation is useful because it shows why both jobs matter. If you use only the class term, the student leans too much toward the text side. If you use only the embedding-matching term, it leans too much toward the image side. The combination works better because it keeps the student close to the repaired CLIP space while still preserving label supervision.

That is the most important technical idea in the second stage. The method is not trying to invent a new satellite-specific head. It is trying to make multispectral data land in a CLIP-like space that is already calibrated for remote sensing labels.

Why the order matters

The stage order is not cosmetic. It is the reason the method works at all.

Directly aligning a multispectral encoder to raw CLIP would be risky because raw CLIP is still underfit to the satellite domain. The student would then learn from a teacher whose geometry is already distorted for the task. Patching first fixes that problem. Only after the teacher’s image space has been moved closer to the target domain does it make sense to distill a new modality into it.

You can think of it like tuning a map before using that map to guide another traveler. If the map is inaccurate, faithfully copying it is not helpful. The first stage corrects the map. The second stage teaches a new traveler to follow it.

What the paper is really buying with this design

The design buys three things at once.

First, it improves satellite classification without needing task-specific heads. The paper reports that patched CLIP reaches large gains on BigEarthNet variants while staying close to its ImageNet behavior, so the repair is not just memorization of one niche dataset.

Second, it extends the model beyond RGB. The aligned satellite encoder can use multispectral inputs, which means the method is not limited to what a human can see in a true-color composite.

Third, it keeps CLIP’s shared semantic space useful for zero-shot classification and retrieval. That is the point of doing the repair through CLIP rather than building a separate satellite backbone from scratch.

The headline numbers are large enough to remember. On BigEarthNet-19, the patched ViT-L-14 reaches 72.04 mAP, while ImageNet stays at 74.22 acc. That is the pattern the paper wants: domain-specific repair without erasing general behavior. The aligned encoder then improves zero-shot classification and retrieval, and when frozen and linearly probed it beats several remote-sensing foundation models.

Where the method is strongest

The paper’s ablations say the proxy dataset matters a lot.

BigEarthNet is the best patching choice among the tested options. EuroSAT is smaller and less effective as a proxy; SEN12MS helps, but not as much as BigEarthNet. The paper’s reading of this is straightforward: the patching dataset needs enough scale and class diversity to move CLIP in a meaningful direction.

The multispectral ablations tell a similar story. Using the full Sentinel-2 signal works better than using only the RGB bands. That is important because it shows the student is not simply reconstructing the visible-color composite. It is learning to exploit information beyond RGB, which is the whole point of doing remote sensing adaptation in the first place.

The paper also checks Sentinel-1 as an additional modality. The same pattern appears there: patching first helps, and patching with the same modality is best. That makes the method feel less like a Sentinel-2 trick and more like a reusable recipe for other remote sensing sensors.

Where it is still fragile

The paper is honest about the limits.

It still depends on labeled satellite data for the patching stage and paired modality data for the alignment stage. So this is not a fully label-free or caption-free solution in the broadest sense.

It also uses BigEarthNet as the main proxy, which means generalization across all remote sensing tasks is still open. The paper explicitly notes that EuroSAT is weaker under the chosen alignment objective, and that suggests the label loss may be better suited to BigEarthNet’s multi-label structure than to simpler multiclass tasks.

A larger satellite encoder might help, and the authors say so themselves. They also suggest that patching and alignment over multiple datasets could improve generality. So the method is strong, but not the final word on remote-sensing CLIP.

What to remember

The easiest way to remember this paper is as a two-step repair.

Patch CLIP first so its image space stops being badly miscalibrated on satellite data. Then distill a multispectral encoder into that repaired space, using the text tower as a frozen semantic anchor and the patched image encoder as the geometric teacher.

That is why the method matters. It does not just make CLIP look better on remote sensing. It shows a practical pattern for domain adaptation: fix the foundation model’s visual space first, then teach new modalities to speak that repaired language.
