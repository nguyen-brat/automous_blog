FLAIR: Fine-grained Language-informed Image Representations

FLAIR is easiest to understand as CLIP with a caption-controlled spotlight. Standard CLIP-style models compress an image into one global vector and then compare that vector with text. That works well when the question is broad, but it is too blunt when the text cares about a small object, a part of an object, or a local scene detail. FLAIR keeps the familiar dual-encoder setup, but changes one crucial step: the caption does not just supervise the image. It helps decide which local image tokens should be pooled into the image representation in the first place.

That is the core idea to keep in mind while reading the rest of the method. The paper is not trying to replace CLIP with a heavier multimodal model. It is trying to make the CLIP-style interface more precise by letting language choose the visual evidence that matters.

The paper’s opening teaser makes the failure mode concrete.

What matters in this image is not the exact color of the heatmap. What matters is the difference in behavior. Older models can still recognize the scene, but they do not consistently light up the exact patch that supports the word in the prompt. FLAIR is aiming for a sharper kind of correspondence: the caption should point at the right visual evidence, not just agree with the image in a global sense.

Why one global vector is too blunt

The problem FLAIR attacks is simple to state. A single image vector has to summarize everything at once, so local details get averaged away. That is fine if the caption only says something broad like “a person in a room.” It is much less fine if the caption asks about the mug on the table, the raised paw of the dog, the color of the truck, or the object in the background.

Once you think about it that way, the paper’s motivation becomes very natural. Retrieval is often not really about matching whole scenes. It is about matching the small detail that distinguishes one image from a near-neighbor. Segmentation is even more direct: it literally asks the model to keep enough local structure around that a prompt can recover a region.

FLAIR’s answer is to stop treating the image as one undifferentiated summary too early. Instead, it keeps local image tokens alive and lets the text query decide which of those tokens should be pooled.

Long captions become many smaller training views

The data side of the method is just as important as the architecture side. FLAIR trains on long synthetic captions generated for each image, then breaks those long descriptions into smaller sub-captions. Each image is paired with several sampled sub-captions, and those sub-captions vary in granularity.

A short sub-caption tends to focus on one local detail. A longer one can describe several parts of the scene at once. That is why the paper samples both narrow and broad views instead of using one fixed caption length. The same image might be described once by a sentence about a specific object and again by a sentence that gives broader scene context.

That sampling strategy matters because it teaches the model that there is no single correct level of description. Sometimes the right query is a tiny detail. Sometimes it is the whole scene. FLAIR wants to be good at both.

The paper keeps the standard CLIP text encoder shape, including the usual 77-token limit. It does not try to stretch the text tower into something heavier. Instead, it squeezes more supervision out of the captions by turning one long paragraph into many training views.

You can think of this as teaching with flashcards instead of one giant textbook page. Each flashcard asks about a different slice of the image. Together, they force the model to remember both the scene and the parts.

Let the caption choose the patches

This is the architectural pivot. The image encoder still produces local patch tokens and a global image token. The text encoder still produces a caption embedding. But FLAIR changes who gets to ask the question. The caption embedding becomes the query, and the image patch tokens become the things being selected and pooled.

What to notice here is the direction of control. Vanilla CLIP compares one global image vector with one global text vector. SigLIP still uses a learned image token to pull information from the patch grid. FLAIR flips that relationship. The text tells the image what to keep.

That is why the method is so useful for fine-grained grounding. The final image representation is no longer “what does this image mean in general?” It becomes “which parts of this image matter for this caption?”

Equation 1 is the formal version of that story. The caption embedding plays the role of the query, the patch features provide the candidates, and attention weights turn the relevant patches into one caption-specific image vector. The exact algebra is not the important part. The important part is the direction of information flow: language selects the visual evidence before the comparison is made.

FLAIR also adds an empty token to the patch set. That is a small but practical detail. It gives the pooling layer an escape hatch when the caption and the image do not line up cleanly. Instead of forcing a bad visual match, the model can fall back to “nothing relevant here.”

Multi-head attention makes the pooling more flexible. Different heads can focus on different parts of the image at the same time, which is useful when one caption refers to more than one object or when a scene has both a main subject and a supporting context.

The full training loop

Once you put the data and the pooling together, the whole method is easy to say in one breath.

Figure 3 is the cleanest way to see how the pieces fit together. The sampled captions feed both the local pooling path and the global alignment path. That is the real design choice in the paper: fine-grained grounding should not replace coarse alignment. It should sit on top of it.

The local loss is the first half of that training signal. FLAIR uses a sigmoid-style contrastive loss for the text-conditioned image embeddings. That choice fits the data structure better than a one-positive-only softmax objective, because each image has multiple valid sub-captions. A single image can legitimately match many text views, so the loss should not pretend there is only one correct caption.

The negative pairs are the subtle part. If the model can solve the task by looking only at the text that produced the query, then the image side stops mattering. FLAIR avoids that shortcut by choosing negatives that make the conditioned image embedding fail when the image content does not actually support the caption. In plain language: the image has to earn the match; the caption alone is not enough.

That design choice is easy to miss, but it is central to the method. Without careful negatives, the model could become good at comparing text to text and still remain weak at grounding.

The second half of the training signal is the multi-positive global sigmoid loss. This keeps the standard global image token aligned with every sampled sub-caption from the same image. That global anchor matters because retrieval still benefits from a coarse shared space, not just from local detail. If you only optimized the caption-specific pooled embeddings, you might get sharper grounding but lose the broad alignment that makes the dual encoder useful.

So the final objective is really a balance. One part says, “use the caption to pull out the right patches.” The other part says, “do not let the whole image space drift away from its global text meaning.”

What the learned representation is good for

After training, the local image tokens are much more semantically useful than in a standard global CLIP model. That shows up first in retrieval. For image-to-text retrieval, the model conditions each image on candidate texts before scoring them. That is more expensive than a pure global dot-product comparison, but it is also more precise when the text is fine grained.

The same local detail is what makes the segmentation results work. Because the image tokens have been trained to stay sensitive to language, a class name or a short prompt can be compared directly against local tokens. In other words, the representation still remembers which part of the image supports which phrase.

What to notice in the attention maps is that the highlighted region follows the caption, not just the biggest object in the frame. That is the sign that the model has learned a real grounding behavior instead of a generic saliency map.

The paper reports that this design helps in several ways at once. Standard retrieval improves, fine-grained retrieval improves, long-caption retrieval improves, and zero-shot semantic segmentation improves a lot. At the same time, the method still trails much larger CLIP models on zero-shot classification. That tradeoff makes sense: FLAIR is optimized to preserve local evidence, not to replace the scale needed for maximum broad concept coverage.

What changed compared with earlier CLIP variants

FLAIR is not a full break from the CLIP family. It keeps the dual-encoder backbone and the usual retrieval-friendly structure. The change is where the detailed supervision enters.

Compared with CLIP, FLAIR does not freeze the image into one global summary before the text arrives. Compared with SigLIP, it does not ask a learned image token to organize the patch grid by itself. Compared with recaptioning-only methods like DreamLIP, it makes the image-text interaction direct rather than indirect.

That combination is what makes the method interesting. Richer captions give the model more ways to ask questions. Text-conditioned pooling answers those questions at the patch level. The local sigmoid loss makes the answer depend on the image. The multi-positive global loss keeps the whole space useful for ordinary retrieval.

If you want one simple mental model, it is this: FLAIR turns the caption into a query that decides which image tokens matter, and then it trains that query-specific image representation without forgetting the global image-text map.

What to remember

The simplest way to remember FLAIR is to remember the order of operations. Do not collapse the image first and ask language to work around the loss of detail. Keep the local tokens, let the caption select the patches that matter, and train that caption-specific representation alongside a global alignment term.

Everything else in the paper supports that idea. Long synthetic captions give the model many different granular views of the same image. Text-conditioned attention pooling turns those views into grounded visual features. The local loss forces the grounding to depend on the actual image content. The global loss keeps coarse retrieval from falling apart.

The tradeoff is that this is not free. Multiple sampled captions mean more text processing, and the best results depend on long recaptioned data. But as a fix for lost local detail, the method is very clean. If the goal is fine-grained alignment, let the text query decide which image tokens matter instead of compressing the whole image into one vector too early.
