WebPerson and GA-DMS for Person Representation Learning

This paper fixes a very specific CLIP problem: person retrieval depends on tiny visual clues, but web captions are noisy and CLIP’s default training treats the whole caption as if every word mattered equally. That is a bad fit for person search, where the difference between two images can come down to a sleeve color, a bag, a hairstyle, or a small accessory.

The paper answers that problem in two moves. First, it builds WebPerson, a large person-centric web dataset with filtered images and structured synthetic captions. Second, it trains a CLIP-style dual encoder with GA-DMS, a token-aware objective that learns which words to suppress and which words to reconstruct.

That is the paper in one line: clean the data, identify the useful words, and make the model spend its effort on those words.

Building WebPerson from the web

WebPerson starts from the huge COYO700M web corpus, but the paper does not trust the raw web distribution. It first asks a simpler question: is there actually a usable person in the image?

The filtering stage keeps images where the person is large enough, the body shape looks like a person image rather than a random scene crop, the detector is confident, and the pose is visible enough to support captioning. The point is not just to remove false positives. It is to avoid wasting the later captioning step on images that are too tiny, too partial, or too ambiguous to describe reliably.

What to notice here is that the pipeline does two kinds of curation at once. It filters the image side, then it normalizes the text side.

The text pipeline starts from existing person-retrieval captions and rewrites them into structured templates. Fine-grained attributes such as clothing, hair, and accessories are replaced with generic slots like colored top, hairstyle, or accessory. Then the paper clusters similar templates, keeps representative ones, and synthesizes new variants so the caption space stays diverse instead of collapsing into a few repetitive patterns.

That matters because the final captions are not meant to sound artistic. They are meant to be controlled. The prompt tells the captioning model to describe only visually confirmed person attributes and to ignore background chatter. In other words, the captions are deliberately structured so the later training objective can tell the difference between helpful words and noise.

So WebPerson is not just a bigger corpus. It is a person-centric corpus with text that is shaped for retrieval.

Why GA-DMS needs token-aware scoring

Even with better captions, person retrieval is still a fine-grained matching problem. A model has to know that words like black jacket, red backpack, or ponytail matter more than words about the street, lighting, or general scene. Standard contrastive learning does not tell the model which words deserve that trust.

GA-DMS adds that missing signal. It assigns each token a gradient-attention similarity score, which is easiest to think of as a two-part vote. The gradient says how much a token affected the image-text match. The attention says whether the text encoder was already using that token when it formed the sentence representation. If both signals agree, the token is probably important. If both are weak, the token is probably background or noise.

What to notice in this figure is not the box layout itself, but the control loop. The same token score drives masking, reconstruction, and alignment. That is why the framework is called synergetic: the score is not just a diagnostic, it is the mechanism.

The score is built from the later text layers rather than the earliest ones, because the later layers still carry the alignment signal the model needs. The paper also adds multi-scale pooling inside the token scorer, so a token is not judged in isolation. It sees a small neighborhood around itself first, which helps for short attribute phrases where meaning is spread across a few adjacent words.

That design is important for captions like “a person in a striped shirt with a bag.” The useful evidence is not just one token. It is the phrase-level attribute. The scorer needs to notice that phrase as a unit.

Two masks, two jobs

Once the paper has token scores, it uses them in two different ways.

The first branch is noise token masking. Tokens with low scores are more likely to be replaced with a mask token. This is the paper’s way of pushing irrelevant words out of the learner’s attention. If a caption contains a lot of extra scene language or unhelpful phrasing, those tokens should not dominate training.

The second branch is masked informative token prediction. Here the paper does the opposite on purpose: it targets the high-scoring tokens, masks them, and asks the model to recover them from the image and the surrounding text context. This is how the model learns that the important attribute words are not just words to keep, but words it should be able to ground in the visual content.

That split is the key intuition behind the method.

  • Noise masking says, “stop trusting the weak words.”
  • Informative token prediction says, “now prove you can recover the strong words from the image.”

If you only do the first part, the model can ignore noise but still stay shallow. If you only do the second part, the model may learn a harder reconstruction task without ever being told what to suppress. Together, the two masks force the model to separate signal from clutter.

The reconstruction side uses a small cross-modal decoder: a cross-attention bridge followed by a few Transformer layers and a final prediction head. The decoder is not there to generate fluent captions. It is there to let the image help recover the missing attribute word.

How the losses fit together

The alignment loss is Similarity Distribution Matching, or SDM. The easiest way to understand SDM is to compare it with ordinary contrastive learning. Standard contrastive training mostly asks whether the right image-text pair is ranked above the wrong ones. SDM asks for something a little richer: it wants the whole similarity distribution across the batch to resemble the ground-truth matching pattern.

That matters when captions are noisy, because a hard yes-or-no separation can be brittle. SDM gives the model a softer and more structured target. The paper applies it in both directions, image to text and text to image, so both towers learn the same shared geometry.

The second loss is masked token prediction. This is the reconstruction objective on the informative-token branch. It forces the model to infer the missing attribute words from the visual evidence instead of letting those words remain passive inputs.

The final objective simply combines the two pressures: keep the retrieval space aligned, and spend extra effort recovering the tokens that actually carry person identity. The ablation results back up that design. The gradient-attention score is better than a plain similarity score, and the full combination of GASS, SDM, and MTP is the strongest masking variant.

What changes compared with plain CLIP

Plain CLIP treats captions as a single blob. WebPerson plus GA-DMS breaks that blob into two kinds of text.

  • Low-value tokens are pushed out of the way.
  • High-value tokens become reconstruction targets.

That is why this is more than a caption-augmentation trick. The paper does improve the data, but it also changes the training signal so the model has to care about the right words. The result is a CLIP backbone that becomes much better at person-specific matching without abandoning the dual-encoder setup.

The reported improvements show that the strategy works. With 5M WebPerson pretraining, the CLIP backbone reaches 77.60 Rank-1 on CUHK-PEDES, 69.51 on ICFG-PEDES, and 71.25 on RSTPReid. The more interesting trend is that performance keeps rising as the dataset grows, which says the curation pipeline is not just a one-off patch. It scales.

The tradeoff

The method is not free. It depends on web image quality, synthetic caption fidelity, and enough compute to build and train on a 5M-scale corpus. It is also specialized for person-centric retrieval, where the useful information really is concentrated in a small set of attributes.

That specialization is part of the reason it works. The method assumes that the important clues are local and textual noise is common. If those assumptions fail, the masking strategy is less compelling.

What to remember

WebPerson and GA-DMS is best remembered as a two-stage repair for person retrieval.

  1. Build a large person-centric dataset from the web, but filter it hard and force the captions into a controlled structure.
  2. Train CLIP with token-aware masking so noisy words are suppressed and useful attribute words are reconstructed.

The deeper lesson is simple: for fine-grained retrieval, better captions are not enough. The training objective also has to learn which words matter.
