CLIP-ViP: Adapting CLIP for Video-Text Retrieval via Video Proxy and Omnisource Cross-Modal Learning

CLIP-ViP is a careful way to turn a strong image-text model into a better video-text model without throwing away what CLIP already knows. That is the key design choice. The paper starts from a simple diagnosis: if you keep post-pretraining CLIP on video data in the most obvious way, the model can overfit quickly, and the subtitle-style text that often comes with video is not very close to the descriptive captions used in retrieval tasks.

So the method fixes two interfaces instead of rebuilding the whole system. On the text side, it adds captions that sound more like downstream captions. On the vision side, it gives the ViT a light temporal bridge so video can be handled with minimal architectural disruption.

Why the paper starts with those two problems

The paper is not trying to argue that CLIP is weak. It is trying to explain why CLIP does not automatically become a good video model just because you keep training it on video-text pairs.

The first problem is scale. Small or medium video corpora can make post-pretraining unstable. The model starts to memorize the new data instead of preserving the general visual-language geometry it learned from image-text pretraining.

The second problem is language mismatch. Subtitles are often conversational, incomplete, or only loosely tied to what is visible on screen. Downstream retrieval captions are usually more direct and descriptive. The paper checks that this mismatch is real rather than assumed, and that is why the generated captions matter so much.

CLIP-ViP is built around a practical belief: if the base encoder already knows rich visual concepts, the safest way forward is to repair the parts of the interface that video exposes.

The whole method at a glance

What to notice in the figure is the separation of concerns.

The text side does not rely on one text source. It can use subtitles, generated captions for the whole video, and captions for individual frames. The vision side does not replace CLIP with a brand-new video backbone. It keeps the ViT backbone and inserts proxy tokens so temporal context has a place to live.

That is the entire philosophy of the paper in one picture: keep the dual-encoder structure, but make both sides more video-friendly.

Fixing the text side first

The simplest repair is also one of the most important. CLIP-ViP generates auxiliary captions from the middle frame of each video using an image captioner.

That choice does two jobs at once.

First, it avoids the leakage risk that comes with training a video captioning model on video-text datasets that later appear downstream. The paper wants the post-pretraining stage to stay agnostic to the evaluation set.

Second, it changes the style of supervision. The new captions look more like the descriptive text a retrieval system will see later. They are less like noisy subtitles and more like compact visual descriptions.

That matters because post-pretraining is not just about seeing more video. It is about seeing video with text that points the model in the right semantic direction. If the text source is too far from the target language, the model can learn the wrong habits very efficiently.

The generated captions therefore act like a language bridge. They do not replace subtitles, but they reduce the gap between video pretraining and downstream caption-style retrieval.

Giving the ViT a video-friendly memory

The second repair happens inside the vision encoder.

Think of the proxy tokens as a small conference table added to the ViT. Each frame is still split into patches. Those patches still carry spatial and temporal position information. But instead of forcing every patch from every frame to talk directly to every other patch, CLIP-ViP inserts a small set of learned proxy tokens that can collect and redistribute global clip context.

Here is the important rule:

  • proxy tokens can attend to everything,
  • ordinary patch tokens can attend to tokens in their own frame and to the proxy tokens,
  • patch tokens do not get unrestricted access to every patch in every frame.

That mask is the clever part. Full attention across all patches would ask the image-pretrained ViT to learn a very different interaction pattern right away. The paper finds that this hurts. The proxy tokens give the model a gentler path: local patches stay mostly local, while the proxies carry the global temporal summary.

At the output, the first proxy token is used as the video representation. So the proxies are not decorative. They are the place where clip-level information is meant to accumulate.

The same trick also makes joint image-video training easier. A still image can be treated as a special one-frame video by using an interpolated temporal position, which lets the same encoder path handle both images and videos in the same batch.

That is a subtle but useful design choice. It keeps CLIP compatibility high while still adding the video structure the backbone needs.

How the cross-modal training is mixed

Once the encoder can handle video, the training problem becomes: which pairs should be aligned together?

CLIP-ViP uses omnsource cross-modal learning, which is just a careful way of saying that multiple text and visual source pairs are trained together instead of one source at a time.

The main pair types are:

  • video with subtitle text,
  • video with generated caption text,
  • frame with generated caption text.

The core idea is plain: one video should be allowed to match more than one valid description, because subtitles and generated captions each capture different parts of the semantics. The subtitle may carry the conversational context. The generated caption may carry the downstream-style visual description. Training on both gives the model a richer target.

The contrastive objective then does the usual retrieval job. Matched pairs are pulled together in embedding space, and mismatched pairs in the batch are pushed apart. What changes in CLIP-ViP is the source mixture, not the basic retrieval philosophy.

The strongest variant is the one that treats subtitles and captions together instead of forcing them into separate silos. In practical terms, that makes the batch more informative. The same video gets several good text views, and the wrong text pairs become more useful negatives. The embedding space becomes less brittle because it has to respect more than one correct description of the same content.

Why the ablations matter

The ablation story is easy to remember because it lines up with the intuition.

Mean pooling is the cheapest video adaptation. It is stable, but it mostly ignores temporal structure.

Full attention across all frame patches sounds more powerful, but the paper shows that it can be worse. That is the cost of asking an image-pretrained transformer to adopt a completely different interaction pattern too aggressively.

Proxy tokens sit in the middle. They add just enough temporal communication to matter, while keeping the backbone close to the original CLIP behavior. That is why the proxy-token version wins.

Then the text-side changes stack on top of that. Once generated captions are added and the model is trained across multiple source pairs, the gains grow again. The method is not winning because of one magic trick. It wins because both sources are repaired at the same time.

The tradeoff the paper accepts

CLIP-ViP is still a post-pretraining method, so it depends on large-scale video data and an auxiliary caption-generation pipeline. That makes it less like a tiny plug-in and more like a careful adaptation recipe.

The upside is that it preserves the strengths of CLIP. It does not discard the pretrained visual space, and it does not force a heavyweight video backbone to relearn everything from scratch. The method is most convincing on retrieval-style video-language tasks, where shared embeddings and contrastive training are exactly the right tools.

What to remember

CLIP-ViP is best understood as a two-part repair job. The text side gets better because generated captions sound more like downstream descriptions. The vision side gets better because a few proxy tokens give the encoder a place to store temporal context without breaking CLIP’s pretrained structure.

That is the takeaway to keep in mind: when a pretrained dual encoder struggles on video, the solution may be less about adding a bigger video model and more about fixing the text mismatch and adding a very light temporal bridge.
