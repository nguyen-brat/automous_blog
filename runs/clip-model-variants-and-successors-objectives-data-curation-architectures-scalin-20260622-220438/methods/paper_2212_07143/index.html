Scaling Laws for Large-Scale Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training

This paper is not trying to invent a new CLIP recipe. It is trying to answer a cleaner question: if you keep the CLIP-style contrastive setup, but scale the model, the data, and the training budget together, what kind of improvement do you actually get on downstream tasks?

That sounds simple, but it is the right question for a scaling study. The paper is really a map of how a shared image-text embedding space grows when you give it more capacity, more data, and more time. The interesting part is that the answer is not the same for every task, and not the same for every training dataset.

The best way to remember the paper is this: scale helps, but scale is not one knob. Model size, dataset size, and training length interact, and the dataset source can tilt the curve toward classification or retrieval.

A controlled way to grow CLIP

The method stays close to the original CLIP setup on purpose. The authors use OpenCLIP, train with the familiar contrastive objective, and keep the dual-encoder structure intact. What changes is the scale axis.

They vary three things together:

  • model size, from ViT-B/32 up through ViT-G/14,
  • data scale, using LAION-80M, LAION-400M, and LAION-2B,
  • training length, measured as 3B, 13B, and 34B samples seen.

That gives them a way to ask whether a larger model only helps if it gets enough data, whether a larger dataset only helps if training lasts long enough, and whether the same recipe behaves differently when the underlying data distribution changes.

To keep the comparison meaningful, they do not let the recipe drift wildly while they scale. They keep the architecture family fixed, adapt the learning schedule to the run length, use large distributed batches, and run control experiments to check that batch size changes are not what is driving the curves. When the larger models become unstable in float16, they switch to bfloat16 rather than changing the core objective. In other words, they repair the training system so the scaling study can stay about scaling.

That matters because a bad scaling study can easily fool you. If you only grow one axis at a time, you can hide a bottleneck and make a real improvement look flat.

The paper also does one very practical thing right: when it fits the scaling curve, it focuses on the Pareto frontier, not every single run. That means the curve is based on the best tradeoff points the system actually achieved at each compute level, which is the right way to read a scaling law.

The zero-shot curve

Figure 1 is the cleanest picture of the whole paper.

What to notice is the shape, not the exact numbers. As compute grows, error falls smoothly, and the same broad pattern appears for both zero-shot ImageNet classification and the robustness benchmarks. The paper is showing that the shared embedding space becomes more useful in a very regular way as the training scale grows.

Zero-shot classification here works the usual CLIP way: turn each class name into a small set of prompt sentences, embed those prompts with the text tower, average the prompt embeddings for a class, and compare them with the image embedding. The model predicts the class whose text vector is closest in the shared space.

That makes this test more than a benchmark trick. It checks whether the representation itself has become more aligned. If the curve improves here, it means scale is making the image-text geometry better, not just making some classifier head easier to tune.

The strongest anchor result is the largest OpenCLIP model they report, ViT-H/14 trained on LAION-2B with 34B samples seen, which reaches 78.0 percent zero-shot ImageNet top-1 accuracy. The important part is not just that the number is high. It is that it sits on the same smooth scaling trend as the smaller models, so the larger run is not a random outlier.

The paper also shows a useful bottleneck effect. If the training run is too short, a larger dataset can look unimpressive because the model has not seen enough samples for the bigger corpus to matter. Once the training length increases, the larger dataset starts paying off. That is a good reminder that scale dimensions are coupled, not independent.

Reading the same embedding space in other ways

Zero-shot classification is the most visible test, but it is not the only one. The paper uses three other lenses to see whether the representation really improves.

Retrieval

Retrieval asks a slightly different question: given a text query, can the model find the right image, and given an image, can it find the right caption?

The mechanics are simple. The model embeds images and text into the same space, then ranks candidates by similarity. The metric is recall at five, so the question is whether the correct match is near the top of the list.

This is where the paper’s data-source story starts to become interesting. On retrieval, OpenCLIP trained on LAION tends to scale better than OpenAI CLIP trained on WIT. The authors do not treat that as an architecture win, because the architectures are largely matched. They treat it as evidence that the training distribution changes what the model becomes good at.

That is the key idea behind the whole study. The model is the same style of dual encoder, but the data source bends the scaling curve in different directions.

Linear probing

Linear probing is the bluntest way to test the representation. Freeze the image encoder, throw away the fancy prompt logic, and train only a linear classifier on top of the cached features.

If the frozen features are already organized well, a simple linear head should do surprisingly well. If the embeddings are messy, the linear probe will struggle.

Figure 2 shows that scaling helps here too.

What to notice is that the gains are not limited to one benchmark or one shot regime. The paper reports better linear probe accuracy on ImageNet, CIFAR-100, and VTAB+, including few-shot and full-data settings. That matters because it says the representation itself is getting cleaner, not just the zero-shot prompt interface.

This is a nice sanity check on the zero-shot story. If the embedding space improves, a cheap linear classifier should also improve. The paper sees exactly that.

Fine-tuning

Fine-tuning is the expensive test. Instead of freezing the backbone, the model is allowed to adapt to labeled downstream data.

For ImageNet, the authors compare direct fine-tuning with a two-stage path that first adapts on an ImageNet-12k subset and then continues on ImageNet. For the smaller task set, they fine-tune a single model across eight datasets, keep the task heads initialized from the zero-shot model, and update only the vision encoder. They also use patching, which blends the fine-tuned weights back toward the zero-shot weights when fine-tuning hurts the original ImageNet behavior.

That setup is worth understanding because it shows what the method is trying to preserve. The goal is not just to memorize labels. The goal is to adapt without destroying the reusable embedding space that made CLIP valuable in the first place.

Figure 4 captures the main point.

What to notice is that scale still helps after supervised adaptation, but the slope depends on the pretraining source. Even when the model is fine-tuned, the training data it started from leaves a fingerprint on how the curve behaves.

Why LAION and WIT do not behave the same

One of the most useful parts of the paper is the comparison between OpenCLIP trained on LAION and OpenAI CLIP trained on WIT. The authors keep the architectures close and mostly follow similar recipes, so a big difference in scaling behavior is unlikely to come from the model family alone.

What they see is task-dependent. WIT-trained OpenAI CLIP scales better on zero-shot ImageNet classification. LAION-trained OpenCLIP scales better on retrieval. The paper’s interpretation is that the data distribution is doing real work here.

That interpretation is supported by the controls. Batch-size changes are small enough that they do not explain the task split, and the 400M data-scale controls suggest that LAION-400M behaves like a lower-scale version of the same broader LAION family. So the simplest explanation is also the most important one: curation and data source shape what scale buys you.

You can think of it this way. A dataset can make a model look ImageNet-friendly, or retrieval-friendly, or both, depending on what kinds of pairs it exposes and how it was filtered. The scaling law then tells you not only whether bigger helps, but which downstream behavior the data is encouraging as the model grows.

What the paper leaves open

The paper is careful about its limits. The scale grid is still sparse because compute is expensive, especially at the largest models. Hyperparameters cannot be fully tuned at every point, so some of the largest runs are only partially optimized. The downstream duplication check is simple, so it may miss some overlaps, even though the measured overlap looks too small to explain the main trends.

That means the paper is best read as a strong directional map, not as the final word on CLIP scaling. It tells you that the curve is real, that the dataset matters, and that the different downstream tasks respond differently to the same pretraining recipe. It does not claim to have exhausted every scale, every curation rule, or every possible backbone split.

The bigger open question is whether future open datasets can close the remaining gap to private-data successors without giving up reproducibility. A related question is whether separate scaling laws would emerge if the image tower and text tower were scaled independently. The paper does not answer those questions, but it gives a clean baseline for asking them.

What to remember

If you only keep three things from the paper, keep these:

  • Scaling CLIP works predictably when model size, data size, and training length grow together.
  • The data source changes the shape of the scaling curve, so classification and retrieval do not always reward the same pretraining distribution.
  • Zero-shot, retrieval, linear probing, and fine-tuning all point to the same lesson: better scale makes the shared embedding space more transferable, but not in a task-neutral way.

That is why the paper matters. It is an open, reproducible scaling study for CLIP-style pretraining, and it shows that the biggest lever is not just more compute. It is the combination of scale and data choice that decides what kind of transfer the model learns to favor.
