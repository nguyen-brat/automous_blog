CLIPTeX: Improving CLIP with Experts

CLIPTeX is a small but pointed upgrade to CLIP. The goal is not to invent a new dual encoder. The goal is to keep CLIP’s image-text alignment and add enough spatial supervision that the vision tower stops being blind to boundaries, geometry, and object layout. The paper’s idea is simple: use public task experts to generate hard pseudo-labels on web images, then train CLIP on both the usual contrastive objective and those extra task losses.

The whole method is easiest to see as a second training signal attached to the image branch.

What to notice is the split in responsibility. The image and text towers still do CLIP’s normal matching job. But the image branch also sends its features into task heads that learn from expert-generated targets. So CLIPTeX is not replacing CLIP, it is telling the image encoder to notice more than just pair identity.

That flow is the right mental model. First preserve CLIP’s alignment behavior. Then use the experts to make the image representation denser and more spatially aware.

The labels come from experts, not from new annotation work

CLIPTeX does not ask humans to draw masks, mark depths, or trace normals. Instead, it runs off-the-shelf task experts over an uncurated web-scale image-text corpus and saves their predictions as hard pseudo-labels.

That choice matters for two reasons. It keeps the method cheap in data terms, because nothing new needs to be labeled by hand. And it gives the image encoder exactly the kind of supervision CLIP usually lacks. A caption can tell you what is in the image, but it rarely teaches you where an object starts, how far it is from the camera, or how a surface is oriented. Expert pseudo-labels fill in those missing pieces.

The labels are generated offline, which is another important practical detail. The experts are not part of the training loop itself. They act like a fixed supervision cache. Once the labels exist, CLIPTeX can train with ordinary losses on top of them.

Why the image branch needs a spatial bridge

The paper is careful about one subtle problem. CLIP’s image encoder is not automatically a dense predictor. It is built to produce a useful global representation, not a rich multi-scale feature map. Dense tasks such as segmentation and depth estimation need more local structure than a single pooled vector.

That is why CLIPTeX inserts a shared multi-scale module between the image encoder and the task heads. The module gives the task branch access to features at more than one spatial scale, which helps the auxiliary tasks read object boundaries and geometry instead of only coarse semantics.

The heads themselves are deliberately lightweight, mostly point-wise convolutional heads. That design keeps the auxiliary branch from turning into a second large model. The point is to use the expert tasks as supervision, not to build a heavyweight dense-prediction system on the side.

If the head output does not match the image resolution, the paper upsamples it with nearest-neighbor interpolation. That is a practical, not glamorous, step, but it keeps the dense losses aligned with the targets without complicating the design.

Training keeps CLIP in the loop

The training objective is just a weighted blend of two things. One part is the usual CLIP contrastive loss on image-text pairs. The other parts are task losses for the pseudo-labeled dense tasks.

This balance is the core trick. If the auxiliary tasks dominated, CLIPTeX could drift away from the promptable zero-shot behavior that makes CLIP valuable. If the contrastive loss dominated, the extra supervision would be too weak to change the visual representation in a meaningful way. CLIPTeX keeps both signals active so the model learns better structure without losing the cross-modal alignment that matters downstream.

The paper uses the same high-resolution training setup for CLIP-FT and CLIPTeX when comparing them. That is important because dense tasks care about pixel detail. It means the gain over CLIP-FT is not just the effect of bigger images. It is the effect of adding expert supervision on top of a fair high-resolution baseline.

The method is also tested across different image backbones, including ViT-B/16, ViT-H/16, and ResNet-50. So the idea is not tied to one exact encoder choice.

What changes once the image encoder learns structure

The effect shows up where CLIP is weakest. Frozen probing on segmentation, detection, depth, and surface normals improves consistently after expert-guided training.

Semantic segmentation is the clearest case. On PASCAL VOC, linear probing jumps by about 10 percentage points for ViT-B/16 and 16.3 points for ViT-H/16 over CLIP-FT. ADE20K also improves, even though most of its classes were not part of the segmentation expert’s original training corpus. That is a good sign that CLIPTeX is learning more general spatial features, not just copying the expert’s label set.

The same pattern carries over to object detection, instance segmentation, depth estimation, and surface-normal estimation. Those tasks all benefit from a representation that tracks local structure and geometry. Plain image classification improves less, which also makes sense. If the task only needs one label for the whole image, the extra spatial detail matters less than it does for localization-heavy problems.

The paper checks the representation with frozen probes on purpose. That isolates the encoder quality from any big task-specific finetuning. In other words, the gains are about the backbone, not about hiding more computation inside the head.

Why the model still feels like CLIP

A common failure mode for this kind of hybrid training is catastrophic forgetting. You add a bunch of auxiliary supervision and suddenly the model is no longer good at the thing that made it useful in the first place.

CLIPTeX avoids that pretty well. Zero-shot ImageNet-1k accuracy stays close to CLIP-FT, and retrieval also remains competitive. So the model does not lose its CLIP identity. It becomes a better CLIP backbone, not a different species of model.

That preservation matters because it keeps the model versatile. The point is not to trade promptability for dense-task strength. The point is to get both, or at least most of both, from one training run.

Where the idea is still limited

The method is strong, but it is not a universal fix.

It depends on the quality and coverage of the public experts. If the pseudo-labels are noisy or too narrow, the auxiliary signal will be weaker. The paper’s own results suggest that the biggest gains appear on dense and localization-heavy tasks, which is exactly where those pseudo-labels give the most useful information.

It also does not fully solve CLIP’s resolution and backbone-bias issues. High-resolution training helps, but the architecture still matters. So the safest way to remember CLIPTeX is as expert-guided refinement, not as a final answer to CLIP’s weaknesses.

What to remember

CLIPTeX is easiest to remember as CLIP plus expert hints.

Keep the contrastive image-text objective so the model stays promptable and zero-shot friendly. Then add hard pseudo-labels from public task experts so the image encoder learns boundaries, depth, and surface geometry instead of only global pair identity. Put a shared multiscale bridge in front of lightweight heads so the auxiliary tasks can read spatial structure without taking over the whole model.

That is the whole method in one sentence. Preserve CLIP’s alignment, then force the vision tower to care about what is actually inside the image. The paper’s results show that this pays off most on dense and localization-heavy tasks, which is exactly where standard CLIP is weakest.
