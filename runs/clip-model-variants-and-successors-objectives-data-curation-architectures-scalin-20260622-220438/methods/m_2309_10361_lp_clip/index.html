Improving CLIP Robustness with Knowledge Distillation and Self-Training

LP-CLIP is a very conservative repair for a practical problem: CLIP can be excellent at zero-shot classification and still be brittle under corruption, distribution shift, and calibration error. Rather than retraining the whole model, the paper asks a simpler question: can CLIP teach a tiny classifier how to be more reliable, using only unlabeled images?

The answer is a teacher-student loop built around CLIP’s own zero-shot predictions. The teacher looks at a weakly augmented image and emits a pseudo-label plus a confidence score. The student sees a harder version of the same image, keeps CLIP’s visual backbone frozen, and learns only a linear head from the teacher’s judgment. The head is trained more strongly when the teacher is confident and more gently when the teacher looks unsure.

What to notice here is the asymmetry. The top half is allowed to be soft and conservative because it is only generating labels. The bottom half has the harder job: it must survive stronger augmentation and still predict the teacher’s class. That is the whole robustness trick in one picture.

The teacher is just CLIP, but used carefully

LP-CLIP does not introduce a separate big teacher network. The teacher is ordinary CLIP doing zero-shot classification with class prompts. For each unlabeled image, CLIP compares the image feature to the text features for the class names, picks the strongest class, and uses the top softmax score as a confidence signal.

That confidence is more than a number for logging. It becomes the weight on the training loss. In effect, the model says: learn hard from the examples I understand, and do not overreact to the ones I am unsure about. This is the key difference from naive pseudo-labeling, where every prediction is treated as equally trustworthy.

This is also why the paper cares about calibration. If CLIP is overconfident on the wrong class, the student can inherit that mistake. Confidence weighting helps, but it does not magically fix a bad teacher. The method works best when CLIP’s confidence is at least correlated with being right.

The teacher still depends on prompt quality, so the paper keeps prompt engineering in the background rather than making it the main story. The point is not to turn CLIP into a prompt-tuning paper. The point is to turn CLIP’s existing zero-shot behavior into a usable training signal.

Why weak augmentation for the teacher and strong augmentation for the student

The second important design choice is the augmentation mismatch.

The teacher gets a weakly augmented view of the image, roughly the kind of preprocessing CLIP already expects. That keeps the pseudo-label stable. If the teacher saw a heavily distorted image, its label might become noisy for the wrong reason.

The student, by contrast, sees a stronger augmentation pipeline. The paper uses ordinary image resizing and cropping, horizontal flip, RandAugment, and Cutout. That makes the student work harder: the image is still the same object, but the view is more perturbed.

This weak-to-strong gap is what turns pseudo-labeling into robustness training. The student is not just memorizing the teacher’s answer. It is learning that the answer should survive visual disturbance.

If you want the intuition in one sentence, it is this: let CLIP speak softly when it is being asked to name the image, then force the small classifier to keep that answer even when the image is noisier.

The student stays small on purpose

The student model is deliberately boring. The CLIP image encoder stays frozen, and only a linear layer is trained on top of the image features.

That choice matters for two reasons. First, it keeps the method cheap. There is no backbone finetuning, no extra multimodal transformer, and no second large model to maintain at test time. Second, it preserves CLIP’s representation instead of overwriting it. The paper is trying to improve how CLIP is used, not replace the semantic space CLIP already learned.

At inference time the teacher disappears. The deployment path is just frozen CLIP features through the linear head.

What to notice here is that test-time complexity stays small. The extra work happens during pseudo-label generation and training, not during every prediction. That is why the method is practical: it adds robustness without turning inference into a multi-model system.

Confidence is the trust signal

The confidence score is the paper’s main control knob. It decides how much each pseudo-labeled example should shape the linear probe.

That is useful because not all pseudo-labels are equally good. Some images are easy and CLIP is clearly right. Some are ambiguous. Some are outside the training distribution entirely. If the method treated them all the same, the teacher’s mistakes would spread quickly through the student.

The paper’s confidence histograms make that point very clearly.

What to notice here is the shape of the histograms. Correct in-distribution examples pile up near high confidence, while OOD samples stay mostly near the low end. Incorrect in-distribution examples sit in the middle. That is exactly the kind of structure you want from a reliability score: high confidence should mean “probably right,” low confidence should mean “probably not a safe answer.”

This is why the paper reports better calibration and stronger OOD detection. Calibration is basically whether confidence matches reality. OOD detection is whether the model can tell familiar inputs from unfamiliar ones using confidence alone. LP-CLIP improves both because its training process already treats confidence as a signal to trust or ignore pseudo-labels.

A useful way to think about it is that the method does not merely train a classifier. It trains a classifier and a trust policy at the same time.

What the representation looks like after self-training

The paper also looks at the feature space directly. That matters because a linear probe can only be as good as the geometry underneath it. If the image features are tangled, a small head has to work too hard. If the features form cleaner clusters, the head can separate classes more naturally.

What to notice here is how much more structured the LP-CLIP side looks. The classes are more separated in both PCA and t-SNE views, while the original CLIP features are more smeared together. The paper reads this as a kind of denoising effect: the self-training loop pushes CLIP’s embeddings toward a cleaner decision geometry without changing the backbone itself.

That is a nice sanity check for the method. The improvement is not only visible in accuracy numbers. It is visible in how the representation organizes itself.

Why the paper argues this is robust, not just accurate

The method is tested on ordinary classification, corrupted datasets, and OOD detection. The pattern is consistent: LP-CLIP improves over raw CLIP, reduces accuracy drops on corrupted inputs, and stays close to supervised linear probing even though it never sees labels. On the reported benchmarks, it can often match or even beat the supervised linear probe, which is a strong sign that the pseudo-label loop is doing useful work rather than just adding complexity.

The important point is not the exact benchmark table. It is the type of improvement. Raw accuracy on clean data is only one piece of the story. If the model also becomes better calibrated and more selective about unfamiliar inputs, then it is more reliable in the cases where CLIP usually looks good on paper but feels shaky in practice.

That is why the paper puts so much emphasis on CIFAR10, CIFAR100, Tiny ImageNet, STL-10, ImageNet, and OOD sets like SVHN, Texture, and ImageNet-O. Those are all ways of asking the same question: does the confidence signal still mean something when the input is not a perfect in-distribution example?

Where the method can still fail

The main limitation is straightforward. LP-CLIP still depends on the quality of CLIP’s own pseudo-labels. If CLIP is confidently wrong on a shifted domain, the student can still learn the wrong answer, just with less weight. Confidence weighting reduces the damage, but it does not guarantee correctness.

The paper also does not provide a theoretical guarantee that the confidence scores are reliable uncertainty estimates. They are useful signals, not proof. And because the teacher is prompt-based CLIP, the method still inherits whatever prompt sensitivity the zero-shot model has.

So the method is not “solve uncertainty once and for all.” It is more modest and more practical: use CLIP’s own beliefs as a cheap source of supervision, but only trust those beliefs in proportion to how believable they look.

What to remember

LP-CLIP is a self-training recipe for making CLIP more robust without labels.

The short version is:

  • let CLIP act as its own teacher on weakly augmented images,
  • keep only a small linear head trainable,
  • weight learning by the teacher’s confidence,
  • make the student survive stronger augmentations,
  • and then use the resulting head for ordinary inference.

That simple loop is enough to improve robustness, calibration, and OOD detection while keeping the backbone frozen. The big idea is not that CLIP was missing a giant new architecture. It was that CLIP’s own high-confidence predictions can be turned into a better training signal if you teach a small head to respect them carefully.
