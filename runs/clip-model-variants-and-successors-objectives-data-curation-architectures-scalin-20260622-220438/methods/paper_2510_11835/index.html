Controlled CLIP and DINO: Disentangling Language Supervision and Data Scale in Vision Encoders

This paper is not trying to invent a new encoder. It is trying to answer a cleaner question: when CLIP beats DINO inside a vision-language system, how much of that comes from language supervision itself, and how much comes from the rest of the training recipe?

The key move is to strip away the usual confounds. Instead of comparing whatever CLIP checkpoint happens to be available against whatever DINO checkpoint happens to be available, the authors retrain both models under the same backbone, the same data, and the same schedule. Then they look at the embeddings directly, plug the encoders into a VLM, and ask what still changes.

Why the comparison has to be controlled

Figure 1 is the whole motivation for the paper.

What to notice is the confound. In ordinary comparisons, CLIP-like models usually differ from DINO in two ways at once: they use language supervision, and they often train on much larger datasets. If CLIP wins, you cannot immediately tell whether the victory comes from the objective, the scale, or both.

The paper’s answer is to hold everything else fixed. Both models use ViT-B/16. Both train on the same 10M-image subset of DataComp. Both run for 20 epochs with the same optimizer settings. The only real difference is the supervision signal: CLIP gets paired captions, while DINO gets only images.

That makes the study valuable as a controlled experiment rather than a leaderboard chase. It is asking what language supervision changes in the representation itself.

A quick sanity check before the deeper analysis

The first thing the authors check is whether the two encoders are at least comparable on broad classification. They freeze each encoder and train a linear probe on top, which is a simple way to test how much useful information is already linearly accessible in the features.

The result is important because it prevents the rest of the story from becoming a generic “better model wins” story. On ImageNet, the controlled CLIP and DINO checkpoints are very close. Their linear-probe numbers are 65.8 and 66.4, which is close enough to treat the encoders as broadly matched. On general tasks like CIFAR-10, they are also similar.

The gap shows up when the task gets more semantically fine-grained. CLIP is much better on Stanford Cars and CUB, while DINO is not dramatically better on the broad benchmarks. That tells you the two encoders are not learning the same notion of similarity. They are both useful, but they organize visual information differently.

How the embeddings disagree

The cleanest way to see that difference is to look for image pairs where the two models strongly disagree about similarity. Instead of choosing random examples, the paper scans the DataComp-10M space for pairs that one encoder treats as close neighbors and the other treats as far apart. Those disagreement cases act like x-rays for the embedding space.

What to notice is the organizing principle. CLIP groups images by meaning. If two images share object identity or contain the same text content, CLIP tends to keep them close even when the visual style changes. DINO behaves differently. It pays more attention to appearance-level cues like color, texture, and style, so semantically similar images can drift apart if their surface form changes.

That is the paper’s central representational claim in one sentence: language supervision pushes the encoder toward semantic abstraction, while image-only self-supervision leaves more weight on low-level visual similarity.

The appendix adds a nice sanity check. When the authors build tiny toy sets of images with different letters or numbers, CLIP keeps the semantically distinct symbols farther apart than DINO does. When they do the same with simple repeating patterns, the trend reverses and DINO becomes slightly better at separating the low-level visual structure. It is a small experiment, but it makes the qualitative picture feel real rather than anecdotal.

What changes once the encoders become the eyes of a VLM

Representation differences matter most if they survive the trip into a downstream multimodal system. To test that, the paper swaps the controlled CLIP or DINO vision encoder into LLaVA-1.5 and keeps the rest of the VLM recipe identical.

That setup has two important details.

First, the training is still controlled. Both versions go through the same pretraining and visual instruction tuning stages, so the encoder is again the only meaningful variable.

Second, the benchmark evaluation is careful. They run the model on VMCBench, which breaks visual question answering into 20 subsets. Because the test labels are not public, they select the best checkpoint by validation performance and report that result instead of pretending to score on hidden test labels.

The pattern is easy to remember. On broad VQA, reasoning, and document-or-chart tasks, the two encoders are close. But on OCR-heavy questions, CLIP pulls ahead by a clear margin. The average OCR gap is 7.5 points. That is exactly what you would expect if language supervision teaches the vision encoder to preserve text cues and other high-level semantics more reliably.

The important nuance is that DINO is not a disaster here. It still works well on most tasks, and it even edges CLIP on some visually grounded subsets. The point is narrower: when the downstream question depends on reading text in the image, CLIP-style supervision gives the VLM a better starting representation.

Trying to improve language supervision does not automatically help

Once that picture is clear, the paper asks a natural follow-up: if language supervision is good, can we make it even better?

The authors test two variants. One replaces the standard CLIP loss with a SigLIP-style sigmoid objective. The other replaces CLIP’s randomly initialized text encoder with a frozen, pretrained Vicuna-7B encoder.

These are sensible probes because they test two different hypotheses. Maybe the gain comes from the exact contrastive objective. Or maybe it comes from having a much stronger language model on the text side.

The result is not encouraging for either idea. Neither variant beats the controlled CLIP baseline. Both come in slightly lower on the VLM benchmark average. That is a useful negative result because it says the benefit is not just “more language machinery.” The particular CLIP-style supervision regime seems to be doing real work in this controlled setting.

So the paper narrows the design space rather than expanding it. If you want a better vision encoder for this family of VLMs, simply swapping the loss or bolting on a stronger text encoder is not enough by itself.

What this paper really teaches

This is not a paper about a new architecture trick. It is a paper about making a comparison honest.

Once architecture, data, and schedule are held fixed, the supervision signal starts to look like the real cause of the observed differences. Language supervision buys semantic alignment, better fine-grained recognition, and stronger OCR behavior in a downstream VLM. Image-only self-supervision keeps more emphasis on appearance-level similarity, which can still be useful when the task is more visually grounded than semantically textual.

That also explains the broader lesson for CLIP-like systems: do not attribute everything to the objective unless you have already controlled the data scale. A model can look better simply because it saw more data or was trained differently. This paper’s value is that it removes that ambiguity and makes the supervision effect visible.

Where the study stops

The authors are careful about the limits. The comparison is done on a 10M-image subset, so it does not settle what happens at billion-image scale. It also does not answer whether the best future recipe is purely language-supervised, purely self-supervised, or some hybrid mixture of the two.

That limitation matters. The paper gives a strong answer about the controlled setting it actually tests, but it does not claim a universal law for every scale regime or every downstream multimodal architecture.

What to remember

If you only keep one idea from this paper, keep this one: supervision shapes the geometry of the embedding space.

CLIP-style language supervision pulls nearby points toward shared object identity and text meaning. DINO-style image-only learning leaves more room for low-level visual structure like color, texture, and style. When those encoders are dropped into a VLM, the same difference shows up again, especially on OCR-heavy tasks.

So the paper’s real contribution is methodological clarity. It shows that to understand why a vision encoder helps a multimodal system, you have to control the architecture and the data before you argue about the loss.
