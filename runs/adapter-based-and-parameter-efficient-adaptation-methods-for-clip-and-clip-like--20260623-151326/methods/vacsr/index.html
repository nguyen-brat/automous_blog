Variational Adapter for Cross-modal Similarity Representation

VACSR is easiest to understand as a confidence-aware similarity adapter for CLIP. The paper is not trying to make image embeddings uncertain and text embeddings uncertain separately. It is trying to make the match itself uncertain when the annotation is too blunt to describe the real semantic relation.

That difference matters because image-text supervision is usually binary, but meaning is not. A caption can be close to an image without being an official match. A negative pair can still carry a lot of shared semantics. When a model is forced to compress that continuum into yes/no, the extra semantic structure often turns into false negatives.

What to notice in that figure is the gradient. The paper is showing that image-text correspondence is not a hard boundary. VACSR is built around the idea that the model should preserve that gradient instead of flattening it into a single binary label.

The big shift: model the relationship, not just the modalities

Earlier probabilistic embedding methods usually place uncertainty on the image branch or the text branch. VACSR moves the uncertainty to the pairwise similarity relation itself. That is the conceptual trick that makes the method different from a generic uncertainty head.

In practice, the pipeline looks like this:

CLIP image/text encoders -> pairwise Hadamard interaction -> variational similarity adapter -> decoded similarity score + calibrated uncertainty

What to notice in the figure is where the adapter sits. It does not replace CLIP, and it does not rewrite prompt tokens or backbone blocks. It takes the cross-modal interaction vector for each image-text pair, maps that into a latent distribution, and then decodes a similarity score while learning how much uncertainty that pair should carry.

Formula card: pairwise similarity vector

Purpose: keep a rich interaction signal for each image-text pair instead of collapsing immediately to a scalar score.

Equation:

si,j=vi⊙tj,S∈RB×B×d\mathbf{s}_{i,j} = \mathbf{v}_i \odot \mathbf{t}_j, \qquad \mathbf{S} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times B \times d}si,j​=vi​⊙tj​,S∈RB×B×d

Intuition: multiply the image and text features element by element so the adapter can reason about alignment dimension by dimension.

Symbols:

  • vi\mathbf{v}_ivi​: image feature for image iii.
  • tj\mathbf{t}_jtj​: text feature for caption jjj.
  • si,j\mathbf{s}_{i,j}si,j​: pairwise similarity vector for image iii and text jjj.
  • S\mathbf{S}S: batch of all pairwise similarity vectors.
  • ⊙\odot⊙: Hadamard product.
  • BBB: batch size.
  • ddd: feature dimension.

Read-aloud meaning: each image-text pair becomes its own ddd-dimensional interaction vector.

Why it matters: VACSR needs a relation-aware input, not just a cosine score, because uncertainty is being modeled on the pair itself.

This is why the Hadamard product matters. A scalar cosine similarity would throw away the channel-wise structure before the adapter ever sees it. The elementwise product keeps that structure alive, so the adapter can decide which parts of the pair look trustworthy and which parts look ambiguous.

Why two Gaussians instead of one

Once the pairwise vector is built, VACSR passes it through a variational encoder-decoder pair. The encoder predicts the latent distribution for the pair, and the decoder reconstructs a similarity score from a sampled latent variable. The paper uses a small multilayer perceptron for this adapter, which keeps the extra capacity lightweight.

The important choice is the posterior form. A single Gaussian is too restrictive for a relation as messy as image-text matching, so VACSR uses a two-component diagonal Gaussian mixture. That gives the latent space just enough flexibility to express different kinds of pair relationships without turning the model into a heavy uncertainty stack.

Formula card: two-component GMM posterior

Purpose: allow the latent similarity relation to live in one of two modes instead of forcing every pair into one Gaussian blob.

Equation:

pϕ(zi,j∣si,j)=∑k=12αkN ⁣(zi,j∣μk(si,j),diag⁡ ⁣(σk2(si,j)))p_{\phi}(\mathbf{z}_{i,j} \mid \mathbf{s}_{i,j}) = \sum_{k=1}^{2} \alpha_k \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mathbf{z}_{i,j} \mid \mu_k(\mathbf{s}_{i,j}), \operatorname{diag}\!\left(\sigma_k^2(\mathbf{s}_{i,j})\right)\right)pϕ​(zi,j​∣si,j​)=k=1∑2​αk​N(zi,j​∣μk​(si,j​),diag(σk2​(si,j​)))

Intuition: some pairs are clearly aligned, some are borderline, and some are ambiguous because the annotation is coarse. Two latent components are enough to separate those rough regimes better than one unimodal Gaussian.

Symbols:

  • zi,j\mathbf{z}_{i,j}zi,j​: latent variable for image-text pair (i,j)(i,j)(i,j).
  • si,j\mathbf{s}_{i,j}si,j​: pairwise similarity vector.
  • ϕ\phiϕ: encoder parameters.
  • αk\alpha_kαk​: mixing weight for component kkk.
  • μk(si,j)\mu_k(\mathbf{s}_{i,j})μk​(si,j​): mean predicted for component kkk.
  • σk2(si,j)\sigma_k^2(\mathbf{s}_{i,j})σk2​(si,j​): variance predicted for component kkk.
  • diag⁡(⋅)\operatorname{diag}(\cdot)diag(⋅): diagonal covariance matrix.
  • N(⋅)\mathcal{N}(\cdot)N(⋅): Gaussian distribution.

Read-aloud meaning: the pair is explained by a weighted mixture of two diagonal Gaussians in latent space.

Why it matters: a pairwise match relation is usually not one clean blob, and the mixture posterior gives the adapter a compact way to admit that.

Because the posterior is a mixture, the exact KL divergence to the standard normal prior is awkward. The paper avoids that by using a conservative weighted sum of component-wise KL terms. That is a practical choice, not a mathematical shortcut for its own sake: it keeps the variational objective tractable without pretending the mixture is simpler than it really is.

Sampling stays differentiable too. The component choice is handled with a Gumbel-Softmax relaxation, and the sample inside each Gaussian uses the usual reparameterization trick. So the adapter can learn a latent similarity space end to end, rather than treating uncertainty as a post-hoc score.

Turning reconstruction error into uncertainty

The decoder maps the latent sample back to a scalar similarity score. Under the Gaussian-likelihood view, that reconstruction term behaves like an MSE-style loss on the target similarity. The paper then adds a variance objective that makes uncertainty follow the size of the reconstruction residual.

There is one small implementation detail worth keeping in mind: because the decoder output is sigmoid-normalized, naive MSE gradients can fade when the score gets very close to 0 or 1. The paper uses a straight-through estimator so the backward pass stays useful even when the forward pass is saturated.

Formula card: variance optimization rule

Purpose: tie predicted uncertainty to how badly the decoded similarity misses the target label.

Equation:

σ^2(si,j)=∥y^−μ(zi,j)∥2\hat{\sigma}^{2}(\mathbf{s}_{i,j}) = \left\lVert \hat{y} - \mu(\mathbf{z}_{i,j}) \right\rVert^{2}σ^2(si,j​)=∥y^​−μ(zi,j​)∥2

Intuition: if the pair is hard to explain with the current decoded similarity, give it larger variance. If it is easy to explain, keep the variance small.

Symbols:

  • σ^2(si,j)\hat{\sigma}^{2}(\mathbf{s}_{i,j})σ^2(si,j​): predicted variance for pair (i,j)(i,j)(i,j).
  • si,j\mathbf{s}_{i,j}si,j​: pairwise similarity vector.
  • y^\hat{y}y^​: binary supervision label used in training.
  • μ(zi,j)\mu(\mathbf{z}_{i,j})μ(zi,j​): decoded similarity prediction from latent sample zi,j\mathbf{z}_{i,j}zi,j​.

Read-aloud meaning: the predicted variance equals the squared prediction error for the pair.

Why it matters: this is the paper’s clearest mechanism for softening dubious pairs without removing them.

The logic is simple once you read it this way. Small reconstruction error means the pair is easy and trustworthy, so the adapter should stay confident. Large reconstruction error means the pair is harder to trust, so the adapter should assign more uncertainty and reduce the harm of a potentially wrong binary label.

Why the hardest negative stays sharp

The paper does not want every hard pair to become uncertain. Positive pairs should be confident, false negatives should be softened, and the hardest negative should still remain informative. That is why the variance objective treats the hardest negative specially: it keeps the negative boundary sharp instead of letting a semantically close negative disappear into uncertainty.

That choice is easy to miss, but it is important. If the hardest negative were treated like a generic uncertain negative, the model could become too forgiving exactly where it needs discrimination most. VACSR tries to walk the line between robustness and sharp ranking.

What the uncertainty looks like when it works

The calibration story is not just a training trick. The paper checks whether the learned uncertainty actually tracks retrieval quality, and the answer is yes. On the EC dataset, higher uncertainty correlates strongly with lower R@1, while lower uncertainty lines up with better top-1 retrieval.

What to notice in that figure is the slope. The uncertainty score is acting like a real confidence signal. When the model is unsure, retrieval quality tends to be worse. When it is confident, retrieval quality improves. That is exactly what you want from an uncertainty-aware similarity adapter.

The same story shows up in the similarity distribution. Baseline losses tend to pile many pairs into a middle band, which makes it hard to separate ambiguous negatives from clear positives. VACSR spreads the similarity scores more usefully across the range, so the model has room to express both confidence and ambiguity instead of collapsing everything toward the center.

What to notice here is that the learned distribution is not just wider for its own sake. It is shaped so the model can keep a small number of true positives high while pushing the bulk of noisy pairs down with more confidence.

Noisy correspondence robustness

This is where VACSR pays off most clearly. The paper’s headline result is not a tiny gain on a clean benchmark. It is the way the adapter behaves when the supervision is noisy.

On COCO 5K with CLIP ViT-B/16, VACSR reaches image-to-text R@1 of 72.2, ahead of PCME++ at 68.7. On ECCV Caption with ViT-B/32, it reaches 80.6 versus 76.6 for PCME++. Under noisy correspondence, the gap remains visible: at 20% noise, VACSR gets COCO 5K R@1 of 57.1, beating NPC at 53.8, and at 50% noise it still leads with 55.6.

That pattern is the point. The adapter is not just learning a slightly better similarity score. It is learning when to trust the score. When annotations are coarse, that extra confidence estimate becomes a practical defense against false negatives.

The method also generalizes beyond retrieval. On ImageNet domain generalization, VACSR reaches 61.6 average, above MMRL at 60.6. Base-to-novel gains are more mixed, which is a reminder that uncertainty-aware similarity modeling is not the same thing as a method designed purely for out-of-distribution prompt tuning. It helps, but it is not a universal cure.

What the method costs

VACSR is lightweight compared with many heavier uncertainty models, but it is not free. The model builds pairwise latent features, so the computation scales with the number of image-text pairs in the batch. In other words, the extra expressiveness comes with a quadratic-style relation cost that scalar similarity scoring does not have.

The paper keeps that cost manageable with chunked computation, and the adapter itself is only a small MLP. Even so, the tradeoff is real: better robustness and better ranking quality come with more pairwise latent work and some extra memory overhead.

A second limitation is that the method depends on useful hard-negative selection and on reasonably calibrated uncertainty. If the latent mixture is a poor fit, or if uncertainty starts inflating on genuinely informative negatives, ranking precision can flatten. The paper’s own ablations line up with that caution. Removing the Gaussian mixture or the variance loss hurts performance, especially on metrics that expose false negatives more clearly.

What to remember

VACSR is easiest to remember as uncertainty on the relationship, not on the image or the caption by themselves. It takes a pairwise similarity vector, maps it into a small latent mixture, reconstructs a similarity score, and uses the reconstruction residual to decide how much confidence that pair deserves.

If you want a one-line memory hook, it is this:

when labels are too coarse, learn a probabilistic similarity space so dubious pairs get softer pressure and hard negatives stay sharp.
