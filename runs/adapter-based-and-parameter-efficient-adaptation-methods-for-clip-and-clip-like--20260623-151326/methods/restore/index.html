RESTORE: towaRds fEature ShifT for visiOn-language pRompt lEarning

RESTORE is easiest to think of as a stabilizer for prompt tuning. It does not try to replace CLIP’s pretrained alignment, and it does not train a big new head. Instead, it watches how much the vision branch and text branch move after prompts are inserted, keeps those movements synchronized layer by layer, and then uses the amount of movement to decide how strongly a tiny adapter should correct the final features.

That sounds subtle, but the problem is very concrete. Prompt tuning often wins on the seen base classes because the learned prompts become very task specific. The cost is that the two CLIP towers can drift away from the embedding geometry that made zero-shot transfer work in the first place. Once image and text stop moving together, novel-class performance usually pays the price.

What to notice in this first figure is the tradeoff, not the absolute score. The zero-shot model still has a broad prior, while prompt tuning can buy base-class accuracy by spending that prior. RESTORE’s main claim is that the right question is not only “did the score improve?” but “did the prompt cause the two modalities to drift out of alignment?”

The method at a glance

Here is what is happening in the pipeline. The CLIP image and text encoders stay frozen. Deep prompts are inserted into both towers, so every transformer block can make a small task-specific adjustment. The model still scores classes by image-text similarity, but now it also keeps track of how far each layer moved because of the prompts. Those per-layer shifts feed two controls: a consistency loss that asks the image and text branches to move by similar amounts, and a surgery adapter that adds a bigger correction when the accumulated shift becomes too large.

That makes RESTORE less like ordinary prompt learning and more like feedback control. The prompts are the actuators, the feature shifts are the sensor readings, and the surgery block is the correction stage.

Feature shift is the paper’s measuring stick

RESTORE starts by giving a name to the thing prompt tuning changes: the feature shift. The idea is simple. For each transformer block, compare the hidden state after prompting with the hidden state the same block would have produced without the prompt. The difference is the prompt-induced movement of that layer.

Purpose: measure how much a prompt changes each transformer block.

Equation:

Intuition: compare the layer output after prompting with the same layer’s output without prompts. The difference is the prompt-induced movement.

Why it matters: this gives RESTORE a way to talk about alignment in terms of representation movement, not just end-of-training accuracy.

The important point is that feature shift is layer-wise. RESTORE does not only look at the final embedding, because by the time the last layer speaks, it is too late to know where the drift started. Watching each layer makes the diagnosis much more precise.

Matching the amount of change

Once the paper has a way to measure movement, the next step is to ask whether the two towers moved by similar amounts. RESTORE does not force the full shift tensors to be identical. That would be too strict, and probably too brittle. Instead, it compresses each shift tensor down to a scalar magnitude and compares those magnitudes layer by layer.

Purpose: align the amount of prompt-induced movement in both modalities and train the model with that regularizer.

Why it matters: this is the paper’s main alignment mechanism. It does not try to make the two towers identical, it tries to keep their updates synchronized enough that CLIP’s shared embedding space still makes sense.

What to notice in this plot is that RESTORE is not merely trying to shrink every shift. The point is to control the shift profile. The paper’s own analysis shows that once the two branches move in a more coordinated way, generalization improves even if the absolute shift does not vanish. That is why the loss is about consistency, not zero movement.

The layer-wise form also explains the ablation on λfs\lambda_{fs}λfs​. As the weight gets stronger, the model tends to sacrifice a little base-class accuracy while improving novel-class behavior. That is a familiar prompt-tuning tradeoff, but RESTORE makes it explicit instead of letting it happen by accident. The paper settles on λfs=1\lambda_{fs}=1λfs​=1 because that is where the base and novel sides stay most balanced.

Why the surgery block is still needed

There is still a loophole. Two branches can drift a lot in the same direction, and the consistency loss may stay small because it only compares magnitudes. In that case, the model can still overfit even though the shift penalty looks healthy. RESTORE calls this the shortcut or hacking case, and it adds a small adapter to catch it.

The adapter sits on the final adapted features, and its strength is scaled by the accumulated feature shift across all layers. The bigger the total movement, the stronger the correction.

Why it matters: shift matching alone can miss the case where both branches drift too far together. The surgery block makes the method sensitive to absolute movement, not only relative movement.

This is also why the paper prefers controllable coefficients over fixed ones. A fixed adapter strength cannot tell the difference between a branch that moved a little and a branch that moved a lot. RESTORE wants the correction to depend on the observed shift, not on a constant guess.

What the results really support

The most useful evidence is not the raw leaderboard, it is the pattern. RESTORE improves base-to-novel harmonic mean over strong prompt-learning baselines, and it also nudges cross-dataset and cross-domain averages upward. The best anchor result is the one that is easiest to remember: RESTORE_p raises harmonic mean from 78.92 to 79.74, and RESTORE_m raises it from 78.50 to 79.55.

The ablations are even more instructive than the headline numbers. When the feature-shift loss gets stronger, base accuracy drops a little while novel-class accuracy improves. That is the right kind of tradeoff if your goal is generalization rather than base-class memorization. The controllable surgery adapter also beats the fixed-coefficient version, which matches the paper’s core intuition that correction strength should follow actual drift.

The visual analysis tells the same story. The t-SNE plots show that once the alignment loss and surgery block are in place, base and novel features separate more cleanly instead of collapsing into a mixed cloud. That is the qualitative signature you would expect if the two towers are being tuned together rather than independently.

What the paper leaves open

RESTORE is convincing, but it is not the last word on multimodal prompt tuning.

  • The discrepancy measure is intentionally simple. Frobenius norm plus MSE is easy to optimize, but it may be too coarse to capture richer kinds of multimodal misalignment.
  • The paper gives a strong explanation for why biased prompts hurt generalization, but the theory is still mostly intuitive rather than formal.
  • The evaluation is centered on CLIP-style few-shot classification, so broader generative multimodal settings remain open.
  • Inference: if two branches drift together in almost the same way, the magnitude-matching loss alone can still miss the problem, which is why the surgery block is necessary.

What to remember

RESTORE treats prompt tuning as a control problem. Measure how each layer moves, keep image and text moving together, and apply a stronger correction only when the movement gets too large.

If you want the short version, it is this: prompt tuning should not only improve labels, it should preserve the shared embedding geometry that made CLIP useful in the first place.
