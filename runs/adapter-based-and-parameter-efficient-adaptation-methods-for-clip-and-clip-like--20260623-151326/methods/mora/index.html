MoRA: Missing Modality Low-Rank Adaptation for Multimodal Learning

MoRA is built for a practical failure mode that CLIP-like models do not handle well by default: one modality disappears. In the lab, image-text inputs are often paired and complete. In deployment, they are not. Privacy rules hide text, collection pipelines lose images, and some tasks only give you one side of the pair. MoRA tries to fix that without turning adaptation into a heavyweight retraining problem.

The paper’s answer is not another prompt stack or a bigger fusion head. It keeps the pretrained encoders frozen, adds a small low-rank update that can move each modality in its own task-specific direction, and then adds a second update path that transfers structure across modalities in a shared rank space. That gives the model two things at once: flexibility for each encoder and a common bridge for cross-modal knowledge.

What to notice in this figure is that the “gap” between modalities is not treated as a problem to erase. The paper argues that the gap itself carries useful complementary information, as long as the two encoders keep pointing in roughly the same direction after fine-tuning. A model that is already aligned can survive missing inputs much better than an unaligned one, but it still needs room to adapt. MoRA is trying to preserve that balance.

Start with the direction, not just the size

The first useful idea in the paper is that a fine-tuned weight is not just a bigger or smaller matrix. It also has a direction. That matters because MoRA wants to preserve the pretrained geometry while still moving the model toward a downstream task.

Formula card: separate magnitude from direction

Purpose: express an adapted weight as a scale times a normalized direction.

Equation:

W=∥W0+ΔW∥F W0+ΔW‾\mathbf{W} = \| \mathbf{W}_0 + \Delta \mathbf{W} \|_{\mathrm{F}} \, \overline{\mathbf{W}_0 + \Delta \mathbf{W}}W=∥W0​+ΔW∥F​W0​+ΔW​

Intuition: the update has two parts. One part says how strong the change is. The other says which direction the change points in.

Symbols: W0\mathbf{W}_0W0​ is the frozen pretrained weight, ΔW\Delta \mathbf{W}ΔW is the learned update, ∥⋅∥F\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{F}}∥⋅∥F​ is the Frobenius norm, and the overline means the matrix normalized by that norm.

Read-aloud meaning: the adapted weight equals its magnitude times its normalized direction.

Why it matters: MoRA uses this split to keep the update interpretable. The paper is not only asking “how much should the weights move?” It is also asking “should vision and text move in the same direction, or in separate task-specific directions?” That is the key design tension behind the method.

The paper’s motivation section makes this tension concrete. If you fine-tune an aligned CLIP model on complete samples and then test with a missing modality, performance drops much less than if you start from an unaligned vision and text backbone. But the best complete-input performance still comes from using both modalities together, not from collapsing one side into the other. So alignment matters, but so does the residual gap between modalities. MoRA keeps both.

Two update paths per encoder

Once that motivation is clear, the architecture becomes easier to read. Each encoder gets two low-rank update paths:

  • a modality-specific branch that can adapt vision and text differently, and
  • a shared branch that lets the two modalities exchange structure.

This is the diagram to keep in mind. The private branch answers the question “how should this encoder move for this task?” The shared branch answers “what structure should be borrowed from the other modality?” MoRA needs both because a fully shared update can be too rigid, while fully independent updates can leave the two towers drifting apart when one modality is missing.

The paper starts with ordinary low-rank adaptation. If the two encoders had the same width, you could imagine mixing their low-rank factors directly. But CLIP-like towers are usually asymmetric. In the paper’s CLIP ViT-B/16 example, the vision encoder and text encoder do not even have the same hidden size, so a naive shared low-rank multiplication does not line up.

That is the core problem MoRA has to solve: how do you share structure across modalities without forcing both sides into one common width?

Formula card: move the sharing into rank space

Purpose: build a compact structural summary for each modality that does not depend on the encoder width.

Equation:

Gv=Sv(Sv)T,Gt=St(St)T\mathbf{G}^{v} = \mathbf{S}^{v}(\mathbf{S}^{v})^{T}, \qquad \mathbf{G}^{t} = \mathbf{S}^{t}(\mathbf{S}^{t})^{T}Gv=Sv(Sv)T,Gt=St(St)T

Intuition: instead of trying to directly splice the wide vision and text factors together, MoRA compresses each side into a small rank-space Gram matrix. That matrix acts like a structural fingerprint.

Symbols: Sv\mathbf{S}^{v}Sv and St\mathbf{S}^{t}St are the shared low-rank parameters for vision and text. Gv\mathbf{G}^{v}Gv and Gt\mathbf{G}^{t}Gt are the corresponding Gram matrices in rank space. The superscripts vvv and ttt refer to vision and text.

Read-aloud meaning: each modality is summarized by a small Gram matrix built from its shared low-rank factors.

Why it matters: this is the paper’s dimension-agnostic bridge. The shared signal no longer has to live in the full encoder width, so the method can transfer information between asymmetric towers without adding a separate projection layer that would remain at inference time.

Think of the Gram matrix as a small meeting room. Vision and text do not have to walk around in each other’s full feature space. They only need to agree on a compact structural summary of their low-rank channels. That keeps the transfer cheap and makes the shareable part much easier to merge back into pretrained weights.

The paper’s eigenvalue analysis supports that story. The Gram matrices concentrate information more tightly than the original pretrained weights, and the vision and text patterns look similar enough that a shared structural space is plausible. Deeper layers become even more converged, which is exactly where you would want the shared bridge to matter most.

What to notice here is not the exact curve shape but the qualitative pattern: the Gram spectra are more compact than the pretrained spectra, and the two modalities look structurally closer than their raw widths would suggest. That is the paper’s evidence that rank space is not just a mathematical convenience. It is a compact place where the shared update can actually live.

What the final update is doing

After the rank-space bridge is in place, MoRA combines the private and shared paths into the actual encoder update. The vision branch and text branch use the same recipe, mirrored to their own widths.

Formula card: the vision update

Purpose: combine a modality-specific low-rank move with a shared cross-modal move.

Equation:

Wv=αv W0v+BvAv‾+αs W0v+(Sv)TGtSv‾\mathbf{W}^{v} = \alpha^{v}\,\overline{\mathbf{W}_{0}^{v} + \mathbf{B}^{v}\mathbf{A}^{v}} + \alpha^{s}\,\overline{\mathbf{W}_{0}^{v} + (\mathbf{S}^{v})^{T}\mathbf{G}^{t}\mathbf{S}^{v}}Wv=αvW0v​+BvAv​+αsW0v​+(Sv)TGtSv​

Intuition: one term says how the vision encoder should adapt for its own task. The other term says how it should be nudged by the shared structure learned from the text side.

Symbols: Wv\mathbf{W}^{v}Wv is the adapted vision weight. W0v\mathbf{W}_{0}^{v}W0v​ is the frozen pretrained vision weight. BvAv\mathbf{B}^{v}\mathbf{A}^{v}BvAv is the modality-specific low-rank update for vision. Sv\mathbf{S}^{v}Sv is the shared low-rank parameter on the vision side. Gt\mathbf{G}^{t}Gt is the Gram matrix computed from the text shared parameters. αv\alpha^{v}αv and αs\alpha^{s}αs are learnable magnitudes that scale the private and shared directions.

Read-aloud meaning: the vision weight is the sum of a private low-rank correction and a shared correction shaped by text-side structure.

Why it matters: this is the actual mechanism that gives MoRA bidirectional transfer. The text encoder has the mirrored update with BtAt\mathbf{B}^{t}\mathbf{A}^{t}BtAt and (St)TGvSt(\mathbf{S}^{t})^{T}\mathbf{G}^{v}\mathbf{S}^{t}(St)TGvSt, so each tower gets to move in its own direction while still borrowing from the other side.

The scalars αv\alpha^{v}αv and αs\alpha^{s}αs are important. They are not decorative. They let the model decide how much to trust the modality-specific direction versus the shared direction. That is how MoRA preserves the pretrained geometry instead of flattening everything into one shared update.

The nice practical detail is that all of this is still mergeable. The learned low-rank pieces and the Gram-based deltas can be absorbed into the pretrained weights before inference, so the runtime model does not carry an extra adapter stack. In other words, the method changes training behavior without adding an inference-time tax.

Why the design works

MoRA’s ablations line up with the story above.

Removing the Gram matrices hurts. The paper reports an average performance drop of 2.66% when that shared rank-space transfer is removed. Replacing the shared parameters with independent ones hurts too, with an average drop of 2.78%. Those two results are important because they separate the two jobs the method is doing: the private low-rank branch gives flexibility, and the shared Gram branch gives cross-modal transfer.

That also explains why MoRA does better than plain low-rank adaptation or prompt-based missing-modality fixes. Plain LoRA-style updates can move each encoder, but they do not create a compact common structure for the two modalities to exchange. Prompt methods can create interaction at runtime, but they pay inference overhead. MoRA tries to get the best of both worlds: structured sharing during training and no extra modules at test time.

The paper’s strongest anchor results are consistent with that reading. It reports an average 5.24% missing-modality improvement over prior approaches, only 0.11% of the trainable parameters compared with full fine-tuning, and 25.90% of the inference time of the best prompt-based competitor. The exact numbers are not the point by themselves. The point is that the method gets robustness from a very small amount of trainable structure.

The direction analysis on Food101 is a good sanity check. MoRA keeps the distance and angle between vision and text embeddings closer to the original CLIP geometry than DCP or full fine-tuning. That is exactly what the paper wanted from the start: preserve the aligned backbone, keep the modalities related, but still let each side adapt enough to handle missing inputs.

Where the paper is careful

MoRA is strong, but it is also scoped.

  • It is validated mainly on image-text datasets.
  • It focuses on binary missing-versus-present settings rather than partially corrupted inputs.
  • The evidence is centered on classification, with one retrieval extension to show the idea is not classification-only.

Those limits matter because they show what the method really claims. MoRA is not a universal multimodal adapter for every possible stack. It is a careful PEFT recipe for CLIP-like dual encoders when one modality may disappear and the update still has to be mergeable.

That said, the open questions are natural ones. The paper itself leaves room for audio-visual settings, richer multimodal stacks, and partially corrupted inputs where a modality is present but degraded instead of absent. It is also fair to ask how much of the gain depends on starting from already well-aligned pretrained encoders. Those are the right follow-ups if you want to extend the idea.

What to remember

MoRA’s central move is simple to state once you strip away the algebra: keep a private low-rank update for each encoder, but let the two encoders exchange structure through a shared rank-space Gram matrix. The private path gives flexibility. The shared path gives cross-modal transfer. The mergeable weight update keeps inference clean.

If you want one sentence to keep in your head, make it this: when modalities can disappear, adaptation should preserve the shared alignment structure while still allowing each modality to move in its own direction.
