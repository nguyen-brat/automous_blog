Cross-Modal Adapter for Vision-Language Retrieval

Cross-Modal Adapter is a retrieval-friendly way to adapt CLIP-like models without giving up the two-tower setup that makes retrieval practical in the first place. The paper starts from a useful frustration: if vision and text each get their own tiny adapter, the towers stay efficient but they can drift apart; if you add explicit cross-modal fusion, alignment improves but retrieval stops being cheap because every query has to interact with every candidate. The solution is small but clever: insert adapters into both encoders, then share part of the adapter expansion so both towers are nudged through a partially common space.

What to notice here is the tradeoff the paper is trying to break. The left side is the cheap but disconnected baseline. The middle side fixes alignment by letting the two modalities talk directly, but that turns retrieval into an expensive pairwise problem. The right side keeps the retrieval pipeline intact and uses weight sharing to create a weaker, implicit form of interaction that is still enough to matter.

Start with the smallest useful update

The method still begins with a normal adapter: a narrow bottleneck that learns a correction to the frozen backbone feature.

Purpose: Add a cheap trainable correction without rewriting the pretrained encoder.

Equation:

Adapter(x)=x+σ(xWdown)Wup\mathrm{Adapter}(x)=x+\sigma(xW_{\mathrm{down}})W_{\mathrm{up}}Adapter(x)=x+σ(xWdown​)Wup​

Intuition: Squeeze the feature into a small latent space, transform it, expand it again, and add the result back to the original representation.

Symbols: xxx is the input token, patch, or frame feature. WdownW_{\mathrm{down}}Wdown​ projects from the model width ddd down to the bottleneck width rrr, where r≪dr \ll dr≪d. σ\sigmaσ is the nonlinearity, implemented as GELU. WupW_{\mathrm{up}}Wup​ expands the bottleneck back to width ddd. The final output is the adapted feature.

Read-aloud meaning: The adapted feature equals the original feature plus a small bottleneck correction.

Why it matters: This is the base efficiency trick. The backbone stays frozen, and the adapter learns only a compact residual change.

The paper then changes one thing that sounds minor but is actually the whole method: the up-projection is no longer fully private to each modality.

This figure is easiest to read after you have the basic adapter in mind. The left panel is the naive version: one adapter in the vision encoder, one in the text encoder, and no reason for them to agree except the final retrieval loss. The middle panel shows the paper’s main move: keep the towers separate at inference time, but let training couple them through shared adapter weights. The right panel makes the split concrete by showing that only part of the up-projection is shared.

Share the expansion, not the whole module

Purpose: Create cross-modal alignment without introducing expensive explicit fusion.

Equation:

AdapterCM(x)=x+Concat[zWup,unique, zWup,share]\mathrm{Adapter}_{\mathrm{CM}}(x)=x+\mathrm{Concat}[zW_{\mathrm{up,unique}},\ zW_{\mathrm{up,share}}]AdapterCM​(x)=x+Concat[zWup,unique​, zWup,share​]

Intuition: First compress the feature into zzz. Then let one part of the expansion stay modality-specific while another part is forced through shared weights that both encoders use.

Symbols: xxx is the input feature from either encoder. zzz is the bottleneck feature after the down-projection and nonlinearity. Wup,uniqueW_{\mathrm{up,unique}}Wup,unique​ is the modality-specific expansion matrix. Wup,shareW_{\mathrm{up,share}}Wup,share​ is the expansion matrix shared by vision and language. The concatenation step joins the private and shared channels into one update.

Read-aloud meaning: The new feature is the old feature plus a correction with both private and shared channels.

Why it matters: This is the paper’s core mechanism for implicit cross-modal interaction. The two encoders still run independently, but they are no longer free to learn entirely separate adaptation directions.

The choice to share the up-projection is not arbitrary. The paper argues, and then checks with ablations, that the expansion side is where modality-specific fingerprints are easier to detect. Sharing there gives the other tower a route into the same reconstructed space. Sharing the compression side can still help, but it is less effective because it leaves the final expansion free to drift back into modality-specific territory.

Where the adapter sits in the transformer

The paper does not place the adapter just once at the end. It inserts adapters inside both encoders, after the self-attention and feed-forward blocks, so the shared correction can influence the representation throughout the stack.

This placement study is important because it shows the method is not only about sharing weights, but also about where the shared correction is allowed to act. Sequential insertion after attention and then after the MLP works better than a parallel layout. That makes sense: the attention sublayer reshapes token relationships, and the feed-forward sublayer can then refine the result before the adapter adds its correction.

Another detail is easy to miss but matters in practice: adapters in both modalities are necessary. If you only tune the vision side or only tune the language side, the model gets a partial correction but not a real cross-modal alignment signal. The paper’s best design uses both encoders and lets the shared adapter channels carry the alignment pressure across them.

How retrieval uses the adapted features

Once the encoders have produced aligned features, the matching step stays simple. For image-text retrieval, the model compares the global image and text embeddings with cosine similarity. There is no extra matching head. That keeps the inference story clean.

Video-text retrieval needs one extra idea because a video is not a single vector until you decide how to summarize its frames. The paper uses the text query itself to choose which frames matter most.

Purpose: Turn many frame embeddings into one query-aware video embedding without adding a trainable matching module.

Equation:

αj=⟨t,fi,j⟩,α^j=eαj/τ∑keαk/τ,v^i=∑jα^jfi,j\alpha_j=\langle t,f_{i,j}\rangle,\qquad \hat{\alpha}_j=\frac{e^{\alpha_j/\tau}}{\sum_k e^{\alpha_k/\tau}},\qquad \hat{v}_i=\sum_j \hat{\alpha}_j f_{i,j}αj​=⟨t,fi,j​⟩,α^j​=∑k​eαk​/τeαj​/τ​,v^i​=j∑​α^j​fi,j​

Intuition: Score each frame against the text query, convert the scores into weights, and then average the frame features using those weights.

Symbols: ttt is the text query embedding. fi,jf_{i,j}fi,j​ is the feature of the jjj-th frame from video viv_ivi​. αj\alpha_jαj​ is the raw relevance score between the query and that frame. α^j\hat{\alpha}_jα^j​ is the normalized weight after softmax. τ\tauτ is the temperature that controls how sharp the weighting is. v^i\hat{v}_iv^i​ is the final query-aware video embedding.

Read-aloud meaning: The video representation is the weighted average of its frames, and the weights come from how well each frame matches the text.

Why it matters: This gives the model a lightweight way to focus on the right temporal evidence while preserving the two-tower retrieval pipeline. A small τ\tauτ makes the video behave like its best-matching frame; a larger τ\tauτ spreads attention across more frames.

Training then uses a symmetric contrastive objective over the batch similarity matrix: each text should score highest with its paired video, and each video should score highest with its paired text. The important point is not the exact loss formula, but that the method trains the shared adapter channels to make the paired items meet in the same space from both directions.

Why the paper says this works

The empirical pattern is consistent across the ablations. Sharing adapter weights improves average retrieval over the no-share variant on all five datasets the paper studies. The gains are strongest when the shared channel is placed in the up-projection, which supports the idea that the expansion side is where modality mismatch is most visible.

The paper also shows that the method does not need a huge number of trainable weights to be useful. On MSR-VTT, text-to-video R@1 reaches 45.0 with only 0.5M trainable parameters, compared with 43.1 for full tuning with 123.5M parameters. On Flickr30K, text retrieval R@1 reaches 89.6, which is higher than AdapterATTN at 86.9 and AdaptFormer at 87.4. The paper also reports roughly 30% lower training time than full tuning on an RTX 3090 while keeping or improving accuracy.

The more general lesson is not just “adapters are good.” It is that implicit sharing can replace a lot of the alignment work that people often try to get from explicit fusion. The model still behaves like a retriever, so offline indexing remains intact, but the shared adapter channels make the two towers less independent during training.

Where the idea stops

The paper is explicit about a few limitations.

  • The sharing design is tailored to adapters, so it may not transfer cleanly to other parameter-efficient methods such as LoRA.
  • The method improves encoder-level alignment, but it does not study a parameter-efficient matching module after the encoders.
  • The strongest evidence is on retrieval tasks, not on generation or reasoning settings.

Those limits are not fatal, but they matter. They mark the boundary between “cheap alignment for retrieval” and a broader theory of shared parameter-efficient adaptation.

What to remember

The cleanest way to remember Cross-Modal Adapter is this: keep the two towers separate, but make part of their adapter expansion shared. That gives you a small common corridor for vision and language to move through, without collapsing retrieval into explicit pairwise fusion.

If full fusion is the expensive answer, this paper’s answer is simpler: share the adapter’s expansion path, preserve offline indexing, and let the towers align through the smallest common bridge that still works.
