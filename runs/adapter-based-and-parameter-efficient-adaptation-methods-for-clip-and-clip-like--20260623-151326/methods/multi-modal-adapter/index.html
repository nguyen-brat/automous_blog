Multi-Modal Adapter for CLIP

Multi-Modal Adapter is a small but telling change to the usual CLIP adapter recipe. The paper starts from a simple complaint: if the image branch and text branch each get their own tiny update block, the model stays efficient but the two modalities can still miss each other. So instead of adapting them separately, the method puts the class prompts and the current image into one masked attention block, lets them exchange information, and then writes a small residual correction back into the frozen CLIP embeddings.

What to notice in the figure is that the adapter is not replacing CLIP. It sits between the frozen encoders and the final cosine-similarity classifier, so the model keeps CLIP’s shared embedding space and only learns a compact joint update.

Start from the right problem

The method is trying to fix a specific weakness in earlier CLIP adapters. CLIP-Adapter and similar methods are parameter-efficient, but they usually adapt the image side and the text side in parallel. That is cheap, yet it treats cross-modal interaction as an afterthought. In a few-shot setting, that matters because the model can overfit the base classes while failing to transfer the right adjustment to unseen ones.

Multi-Modal Adapter takes a more direct route. It treats the prompt set and the image feature as a short conversation. First the conversation is formed, then a small attention block is allowed to mediate it, and only after that does the method return to CLIP’s original embedding space.

Put the prompts and the image into one sequence

The first move is to make the adapter see both modalities at once. For each image sample, the model takes the text prompt embeddings for the candidate classes and the CLIP image embedding, then concatenates them into one sequence.

What to notice here is the unit of adaptation. The adapter is not processing unrelated tokens from a long generic sequence. It is looking at one image and the class prompts that compete for that image. That is exactly the scale where few-shot classification makes decisions.

Read-aloud meaning: The attention block receives one mixed sequence made from the prompt embeddings and the image embedding.

Why it matters: This is the first step that makes the adapter joint instead of separate. The method is no longer adapting two branches in isolation.

The mask is the actual trick

If you simply feed the combined sequence into ordinary self-attention, the adapter can waste capacity on easy same-modality shortcuts. The paper avoids that by using a mask that keeps the cross-modal links open and blocks the rest. In plain language, text can look at the image, and the image can look back at the text, but text does not get to spend the whole block re-encoding text, and the image does not get to spend the whole block re-encoding image.

That routing rule is the real design choice. It keeps the block focused on the interaction the paper cares about.

Formula card: masked cross-modal attention

Purpose: Let text and image exchange information while blocking same-modality shortcuts.

Equation:

MaskedAttention(Q,K,V)=softmax(QKTdk+Maski,j)V\mathrm{MaskedAttention}(Q,K,V)=\mathrm{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}+\mathrm{Mask}_{i,j}\right)VMaskedAttention(Q,K,V)=softmax(dk​​QKT​+Maski,j​)V

Intuition: Compute the usual attention scores, then add a mask that removes the links the adapter is not allowed to use.

Symbols: QQQ is the query matrix, KKK is the key matrix, and VVV is the value matrix. dkd_kdk​ is the key dimensionality used for scaling. Maski,j\mathrm{Mask}_{i,j}Maski,j​ is the cross-modal gate, with 000 for allowed text-image links and −∞-\infty−∞ for blocked links. After softmax, allowed links survive and blocked links vanish.

Read-aloud meaning: The adapter mixes values only through the permitted cross-modal paths.

Why it matters: This is what makes the method different from independent image and text adapters. The update is built around interaction, not just separate branch tuning.

A useful way to think about the mask is that it forces the model to solve the few-shot problem by negotiation. The prompt embeddings and the image embedding have to influence each other through a narrow bridge instead of taking their own private routes.

Shrink first, then expand back

The paper does not leave this bridge wide open. It shrinks the CLIP embeddings before attention so the adapter stays cheap, then expands the features back afterward through a two-layer upsampling block with GELU in the middle. That is how the method keeps the trainable budget tiny, around 107,712 parameters, instead of turning the adapter into a second backbone.

This also explains why the exact middle block is not the star of the paper. The appendix reports little difference between a plain multi-head attention block and a transformer-encoder variant, and little difference between linear and MLP sampling blocks. In other words, the main win is not some exotic layer stack. The main win is the cross-modal bottleneck itself.

After the upsampling step, the adapted sequence is split back into text-side and image-side outputs, normalized in embedding space, and blended with the frozen CLIP embeddings.

Formula card: blend the adapted features back into CLIP

Purpose: Keep the pretrained CLIP features as the anchor and add only a small learned correction.

Equation:

e~text=λetext+(1−λ)mtext,e~image=λeimage+(1−λ)mimage\tilde{e}_{\text{text}}=\lambda e_{\text{text}}+(1-\lambda)m_{\text{text}},\qquad \tilde{e}_{\text{image}}=\lambda e_{\text{image}}+(1-\lambda)m_{\text{image}}e~text​=λetext​+(1−λ)mtext​,e~image​=λeimage​+(1−λ)mimage​

Intuition: The adapter proposes a cross-modal correction, then the residual mix decides how much of that correction to trust.

Symbols: etexte_{\text{text}}etext​ and eimagee_{\text{image}}eimage​ are the original CLIP text and image embeddings. mtextm_{\text{text}}mtext​ and mimagem_{\text{image}}mimage​ are the adapted outputs of the multimodal adapter after upsampling and normalization. λ\lambdaλ is the residual weight, set to 0.20.20.2 in the paper. e~text\tilde{e}_{\text{text}}e~text​ and e~image\tilde{e}_{\text{image}}e~image​ are the final blended embeddings used for prediction.

Read-aloud meaning: The final embeddings are a weighted mix of frozen CLIP and the adapter’s cross-modal update.

Why it matters: This is the safety valve. The method can move the representation, but it never throws away CLIP’s original geometry.

The final classifier stays simple. The model computes cosine similarity between the adapted text and image embeddings, then trains the adapter weights with standard cross-entropy on those logits. So the whole method is still a CLIP-style classifier. The difference is that the text and image features have already had a chance to correct each other before the similarity step.

Why the paper thinks this helps generalization

The paper’s most interesting evidence is not that it wins every dataset. It is that it seems to forget less. Across the few-shot base/new split, its average new-minus-base gap is about -7%, which is much smaller in magnitude than the gaps reported for CoOp, ProDA, and CLIP-Adapter. That is the sign the paper leans on to argue that the joint block is more conservative about overfitting the seen classes.

The text side matters here more than you might expect. When the authors remove text adaptation, performance drops on every one of the four datasets they inspect, and the Oxford Flowers base split takes a particularly large hit. So even though it is tempting to think of the image branch as the main place to spend capacity, the paper’s ablation says the text branch is part of the regularization story too.

There is a nice pattern in that result. The adapter is not trying to learn a giant new classifier from scratch. It is trying to preserve CLIP’s semantic space while letting the image and prompt embeddings re-balance each other just enough to fit the task.

Where it works, and where it is more cautious

The paper reports best results on some datasets such as Caltech101 and Food101, but it does not dominate every benchmark. Average accuracy still trails several stronger CLIP adaptation methods, and some dataset gains are inconsistent across domains. That tradeoff is important, because it tells you what kind of improvement this method is really buying.

It is better to think of Multi-Modal Adapter as a stability-first method than as a pure peak-accuracy method. The joint attention block and small residual mix seem to help the model transfer more gracefully from base classes to new ones, but they do not magically add task-specific capacity where the adapter is already too small.

That is also why the paper keeps the design simple. A deeper or more elaborate block might have increased capacity, but the appendix evidence does not show a large gain from swapping the attention block for a transformer encoder or from changing the sampling blocks. The method’s power comes from changing the interaction pattern, not from making the block larger.

What the paper leaves open

The paper is also explicit about what it does not settle. It does not explore many routing variants, and it leaves positional encodings for the mixed text-image sequence as future work. It also does not answer how the same idea scales to larger CLIP-like backbones, or whether stronger masking and routing would improve both base and new accuracy at once.

That is useful to know, because it tells you the method is promising but not fully mapped out. The core idea looks solid; the design space around it is still open.

What to remember

The cleanest way to remember Multi-Modal Adapter is this: keep CLIP frozen, put the class prompts and image into one short sequence, let a masked attention block allow only cross-modal interaction, then blend the result back into the original embeddings with a small residual weight.

That is a very small change in code, but a meaningful change in behavior. Instead of asking two independent adapters to guess how text and image should meet, the paper makes their interaction the thing that gets adapted.
