How Deep Research Agents Search, Browse, and Draft in a Single Loop

Deep research agents are built for a harder job than ordinary retrieval systems. They are not just supposed to fetch a few relevant pages. They have to decide what is missing, search for it, inspect what they find, keep the useful evidence alive across many steps, and turn that evidence into a coherent answer or report before the context window or the plan falls apart.

That is why the core problem in this area is not a single algorithm. It is a shared interface problem. The agent has to keep reasoning, retrieval, navigation, note-taking, and writing aligned over a long interaction. When that alignment breaks, the system becomes shallow, forgetful, brittle, or wasteful with tools.

The Basic Loop

The recurring setup in this family is simple to state:

In practice, the loop is iterative rather than linear. A draft changes what the agent searches for next. New evidence changes what the draft should say. Missing evidence forces another round of navigation. Several systems in this family are best understood as attempts to keep that loop tight instead of treating search and writing as separate phases.

Why This Is Hard

The common theme across these methods is not that they all share one mechanism. They attack different failure points inside the same loop.

The first failure point is shallow retrieval. A search agent can stop too early, accept the first plausible page, or fail to click far enough into the web. The result is thin evidence and weak synthesis.

The second is context loss. Long research tasks accumulate partial answers, discarded leads, draft fragments, and unresolved questions. If the system cannot preserve the right parts of that state, later searches become disconnected from earlier findings.

The third is long-horizon reliability. Even when each step looks reasonable, a twenty-step or two-hundred-step trajectory can drift. Tool failures, weak subgoals, and noisy intermediate outputs compound over time.

The fourth is tool-use reasoning. The agent has to decide not only what is true, but also which tool should be used next, what observation matters, what can be discarded, and when to stop.

The Main Design Axes

The embedded family synthesis points to a few recurring design choices.

search_depth: deep means these systems are designed to keep going past top-level retrieval. They are built for multi-step browsing, recursive search, and repeated evidence gathering.

writing_cadence: incremental and draft_centricity: high mean writing is part of the control loop. The draft is not only an output. It is also a working memory that shapes the next search step.

tool_control: model-driven means the language model is not just answering over retrieved text. It is deciding when to search, click, extract, revise, or verify.

retrieval_frequency: iterative means retrieval is revisited throughout the task rather than front-loaded.

training_style: online preference optimization highlights another important trend: better prompts are not enough. Several systems try to improve the agent by training on tool-using trajectories and preferences over better versus worse research behavior.

Going Beyond Shallow Retrieval

A deep research loop needs a way to notice that the current evidence is insufficient and then keep digging. This is the lesson illustrated by WebThinker. Its core idea is not merely “search the web.” It interleaves reasoning, recursive navigation, evidence accumulation, and report drafting inside one loop.

That matters because retrieval depth changes what information is even available to the writer. If the system cannot click deeper, inspect linked pages, and revise its draft as evidence arrives, it never develops a rich enough picture of the topic to produce a serious report.

The strongest support for this idea in the compact input is an ablation result: removing the Deep Web Explorer or link clicking lowers complex-task performance, and removing autonomous drafting hurts report quality the most. That is a useful conceptual result because it shows the loop is not just “search first, write later.” The drafting component is part of why retrieval stays focused.

Keeping the Draft Alive

Long-form research often fails because the draft and the search history drift apart. The system finds facts, but they never become part of a stable working picture. Or it keeps revising locally while losing the global structure of the report.

That is the bottleneck taught most clearly by Test-Time Diffusion Deep Researcher. Its draft-first loop treats the report as a living sketch. Search fills gaps in the sketch, retrieval corrects it, and revision denoises early mistakes instead of forcing the model to regenerate the whole answer from scratch each time.

The important idea here is not diffusion as branding. It is draft-guided retrieval. The evolving report becomes a control surface for later tool use. If the draft is stale or disconnected from retrieval, the agent wastes searches on questions that no longer matter.

The compact input gives one strong anchor for this pattern: TTD-DR reports a 69.1% win rate on LongForm Research and 74.5% on DeepConsult against OpenAI Deep Research. The exact benchmark details matter less than the conceptual takeaway that an explicit evolving draft can improve both coherence and search efficiency.

Making Long-Horizon Loops Reliable

Once a research task involves decomposition, browsing, extraction, verification, and retries, a single monolithic loop becomes hard to trust. This is where modularity becomes a practical control strategy rather than an architectural preference.

Cognitive Kernel-Pro illustrates this well. Its lesson is that long-horizon research improves when orchestration and execution are separated into roles that can be trained and checked more directly. A main agent decomposes the task, sub-agents handle specialized web or file work, and training uses verifiable trajectories instead of only end-to-end imitation.

The paper’s reinforcement idea can be stated in plain language: give the model credit both for making local progress on a subgoal and for reaching the final correct outcome. That matters because pure final reward is often too weak for long, noisy tool trajectories. Local signals help the agent stay on track before the whole episode collapses.

The evidence in the compact input is thinner here than for WebThinker or TTD-DR, but it is still enough to anchor the claim: Cognitive Kernel-Pro reports state-of-the-art results among open-source and free agents on GAIA, and its subgoal-aware reinforcement plus verification improves pass@3 further. The key point is that reliability comes from role separation and verifiable intermediate progress, not only from scaling the backbone model.

Teaching the Agent to Reason With Tools

Tool-use reasoning becomes even harder when the evidence is multimodal or when the interaction chain is very long.

WebWatcher highlights the multimodal side of the problem. A deep research agent that must combine image clues, text search, OCR, page visits, and code execution cannot rely on plain instruction tuning alone. It needs tool-use trajectories that show how cross-modal evidence should be gathered and combined. The shared lesson is that multimodal research is not just “add images.” It changes the planning problem.

Tongyi DeepResearch emphasizes a different part of the same challenge: stable environments, synthetic trajectories, and compressed running summaries. Its lesson is that tool-use instability is often a systems problem. If memory compression, training data quality, and the tool environment are weak, long-horizon autonomy becomes brittle no matter how capable the base model is.

MiroThinker v1.0 pushes on interaction depth directly. Its central claim is that better research agents can come from deeper agent-environment interaction, not just larger models or longer prompts. That is useful because it reframes “scaling” as a third axis: how many meaningful search, check, and synthesis steps the system can survive before context or tool quality degrades.

What the Evidence Suggests

The compact input supports a few durable conclusions.

First, interleaving search and drafting is not cosmetic. WebThinker reports that removing deep exploration or autonomous drafting measurably hurts the outcome, and its reported scores also show that the same loop can stay competitive across reasoning and report-generation settings.

Second, explicit draft revision is a real control mechanism, not just a writing preference. TTD-DR’s win rates suggest that retrieval-guided revision can outperform a simpler search-then-write workflow on long-form research tasks.

Third, long-horizon quality depends on interaction management. MiroThinker reports strong results while explicitly treating interaction depth as a scaling dimension, and WebWatcher shows that multimodal research performance benefits from curated tool trajectories rather than only better base perception.

This is enough evidence to support the family-level concept, but the compact input does not give equally deep quantitative detail for every method. That is fine for this tutorial, because the main lesson is structural: these systems work by tightening the loop between search, state, and synthesis.

What to Remember

Deep research agents are not defined by having a search box attached to a language model. They are defined by whether they can keep a research loop coherent over time.

The important design question is usually not “Which method name should I copy?” It is “Where does my loop fail?” If the system stops too early, deepen retrieval. If it forgets why it searched, make the draft an active memory. If it drifts over long tasks, separate roles and reward intermediate progress. If it misuses tools, train on trajectories that teach when and how to act.

The common theme is not a single algorithm. It is a shared interface problem: keeping planning, evidence gathering, working memory, and report writing synchronized long enough to produce grounded long-form answers.
