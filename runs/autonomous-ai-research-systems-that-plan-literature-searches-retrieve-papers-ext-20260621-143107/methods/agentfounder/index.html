AgentFounder: Agentic Continual Pre-Training for Deep Research Agents

AgentFounder is not trying to invent a new search policy from scratch. It is trying to move agent behavior earlier in the training life cycle. The paper’s core claim is that a deep research model should not wait until post-training to learn how to plan, choose tools, and keep a long investigation coherent. Those behaviors are too important to be learned only from a thin layer of trajectories at the end. AgentFounder therefore inserts an agent-centric continual pre-training stage between base pre-training and downstream post-training, so the foundation model already has a bias toward acting like a researcher before alignment begins.

What to notice in Figure 2 is the new middle block. The model still starts with ordinary pre-training, but the paper adds a second stage whose job is to teach agentic habits at scale. Post-training does not disappear. It just stops carrying the whole burden alone.

That matters because deep research is not a single skill. It is a stack of smaller habits: make a good first move, keep track of what the tools revealed, revise the plan when the evidence changes, and only then write the answer. If the base model never learns those habits, post-training has to teach both the skill and the job at the same time.

Why post-training alone hits a wall

The paper keeps coming back to the same bottleneck: dual-burden training. A normal supervised or reinforcement learning recipe for an agent has to teach general language competence, task-specific alignment, and long-horizon tool behavior all at once. That is a bad trade. The model is asked to imitate expert trajectories while also discovering how to behave like an agent. It is like teaching someone both the subject matter and the research workflow in the same final pass.

There is a second problem too. Full trajectories are expensive and brittle. If you only train on complete traces, the model can end up copying one lucky route instead of learning how to make good decisions in a new state. AgentFounder’s answer is to break the trajectory into reusable pieces and train those pieces before post-training.

Turning broad knowledge into research-style questions

The first step is to turn ordinary corpora and old trajectories into a living memory of entities and facts. AgentFounder does not try to build a classic knowledge graph with rigid relationships. Instead, it creates an entity-anchored memory where each entity points to a dense set of declarative statements. The point is density, not schema. One entity can carry many recent, specific, and overlapping facts, which makes it easier to synthesize questions that feel like real research prompts.

What to notice here is that the same memory can produce many different question styles. A single entity cluster can lead to a factual lookup, a numeric query, a multi-hop comparison, or a synthesis task. That variety matters because the model should practice different kinds of investigations, not just different phrasings of the same prompt.

A useful way to think about this is that the paper is converting static knowledge into dynamic problem setups. The raw source material might just be a pile of facts, search snippets, and old rollouts. Once it is attached to entity anchors, it can be sampled into question forms that look much more like the situations a research agent will face at test time.

The paper prefers this to hand-built relation graphs because rich statement clusters can already create natural intersections. The agent does not need an explicit edge list to learn that several facts about the same entity can be combined into a useful query.

First-order action synthesis: teach the model the first move

Once the questions exist, the next challenge is obvious: a question alone is not enough. A research agent also needs to learn what to do first.

That is what first-order action synthesis, or FAS, is for. FAS builds short planning and reasoning targets without actually calling external tools. Instead of paying for live search or browsing during data generation, the paper uses offline synthesis to create the kind of first-step thinking a research agent should practice.

Figure 4 is the bridge from question to action. The important thing is not the exact wording of the generated sample. The important thing is that the model is being taught the opening move: what to inspect, what to search for, and what the next step should probably be.

The planning branch works like this. Given a question, the model produces several analysis-and-action candidates. Those candidates are not full trajectories. They are first-step plans. The paper then improves diversity by varying the questions themselves rather than just sampling many answers to the same question. That is a smart move. It means the model sees more contexts, not just more paraphrases.

There is a simple intuition behind this choice. If you always ask the same question, the model can start memorizing the wording. If you vary the question but keep the same underlying memory cluster, the model has to learn the underlying decision pattern instead of the surface form.

The paper also filters bad samples with a judge that checks whether the proposed reasoning and action are likely to reach the needed knowledge. That judge is not pretending to prove correctness. It is simply removing the obvious misses so the training set keeps the useful opening moves and drops the low-quality ones.

The reasoning branch is similar, but it aims at a different skill. Instead of first-step tool use, it teaches the model how to reason from a question toward an answer when the evidence is already known in principle.

The trick is a two-step generation process. First, the model breaks the question into smaller sub-questions and writes tentative answers from its internal knowledge. Then it revises those tentative answers using the question and the mapped knowledge needed to solve it. The paper explicitly avoids external tools during this synthesis. That matters because if the answer facts are handed over too directly, the model can cheat by stitching them together mechanically instead of learning how to think through the problem.

So FAS is really teaching two things at once:

  • how to make a plausible first move,
  • how to build a short internal reasoning chain that leads toward the answer.

That is enough to give the base model agent-like instincts without requiring full live trajectories for every sample.

High-order action synthesis: turn trajectories into decisions

FAS teaches the first move. High-order action synthesis, or HAS, teaches something harder: how to make choices inside a long trajectory.

This is where the paper addresses trajectory scarcity and the wastefulness of trajectory-level supervision. In many post-training setups, a long rollout is either kept or thrown away as a whole. That is coarse. A single trajectory can contain useful local decisions even if the final result is imperfect. HAS tries to reuse those local decisions instead of discarding the whole trace.

What to notice in Figure 5 is that the original trace is no longer treated as a brittle transcript. It is rewritten as a decision process. For each step, the paper expands the local option set, shuffles the plausible choices, and makes the model explicitly pick the original one before showing the real tool response. The final sample then ends with a simple correct-or-incorrect judgment.

That may sound like a small formatting change, but it changes the learning problem in a big way. The model is no longer just copying a sequence of actions. It is practicing step-wise decision making under uncertainty.

A good way to picture this is to imagine a research investigation where, at one point, several plausible next moves exist:

  • search a different query,
  • inspect a source page,
  • refine the question,
  • or stop and synthesize what has already been learned.

A trajectory clone would only teach the single move that happened in the original trace. HAS teaches the choice point itself. That makes the data richer and the model less likely to overfit to one brittle path.

This is also why the paper stresses that every step sits in its own context. The question, the prior steps, and the actual feedback from the environment define a new decision state. HAS turns that state into a training example. The method keeps the task intent, the decision context, and the tool-use structure, but it discards the rigidity of one exact trajectory.

The result is a better use of old trajectories. Instead of one episode training one episode, a trajectory becomes a family of local decision samples.

Why the training is split into two stages

AgentFounder does not throw all of this data at the model in one shot. It uses a two-stage continual pre-training schedule.

The first stage focuses on FAS and shorter HAS sequences in a 32K context window. That is the efficient stage. It teaches the model how to make agentic moves without forcing it to absorb very long histories immediately.

The second stage shifts to high-quality HAS data and extends the context window to 128K. This is where the model learns more realistic long-horizon exploration. Now it can see richer decision histories and longer chains of tool use, which matters for deep research tasks that do not resolve in a few turns.

The curriculum logic is pretty clear:

  • first teach the model how to act like an agent,
  • then teach it how to stay coherent over longer investigations.

That progression is important because long-context training is expensive. If the model does not already have some agentic bias, paying for long sequences is wasteful. The two-stage schedule makes the long-context phase feel like a refinement rather than a rescue mission.

What changes downstream

The strongest sign that the method works is not just the final benchmark score. It is that the base model becomes easier to fine-tune afterward.

The paper reports that the agentic base model reduces downstream supervised fine-tuning loss and scales smoothly with both model size and training data. In other words, the model is not only learning task outputs. It is learning a better starting state for agentic alignment.

The reported results line up with that story. AgentFounder-30B reaches 39.9 percent on BrowseComp-en, and the paper also shows strong gains on broader deep research tasks such as GAIA. But the more interesting result is structural: the model behaves like a better foundation for post-training because the agentic habits are already present.

That is the main payoff of Agentic CPT. It moves part of the learning burden out of the most fragile stage and into a stage where broad unsupervised training can build the right inductive bias.

Where it can fail

The method is not free.

It depends on synthetic data quality, which means rejection filtering has real consequences. If the synthesized plans and reasoning chains are noisy, the model learns the wrong habits. The paper tries to control that with judge-based filtering, but the whole pipeline still lives or dies by the quality of the synthetic recipe.

The evaluation setup also leans on Google Search-style tooling assumptions. That means some of the measured gains may depend on how well the search stack behaves, especially across languages and regions.

And of course long-context training is costly. The second stage is useful, but it is not cheap. This is the price of teaching more realistic exploration histories.

There is also an open generalization question. The paper is convincing for web research and tool-using agents, but it is still open how far the same synthetic recipe transfers to other tool ecosystems or to tasks that are less about search and more about different forms of action.

What to remember

AgentFounder’s lesson is simple but important: if a model needs to plan, search, and decide over many steps, do not wait until post-training to teach those behaviors.

The paper pre-aligns the model with agent behavior first. It does that by:

  • turning broad knowledge into many question styles,
  • synthesizing first moves and short reasoning chains offline,
  • rewriting full trajectories as local decision problems,
  • and then training in a short-context stage followed by a long-context stage.

That is why the method feels different from ordinary supervised or reinforcement learning. It is not just teaching the model how to answer. It is teaching the model how to start, how to continue, and how to choose at each step so post-training has a foundation worth building on.
