WebWeaver: A Dual-Agent Framework for Open-Ended Deep Research

WebWeaver is easiest to understand as a research system that refuses to freeze the plan too early. That is the paper’s main idea. Instead of treating searching and writing as two separate jobs, it lets them shape each other. The planner keeps collecting evidence, the outline keeps changing as new facts appear, and the writer only begins section-by-section synthesis once the outline has enough grounded structure to guide it.

That may sound like a small change, but it attacks two of the biggest failure modes in open-ended deep research. If you fix the outline before you have enough evidence, the search gets trapped inside a weak scaffold. If you throw every page and snippet into one giant final prompt, the model has to fight noise, redundancy, and long-context distraction. WebWeaver is built to avoid both.

The whole workflow fits a simple picture:

Why a fixed outline is not enough

The paper begins by pushing back on two common patterns. The first is search-then-generate: gather a pile of sources first, then write the report in one pass. The second is static outline-guided research: decide the outline up front, then search to fill it in. Both sound reasonable, but both break when the question is broad and open-ended.

A static outline can lock the agent into the model’s initial guess about the topic. That guess is often incomplete or outdated. A search-first pipeline has the opposite problem: it collects evidence before it knows what structure the final answer should have, so the search can become broad without being focused. WebWeaver tries to get out of that trap by making the outline and search strategy co-evolve.

Think of it like researching with a notebook that keeps rewriting its own table of contents. Every new discovery can change what the next search should look for. Every new section in the outline can reveal a gap that the next search must fill. That feedback loop is the real contribution.

The planner is a moving target, not a one-shot outline

WebWeaver splits the research job into two agents. The planner is responsible for discovery and structure. It runs in a ReAct-style loop, so each turn has a thought, an action, and an observation. The planner’s action space is small on purpose: search, write outline, or terminate.

The search step is more than a raw web query. The planner asks the search engine for candidates, sees titles and snippets, and then filters the results so only promising pages get read. For the pages that survive, the system does two different things at once:

  • it writes a short summary that stays in the planner’s live context, so the next search can build on what was learned;
  • it extracts more concrete evidence, such as useful claims or details, and stores that evidence in a memory bank.

That split matters. The summary keeps the planner oriented. The memory bank keeps the proof. The planner does not need to drag every raw page through every turn.

Once some evidence is in hand, the planner switches to outline optimization. This is not a one-and-done drafting step. The outline can grow, shrink, gain new sections, or be reorganized if the evidence suggests a better structure. Most importantly, the outline is citation-grounded: each section points to specific evidence IDs in memory. The outline is therefore not just a table of contents. It is the map that tells the writer where support already exists and where more work is still needed.

The workflow is really a discovery loop

What to notice is the division of labor. The left side is exploratory: search, summarize, refine, repeat. The right side is selective: retrieve only what the section needs, then write. The model is not asked to keep one giant context alive forever. It is asked to keep the right parts alive at the right time.

That is what makes the method feel human. A good researcher does not reread every note before writing each paragraph. They keep a working outline, pull the relevant source notes for the current section, and then move on once that section is done. WebWeaver is trying to give the agent the same habit.

The writer only sees the evidence a section needs

After planning is finished, the writer takes over. The writer also uses a ReAct-style loop, but its action space is different: retrieve, write, or terminate. That difference is important. The writer is not trying to discover the topic from scratch. It is trying to turn a structured, citation-linked outline into a coherent report.

The writing process is hierarchical. The writer handles one section at a time instead of trying to compose the whole report at once. For each section, it first retrieves the evidence that the outline points to. Then it thinks about how that evidence should be organized. Only after that does it write the prose.

The paper’s internal control flow here is worth keeping in mind:

The pruning step is easy to miss, but it is one of the most useful details in the whole method. Once a section is written, the used source material is removed from the live context and replaced with a placeholder. That keeps the next section from inheriting stale notes and prevents different parts of the report from bleeding into one another.

This is the main reason hierarchical writing works better than brute-force writing. The model is not asked to sift through the entire memory bank for every paragraph. It gets a smaller, cleaner slice of evidence for the task it is doing right now. That makes the section more focused, improves coherence, and reduces the chance that unrelated facts leak into the wrong place.

Why the memory bank matters

The memory bank is the other half of the design. Web research can easily become huge. The planner may search and parse more than a hundred pages, and the total context can grow far beyond what it would be wise to keep in one prompt. The memory bank lets the system keep that material without paying the full attention cost all the time.

The planner only needs short summaries during search. The writer only needs the exact evidence linked to the current section. Everything else can stay stored until it becomes relevant again. That is a much healthier way to manage long research runs than stuffing raw pages into a single context window and hoping the model can sort them out.

This also explains why the paper is so worried about long-context failure modes. The issue is not just token count. It is attentional overload. If too much unrelated material stays live, the model starts missing important details, mixing sections together, and drifting away from the report’s structure. WebWeaver’s memory-grounded design is basically a control strategy for avoiding that kind of drift.

What changes compared with older pipelines

The best way to remember WebWeaver is to compare it with the pipelines it replaces.

In a search-then-generate system, the search phase and the writing phase are disconnected. The system may retrieve a lot of useful information, but it still has to invent the structure at the end. That often produces a report that is knowledgeable but incoherent.

In a static outline system, the structure comes first, but the structure may be wrong. The search is then forced to obey an outline that was designed before the evidence was known. That can make the research look organized while quietly missing better angles.

WebWeaver keeps both problems in view at once. The planner is allowed to change the outline as evidence arrives. The writer is allowed to retrieve only the evidence needed for the current section. That is what makes the system more robust than a single-pass retrieve-and-write pipeline.

The paper’s results support that intuition. WebWeaver reaches very strong citation accuracy on DeepResearch Bench, which is exactly the kind of metric this design is trying to improve. If the outline is grounded and the writer only sees the section-specific evidence, the model has a much better chance of keeping the citations honest.

Where the method is strong, and where it gets expensive

WebWeaver is not a free lunch. The more you separate planning, memory management, and section writing, the more orchestration you need. Search quality matters. The memory organization matters. The planner has to be good enough to keep revising the outline in a useful direction instead of wandering forever. So the method buys reliability by adding system complexity.

That tradeoff is visible in the paper’s own framing. The approach is aimed at open-ended deep research tasks, where long horizon search and long-form writing are both necessary. It is not presented as a universal fix for every knowledge task. The strongest evidence is on OEDR-style benchmarks, where broad discovery and citation-grounded synthesis are the whole game.

So the right mental model is not “more agentic is always better.” The right model is “separate discovery from synthesis, but let them keep talking to each other.” That is the part WebWeaver gets right.

What to remember

WebWeaver is a two-agent research loop. The planner searches, stores evidence, and keeps refining a citation-grounded outline. The writer then uses that outline to retrieve only the evidence each section needs, writes one section at a time, and prunes the used context so the next section stays clean.

If you remember one sentence, make it this: discovery and synthesis should co-evolve instead of being frozen into separate one-way stages. That is the core idea WebWeaver turns into a working system.
