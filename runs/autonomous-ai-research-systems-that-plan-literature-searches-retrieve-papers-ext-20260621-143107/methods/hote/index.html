Hybrid Open-Ended Tri-Evolution Makes Better Deep Researcher

Open-ended deep research is hard to train because there is no single gold answer waiting at the end. A long report can be useful even when several parts are phrased differently, and a question can be solved in more than one valid way. HOTE is the paper’s answer to that problem. Instead of training only the answerer, it lets three parts of the system improve together: the solver that writes the research report, the judge that scores and reshapes the rubric, and the proposer that keeps finding harder but still solvable tasks.

That is the real shift. HOTE does not treat deep research as a static benchmark with a fixed answer key. It treats it as a living loop. The questions change, the scorecard changes, and the report-writing policy changes with them. When that loop works, the agent is not just getting better at one task. It is learning how to keep discovering what the next task should be.

The system starts with a simple research loop

The paper builds on a concise ReAct-style setup, which is a good choice here because it keeps the control flow easy to inspect. The solver can think, call a search tool, write an answer, and attach citations. The proposer follows the same general pattern when it is generating new tasks, but it does not need citations because it is not producing the final report. HOTE also runs in two operating modes: tool-use and no-tool.

What to notice in Figure 1 is that the same agent family can be trained in two very different ways. In tool-use mode, the model learns how to search, read, and cite evidence. In no-tool mode, it learns how to write a coherent report directly from its internal state. That split matters because a strong research agent needs both habits. It needs grounding when the search tools are useful, but it also needs the ability to synthesize cleanly when the search path is noisy or unnecessary.

The judge is not just scoring, it is rewriting the scorecard

The most unusual part of HOTE is the judge. In many agent systems, the judge is a fixed evaluator or a brittle verifier. HOTE uses a different idea. The judge looks at several solver responses for the same research task, then updates the task’s rubrics based on what those responses reveal.

The key distinction is between persistent rubrics and active rubrics. Persistent rubrics stay in place because they capture stable task requirements. Active rubrics are the flexible part. They can be added, removed, or revised when the solver exposes a new strength, a missing criterion, or a recurring shortcut. That is how the evaluation keeps up with an open-ended task instead of freezing into a stale checklist.

The judge also creates two kinds of rubric updates. Positive rubrics capture useful new knowledge or good behaviors that should be kept. Negative rubrics capture weak habits, reward hacking, or other ways the solver tried to look good without really improving the report. This is a practical defense against a very real failure mode: if the scoring rules stay fixed, the solver can slowly learn to game them.

The reward for each response comes from combining the rubric votes with their weights. Each individual rubric vote is simple, but the weighted blend gives the solver a smoother training signal than a single yes-or-no verdict would. That is important because open-ended research reports are not binary objects. They are bundles of coverage, clarity, relevance, and synthesis quality.

One subtle but important design choice is that the judge itself is not constantly being retrained. Instead, HOTE uses a fixed instruction model for the judge and lets the rubric set do most of the evolving. That keeps training cheaper and focuses the evolution on what really needs to move: the evaluation criteria.

The proposer keeps the tasks hard, but not impossible

If the judge is the examiner, the proposer is the tutor who keeps writing better exam questions. Its job is not to be adversarial for its own sake. It is supposed to find research tasks that expose the solver’s weaknesses while still leaving a path to success. Too easy, and the solver learns nothing. Too hard, and the training signal disappears.

HOTE makes that balance explicit with meta rubrics. The judge takes the long, detailed assessments from the solver and compresses them into shorter shared rubrics. Those meta rubrics serve two purposes at once. First, they give the proposer a compact summary of what the solver is still bad at. Second, they create a shared basis for comparing multiple proposed tasks against one another.

That shared basis matters more than it may first appear. Without it, every proposed task is evaluated in a slightly different local frame, and the proposer has a hard time learning which kind of challenge is actually useful. With meta rubrics, the proposer can see a common pattern of weakness and move toward tasks that are difficult in the right way.

The reward signal for the proposer is therefore not “make harder questions” in the abstract. It is closer to “make questions that are challenging, but still solvable by a capable solver.” The proposer is rewarded when the task reveals a weakness, yet some solver responses still manage to satisfy the relevant rubrics. That is the sweet spot HOTE wants.

There is one more guardrail here: diverse proposing. If the proposer only keeps chasing the same narrow failure mode, the synthetic curriculum collapses into one topic and the solver stops getting broad practice. HOTE avoids that by sampling multiple combinations of tasks and assessments each step, so the next batch is not just a repetition of the last one.

Why the hybrid training mode matters

HOTE would be less interesting if it only changed the judge and proposer. The other important idea is the hybrid training schedule. The paper finds that tool-use training and no-tool training each have a different strength, so it mixes them instead of picking one.

No-tool training is surprisingly valuable because it teaches the model the shape of a good research report without forcing every step through a search tool. That can make the writing cleaner and faster to learn. But no-tool training by itself can push the model too far toward internal memory and away from grounded retrieval. Tool-use training fixes that by keeping the system honest, but it is slower and more sensitive to search noise.

HOTE uses both. The paper first warms the solver up with no-tool training, then switches to a hybrid phase where half the batch uses no-tool and half uses tool-use. That ordering makes sense. The model first learns how a good report should read, then it learns how to keep that quality while pulling in evidence from tools. The end result is better research behavior and lower average training time per step than a tool-use-only setup.

This is one of the nicer parts of the paper because it turns a practical nuisance into a design principle. Search tools are useful, but they are not always the right place to spend compute. Mixing the two modes lets the model benefit from both the simplicity of direct synthesis and the grounding of tool-assisted research.

The training loop is a conversation, not a one-way pass

The full HOTE loop is easy to remember once you see the roles clearly. Real and synthetic tasks are mixed in the same batch. The solver responds in either tool-use or no-tool mode. The judge updates the active rubrics and scores the responses. The solver learns from those scores. The judge compresses the assessment into meta rubrics. The proposer uses that compressed feedback to invent the next round of tasks.

That means the system is always learning in three directions at once.

  • The solver learns to produce better reports.
  • The judge learns which criteria matter and which shortcuts should be blocked.
  • The proposer learns which questions are hard in a useful way.

The interesting part is that these three directions are coupled. A stronger solver forces the judge to become more discriminating. A sharper judge gives the proposer a better map of weaknesses. A better proposer pushes the solver into harder territory. This is why the authors call it tri-evolution rather than just multi-agent training.

If you want a good mental model, imagine a tutor, student, and examiner improving together. The examiner updates the rubric, the student practices writing better reports, and the tutor keeps inventing questions that expose the student’s blind spots. The loop only works because all three parts keep adapting.

What the experiments are really showing

The experimental story is straightforward once the method is understood. HOTE-8B outperforms stronger open 8B to 32B baselines and state-of-the-art deep research training methods on HealthBench, ResearchQA, and DRB. The paper also shows that dropping judge evolution or dropping proposer evolution weakens the result, which is important because it means the tri-evolution setup is not decorative. Each module carries real weight.

The hybrid mode adds another practical win. Because the later training phase only uses tool calls for part of the batch, the average time per step is lower than tool-use-only baselines. So HOTE is not just chasing a better score. It is also getting a more efficient training loop.

The ablations line up with the intuition from the method. If the proposer gets too static, the synthetic tasks drift toward repetition and the solver stops being challenged. If the judge stays static, reward hacking and weak rubrics start to dominate. If the hybrid mode is removed, the model either over-relies on tools or over-relies on parametric memory. The full system is better because it keeps all of those pressures in balance.

Where the idea still bends

The paper is also clear about what HOTE does not solve. Evolution slows down over time, so the method does not keep improving forever. It still depends on the initial training data, which means it is not a pure zero-data discovery system. And scaling the recipe to larger models remains untested.

Those limits are important because they keep the paper honest. HOTE is a strong recipe for open-ended research agents, but it is not a complete answer to autonomy. It improves the loop, but it does not eliminate the need for good starting data, good tools, or good scaling behavior.

What to remember

The simplest way to remember HOTE is this: open-ended research agents improve faster when the task generator, the evaluator, and the answerer evolve together.

The solver learns to write better reports. The judge learns to score them with rubrics that move as the task gets harder. The proposer learns to create the next question that is hard enough to matter, but still solvable enough to teach from. And the hybrid tool-use plus no-tool schedule keeps that learning grounded without making every step pay the full search cost.

If you keep one sentence from the paper, make it this: when there is no gold answer, do not freeze the exam. Let the questions, the rubric, and the answerer co-evolve.
