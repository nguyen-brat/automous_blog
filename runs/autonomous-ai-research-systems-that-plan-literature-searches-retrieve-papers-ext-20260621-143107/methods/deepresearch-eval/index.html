DeepResearchEval: Automated Task Construction and Agentic Evaluation for Deep Research Systems

DeepResearchEval is easiest to understand as a benchmark that refuses to be passive. Instead of asking humans to hand-design a fixed test set and then score reports with one static rubric, the paper builds a pipeline that can generate hard research tasks, filter out the shallow ones, and evaluate the final reports in a way that fits the task itself.

That matters because deep research systems are not ordinary question-answering systems. They browse, compare sources, track evidence across pages, and write long reports. A benchmark for that kind of system has to check more than whether the answer sounds good. It has to check whether the task really needed research, whether the report followed the task, and whether the claims survive external verification.

The paper’s core idea is simple once you see the whole flow: first make the task real, then judge the report in a task-aware way, and finally verify the claims one by one.

Making the benchmark feel like a real user request

The task construction side starts from personas, not from abstract benchmark prompts. That is a useful design choice because real deep research requests usually come from someone with a concrete role, domain, and information need. A finance analyst, a policy researcher, and a software engineer do not ask the same kind of question, even if they all want “research.”

The pipeline first synthesizes personas across representative domains, then asks a language model to generate candidate tasks for each persona. The generation constraints are intentionally strict. The tasks should require multiple rounds of web search, combine evidence from different kinds of sources, and demand actual analysis rather than a single lookup. In other words, the prompt should feel like work, not trivia.

What to notice in Figure 2 is the two-step narrowing process. The pipeline does not trust the first generated question just because it looks plausible. It keeps asking, “Does this really require live research?” and “Could a strong model answer this from internal knowledge alone?” Only tasks that survive both questions become benchmark material.

That second question is especially important. A prompt can look sophisticated and still be easy to answer from memory. If that happens, the benchmark stops measuring deep research and starts measuring the model’s parametric knowledge. DeepResearchEval explicitly tries to avoid that failure mode.

The paper’s filtering stage uses two gates for exactly that reason. First comes a task qualification check, which asks whether the candidate genuinely needs up-to-date evidence, multi-source synthesis, and persona fit. Then comes a search necessity check, which makes a model attempt the task without tools. If the no-search attempt already looks strong, the task is dropped. The benchmark only keeps questions that actually force retrieval.

That design gives the dataset a nice property: the benchmark is not frozen in time. Because the task generator can keep producing new persona-grounded prompts, the framework can behave like a live benchmark rather than a one-off static list.

Why the rubric changes with the task

Once a system has produced a long report, the evaluator faces a harder problem than ordinary QA. A deep research report can be well written, topically relevant, and still miss the exact thing the task asked for. Static rubrics tend to blur those distinctions. They also overvalue generic qualities that are nice everywhere but not equally important everywhere.

DeepResearchEval solves that by splitting evaluation into two parts. The first part is a task-adaptive quality rubric. The second is active fact-checking. They are related, but they are not the same.

What to notice in this overview is the clean separation between “how good is the report for this task?” and “are the factual claims actually true?” The paper treats those as separate jobs because a fluent report can still be wrong, and a factual report can still be thin or poorly targeted.

The quality side starts with a small set of general dimensions that matter across tasks: coverage, insight, instruction-following, and clarity. Those are the basics of a good research report. Coverage asks whether the report actually reaches the relevant material. Insight asks whether it does more than restate sources. Instruction-following checks whether it answers the specific request. Clarity checks whether the reader can follow the structure.

The important move is that the evaluator does not stop there. It also generates task-specific dimensions for the particular prompt. That means the rubric can adapt when the task calls for things like metric choice, comparative synthesis, trend interpretation, or domain-specific framing. A policy comparison task and a literature synthesis task should not be judged by exactly the same emphasis.

This is where the “point-wise” part matters. The judge does not jump straight to one global score. It scores smaller criteria first, then rolls those into a dimension score, and then combines the dimensions according to their task importance. That gives the evaluator two useful things at once: interpretability and flexibility.

In plain language, the rubric works like a layered checklist. First ask whether the report did the obvious general things well. Then ask what this exact task needed that generic reporting would miss. Then weight those answers so the report is judged in context, not against a one-size-fits-all template.

That is the main reason the paper finds task-specific scores to be more demanding than the general ones. A deep research system can look strong on broad qualities while still missing the particular success condition that mattered for the task. DeepResearchEval is built to expose that gap instead of hiding it.

Checking claims instead of just citations

The second half of the pipeline is more aggressive than citation checking. It does not ask, “Does the report cite something?” It asks, “Can we verify what the report actually says?”

That distinction matters because citation-only evaluation leaves a blind spot. A report can contain uncited claims. It can cite a source that is related but not actually supportive. It can also mix a true claim and a weakly grounded claim in the same paragraph. If the evaluator only checks the citation links, those problems can slip through.

DeepResearchEval’s answer is active fact-checking. The verifier first breaks the report into smaller parts so the long context is manageable. Then it extracts checkable statements from each part, focusing on claims with concrete entities: numbers, dates, events, locations, names, and similar details. That is the level where factual verification becomes meaningful.

From there, each statement is checked against external evidence. The verifier searches the web, reads relevant pages, and compares the statement with the retrieved evidence while still keeping the surrounding report segment and task context in view. That last detail is important. The checker is not looking at isolated claims in a vacuum; it is judging them in the context of the original research task.

The result is a three-way label:

  • Right, when the evidence supports the statement.
  • Wrong, when the evidence contradicts it.
  • Unknown, when the verifier cannot find enough evidence either way.

That Unknown label is not a throwaway category. It is one of the most useful parts of the design, because it separates unsupported claims from confirmed errors. In research reports, those are different failures. A claim can be risky because it is false, or because it simply does not have enough evidence behind it yet.

The output is returned in structured JSON, which makes the fact-checking stage usable as an analysis tool rather than just a score generator. The benchmark can then report the share of statements labeled Right, along with the balance of Wrong and Unknown claims. That is a much better picture of factual reliability than a single pass/fail number.

Why the split between quality and factuality is the right one

The paper’s evaluation design is stronger because it refuses to collapse everything into one score too early.

Quality scoring captures whether the report is the right kind of answer for the task. Factual checking captures whether the answer is grounded. Those are related but not interchangeable. A report can be carefully structured and still miss important facts. It can also be factually cautious and still fail to answer the user’s actual request.

By separating the two, DeepResearchEval makes the evaluation easier to interpret. If a system scores well on the rubric but poorly on factuality, the problem is likely unsupported claims or citation hygiene. If factuality is strong but quality is weak, the system may be accurate but not useful enough. If both are weak, the system is probably not handling the task well at all.

That separation also explains why the benchmark can work without a gold reference report. The task-adaptive rubric handles usefulness, and active fact-checking handles truthfulness. Together, they reduce the need for hand-built reference answers while still giving a detailed view of system behavior.

What the validation is really showing

The paper does not stop at proposing the pipeline. It also checks whether the evaluator behaves sensibly. The quality scorer is compared across judges and across repeated runs, and the factual verifier is checked against human annotations.

The broad message is that the framework is stable enough to be useful and strict enough to catch real weaknesses. That is important because an evaluation system is only as good as the judgments it makes. If the rubric changes too much from run to run, or if the fact-checker is too noisy, the benchmark becomes hard to trust.

The validation also supports a practical intuition: a more demanding verifier often finds more weaknesses than a purely citation-based check. That does not mean every machine judgment is perfect. It does mean the benchmark is looking at the right failure surface. The paper is trying to catch unsupported claims, not just polished writing.

Where it is strong, and where it bends

DeepResearchEval is a strong step forward, but it comes with obvious tradeoffs.

The first tradeoff is cost. Active verification is expensive because it relies on repeated web searches and tool-driven checking. Task-adaptive scoring also depends on frontier-model judgments. That makes the pipeline heavier than a simple static benchmark.

The second tradeoff is language coverage. The framework is conceptually language-agnostic, but the current setup is still grounded in English-speaking evidence ecosystems. Multilingual research remains an open area.

The third tradeoff is scalability. Multi-turn fact-checking is powerful precisely because it is thorough, but that same thoroughness limits how cheaply the benchmark can be run at large scale.

So the paper is not claiming that this is the cheapest possible evaluator. It is claiming that deep research deserves a more faithful one.

What to remember

The easiest way to remember DeepResearchEval is as a three-step filter:

  1. Make the task look like a real user’s research need.
  2. Judge the report with a rubric that adapts to that exact task.
  3. Verify the factual claims with external evidence, not just citations.

That is the big contribution. The paper is not only proposing another benchmark. It is proposing a reusable way to create hard research tasks and then evaluate them in a way that matches how deep research systems are actually used.
