PaperQA: Retrieval-Augmented Generative Agent for Scientific Question Answering

PaperQA starts from a simple but important idea: scientific question answering should act like a careful literature researcher, not a chatbot that guesses from memory. Instead of asking one retrieved snippet to carry the whole answer, PaperQA searches papers, gathers the best passages, scores them, and only then writes a cited response.

The workflow diagram makes the control loop easy to see.

What to notice in the figure is the separation of jobs. Search, evidence gathering, and answering are not one blurred block. They are three different decisions, and that gives the agent room to go back and search again when the first pass is not enough.

Why the first pass is not enough

Scientific questions often hide the answer in the body of a paper rather than in the abstract. A keyword query may find the right field but the wrong paper. It may find the right paper but miss the sentence that actually answers the question. Or it may return several papers that sound close but do not contain the needed detail.

PaperQA is built around that failure mode. The agent can revise its search terms, gather evidence from multiple papers, and keep going until the evidence stack is strong enough. That is the main reason it is more robust than a fixed retrieval pipeline.

The paper’s benchmark choice makes the point especially well. LitQA is built from recent biomedical papers published after the common training cutoff, and the questions are written from the body of the paper rather than the abstract. The benchmark is trying to force the system to do real literature work, not just parrot familiar facts.

How the system is split into tools

PaperQA turns the job into three tools, each with a narrow role.

  • search finds relevant full-text papers in databases such as arXiv and PubMed. The first search is always run with the full question so the agent starts from a real bibliography instead of an empty workspace. The papers are broken into overlapping chunks and embedded into a vector database.
  • gather_evidence takes the question, retrieves candidate chunks by vector search, and then applies maximal marginal relevance so the shortlist is diverse instead of repetitive. A summary model reads each chunk, scores how useful it is, and the strongest passages are collected into a context library. The agent sees the best chunk, while the broader library stays available for final synthesis.
  • answer_question lets the model surface any useful latent knowledge it already has, then asks the final answer model to write from the evidence library and the question.

That separation is the core architectural move. Search creates breadth. Evidence gathering filters and compresses. Answering turns the filtered evidence into a final response.

The interesting part is the middle step. gather_evidence behaves like the map side of a map-reduce workflow. Each chunk is read in the context of the question, summarized, scored, and either kept or discarded. Because the chunks can be handled independently, this step can also be run in parallel to save time. The result is a small evidence library instead of one giant blob of raw retrieval text.

Why the evidence library matters

A normal RAG system often dumps retrieved text straight into the prompt and hopes the model can sort it out. PaperQA does something more deliberate. It builds a small evidence library first.

That library matters for three reasons.

First, it lets the agent reject junk. If a retrieved chunk is irrelevant, weak, or redundant, it does not need to survive into the final answer.

Second, it makes the system less fragile to PDF parsing problems. The summary model can often salvage meaning from partially garbled text because the question gives it context.

Third, it gives the answer model a curated stack of support instead of a noisy wall of text. That is what makes the final answer feel more like a careful synthesis than a direct completion.

PaperQA also combines vector similarity with a second layer of model judgment. Embeddings are good at finding likely neighbors. They are not enough to decide whether a passage really supports the question. The summary model acts like a second filter and provides the relevance score that decides what stays.

This is also where the a priori and a posteriori prompting idea comes in. Before answering, PaperQA asks the model to surface any useful prior knowledge. After evidence gathering, it asks the final answer model to check the retrieved context and commit only if the evidence supports the response. In plain language: let the model bring its background knowledge to the table, but do not let it freewheel past the sources.

Why LitQA is the right stress test

The paper’s benchmark choice is a big part of why PaperQA is convincing.

LitQA is a 50-question biomedical dataset assembled from recent papers, all after the model cutoff period. The questions are written so that the answer is not sitting in the abstract and is often hard to recover without reading the paper itself. Human researchers were allowed to use the internet and could answer “unsure” when the evidence was not there.

That setup is closer to actual literature work than a standard multiple-choice benchmark. It tests two different skills at once: can the system find the right paper, and can it extract the right passage from that paper?

PaperQA also runs on a blind version of PubMedQA, where the supplied context is removed. That variant is useful because it forces the system to recover evidence on its own instead of leaning on a prebuilt answer bundle.

What the numbers suggest

The reported results line up with the design.

On LitQA, PaperQA reaches 69.5 percent accuracy and 87.9 percent precision, which is close to the expert human precision reported in the paper. On blind PubMedQA, it reaches 86.3 percent, beating the GPT-4 baseline in that setting. In the citation audit, PaperQA produced valid citations every time in the reported evaluation.

The most important lesson in those numbers is not just that the scores are higher. It is that the system knows when it is uncertain. Scientific QA is not a place where a fluent wrong answer is good enough. A system that can say “unsure” and avoid a fake citation is often more useful than a system that always sounds confident.

The paper also compares against AutoGPT and several commercial science search products. The exact ranking is less important than the pattern: the agentic loop, not a one-shot retrieval prompt, is what makes grounded scientific QA work better.

Where the method is still fragile

PaperQA is a strong design, but it is not a magic fix.

It depends on the source papers being correct. If the literature is wrong, the answer can still be wrong. The paper also acknowledges that benchmark answers can drift as science changes, which is a real issue for any literature-grounded system.

There is also a systems cost. The loop is more complex than a fixed retriever, because it depends on multiple prompts, multiple model roles, search quality, and PDF parsing. The paper is honest that prompt and tool design are not guaranteed optimal.

The broader open question is how well this approach generalizes beyond biomedical literature. The idea should transfer, but the paper’s strongest evidence is still in a domain where full-text paper search and citation grounding are especially important.

What to remember

PaperQA is best remembered as a research assistant with an evidence notebook.

It does not stop at one retrieval pass. It searches, gathers, scores, revises, and only then answers. That is the whole point. When the knowledge lives in a large literature, the system needs a control loop that can keep looking until the evidence is good enough to support a cited answer.
