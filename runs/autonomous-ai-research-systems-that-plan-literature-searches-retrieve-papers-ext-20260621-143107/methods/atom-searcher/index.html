Atom-Searcher

Atom-Searcher is built around a simple but important correction to outcome-only RL for deep research: a research agent should not be rewarded only for the final answer. It should also get credit for the quality of the reasoning and search path that led there.

That matters because deep research is not a single-shot QA problem. The model has to decide what to look for, when to search, what to verify, and when the evidence is finally good enough to stop. If the final answer is wrong, a purely outcome-based reward can still erase a useful chain of intermediate reasoning. Atom-Searcher tries to fix that by teaching the agent to think in smaller functional units and by scoring those units directly.

What to notice here is not the tag syntax. The important part is the decomposition. Instead of treating a reasoning burst as one opaque block, the model breaks it into smaller moves such as planning, reflection, verification, or risk checking. That makes the search trajectory easier to inspect, and it gives the reward model something much more concrete to judge.

Why atomic thoughts exist

If you only score the ending, the middle becomes invisible.

That is the core failure mode Atom-Searcher is trying to solve. Suppose the model asks a sensible follow-up question, finds a relevant source, and builds a good partial chain of reasoning, but then misses the exact final span needed for the benchmark answer. Outcome-only RL will still punish the whole episode. The model then learns a misleading lesson: a useful search path is bad if it does not land perfectly.

Atomic Thought changes the unit of analysis. The paper treats reasoning like a complex motion in sports or robotics: you understand it better if you can break it into functional steps instead of judging the whole movement as a blob. In this setting, the functional steps are the smallest coherent pieces of thought that still do real work.

The paper is also careful not to hard-code one universal thought taxonomy. It does not say every task must use the same set of sub-thoughts forever. Instead, it encourages the model to induce the right atomic structure for the task at hand. That matters for generalization. A research question about a biomedical fact and a question about a historical claim do not need identical thought templates, even if both benefit from fine-grained reasoning.

The two-phase pipeline

Once you understand atomic thoughts, the rest of the system becomes much easier to read. Atom-Searcher is trained in two stages.

What to notice in Figure 3 is the division of labor. First the policy is taught to produce atomic thoughts through supervised fine-tuning. Then those atomic thoughts are scored, folded into the final reward, and used to train the policy again with reinforcement learning. That means the model is not being asked to discover the atomic structure and optimize against it at the same time from scratch. It gets a scaffold first.

The agent is trained as a finite search-and-answer episode. The state is the current evidence plus the history of previous actions. The actions are: generate a thought, invoke search, or produce the final answer. The episode ends when the task is complete or when the interaction budget runs out.

How the model learns to speak in atoms

The supervised stage is doing more than warming up the policy. It is teaching the model what an atomic reasoning style looks like.

The paper builds a dataset of about 1,000 annotated examples in a bootstrapped way. It starts from a small set of seed prompts that include example atomic thoughts, expands those prompts with a stronger teacher model, pairs them with questions and search tools, and then samples full reasoning trajectories. High-quality samples are kept with majority voting. The point of all of this is not to create a rigid script. The point is to give the policy enough good examples that it starts to internalize the habit of breaking its own thinking into smaller moves.

This is a subtle but important design choice. If you try to jump directly to RL without that scaffold, the model has to discover both the structure of good reasoning and the reward signal at the same time. That is usually too much ambiguity. The supervised atomic-thought examples reduce that ambiguity by telling the model, in effect, “this is the kind of internal rhythm we want.”

You can think of the dataset as teaching the model how to write notes for itself. The notes are not the final answer. They are the working moves that help the final answer become better.

Turning thought quality into a reward

The next step is the reward model.

Atom-Searcher uses a reasoning reward model, or RRM, to score the atomic thoughts inside a trajectory. The key point is that the RRM is not judging the entire episode as one flat sequence. It is looking at the smaller thought units and assigning a fine-grained score to each one. Those scores are then aggregated into an atomic-thought reward for the whole trajectory.

Why does that help? Because the reward becomes closer to the actual behavior you want. A good query reformulation, a useful verification step, or a careful risk check should not disappear into the noise of the final answer. Fine-grained process reward keeps those useful partial steps visible.

The ablation story makes the idea clearer. Adding the reward model by itself gives only a small improvement over the base system. The bigger jump appears when the reward model is paired with atomic thoughts. That suggests the reward model needs a cleaner structure to latch onto. The atomic decomposition gives it those anchors. Without them, the reward signal is too diffuse to change the search behavior much.

So the real contribution is not just “use a reward model.” It is “make the reasoning legible enough that the reward model can score the right thing.”

Why the process reward fades over time

Atom-Searcher does not keep the process reward weight fixed throughout training. It uses a curriculum-like schedule that gradually reduces the influence of the atomic-thought reward as training progresses.

That schedule matches how the agent actually improves. Early on, the model is still exploring and often gets the final answer wrong even when parts of the reasoning path are useful. In that phase, a stronger process reward is helpful because it prevents useful intermediate reasoning from being crushed by a bad endpoint. Later in training, the policy is better aligned with the task, and too much process supervision can become noisy. At that point, the model should rely more on the final answer signal.

A simple way to remember the logic is: early training needs training wheels, later training needs less hand-holding. Atom-Searcher starts by leaning on process reward and then steadily hands control back to the outcome reward.

The outcome reward itself is still important. The final answer is scored by correctness, with a penalty for malformed outputs. So the model is never allowed to ignore the endpoint. The curriculum just makes the endpoint less lonely during the early phase, when sparse reward would otherwise make the learning signal unstable.

The RL update is careful about what counts as trainable

For reinforcement learning, the paper uses a group-relative update. In plain language, the model samples several trajectories for the same prompt, compares them against one another, and then pushes the policy toward the better ones. A KL penalty keeps the policy from drifting too far away from a reference model.

That relative setup matters for research tasks because a single trajectory is often too noisy to judge on its own. Comparing several rollouts gives the optimizer a better sense of which reasoning path is actually stronger.

The paper also adds two training-stability tricks that are easy to miss but worth understanding.

First, it masks retrieved text out of the loss. That makes sense once you remember where the retrieval text comes from: it is produced by the environment, not by the policy. The model should learn the decision to search and the query it writes, not be updated as if it had authored the returned evidence itself. Masking keeps the policy gradient focused on the model’s own reasoning and search actions.

Second, the appendix adds an entropy guardrail. If the policy entropy drops too sharply too early, exploration can collapse and the model can become overconfident before it has really learned anything robust. The training loop watches that collapse and responds by raising temperature and resampling. In effect, it tries to keep the policy curious long enough to discover better search behavior.

What changes in practice

The method is not just cleaner in theory. It changes the model’s behavior.

The paper reports stronger in-domain and out-of-domain QA performance, including 44.0 on NQ and 50.3 average F1 overall. More interesting than the headline score is the behavioral shift: Atom-Searcher tends to generate more test-time tokens and more tool calls than DeepResearcher. That suggests it has learned to spend extra computation when the question deserves it, instead of rushing to a shallow answer.

The case studies reinforce that picture. The model’s reasoning looks more like a human researcher working through a problem: analyze, hypothesize, verify, assess risk, plan the next step, and search again if needed. That is exactly the kind of behavior the atomic-thought setup is meant to encourage.

What this does not solve

The paper is also honest about the tradeoffs.

Atom-Searcher needs an additional reasoning reward model, so the training stack is more complex than a pure outcome-based setup. It also needs atomic-thought supervision data, which means the method depends on a dataset-building pipeline before RL can even begin.

And the evaluation scope still matters. The paper is tested on QA-style deep research benchmarks, not on fully end-to-end scholarly writing. That means the method is best read as a deep research training recipe, not as a complete solution for every long-form research workflow.

There is also a real open question about transfer. The paper’s central trick should plausibly help other long-horizon search-and-synthesis tasks, but the exact reward schedule and reward model may need retuning when the backbone or the domain changes.

What to remember

Atom-Searcher is easiest to remember as a three-part move:

  • teach the model to think in atomic pieces,
  • score those pieces with a reasoning reward model,
  • fade the process reward over training so final-answer correctness can take over.

That is the paper’s main idea. Instead of judging a research agent only by the last answer, Atom-Searcher tries to reward the search path that makes the answer possible in the first place.

This is an appealing direction for autonomous research systems because it targets the real bottleneck: sparse credit assignment. If the agent only gets feedback at the end, good intermediate reasoning can be lost. If the agent is judged partly on the quality of its reasoning trajectory, it has a much better chance of learning how to search, verify, and synthesize like a careful researcher.
