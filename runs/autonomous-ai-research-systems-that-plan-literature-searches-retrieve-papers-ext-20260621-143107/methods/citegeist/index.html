Citegeist: Dynamic Retrieval Augmented Generation on the arXiv Corpus for Related Work Generation

Citegeist is a related-work generator that behaves less like a chat model and more like a librarian with a funnel. It starts from an abstract or a multi-page draft, searches arXiv for nearby papers, removes near-duplicates, opens only the most promising pages, and then turns that evidence into a cited related-work section. The point is not just to retrieve text. The point is to keep the model grounded while it writes something that still reads like a human literature review.

What to notice in Figure 1 is the shape of the system. It is not a single search box with generation attached. It is a staged funnel. The first stage finds plausible neighbors. The middle stages decide which papers and which pages are actually worth reading. The last stage writes the prose only after the evidence has been compressed enough to fit together.

A literature funnel, not a single prompt

The motivation is easy to understand if you think about how people actually write related work. Nobody reads the whole field at once. You begin with a rough query, find a few plausible neighbors, notice that some of them are too similar, open the ones that look central, and then summarize the common story. Citegeist copies that workflow in software.

That matters because large language models are bad at two things academic writing depends on: source discipline and freshness. If you ask a model to write related work directly, it may produce polished prose with weak or invented citations. If you give it a noisy pile of search results, it may drown in redundancy. Citegeist tries to solve both problems by splitting the task into search, filtering, summarization, and synthesis.

The design is also tuned for scale. arXiv is too large to read naively, and the full-text corpus is far too big to keep shoving through the model. So the system starts with a cheaper representation, uses that to narrow the field, and only fetches full papers when a candidate looks promising.

Why the first pass works on abstracts

The index begins with abstract embeddings. That is a pragmatic choice, not an arbitrary one. Abstracts are short enough to embed efficiently, but still rich enough to expose the main topic, the method, and the application area. The paper uses a sentence embedding model that is comfortable with paragraph-length text, then stores the vectors in Milvus for similarity search.

This gives Citegeist a fast first pass over millions of papers. Instead of loading PDFs immediately, it compares the input abstract against abstract vectors and retrieves the nearest neighbors. That is the main reason the system can stay responsive on a corpus as large and fast-changing as arXiv.

The paper also keeps a hash next to each record. That sounds like a low-level implementation detail, but it is actually part of the design story. If the corpus changes, the hash lets the system tell whether a paper is unchanged, modified, or newly added without rebuilding everything from scratch.

Choosing a diverse longlist

Once the initial neighbors are found, Citegeist does something important that simple nearest-neighbor search does not do: it tries to keep the candidate set diverse.

If you only pick the closest papers, you often get near-duplicates. They may all be highly relevant, but they cover the same narrow slice of the literature. If you only optimize for novelty, you drift away from the query. Citegeist sits between those extremes. It repeatedly chooses the next paper by balancing similarity to the input against dissimilarity from the papers already chosen.

Figure 2 is useful because it turns that idea into a picture. Imagine the points as paper embeddings. Breadth tells the system how wide the first net should be. Diversity tells it how willing it is to move away from a cluster of almost identical papers. The longlist ends up looking less like a top-k list and more like a curated shelf: still close to the query, but not clogged with clones.

That is the right mental model for the selection rule. It is not “find the most relevant paper, then the second-most relevant paper, and so on.” It is “keep the papers that are relevant without letting relevance collapse into repetition.”

Breadth, depth, and diversity are the three knobs that make this work. Breadth controls how many candidates enter the funnel. Diversity controls how much redundancy is allowed in that candidate pool. Depth controls how much of each selected paper survives into the final synthesis. Together they decide whether the system behaves like a narrow recommender, a broad literature scout, or something in between.

From papers to pages

After the longlist is chosen, Citegeist fetches the full papers by arXiv ID. This is where the pipeline becomes more selective. It does not feed raw PDFs directly into the writer. First it strips obvious noise such as citations, appendices, and other irrelevant fragments with regex-based filtering. Then it embeds each page and scores those pages against the input abstract.

That page-level pass is what the paper calls depth. In plain English, depth is how many pages per paper the system is willing to keep. The same intuition from the paper-level selection applies here: a little depth helps because it surfaces the most relevant evidence inside each source, but too much depth starts to blur the signal.

The shortlist is therefore built from two layers of evidence. The abstract gets the paper into the running. The selected pages prove that the paper actually contains useful material for the query. The system then treats the abstract plus those selected pages as the source bundle for summarization.

There is also a useful fallback when the input is not just an abstract but a full multi-page draft paper. In that case, Citegeist scores all pages of the input document, aggregates those scores, and uses the whole draft to drive source retrieval before it falls back to the same pipeline. It is not a different architecture. It is the same funnel with a richer query representation.

Summarize first, then write

Once the source bundle is ready, Citegeist changes jobs. Retrieval stops and writing begins.

The paper first summarizes the shortlisted source pages together with the source abstract. That extra abstract is not redundant. It helps keep the summary anchored to what the source paper is actually about, instead of letting a single page snippet dominate the interpretation. After that, the system feeds the summaries into a synthesis prompt that asks for a joint related-work section.

This two-step writeup is a practical anti-hallucination trick. Summarization gives each paper a small, focused memory. Synthesis then stitches those memories into one narrative. Because the citations are extracted from arXiv metadata rather than invented in the prose pass, the final section can be checked and linked back to the source pages.

The important point is that Citegeist does not trust generation to do retrieval’s job. It lets retrieval decide what is worth reading, lets summarization decide what is worth keeping, and only then lets generation decide how the final story should read.

Keeping arXiv fresh

The other major idea in the paper is that the index has to stay current. A static literature database would be outdated almost as soon as it is built. Citegeist therefore refreshes the corpus incrementally.

The update logic is simple but effective. Each arXiv record is serialized and hashed with SHA-256. On sync, the system compares incoming hashes with stored hashes and handles three cases:

  • If nothing changed, it skips the record.
  • If the paper ID already exists but the hash changed, it recomputes the embedding and updates the stored entry.
  • If the paper is new, it computes the hash, embeds the record, assigns a topic label, and inserts it into the database.

That means the system can keep pace with a growing corpus without rebuilding everything every time. The tradeoff is time. The paper notes that updates can still take hours on a large corpus without GPU acceleration, so freshness is cheap only relative to a full rebuild, not free in an absolute sense.

This is a good example of the paper’s overall design philosophy. It does not chase elegance for its own sake. It chooses the machinery that keeps the retrieval index close to reality.

What the evaluation says

The paper evaluates the system in a way that matches the problem it is trying to solve: source relevance and writing quality are judged separately. That split matters because a related-work section can sound good while citing the wrong papers, or cite the right papers while sounding awkward. A single score would blur those two failure modes together.

The headline result is straightforward: Citegeist beats direct GPT-4o prompting on both relevance and related-work quality. The base abstract-driven pipeline gives the strongest relevance because the search signal and the query format match closely. When the input is a full paper, quality tends to improve because the retrieval step has more context to work with.

The ablations are even more instructive than the headline. Increasing diversity slightly lowers relevance but can improve quality, which makes sense if the model needs a little extra variety to cover the field without repeating itself. Increasing depth from two pages to six reduces relevance, and the paper argues that this happens because the summarizer has to juggle too much text at once. In other words, more evidence is not automatically better evidence.

They also compare against a simple agentic keyword-search baseline. That baseline is better than raw prompting, but still behind Citegeist. The lesson is not that keyword search is useless. The lesson is that keyword search alone does not give you enough control over breadth, redundancy, and page-level focus.

One caution is worth keeping in mind. The relevance metric leans on abstract similarity, so the pipeline is somewhat advantaged in the relevance comparison because it also starts from abstracts. The authors acknowledge that, which is the right thing to do. Treat the evaluation as directional evidence, not as a perfect universal ranking.

Where the method bends

Citegeist is strong, but it has clear boundaries.

The first boundary is scale. Large updates can be slow, especially without GPU acceleration. The system is dynamic, but dynamic does not mean instant.

The second boundary is depth. If you keep too many pages, the summarizer gets overloaded and relevance starts to drift. That is the same tradeoff you see in a lot of retrieval systems: more context helps until it starts to become noise.

The third boundary is output length. The paper notes that generated sections generally struggle to cover more than about 10 to 12 works. If you need a very broad review, you probably need to split the synthesis across multiple prompts or use a larger-context model.

There is also a corpus bias to remember. Because the system is built around arXiv, the topic distribution reflects arXiv rather than the broader world of scientific publishing. That is fine if your task is arXiv-centered literature work. It is less ideal if you want a more general scientific panorama.

What to remember

Citegeist is a literature search funnel, not a one-shot writer.

That is the simplest way to remember it. Start broad with abstract retrieval. Keep the candidate set diverse enough to avoid repetition. Read only the pages that matter. Summarize each source before synthesis. Keep the index fresh with incremental updates. The whole point is to make related-work generation feel like careful evidence management instead of fluent guessing.

The paper’s broader lesson is useful beyond related work too. If a research assistant has to stay grounded, the pipeline should look like this: broad search, controlled diversity, local evidence filtering, and citation-aware synthesis. The output can be a related-work section, or it can be a citation-backed answer to a scientific question. In either case, the method is the same: keep narrowing until the remaining evidence is strong enough to trust.
