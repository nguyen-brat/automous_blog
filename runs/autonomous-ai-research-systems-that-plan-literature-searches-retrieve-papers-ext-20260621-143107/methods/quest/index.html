Quest: An Open Recipe for Deep Research Agents

Quest is easiest to understand as a training recipe for a research agent, not as a single model. It is trying to teach one system to do three things at once: find hard facts, support those facts with citations, and turn the evidence into a readable report. That sounds straightforward until you try to train it. Search traces get long, the useful evidence is scattered across pages, and the reward often arrives only after the whole investigation is finished.

The opening comparison in the paper makes the target clear.

What to notice here is not just that Quest performs well on one benchmark flavor. The point is broader: the recipe is meant to cover fact seeking, citation grounding, and report synthesis together, so the training signal matches the full job of a deep research agent.

The first trick is to turn tasks into rubric trees

Quest does not train on a single gold answer whenever it can avoid it. Instead, it builds a rubric tree, which is just a hierarchical checklist of what a good response must satisfy. The leaves are checks that can be verified directly. The branches group related checks into larger criteria. That gives the method a way to handle tasks with one correct answer, tasks with several valid answers, and open-ended report tasks that are judged by how well they cover and organize evidence.

The useful thing to notice in the tree is that the method is not really scoring a string. It is scoring constraints. A leaf might check a factual detail or a source attribution. Higher nodes then combine those checks into a larger judgment. That makes the evaluation more legible and more reusable than answer-centric supervision.

For objective tasks, Quest starts from topical seeds, lets a strong model browse the web, and extracts verifiable constraints from what it finds. Those constraints are assembled into a rubric tree, refined, and checked. If the tree still cannot be made consistent and reliably evaluable, the task is thrown away. Only after that does the system turn the rubric into a natural-language question and an executable evaluation script. In other words, the question is not the source of truth; the rubric is.

That is important because objective tasks are not all the same. Some have a unique answer. Some have multiple acceptable answers. Some are really about satisfying a bundle of constraints rather than naming one fact. The rubric tree gives Quest one framework for all of those cases.

Open-ended tasks work differently, but the same idea carries through. Quest fixes the top-level evaluation lenses to four shared criteria: instruction following, comprehensiveness, readability, and insight. Under those shared lenses, it generates task-specific subcriteria. It also creates a reference report for the same question and asks a judge to compare the candidate report against that reference report directly.

That comparison step matters. A pointwise score in isolation is too generous, because judges tend to inflate scores when they have no baseline. A simple win, tie, lose scheme is too brittle, because early candidates can be so much weaker than the reference that everything collapses into losses. Quest avoids both failure modes by comparing the candidate and reference side by side and normalizing the result. If the candidate beats the reference, the score rises above the midpoint; if not, it does not.

That gives the open-ended side of the recipe a much more realistic target. The model is not asked whether its report is merely fluent. It is asked whether its report is better than a strong reference on the same task.

Quest remembers research as a structured state, not a transcript

Long research is not only a reasoning problem. It is a memory problem. If the agent keeps every raw search result and every tool trace forever, the context window fills with noise. If it discards old work too aggressively, it loses verified facts and has to rediscover them later. Quest solves that by compressing the research history into a structured context state instead of treating the entire transcript as sacred.

The three buckets are the heart of the design. Trusted entries are facts the agent has already checked against source URLs and can reuse directly. Untrusted entries are claims that conflict with other evidence and should be treated cautiously. Uncertain entries are partially supported leads that still need follow-up, usually with a URL to revisit or a search query to rerun.

That is a much more useful memory than raw chat history. It tells the next step what is proven, what is doubtful, and what still needs work. When the context window gets too full, a condenser model rewrites the accumulated history into this state and restarts the agent in a fresh window. The agent does not lose its investigation; it loses only the clutter.

This is also why Quest is more than a bigger context window. A larger window helps, but it does not solve the question of what should survive. Quest answers that directly: keep the status of the research, not the transcript of the struggle.

The training stack teaches the model in layers

Once Quest has the right task shape and the right memory shape, the training recipe can reinforce both.

The first stage is trajectory collection. A teacher agent solves the synthetic tasks by using tools step by step. Objective tasks are kept only if they pass a score threshold; if they fail, the teacher can try again with the evaluation feedback folded back into the prompt. Open-ended tasks use a lower threshold because the judgment is more subjective. The important part is that Quest does not keep every attempt. It filters for trajectories that are actually good enough to teach from.

After that, the paper retrofits inline citations onto the answers. Each factual claim gets linked to the URL that supports it, using the reasoning trace and the structured context state as the evidence trail. That is more than just a formatting choice. It sets up the output style that later fact-checking rewards will reinforce.

Mid-training adds two auxiliary skills. The first is context summarization: given a long history, the model must produce the structured context state itself. The second is relevant information extraction: given noisy HTML and a goal, the model must extract the parts that matter and ignore navigation chrome, ads, and other distractions. These are not side quests. They teach the model how to live inside web research traces before the harder optimization stages begin.

Supervised fine-tuning comes next. Quest does not train on whole trajectories as one giant blob. It splits each trajectory into sessions between context-condensation events. That keeps the unit of training aligned with the unit of inference. The model learns on the same kind of working slice of the investigation that it will actually see at runtime, which is a better match than asking it to swallow the entire trace at once.

Reinforcement learning is the last layer, and it is where the rubric finally becomes a reward. Quest uses a GRPO-style update on full rollouts. The reward has two parts. The main part comes from the rubric score, which measures whether the task was actually solved. A smaller part comes from fact checking, which measures whether the inline citations are supported by the pages they point to. The reward is intentionally conservative: a response cannot be rewarded for having nice citations if the underlying answer fails the task rubric.

That balance is the point. Quest wants answers that are both correct and grounded, but correctness still comes first. A carefully cited wrong report should not outrank a genuinely successful one.

The reward is also propagated carefully. Each prompt is sampled into a group of rollouts. Every rollout gets one scalar reward, and that reward is shared across all sessions that came from the same rollout. Then the rewards are normalized within the rollout group so long trajectories do not dominate just because they were split into more sessions. This sounds like a small bookkeeping detail, but it is actually a major part of making session-level training behave sensibly.

The infrastructure is part of the algorithm

Quest would be much harder to run if every search query and every page visit had to hit the live web again. The pipeline uses a cache for both search and page visits. Search first tries an exact query match, then a semantic match against previously seen queries. Page visits reuse exact URLs. That helps because the same kinds of queries recur during data synthesis, trajectory collection, and reinforcement learning.

The pipeline also runs rollout, evaluation, and training asynchronously. That matters because rubric-tree evaluation is slow. A complex sample can take minutes to judge, and long-tail cases take much longer. Instead of stalling the whole system while the slowest sample finishes, Quest pushes scored samples through a queue and trains whenever enough of them are ready. The result is a much smoother training loop.

This is one of the paper’s more practical contributions. Deep research agents are easy to describe as models, but they only become trainable systems when the tool layer, the evaluator, and the trainer are all designed to avoid waiting on each other.

What the experiments suggest

The paper trains on about 8K synthesized tasks and evaluates Quest across objective and open-ended deep research benchmarks. Quest-35B reaches 64.6 on BrowseComp, 30.7 on Mind2Web 2, and 48.2 on DeepResearch Bench. Those numbers matter less as scoreboard entries than as evidence that the recipe transfers across task types: hard fact seeking, citation-grounded web work, and report synthesis.

The controlled studies are even more informative. The full mid-training plus supervised fine-tuning plus reinforcement learning recipe performs better than partial-stage variants. That is a strong sign that the stages are complementary rather than redundant. Mid-training teaches the model how to handle long research state. Supervised fine-tuning teaches it the shape of good trajectories. Reinforcement learning then pushes the policy toward better final behavior.

Smaller models are interesting too. Quest reports that even 2B to 9B models can be strong on fact-seeking tasks. The open-ended report-synthesis tasks are harder and still benefit most from larger models, which is a useful reminder that not every deep research capability scales the same way.

The main tradeoff is honest and visible: the recipe is strong for reproducibility and broad coverage, but the hardest open-ended synthesis tasks still reward more capacity.

What the authors tried and dropped

The paper’s negative results are useful because they show what did not fit the task structure well enough. Search-result prediction in mid-training helped a little on its own, but it hurt when mixed into the current objective set. Rubric-based error identification barely moved the needle, likely because the model could not see external evidence at that stage. Direct policy optimization for report comparison was unstable and did not improve the final reports reliably.

The scoring experiments for open-ended tasks were also revealing. Pointwise scoring inflated too easily. Simple pairwise win, tie, lose scoring collapsed when the student model was much weaker than the teacher. The final pairwise normalization scheme was the compromise that made the signal usable.

The broader lesson is simple: if the reward does not match the structure of the research task, the optimization usually fights the task instead of teaching it.

What to remember

Quest’s core idea is not mysterious once you strip away the implementation details. Train on tasks that look like real research. Keep memory as a structured record of what is proven, doubtful, and still missing. Split long investigations into sessions that match the working context. Reward the agent for solving the rubric, but keep citation support in the loop.

That combination is why the paper matters. It gives an open recipe for deep research agents that is not just a bigger model with search tools attached. It is a training system built around the actual shape of research work.
