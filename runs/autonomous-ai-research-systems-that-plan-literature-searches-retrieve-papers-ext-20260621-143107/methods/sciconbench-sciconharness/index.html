Can AI Agents Synthesize Scientific Conclusions?

This paper is asking a stricter question than “can an agent find relevant papers?” It asks whether an agent can turn open-web evidence into a scientific conclusion that still matches the authoritative review after we remove the easiest shortcut: reading the answer verbatim from the web. SciConBench is the benchmark. SciConHarness is the guardrail. Together, they let the authors test genuine synthesis instead of shortcut retrieval.

At a glance, the flow is:

Here is the whole machine in one picture.

What to notice in the figure is the separation of responsibilities. The benchmark defines the task, the harness controls what the agent can see, and the evaluator scores the final conclusion at the fact level. That separation is the point of the paper.

Why this benchmark exists

The paper starts from a simple but important complaint: most deep-research benchmarks still score the wrong thing. They look at retrieval quality, citation formatting, short factual answers, or summaries, but not at the final long-form scientific conclusion. That matters because the real job of these systems is not to collect evidence and stop. It is to read, reconcile, and write a conclusion that a person could actually rely on.

SciConBench is built from the Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews because those reviews already have the structure the task needs. Each review has an Objective that states the question and an Authors’ Conclusion that states the expert synthesis. That gives the benchmark a clean input-output pair: a scientific question on one side, an evidence-based conclusion on the other.

The benchmark is also live, which is a big deal in this setting. Instead of freezing one static dataset and hoping nobody can memorize it, the authors keep pulling in newly released Cochrane reviews over time. That makes the benchmark more current and reduces the chance that an agent is simply recalling a memorized answer from training data.

The question generation step is not just a mechanical rewrite. The paper turns the review Objective into a sentence-style question, using the population, intervention, comparator, and outcome structure that clinical researchers already use. That choice makes the benchmark feel like a real research query instead of a keyword search puzzle. The generated questions are then checked by medical students for three things: whether they preserve the original meaning, whether they keep the important PICO ingredients, and whether they are clear enough to answer.

That validation step matters because if the question drifts, the whole benchmark drifts with it. The authors are not trusting the model rewrite blindly. They are checking that the benchmark question still means what the review meant.

SciConHarness is the guardrail

Once the task is defined, the paper has to stop the agent from cheating. That is what SciConHarness does.

The harness is an MCP-based control layer, which just means the model talks to search tools through a standardized interface instead of a custom, one-off wiring job. The model can call web search, paper search, and webpage browsing tools, but the harness keeps orchestration and tool execution separate. That makes the setup easier to reuse across different model providers and easier to audit when something goes wrong.

The practical tool flow is straightforward:

  • google_search finds search results.
  • paper_search pulls relevant passages from open-access papers.
  • web_browse opens pages and returns text, often summarized before it goes back into the model context.

The interesting part is not that the tools exist. It is that the harness decides what evidence is allowed to come back. The clean-room filter blocks direct access to ground-truth Cochrane material by rejecting search results and pages that look like the answer source itself. It looks for Cochrane domains, titles that match the review, and pages published after the review date that are likely to be derivative leaks. For browsing, it also blocks pages whose text explicitly mentions the review title or Cochrane.

That filtering is intentionally conservative. It can throw away some legitimate evidence. But that is the right tradeoff if the goal is to measure synthesis rather than memorization or direct answer lookup. If the benchmark lets the model see the review conclusion in near-final form, then the score mostly reflects retrieval skill, not reasoning.

You can think of the clean room as the paper’s main control experiment. It asks: what is left after the shortcut is removed? The answer turns out to be much smaller than many people would hope.

Turning a paragraph into facts

The second big idea is that a long scientific conclusion should not be scored as one blob. A conclusion can be partly right, partly vague, and partly wrong all at once. If you only assign one score to the whole paragraph, you miss that mix.

So the paper breaks both the generated conclusion and the reference conclusion into atomic facts. An atomic fact is just one self-contained claim. The pipeline is designed to make those claims easier to judge:

  • split the paragraph into sentences,
  • decompose each sentence into atomic facts,
  • rewrite facts so they do not depend on hidden context,
  • repair incomplete facts by adding missing referents or conditions,
  • keep only facts that are relevant to the original question,
  • remove redundant facts that say the same thing twice.

This is the part of the paper that makes the evaluation feel like a real reading task instead of a simple string match. The point is not to count words. The point is to see whether the conclusion preserved the actual scientific claims that matter.

The paper also keeps the cost under control by using stronger models where the structure is being created and smaller models where the step is mostly classification or filtering. That is a sensible design choice. Fact decomposition is expensive at scale, so the pipeline has to spend its budget where it actually changes quality.

Once the facts exist, the scoring becomes intuitive.

  • Factual precision asks whether the generated facts are supported by the review and not contradicted by it.
  • Factual recall asks whether the generated conclusion covers the important facts from the reference conclusion.
  • Factual F1 combines both, so a model cannot get away with being only cautious or only verbose.

That balance is important. A short answer that is mostly right can still fail recall. A long answer that sounds polished can still fail precision if it invents details, flips the direction of an effect, or adds claims the review never supported.

How the fact judges scale

Manual fact labeling is too slow to run on every conclusion, so the authors build a gold-standard dataset with medical doctors first, then train a judge by prompt design rather than by model fine-tuning. The expert labels are used to validate whether a language model can act as a dependable substitute annotator.

That judge sees the same kind of evidence a human annotator sees. For precision, it compares a fact against the open-access parts of the review that summarize the study. For recall, it checks whether a reference fact from the Authors’ Conclusions is supported by the generated conclusion. The judge labels facts as supported, contradicted, or not supported, depending on the task.

The nice surprise is that a carefully prompted smaller model can do this well enough to scale the benchmark. The paper finds that gpt-5.4-mini, with the right prompt and settings, aligns closely with the medical experts and is strong enough to use downstream. That is a good reminder that evaluation quality is not only about the largest model available. It is also about the prompt, the task definition, and the source text you feed the judge.

This also explains why the paper spends so much time validating the judge and the decomposition pipeline with experts. The authors are not treating the LLM judge as magic. They are testing whether it behaves like a reliable annotator on the exact kind of fine-grained factual judgments the benchmark needs.

What the scores are really saying

The headline result is that current frontier systems still struggle with the task once the shortcut is removed. Under clean-room evaluation, the best system reaches only a factual F1 of 0.337. That number is the paper’s clearest warning sign: even strong agents are not yet reliable at end-to-end scientific conclusion synthesis.

The clean-room setting also consistently lowers scores compared with unconstrained evaluation, by roughly 0.02 to 0.172 depending on the system. That gap is the leakage story in one sentence. When the agent can reach the ground-truth review or a close derivative, performance looks better than it really is. When that path is blocked, the true synthesis ability is much lower.

The authors also audit consumer-facing systems such as Google AI Overview, Google AI Mode, and OpenEvidence. The pattern is the same: these tools can sound fluent and helpful, but they still omit key facts and sometimes contradict the review. That is exactly the kind of failure that matters in health and science, because a conclusion can look polished and still be wrong in the places that matter most.

There is another subtle result worth remembering. Longer conclusions tend to gain recall while losing precision. So simply writing more does not solve the problem. The benchmark rewards correctness and coverage together, which means verbosity alone cannot fake a strong score.

The limits you should keep in mind

The paper is strong as an evaluation framework, but it is not a universal measure of all scientific synthesis.

  • The domain is mostly health and evidence-based medicine, so the results may not transfer cleanly to legal, social science, or other synthesis tasks.
  • Some indirect leakage can still sneak through, especially via derivative pages that paraphrase the review without naming it directly.
  • The benchmark relies on LLM-based extraction and judging, so errors in those steps can still affect the final score even after expert validation.
  • The metrics measure factual quality, not downstream decision impact. A small factual mistake can matter a lot or a little depending on the clinical or policy context.

Those limits do not weaken the main contribution. They just define its scope. The paper is strongest as a warning that deep-research evaluation has to be leakage-aware and fact-aware if it wants to measure the actual synthesis task.

What to remember

The simplest way to remember this paper is:

  • SciConBench defines the scientific question.
  • SciConHarness keeps the answer out of reach.
  • Atomic facts turn a long conclusion into something you can judge precisely.
  • Precision and recall together tell you whether the model stayed accurate and complete.

If you only remember one sentence, make it this: when an agent can read the answer verbatim, you are benchmarking retrieval; when you block that shortcut and score the conclusion fact by fact, you are finally benchmarking synthesis.
