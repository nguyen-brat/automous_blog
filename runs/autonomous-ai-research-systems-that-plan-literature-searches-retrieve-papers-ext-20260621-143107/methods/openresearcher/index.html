OpenResearcher: A Reproducible Pipeline for Long-Horizon Deep Research Trajectory Synthesis

OpenResearcher is a way to build deep research training data without hitting live search APIs for every example. The core idea is to separate two jobs that are usually tangled together: getting the right evidence into reach, and generating realistic long-horizon browsing traces inside that evidence world.

That split is what makes the method interesting. Instead of asking a teacher model to both discover the corpus and learn from it at the same time, OpenResearcher pays the coverage cost once, freezes the result into an offline search environment, and then lets the teacher practice search-heavy research over and over again.

Think of it as a research gym. The room is fixed, the library is noisy, and the paths through the shelves are reproducible.

The whole pipeline is easiest to remember as a two-step loop: first build the evidence pool, then let the agent browse inside it.

What to notice is the order. OpenResearcher does not ask the teacher to hunt for an answer while also wondering whether the corpus is missing the key document. It removes that ambiguity first. Once the answer-supporting documents are present, search becomes a controlled environment for learning how to find, inspect, and verify evidence.

Why the bootstrap comes first

A deep research agent is only as good as the evidence it can actually reach. If a trajectory fails because the answer is absent from the corpus, that failure is hard to interpret. Maybe the search strategy was weak. Maybe the model needed more turns. Or maybe the decisive document was never there in the first place.

That is why the paper bootstraps gold documents before synthesis. Here, gold documents are the pages that together contain enough evidence to recover the answer, even if no single page says everything directly. The system performs one live search pass for each hard question, collects documents that support the answer, cleans and deduplicates them, and then freezes them into the offline corpus.

The search query is also strengthened during this bootstrap step by combining the question with the reference answer. That improves recall at the moment the corpus is being built, which is exactly when recall matters most.

This is the subtle but important move in the method. OpenResearcher does not try to solve corpus coverage and trajectory generation at the same time. Coverage is handled once. Trajectory generation happens after the environment is fixed.

The large distractor corpus matters just as much as the gold documents. If the offline environment were tiny or too clean, the teacher would not have to search hard enough to learn real research behavior. By keeping a large noisy background corpus around the bootstrapped evidence, the method preserves the feel of a real web search task while still making the target answer reachable.

That also makes analysis much cleaner. Once you know the gold evidence exists, you can separate a retrieval miss from a reasoning mistake instead of collapsing both into one vague failure.

Search, open, find: three scales of evidence

The browser side of OpenResearcher is built around three primitives, and the paper is right to make that stack explicit.

  • search returns ranked results with titles, URLs, and snippets. This is the broadest move. It helps the agent identify candidate sources and decide where to look next.
  • open fetches the full document. This is the moment the agent stops trusting the snippet and starts reading the actual page.
  • find locates exact strings inside the opened document. This is the precision tool. It helps the agent jump to the line, table, or sentence that actually settles the question.

What to notice in Figure 3 is the change in scale. Search works at the corpus level. Open works at the document level. Find works at the passage level. Deep research gets much easier to reason about when those three levels are separate, because the agent can move from broad discovery to exact verification instead of trying to do everything from snippets alone.

That separation also explains a common failure mode. Search-only agents can get stuck in a loop of reformulating queries because the decisive clue lives inside a page, not in the snippet. The model can sense that it is close, but it has no clean way to inspect the document itself. Adding open and find fixes that. It gives the agent the same tools a human researcher uses when moving from a search result to the relevant sentence.

A good memory hook is this: search chooses the shelf, open pulls the book, and find jumps to the line that matters.

How the teacher generates trajectories

Once the offline corpus and browser tools are ready, the teacher model is prompted to behave like a careful research assistant. It gets the question, but not the reference answer. At each step it reasons, chooses one of the browser actions, reads the observation, and decides whether it has enough evidence to continue.

The important part is that the teacher is not allowed to cheat by seeing the answer up front. It has to rediscover the answer through search, document inspection, and evidence localization. That is what makes the resulting traces useful for supervised fine-tuning. The student is not just learning what the answer is. It is learning how to get there.

The generator also uses a long turn budget, because deep research questions are genuinely long-horizon. Some trajectories finish quickly once the right document is found, but many others need far more turns before the evidence becomes clear. That long tail is not a bug. It is part of the point. The paper wants the student to see what sustained search looks like, not just short retrieval bursts.

Some trajectories stretch past 100 tool calls, which is exactly the kind of behavior you expect when the answer is buried and the agent has to keep checking, narrowing, and revising its search plan.

After generation, the pipeline applies lightweight filtering. It drops traces that exceed context limits, contain malformed tool calls, or never reach a conclusive answer inside the interaction budget. The surviving trajectories are then used for supervised fine-tuning, with a stricter filter that keeps only answer-correct traces for the student training set.

That final filter matters because it turns the trajectories into usable demonstrations. A bad trace can still be interesting for analysis, but it is risky as training signal. OpenResearcher keeps the long traces for study, but it only teaches the student from the trajectories that actually end at the right answer.

The scale is the other important detail. After filtering, the pipeline still produces 97K-plus trajectories, which is large enough to expose the student to both short and very long research paths.

Why multiple trajectories matter

One of the nicest things about the offline setup is that it lets the paper study solution diversity directly. If you sample multiple trajectories for the same question, you do not get the same search path every time. Some runs find the key document quickly. Others wander. A few take a surprisingly long route but still recover the right answer.

What to notice here is not the exact score. It is the shape of the curve. As you allow more sampled trajectories, more questions become solvable. That tells you the task space is not a single brittle path. It is a set of partial routes through the evidence, some short and some long. If one path misses the clue, another path can often recover it.

This is also why long-horizon trajectory data is valuable for training. A single search trace can make the task look more deterministic than it really is. A bundle of trajectories shows the model that the same question can be reached from several angles, and that exploration sometimes matters more than the first guess.

What the offline setup buys you

The offline design is not only about cost. It gives the paper three practical advantages.

First, it is reproducible. The same corpus, the same index, and the same browser actions can be replayed.

Second, it is instrumented. Because the environment is fixed, you can tell whether the model found the right document, opened it, or kept spinning in search.

Third, it is scalable. Live web APIs are expensive and rate-limited. A local corpus is much easier to run in parallel, especially when you need tens of thousands of long trajectories.

Those properties are what make the method scientifically useful. The pipeline is not just generating data faster. It is generating data in a way that you can inspect and reason about afterward.

Where the method still breaks

OpenResearcher is powerful, but it still has real limits.

  • It depends on bootstrapped gold-document coverage. If the decisive evidence never enters the offline corpus, the search loop cannot recover it.
  • The offline snapshot cannot perfectly match the live web. It is a controlled approximation, not the entire internet.
  • Finding the right document is not the same as reasoning about it correctly. The final answer still depends on careful evidence interpretation.

That last point is easy to miss. Retrieval success and answer correctness are related, but they are not identical. A model can locate the right evidence and still draw the wrong conclusion from it. OpenResearcher therefore solves the supervision problem only partway: it makes the search process teachable, but the reasoning step still has to be learned.

What to remember

OpenResearcher is best remembered as a reproducible research gym built in two moves.

First, it bootstraps answer-supporting documents once so the evidence is actually present.

Second, it lets a teacher model generate long search -> open -> find trajectories entirely offline, then keeps the answer-correct traces for student fine-tuning.

If you want the one-sentence version, it is this: build the evidence pool once, then train the agent inside a fixed but realistic browsing world. That is the core idea behind the paper.
