Sage: A Benchmark for Scientific Agentic Retrieval Evaluation

Sage is not just another retrieval benchmark. It is a stress test for the seam between a research agent and the retriever it depends on.

That seam matters because deep research agents do not search the way a human library searcher does. They break a question into smaller subqueries, search again after reading what came back, and keep revising their plan as evidence accumulates. Sage asks a very specific question: if the agent searches that way, does a semantic retriever actually help, or does a simpler lexical retriever fit the agent better?

The paper’s answer is surprisingly sharp. In this setting, the agent’s own query style matters so much that BM25 can beat LLM-based retrievers.

What to notice in Figure 1 is that Sage does not treat retrieval as a standalone ranking task. It evaluates the whole research loop. The agent reasons, asks a search tool for papers, reads the results, and decides what to do next. That is a much harder setting than “rank these papers by relevance,” and it is also much closer to how real deep research systems behave.

A benchmark built around a locked library

Sage is built around scientific literature because it gives the authors something web search does not: a fixed, controllable corpus.

The benchmark spans 1,200 queries over 200,000 papers across four domains. That size matters, but the bigger idea is the shape of the corpus. The papers are current, open access, and split across domains that naturally produce different kinds of research questions. That makes the benchmark useful for asking whether a retriever works inside a real agent loop, not just in isolation.

The paper is also careful about why scientific literature is the right stress test.

  • The task is common and meaningful. Researchers really do spend time searching for related work, checking whether an idea has already been explored, and collecting evidence from multiple papers.
  • The corpus is controllable. Unlike the open web, the paper collection is known and fixed, so retriever behavior is easier to compare.
  • The benchmark stays current. Using recent papers avoids the problem where the model already “knows” too much from pretraining and the retrieval task becomes too easy.

That last point is important. Sage is trying to measure retrieval behavior, not memory recall.

Short-form questions are clue solving

The short-form side of Sage is designed like a paper detective story. The answer is unique and fixed, but the clues are scattered.

The paper builds these questions from three kinds of signals: paper metadata, figures and tables, and relationships between papers. Two papers count as related if they share at least four references. An LLM then turns those clues into questions that force the agent to reason across multiple sources. The target answer is the seed paper itself.

What to notice in Figure 2 is that the benchmark is not asking for a definition or a factoid. It is asking the agent to reconstruct the right paper from clues spread across the paper and its neighbors. That is why exact match is the right metric here: either the agent identified the paper, or it did not.

This design exposes a classic failure mode in agentic retrieval. A subquery may be keyword-like and still be correct in spirit, but if the retriever does not surface the right paper, the agent never gets the chance to stitch the clues together. Short-form questions make that failure visible fast.

Open-ended questions look more like literature review

The open-ended side is closer to what researchers actually do when they survey a topic.

Each question combines background context with the shared citations between two papers. The benchmark then builds a hierarchy of ground truth: the selected paper pair matters most, and the papers they both cite matter next. That gives the evaluation room to reward partial success instead of forcing a single exact answer.

Figure 3 is useful because it shows why a different metric is needed. In open-ended literature search, there are many acceptable directions, not just one correct paper. Sage therefore uses weighted recall: retrieved papers get more credit when they are more relevant, and the score is judged against the full set of relevant papers rather than one exact label.

In plain language, the metric asks a simple question: did the agent recover the important papers, and did it recover them in the right order of importance?

That is a better fit for literature review than exact match. It rewards the agent for finding the right neighborhood of the literature, not just one exact target.

How the corpus is assembled

The corpus construction is part of the method, not just background plumbing.

For each domain, Sage starts with the papers the question will depend on: the target paper, its highest-overlap partner, the intersection of their references, and the union of their references. Then it expands the corpus with recent papers from major venues until it reaches about 50,000 papers per domain. The humanities side ends up smaller because the authors intentionally exclude older literature and there are fewer recent open-access papers available.

This is a good benchmark design because it seeds the library with the right neighborhood while still making retrieval nontrivial. The target papers are in the corpus, but they are not sitting in a tiny hand-picked toy set. The agent still has to find them.

The result is a controlled scientific library rather than a random pile of PDFs.

What happens when a real agent searches it

The paper first tests deep research agents with their native web-search tools. That matters because it reveals how the agents actually decompose questions before the retriever changes.

The main finding is not that more search always helps. In fact, search quantity is not the main driver of accuracy. Some agents search more often or return more references and still do worse than stronger systems that ask better subqueries. The important difference is query decomposition.

The stronger agents tend to generate cleaner, more phrasal searches. DR Tulu, by contrast, more often produces keyword-like concatenations. That matters because keyword-shaped subqueries fit sparse lexical retrieval better than dense semantic retrieval.

Once the authors swap web search out for corpus search inside the same DR Tulu backbone, the retriever effect becomes even clearer. BM25 beats LLM-based retrievers by about 30 percent on short-form questions. On open-ended questions the gap narrows, but the pattern still says the same thing: the retriever has to match the agent’s search style.

Figure 4 shows the failure mode very clearly. A semantically plausible query can still pull the dense retriever toward the wrong papers if the surface wording is slightly off. Once the wrong papers are returned, later search rounds get conditioned on the same bad clue, and the error compounds. BM25 is more boring, but in this setting that boring lexical anchoring is exactly what keeps the agent on target.

There is also a long-document effect here. The paper embeds the first 32,000 tokens of each markdown file to stay within retriever limits, so the dense retrievers are already working with compressed paper representations. Under that kind of pressure, it becomes easier for different papers to blur together and harder for the retriever to preserve diversity.

That is why increasing the per-search top-k helps. A larger candidate set gives weaker first-pass ranking a better chance to recover the right paper later in the search loop. It is not a cure, but it gives the retriever more room to fail less badly.

The open-ended side is narrower because the agent itself does not explore the evidence space very widely. If the decomposition stays low-diversity, the retriever can only work with the slice of the literature the agent asks for. In that regime, retriever differences matter less because the search plan is already the bottleneck.

The corpus-side scaling trick

Sage’s second contribution is a simple but effective adaptation step: instead of retraining the agent, enrich the corpus.

The authors prepend salient signals to each paper, including bibliographic metadata and a small set of LLM-generated keywords. That means a paper no longer enters retrieval as plain markdown. It carries its venue, year, authors, citation count, and a compact semantic hint about what it is about.

This is test-time scaling on the corpus side. The query stays the same. The documents get easier to find.

That choice is clever because it fits the agent’s actual behavior. If the agent asks keyword-heavy subqueries, then surfacing more keyword-like evidence in the corpus helps immediately. BM25 benefits the most from that change, gaining a little over eight points on short-form questions. The LLM-based retrievers also improve, but only modestly, because the underlying mismatch is still there.

The gains are smaller on open-ended questions. That makes sense. Open-ended search needs breadth and coverage, not just easier keyword hits. If the agent does not search widely enough, making each document more searchable can only do so much.

So the adaptation is useful, but it is not magic. It is a way to make the corpus speak the agent’s language more clearly.

What Sage leaves open

The paper is strongest as an interface lesson: a retriever can look strong in isolation and still lose inside a deep research loop if the agent’s subqueries do not match it well enough.

It also leaves a few natural follow-ups. The authors do not instruction-tune the open-source agents to be retriever-aware, so it remains open whether that kind of training would close part of the gap with BM25. The analysis is also centered heavily on DR Tulu, so different agent recipes may produce different query styles and different retriever preferences. And because the corpus scaling is keyword enrichment rather than deeper rewriting, it is not yet clear how much more aggressive document-side adaptation could help.

That is a healthy set of open questions. The paper does not pretend the mismatch is solved. It shows where the mismatch comes from.

What to remember

Sage is easiest to remember as a benchmark for retriever-agent compatibility.

The short-form questions test whether an agent can identify a paper from scattered clues. The open-ended questions test whether it can recover the right neighborhood of the literature. The web-search experiments show that stronger search behavior is not just about calling search more often. The corpus-search experiments show that BM25 can beat semantic retrievers when the agent’s subqueries are keyword-like. And the corpus-scaling trick shows that adding metadata and keywords to documents can help off-the-shelf retrievers without retraining the agent.

If you want the one-sentence takeaway, it is this: a retriever only looks smart if it matches the way the agent actually asks for evidence.
