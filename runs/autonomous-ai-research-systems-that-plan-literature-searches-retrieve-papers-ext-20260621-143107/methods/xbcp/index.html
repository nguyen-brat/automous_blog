Beyond Monolingual Deep Research: Evaluating Agents and Retrievers with Cross-Lingual BrowseComp-Plus

XBCP asks a deceptively simple question: what happens when an English deep-research task has to be solved from evidence written in other languages? The paper does not change the question language or the answer language. It changes the evidence side. That small shift is the whole point, because it lets the authors watch language mismatch propagate through the full research loop instead of hiding inside a single retrieval score.

That makes XBCP different from a normal multilingual retriever benchmark. The benchmark is not only asking, “Can a retriever rank documents across languages?” It is asking, “Can an agent still search, read, cite, and answer correctly when the right sources are not in the same language as the user?”

Here is the mental model to keep in mind:

What the figure makes clear is that the benchmark is built like a controlled experiment. The task stays the same, but the language of the supporting evidence changes. That lets the paper separate language effects from task effects.

The core trick: translate the evidence, not the task

XBCP is built from BrowseComp-Plus, but only the evidence side is translated. The benchmark keeps the BrowseComp-Plus questions in English and still evaluates final answers in English. The supporting documents are the part that moves across languages.

That sounds small, but it changes the entire failure mode. If the agent misses an answer now, you no longer know whether it failed because the question was hard, because the retriever was weak, because the evidence was in the wrong language, or because the model could not carry foreign evidence back into an English answer. XBCP is designed to expose exactly that ambiguity.

The corpus is organized in two complementary ways:

  • In the cross-lingual setting, each query is assigned one evidence language, and all supporting documents for that query are in that language.
  • In the multilingual setting, the evidence corpus is spread across 12 languages, with the documents assigned equally and randomly across those languages.

The first setting isolates a clean language shift for each query. The second creates a mixed-language corpus where the agent has to cope with a more realistic multilingual search space. If both settings degrade in similar ways, then the bottleneck is not just the corpus layout. It is the language mismatch itself.

The language set is also intentionally uneven. It includes higher-resource languages such as Chinese, French, German, Japanese, Korean, Portuguese, and Spanish, plus lower-resource languages such as Swahili, Wolof, Yoruba, and Zulu. That matters because a benchmark that only covers high-resource languages can make multilingual search look more robust than it really is.

Why the translation step is trusted

The benchmark only works if the translated evidence is good enough to serve as evidence. To make that credible, the paper uses GPT-5.4 to translate the supporting documents into the 11 non-English languages while preserving URLs, email addresses, formulas, code blocks, titles, terminology, and metadata field names according to the target language.

The authors then run an expert verification study. They sample 200 translated documents from each non-English language, for 2,200 total translation instances, and ask bilingual annotators to score accuracy, fluency, and completeness on a 1 to 5 scale. Every language averages above 4.0 on all three dimensions.

That verification step is important for interpretation. If the benchmark fell apart because the translations were unusable, the results would be hard to read. Instead, the paper is saying something sharper: the translations are generally good enough for controlled evaluation, yet cross-lingual deep research still breaks down.

What the benchmark actually measures

XBCP evaluates the whole research loop, not just retrieval in isolation. The paper pairs four deep-research agents with sparse and dense multilingual retrievers, then runs them over the original corpus, the multilingual corpus, and the cross-lingual corpus.

The metrics are chosen to mirror the path from search to answer:

  • Accuracy checks whether the final English answer is correct.
  • Evidence recall checks whether the search trajectory actually surfaced the gold supporting documents.
  • Search calls measure how much exploration the agent needed.
  • Calibration error checks whether the agent’s confidence matches its actual correctness.
  • Citation coverage, precision, and recall check whether the final answer preserves the evidence trail in a faithful way.
  • Oracle retrieval removes search ranking entirely by giving the agent all gold evidence directly, which helps isolate reasoning failures from retrieval failures.

That mix of metrics is the real strength of the benchmark. A system can look good on one metric and still be weak on the others. For example, a model can sound confident but be wrong, or it can retrieve the right document but fail to cite it in the final answer.

When retrieval crosses languages, the floor drops

The main result is that translated evidence is hard, even for strong multilingual retrievers.

The best anchor configuration in the paper is DeepSeek-V4-Pro with Qwen3-Embedding-8B. On the original corpus it reaches 64.70% accuracy, then drops to 48.80% in the multilingual setting and 42.29% in the cross-lingual setting. Other agent-retriever pairs show the same basic pattern: once the evidence is translated, accuracy and evidence recall fall sharply.

BM25 shows the clearest collapse because lexical matching is a poor fit for English queries over non-English documents. Dense multilingual retrievers do better, but they still do not erase the gap. That is the key lesson: multilingual retrieval quality is helpful, but it does not automatically become robust agentic search.

Why not? Because the agent is not just ranking a corpus once. It is searching iteratively. Evidence has to be surfaced at the right time, understood in context, and carried into the answer-writing stage. A retriever that looks fine in isolation can still fail inside the loop if it does not expose the right document when the agent needs it.

The evidence-recall metric makes that visible. It is computed over the union of documents returned across the full search trajectory, so it measures whether the agent actually encountered the gold evidence at any point. That is more revealing than a one-shot top-k score because the benchmark is about interactive search, not static ranking.

Citations are part of the failure

XBCP does not stop at retrieval recall. It also checks whether the retrieved evidence survives into the final answer as a faithful citation trail.

Once the evidence is translated, citation coverage, precision, and recall all drop. That means the problem is not only that the right source is hard to find. It is also that the model struggles to keep the source attached to the right claim once it starts writing in English.

This is one of the paper’s more useful distinctions. Retrieval failure and citation failure are related, but they are not the same thing. A document can be surfaced and still be cited incorrectly, or it can never surface at all. XBCP exposes both cases.

Oracle retrieval shows a second bottleneck

The oracle setup is where the paper becomes especially instructive. By supplying all gold evidence directly, it removes ranking and search from the equation. That should help a lot if retrieval is the only bottleneck.

It does help a lot. But it does not remove the gap between original-English evidence and translated evidence.

That tells you something important: the model is still doing cross-lingual reasoning even after retrieval has been solved for it. It has to recognize which facts matter, align those facts with an English question, and produce an English answer without dropping the evidence constraint. In other words, there is an agent-side bottleneck on top of the retrieval bottleneck.

This is why the paper argues that cross-lingual deep research is not just “multilingual retrieval plus a chat model.” The agent has to bridge language mismatch at multiple stages: search, reading, synthesis, and citation generation.

The agent’s own reasoning can help retrieval

One of the most practical follow-up experiments in the paper is reasoning-trace query expansion. The idea is simple: before the retriever sees the search query, the system concatenates it with the agent’s current reasoning trace.

That matters because the reasoning trace usually says more than the query string does. It can spell out what the agent has already learned, what clue it is chasing next, and what evidence is still missing. In that sense, the reasoning trace acts like a compact working note that clarifies intent.

The paper tests this lightweight trick without retriever training or index changes. The result is consistent across corpus conditions: accuracy improves, evidence recall improves, calibration gets a little better, and search calls stay flat or go down slightly.

The takeaway is not that reasoning magically solves multilingual search. It does not. The gains are smaller once the evidence has been translated, which is exactly what you would expect if language mismatch is still the hard part. But the result is still valuable because it shows that the agent’s own reasoning is a real retrieval signal, not just internal chatter.

More thinking helps, but it is expensive

The paper also varies reasoning effort with a fixed retriever. Higher effort improves accuracy and evidence recall in all three corpus conditions, and it improves calibration too. That means more deliberation does help the agent make better use of the evidence it sees.

The cost is search budget. High-effort runs require many more search calls than low-effort runs, and the cross-lingual and multilingual settings still do not catch up to the better original-corpus runs. So extra reasoning can soften the failure, but it does not turn translated evidence into monolingual evidence.

That is a useful reminder for system design. When the bottleneck is language mismatch, brute-force search depth is not a substitute for better cross-lingual alignment.

What XBCP adds to the field

The biggest contribution of XBCP is conceptual. Most multilingual retrieval benchmarks ask whether documents can be ranked in a fixed collection. Most deep-research benchmarks ask whether an agent can search and synthesize in a single language. XBCP connects those two traditions.

It shows that cross-lingual retrieval has to be evaluated inside the agent loop, because the loop changes the failure surface. A retriever that works well as a standalone ranker may still fail when the agent has to revisit the search space, keep track of partial evidence, and cite the final answer faithfully.

So the benchmark is not just a new dataset. It is a controlled way to ask whether multilingual search survives contact with actual deep research behavior.

The limits to keep in mind

The paper is careful about the caveats. It reports a single evaluation run for each configuration, does not estimate multi-seed variance, and keeps inference hyperparameters fixed. Those choices make the comparisons controlled, but they also mean the exact numbers should be read as a strong snapshot rather than a full statistical study.

There is also a translation caveat. Even with expert verification, some residual artifacts may remain, especially in lower-resource languages. So the benchmark is best read as a high-quality controlled stress test, not as a perfect simulation of every multilingual search environment.

What to remember

XBCP’s core idea is easy to keep in your head: keep the question in English, translate the evidence, and see where the deep-research pipeline breaks.

The answer is that it breaks in two places. First, the retriever has trouble surfacing the right evidence across languages. Second, even when the right evidence is provided, the agent still has to understand it, align it to the English question, and carry it into a faithful cited answer.

If you remember only one thing, remember this: cross-lingual deep research is not a ranking problem with translation sprinkled on top. It is a full agentic search problem where language mismatch can hurt retrieval, reasoning, and citation transfer at the same time.
