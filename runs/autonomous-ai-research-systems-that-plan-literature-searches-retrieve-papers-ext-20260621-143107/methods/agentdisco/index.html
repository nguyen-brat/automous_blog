AgentDisCo: A Disentangled and Collaborative Agentic Architecture for Deep Research

AgentDisCo is easiest to understand if you picture a research notebook that refuses to do everything in one pass. Instead of asking one model to search, think, and write at once, the paper splits the job into specialized roles: a planner sets the intent and response style, a critic spots what the outline is missing and turns those gaps into search blueprints, a generator goes out to retrieve evidence and repair the outline, a writer turns the stabilized outline into a report, and a render agent repackages the report into posters or slides.

The big idea is that the outline is not a temporary scrap of text. It is the shared workspace that lets search and synthesis correct each other.

What to notice in the figure is the separation of responsibilities. The planner sits at the top and prepares the task. The critic and generator form the middle loop where research actually improves. The document bank keeps evidence from getting lost between rounds. The writer and render agent sit downstream, which means the final presentation only happens after the outline has already been strengthened.

Why the outline comes first

A final report is too brittle to use as the main intermediate state. Every time you rewrite it, you risk blurring what was already correct. An outline is easier to move around. It can grow a new branch, drop a weak branch, or ask for more evidence in one section without forcing the whole report to be rewritten.

That is why AgentDisCo optimizes outlines instead of final reports. The critic does not ask, “Is the report polished?” It asks, “What is still missing, and where should the next search go?” The generator then uses those targeted blueprints to collect evidence and update the outline in place.

A good mental model is a person writing a literature review with a whiteboard next to the desk. The whiteboard is where missing gaps are marked, sources to chase are listed, and the structure stays visible while the reading continues.

Before the loop starts, the planner also infers the intent and response style, so the same system can answer a fact-finding question, a comparison request, or a more open-ended deep-exploration task without rewriting the rest of the pipeline.

The paper also gives this loop a clean formal shape: the critic and generator take turns, and each turn’s output becomes the next turn’s state. You do not need the notation to understand the system, but the important consequence is worth keeping in mind. The loop stays explicit. Search does not hide inside the writer, and writing does not silently absorb retrieval.

The critic is really a gap detector

The critic’s job is not to rewrite everything from scratch. It looks at the current outline, checks the attached references, and asks whether the outline has enough coverage and enough grounding. If it finds a hole, it does something very specific: it turns that hole into a blueprint and attaches targeted search queries to it.

That blueprint is the key unit of control. It is not the full outline and it is not just a free-form note. It is a coverable point that should appear in the final report, plus the search instructions needed to fill it. In practice, that means the critic can focus attention exactly where the outline is thin. If the housing section is weak in a Japan aging example, the critic does not broaden the whole report. It asks for housing-specific evidence.

The loop has a few guardrails so it does not become chaotic. The critic can stop the process when the outline is good enough, but the system also keeps a minimum number of rounds and a maximum round cap. That prevents two bad failure modes: quitting too early and wandering forever.

The generator receives those updated blueprints and queries, searches for evidence, and then revises the outline and its references together. It is allowed to discard redundant results, stale evidence, and weak branches, but it has to preserve validated evidence and the structure that already works.

The document bank is the loop’s memory

Search loops usually fail in one of two ways. They either forget what they already learned, or they keep too much junk and drown in it. AgentDisCo inserts a document bank to avoid both problems.

The document bank takes retrieved documents and turns them into a cleaner working memory. It breaks documents into smaller evidence pieces, scores them for relevance, summarizes them, and extracts compact evidence records before the generator sees them. Low-scoring documents are filtered out early, which keeps stale or noisy material from taking over the context window.

That matters because the loop is iterative. If the system found a good source in the previous round, it should still be there in the next round. AgentDisCo therefore carries the validated references forward instead of starting over each time. The outline’s backbone is also protected: blueprints can be modified or expanded, but the system resists deleting earlier key points. That makes the research process feel incremental instead of reset-heavy.

So the document bank is not just storage. It is the mechanism that keeps the evolving outline grounded while still giving the critic room to push the search in new directions.

The harness makes query strategy improve over time

The paper’s outer loop is especially interesting because it treats search-query design as something worth optimizing on its own.

What to notice here is that the harness does not look at one document in isolation. It evaluates the search bundle as a bundle. That fits the way the critic works: blueprints define a set of gaps, and the quality of the search should be judged by how well the full set covers those gaps.

The score agent uses a small set of practical criteria, including completeness, diversity, search coverage, and internal correlation. In plain language, it asks four questions:

  • Did the search cover the important parts of the outline?
  • Did it avoid pulling back the same thing over and over?
  • Did it find enough evidence to support the claim?
  • Do the returned pieces actually fit together?

The harness then writes the useful traces into a policy bank. Later rounds can retrieve those traces with a simple BM25-style lookup and reuse them as guidance. That gives the system a read-write loop over its own search strategies: look up a past trace, try a new query plan, record the result, and keep the best parts for later.

This is a smart choice because it keeps the optimization focused. The harness is mainly trying to improve query generation and retrieval behavior. It is not trying to solve every possible quality problem in the whole pipeline. That narrower scope makes the feedback loop easier to control and easier to reason about.

Writing becomes easy once the outline is stable

Once the outline has enough coverage, the writer does not start from a blank page. It follows the outline section by section, using the attached references from the document bank as the evidence pool for each chunk.

That chunking matters. Long-context writing gets messy when the model has to remember everything at once. AgentDisCo reduces that burden by letting each section behave like a self-contained subtask. The writer can continue from the previous chunk, but it only needs the evidence relevant to the current section. That makes the writing process more local and more coherent.

The final report is written in Markdown with citations linked back to their URLs, so the output is not just fluent prose. It is traceable prose. And because the planner already set a response style, the writer can keep the report aligned with the user’s intent instead of drifting into generic exposition.

The render agent sits one step further downstream. Its job is to take the structured report and turn it into a presentation that people can actually consume: an HTML page, a slide-style layout, or a poster-like visual summary. The important point is not the cosmetics. It is the handoff from research artifact to presentation artifact without forcing the research loop itself to change shape.

What the split buys you, and what it costs

AgentDisCo’s main advantage is control. By separating exploration from exploitation, the system can ask “what is missing?” before it asks “how should I write this?” That usually produces better coverage, better grounding, and a cleaner path from search to report.

The tradeoff is obvious too: more moving parts and more orchestration complexity than a single-pass generator. The harness also focuses mainly on search-query generation, so it is not a full solution to every writing weakness. And like many open-ended research systems, the paper still relies on LLM judges for evaluation, which is useful but not the same as an objective ground-truth score.

What makes the design memorable is the division of labor:

  • the critic asks where the gaps are,
  • the generator fetches evidence and repairs the outline,
  • the writer turns the outline into a grounded report,
  • the render agent turns the report into something people can use,
  • the harness keeps improving the search strategy in the background.

If you keep one sentence in mind, make it this: AgentDisCo treats deep research as a conversation between a gap-finder and an evidence-finder, with the outline as the shared notebook they keep rewriting together.
