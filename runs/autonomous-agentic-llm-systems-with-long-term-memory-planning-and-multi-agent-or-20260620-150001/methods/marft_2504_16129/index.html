MARFT: Multi-Agent Reinforcement Fine-Tuning

MARFT is easiest to understand if you stop thinking about a team of LLM agents as a handful of separate models that happen to share a reward. The paper is really about training a workflow. One agent writes something that another agent consumes, a verifier may inspect the result, and the whole chain may use different tools, different prompts, or even different base models. When that is the real structure of the system, a single-agent fine-tuning recipe is too flat.

The paper’s main move is to make that structure explicit. It turns a LaMAS team into a Flexible Markov Game, gives it dependency links between agents, and then trains the team with PPO-style trust-region updates, a centralized critic, action-length normalization, and an optional token-level refinement path. In other words, MARFT tries to make the training objective look like the actual agentic workflow.

Table 2 is the blunt summary of why this matters: MARFT assumes asynchronous, role-aware, heterogeneous agents, not synchronous identical workers. That sounds like a small change in wording, but it is the difference between “a swarm of copies” and “a chain of dependent role decisions.”

Why a normal MARL view is too flat

The old MARL picture assumes agents are mostly symmetric. They act together, observe together, and are usually optimized as if they were interchangeable pieces of one system. That works well when the environment is built that way. It breaks down when an LLM team has a planner, a solver, a reflector, and a verifier, each with different responsibilities and different information.

MARFT is trying to match the training setup to that kind of team. A planner’s output is not just another action. It becomes input for the solver. The solver’s answer is not just another action either. It becomes evidence for the verifier. Once actions feed other agents, the team is no longer a flat collection of policies. It is a dependency graph.

That is why the paper does not start from “how do we coordinate identical agents?” It starts from “how do we train a sequential, role-aware, partly asynchronous agentic workflow without destroying the language ability of the base models?”

Flex-MG is the game board

The formal object behind MARFT is the Flexible Markov Game, or Flex-MG. The useful intuition is simple: the game does not only track the current state and the current joint action. It also tracks which agent’s action should influence which later agent.

Figure 2 is the key visual here. What to notice is the dependency logic. Later agents do not just read the global state. They can also condition on earlier agents’ actions, and that dependency can change across timesteps. So if a planner writes a rough outline, the solver can read that outline before acting, and the verifier can inspect both of them. The graph can be as simple as a chain or as rich as a dynamic routing structure.

There is also an important limiting case. If the dependency links disappear, Flex-MG collapses back into a standard decentralized multi-agent setting where agents are effectively independent. That is useful because it shows MARFT is not throwing away MARL. It is extending it to a more realistic agentic regime.

The three knobs MARFT cares about

Once you accept that LaMAS teams are not homogeneous, the paper organizes the design space around three questions.

  • Parameter sharing: should every role use the same base model, or should some roles get their own LoRA adapters or even separate base models?
  • Execution synchronicity: do agents act at the same time, in sequence, or in some hybrid pattern where some outputs trigger later steps?
  • Update simultaneity: do you update all policies together, or do you update them one by one so they do not chase each other during training?

These sound like implementation details, but they are really the core of the method. If all agents share a base model, MARFT can stay lightweight by attaching role-specific LoRA adapters. If agents are genuinely heterogeneous, the framework still works, but now each role needs its own policy surface. That flexibility is what makes the method fit actual multi-agent LLM systems instead of only benchmark toy worlds.

The synchronicity axis matters for the same reason. LLM teams often behave asynchronously because later steps depend on earlier outputs. A verifier cannot inspect a solver result that has not been written yet. A tool-using agent may need a function call to finish before the next role can continue. MARFT treats that as the normal case, not an exception.

The update axis is where the RL trick really shows up. If every policy changes at once, the other agents become moving targets. The paper therefore favors sequential, agent-by-agent updates when it wants stability. That is the same instinct behind trust-region methods in general: move carefully enough that the critic and the rest of the team can keep up.

From rollout to update

Figure 3 compresses the whole method into one loop.

What to notice here is the rhythm. Inference and training alternate. The system collects a rollout, evaluates the joint trajectory, updates policies, then goes back out and acts again. The figure also hints at something important for real LLM agents: each role may have its own private APIs, tool pool, database, or other resources. MARFT is not trying to flatten those differences away. It is trying to train around them.

The loop is roughly:

  1. Start from the current task state, the agent profile, and any predecessor actions that this role depends on.
  2. Let the current agent produce its action under the Flex-MG dependency structure.
  3. Score the resulting joint trajectory with a centralized critic.
  4. Update the policies with a clipped PPO-style objective so the new policy does not drift too far from the old one.
  5. Repeat, optionally updating one agent at a time.

That centralized critic is the other half of the method. It sees the joint trajectory, not just one agent’s local view, so it can assign credit at the team level. That matters because a good verifier may only look good if the solver did the earlier work correctly, and a good solver may only look good if the planner made the right decomposition. The critic is the thing that keeps those dependencies visible during training.

Why action length needs a fair score

One subtle problem appears as soon as the actions are natural language. A long answer usually has a lower raw joint probability than a short answer, even if the long answer is much better for the task. MARFT addresses that with action normalization, which rescales the score by the action length.

Formula Card: length-aware action score

Purpose: keep long responses from being unfairly penalized just because they contain more tokens.

Intuition: total log-probability gets more negative as the response gets longer, so the score should be normalized by length before comparing actions across roles.

Formula:

Norm(log⁡P(ai∣context))=log⁡P(ai∣context)Z\text{Norm}(\log P(a^i \mid \text{context})) = \frac{\log P(a^i \mid \text{context})}{Z}Norm(logP(ai∣context))=ZlogP(ai∣context)​

Symbols:

  • a^i is the full action produced by agent i.
  • context is the current state plus any predecessor actions that agent i conditions on.
  • P(a^i | context) is the probability of that whole action under the policy.
  • Z is the token or word length of the action.

Read-aloud meaning: compare actions using average log-probability per token, not just total log-probability.

Why it matters: it keeps short and long answers on the same footing. Without this, a terse response can look artificially attractive simply because it has fewer tokens.

Why the token-level version exists

The action-level version is the main instantiation, and it already captures the basic MARFT idea. But the paper also explores a token-level refinement, because LLM actions are often long enough that one coarse decision can hide where the useful credit should really go.

The token-level view says: treat each token as a smaller action. That gives finer credit assignment, which is attractive when a role produces long reasoning, code, or structured answers. Instead of only asking whether the whole response was good, the critic can estimate value at the token level and GAE can estimate token advantages.

That is powerful, but it comes with a catch. If you discount too aggressively inside a single action, the token-level objective can drift away from the action-level objective. The appendix shows that intra-action discounting inserts extra decay factors that do not belong to the original macro-level problem. The practical takeaway is straightforward: if you want token-level MARFT to stay aligned with action-level MARFT, remove the intra-action discounting bias. In the paper’s terms, that means setting the token-level discount inside an action to 1.

So the token-level version is best read as a refinement, not a replacement. It is there when action granularity is too coarse, but it has to be used carefully if you want the same optimality target as the action-level system.

What the update is trying to protect

The paper keeps coming back to the same training priority: preserve language ability while improving coordination. That is why the policy update is clipped instead of being allowed to wander freely. A multi-agent LLM can look better on reward and still get worse at natural language if the optimization is too aggressive. The trust-region style update is the safety rail.

This is also why the paper leans on sequential or agent-by-agent updates when stability matters. If every role moves at once, the dependency chain changes under your feet. If you update one role at a time, the others stay fixed long enough for the critic to make sense of the new policy.

The paper also uses a standard multi-agent advantage decomposition idea to justify that sequential view. The intuition is that if each agent conditions on what earlier agents have already done, the team advantage can be broken into agent-level pieces. That makes it much easier to assign credit without pretending the team is one flat policy.

What the experiments actually tell us

The headline result is not that bigger teams are always better. It is that MARFT makes the team use its channels better.

On the four-agent coding setup, MARFT reaches 52.67, ahead of IPPO at 51.30 and vanilla at 47.16. Across the smaller and larger team settings, it generally improves over both baselines on most math and coding tasks. The training curves are also cleaner: MARFT improves more smoothly, while IPPO shows a Duo crash around the middle of training.

The role-specification ablation is even more interesting. When the agents are left anonymous at the start, the system can outperform a pre-configured Planner/Solver/Verifier style setup. That suggests hard-coding roles too early can slow down adaptation. The team sometimes does better when it is allowed to discover its division of labor before the labels become fixed.

There is a second, more subtle lesson here. Simply adding more identical agents does not guarantee large gains, because the agents often produce highly correlated trajectories. In that case, the extra channels are redundant. MARFT is valuable because it improves how the workflow uses those channels, not just how many channels exist.

What still feels open

The paper is clear about its own limits. Dynamic agentic training environments are still immature. Sample efficiency is still poor compared with the amount of interaction the system would like to consume. High-quality synthetic trajectories are still scarce, which makes cold-start training hard. And the communication protocol space is still fragmented, so there is no single standard for how heterogeneous agents should talk to each other.

There is also a practical scope limit in the current implementation. The framework can represent more general dependency graphs, but the implemented training path stays sequential for scalability. Extending it to more general parallel rollouts would require a more complex objective to deal with off-policy effects. That is a real research direction, not a minor engineering tweak.

What to remember

MARFT’s core lesson is simple: when agents depend on each other, train the workflow as a workflow.

The method gives that idea three concrete pieces. Flex-MG makes dependencies explicit. Centralized, clipped policy updates keep the team stable enough to improve. Action normalization and the optional token-level path make the scoring fair for natural language outputs instead of punishing longer, more expressive actions.

If you want the shortest possible memory hook, it is this:

Model the team as a dependent decision chain, keep the critic centralized, and update conservatively enough that language ability survives the optimization.
