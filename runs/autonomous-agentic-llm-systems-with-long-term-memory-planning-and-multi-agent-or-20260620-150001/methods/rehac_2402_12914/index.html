ReHAC: Reinforcement Learning-based Human-Agent Collaboration for Complex Task Solving

ReHAC is not trying to make an LLM answer every step by itself. It is trying to learn when the next step should stay automated and when the system should hand that step to a human. That sounds small, but it is the real bottleneck in conditional automation. In many long tasks, the hard part is not generating the next action. It is deciding who should be trusted to take it.

The paper starts from that control problem, not from a generic agent benchmark. Figure 1 shows the spectrum it cares about: no automation, full automation, and the middle ground where humans only step in on selected sub-tasks.

What to notice here is the middle panel. ReHAC lives there. It does not try to replace humans completely, and it does not keep humans involved in every step. It learns a gate that keeps most of the work automated while reserving human intervention for the steps that are actually worth the cost.

A useful way to picture the whole method is as a short control loop:

That loop is the whole paper in miniature. ReHAC first looks at the current trajectory history, then decides who should own the next action, then scores the result with a reward that combines task success and help cost. The actual answer generator is still there, but it is no longer the main decision. The main decision is routing.

The split that matters

The method becomes much easier to understand once you separate two things that many systems blur together:

  1. Who should act next?
  2. What action should that actor take?

ReHAC treats those as different problems. The paper frames collaboration as an MDP over trajectory states, but the important design choice is simpler than the notation. First, a collaboration policy chooses whether the next step belongs to the agent or to a human. Then the chosen side produces the concrete task action.

That matters because the failure mode is often not “the agent does not know anything.” It is “the agent knows enough to continue, but not enough to continue safely or confidently.” A good gate needs to notice that difference.

In the paper’s QA setup, the task action is a ReAct-style step such as Search, Lookup, or Finish. In InterCode, the task action comes from the Try Again loop and is an SQL command. The collaboration policy is shared across those settings. Only the task executor changes.

Why the reward has to price human help

If the reward only measured task success, the easiest solution would be to ask a human all the time. ReHAC avoids that by giving human intervention an explicit cost.

Formula Card: reward minus intervention cost

Purpose: define the tradeoff between solving the task well and relying on human help.

Intuition: the gate should be rewarded for improving the task outcome, but it should lose credit when it spends too much human attention.

Formula:

R(s,a)=T(s,a)−λC(s,a)R(s,a)=T(s,a)-\lambda C(s,a)R(s,a)=T(s,a)−λC(s,a)

Symbols:

  • R(s,a): final reward for choosing action a in state s
  • T(s,a): task reward after that action
  • C(s,a): human-intervention cost along the trajectory
  • \lambda: penalty weight that makes help more or less expensive
  • s: current state, which includes the unfinished task history
  • a: the chosen collaboration assignment for the next step

Read-aloud meaning: reward equals task quality minus a penalty for human help.

Why it matters: this is the knob that turns escalation into a learning problem. Without the penalty term, the safest policy would just ask for help constantly. With it, the policy has to learn when human input is actually worth paying for.

For QA datasets, the task reward is the F1 score. For InterCode, it is intersection-over-union. The paper estimates the final reward from whole trajectories with Monte Carlo sampling, so the gate is judged by downstream outcome rather than by a hand-written local label.

The penalty coefficient \lambda is the other important control. When it goes up, human help gets more expensive, so the gate becomes stricter about escalation. That is exactly the kind of tradeoff ReHAC is designed to learn.

How the gate learns from past collaboration

ReHAC does not learn by trial and error online. It learns offline from recorded collaboration trajectories. That is a good fit for the problem because real human help is expensive, and because the policy is supposed to learn a routing rule from examples, not memorize a single fixed workflow.

The paper uses a REINFORCE-style objective, but the useful idea is easy to state in plain language: if a particular collaboration choice led to a better trajectory reward, the policy should become more likely to choose that kind of collaboration again.

Three small details keep that learning stable:

  • The advantage term compares one action against the average reward of the available actions in the same state, so the policy learns relative preference instead of raw reward alone.
  • The importance-sampling ratio is clipped, which matters because the policy is updated from offline data and the current policy is not the same as the behavior policy that generated the trajectories.
  • Entropy regularization keeps the gate from becoming too deterministic too early. The model should still explore both “human” and “agent” routes while it is learning.

That last point is especially important. A gate that collapses immediately into “always human” or “always agent” has not learned the routing problem. It has only found a shortcut.

This is also where imitation learning becomes a weak baseline. Imitation learning can copy the collaboration choices seen in the training traces, but it does not directly optimize the reward tradeoff. ReHAC is trying to learn the tradeoff itself, which is why it generalizes better in the paper’s learning curves.

The paper also uses GPT-4 to simulate the human side when real collaboration data is expensive to collect. That is not the main method, but it is an important practical move. More consistent simulated traces give the policy more data, and the learned gate can still transfer back to the real human-agent setting.

What the experiments are really saying

The experiments are not just there to show a score bump. They answer a narrower question: does a learned gate beat prompts, random routing, and imitation when the cost of help is real?

On HotpotQA GPT-4-agent testsets, ReHAC reaches 31.38 reward at 51.46% human intervention rate, compared with Prompt at 29.26 reward and 34.06% HIR. Across HotpotQA, StrategyQA, and InterCode, it outperforms Random, Prompt, and imitation learning on reward. That pattern is the key result. ReHAC is not winning by always asking for more help. It is learning where help buys the most improvement.

The learning curves tell the same story in a different way. ReHAC keeps rising on both train and test, while imitation learning can look good on the train set and then weaken on the test set. That is what you would expect if ReHAC is learning a routing policy instead of memorizing a set of collaboration examples.

The scaling result is also useful. Moving from LLaMA-7B to 13B gives only a small gain, which suggests the main bottleneck is not brute-force capacity. The hard part is learning the right escalation rule.

Where the gate helps most

The case study makes the intuition concrete. Figure 5 shows a HotpotQA question asking which historical event happened first. The agent can handle the straightforward retrieval steps, but the final comparison becomes ambiguous because one of the entities has multiple candidate dates. On its own, the agent decides the query is too ambiguous and falls back to “unknown.” ReHAC routes that final step to a human, who can use the retrieved evidence and a bit of judgment to make the likely call.

What to notice here is not that humans are magically better at everything. It is that humans are better at a narrow slice of the trajectory when the problem stops being routine and starts being ambiguous. That is exactly the slice ReHAC is trying to find.

The paper’s discussion makes the same point in a broader way. Hallucination reduction is not the only goal. Some tasks really do need imagination, inference, or contextual judgment. Conditional automation is a way to reserve human creativity for those moments without turning the whole task back into manual work.

What the paper still leaves open

The assumptions are straightforward, and they matter.

  • The method is only evaluated around ReAct and Try Again style loops, so it is not yet shown for memory-heavy or self-reflective agent architectures.
  • It depends on offline collaboration trajectories, so the quality of the learned gate is tied to the quality of the labeled collaboration data.
  • It assumes humans are better than the agent at the chosen intervention points. If the agent later becomes stronger than the human on those steps, the cost model will have to change.

Those are not minor caveats. They are the next layer of the problem. ReHAC solves “when should we hand over control?” under today’s assumptions. It does not yet fully solve “what should the policy do when the agent itself becomes the stronger collaborator?”

What to remember

ReHAC is easiest to remember as a policy over help. It watches the unfinished trajectory, prices human intervention, and learns when to hand over the next step. The core idea is simple enough to fit in one sentence: do not hard-code when the agent should ask for help, learn that decision as its own policy.
