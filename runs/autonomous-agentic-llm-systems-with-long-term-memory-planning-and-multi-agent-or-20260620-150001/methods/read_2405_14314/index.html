ReAd: Reinforced Advantage Feedback for LLMs in Embodied Multi-Agent Collaboration

ReAd tackles a simple but expensive problem: when several embodied agents must coordinate, a plan can sound sensible in language and still waste a turn, violate a task constraint, or send the team in the wrong direction once the robots actually act. If the system waits for physical feedback, it burns environment steps. If it relies on self-reflection alone, it often rewrites the same weak idea again. ReAd shifts the correction point earlier in the loop. It trains a critic on offline LLM-planned trajectories, turns that critic into an advantage score, and uses that score to keep, rewrite, or reject candidate actions before execution.

Figure 1 makes the shift easy to see.

What to notice in the figure is the timing. RoCo asks the environment whether the plan was good after the action has already been tried. ReAd asks a critic first, so weak proposals can be thrown away before the robots spend a step on them. That is the main efficiency gain: correction moves from the world to the prompt.

You can think of the whole method as a small control loop:

Where the score comes from

The critic is the part that makes the loop work. It is trained offline from LLM-planned trajectories and rewards, so the system does not need to probe the environment just to learn whether a move looks promising. Once the critic has learned to predict state-action value, the planner can ask a much more useful question than “what should I say next?” It can ask “what action is actually better than the no-op baseline?”

That baseline matters. In embodied tasks, WAIT is the cleanest possible reference point because it means the agents do nothing and the environment stays unchanged. If a candidate move does not beat waiting, the move is probably wasting time or attention.

Formula card: scoring a joint action against WAIT

Purpose: define a score that says whether a candidate joint action is better than the no-op baseline.

Intuition: compare the proposed move with doing nothing. If the move is truly useful, it should beat WAIT by a positive margin.

Formula:

Aπ(s,a)=Qπ(s,a)−1γQπ(s,w)A_{\boldsymbol{\pi}}(s,\boldsymbol{a}) = Q_{\boldsymbol{\pi}}(s,\boldsymbol{a}) - \frac{1}{\gamma}Q_{\boldsymbol{\pi}}(s,\boldsymbol{w})Aπ​(s,a)=Qπ​(s,a)−γ1​Qπ​(s,w)

Symbols:

  • A_{\boldsymbol{\pi}}(s,\boldsymbol{a}) is the joint advantage score for the proposed action bundle.
  • Q_{\boldsymbol{\pi}}(s,\boldsymbol{a}) is the value of taking the proposed joint action \boldsymbol{a} in state s.
  • Q_{\boldsymbol{\pi}}(s,\boldsymbol{w}) is the value of the joint WAIT action \boldsymbol{w}, where every agent does nothing.
  • \gamma is the discount factor.
  • s is the current environment state.
  • \boldsymbol{a} is the joint action vector for all agents.
  • \boldsymbol{w} is the joint WAIT action.

Read-aloud meaning: the score of a candidate team move is its value minus the value of everyone waiting.

Why it matters: the critic does not need a separate value head to tell the planner what is good. It can compare the plan against a built-in no-op baseline and use that difference as feedback.

The derivation in the paper uses the fact that if everyone chooses WAIT, the next state is unchanged and the reward is zero. That makes the no-op action a natural anchor for advantage scoring. The same score can then be broken into local pieces as well: the paper’s advantage decomposition says the whole team score is the sum of the per-agent contributions along a chosen order. In plain language, ReAd can either score one joint move or score one agent at a time.

Turning scores into rewrites

Once the critic can score actions, the rest of the method becomes a prompt-controlled repair loop. The LLM proposes an action, the critic scores it, and the score decides whether the action is executed or rewritten. If the score falls below a threshold, the failed draft is fed back into the prompt and the model tries again. That threshold is the thing that turns the score from a passive number into an active control rule.

Figure 2 shows the loop in its simplest form.

What to notice here is that the failed draft is not discarded silently. It is kept as prompt history, scored, and used as the starting point for the next rewrite. The model is not being asked to restart from scratch. It is being told to improve the previous attempt until the advantage is high enough.

ReAd-S: score each agent in sequence

ReAd-S is the sequential version. At each timestep, it builds the team action one agent at a time, in a fixed order. The prompt carries the recent state-action history together with the advantage scores of earlier choices, so the LLM can see which kinds of moves have been rewarded before. The current agent is then asked to choose the action with the highest advantage.

If the critic says the draft is too weak, the system does not execute it. It appends that failed draft to the history, asks the LLM to repair the choice, and only moves on once the score clears the threshold. That is why ReAd-S can reduce wasted environment steps: the bad move is rejected before the robot acts.

This is also the more scalable variant. Because each agent is scored locally, ReAd-S avoids asking one critic to judge an exploding joint action space all at once. The tradeoff is that the planner has to move through the agents in order, which adds its own coordination cost.

ReAd-J: score the whole team at once

ReAd-J uses the same idea but asks for one joint action bundle for all agents at once. The critic scores the joint move as a whole against the WAIT baseline, and the whole bundle is rewritten if the score is too low.

This is the cleaner story when the team is small and coordination is tight. It is also easier to think about: one state, one joint proposal, one joint score, one rewrite rule. The tradeoff is obvious too. As the number of agents grows, the joint action space gets large very quickly, so joint refinement scales worse than the sequential version.

Why the threshold matters

The paper’s theory section is basically an argument for why “keep the good moves, filter the bad ones” should improve the base LLM policy. The bridge is advantage-weighted regression. Actions with positive advantage get more weight, actions with negative advantage get less, and when the weighting becomes sharp enough it starts to look like binary filtering.

That is the key conceptual move behind the prompt design. ReAd does not ask the model to reflect in the abstract. It asks the model to maximize a score that already encodes whether an action helped the task. If the score is low, the move is rewritten. If the score is high, the move is kept. The paper argues that this filtered behavior is enough to give monotonic improvement over the base LLM policy.

This is why the method feels more like coaching than judging. The critic is not there to explain the world. It is there to grade the next move before the team pays the cost of trying it.

Why the critic is trained offline

The critic is not learned from live negotiation. The paper collects LLM-generated trajectories and rewards offline, then regresses Q-values from Monte Carlo returns. It also augments the dataset with extra trajectories and unusual reset states so the critic sees more than the easiest paths through the task.

That extra coverage matters because a critic can only score what it understands. If a plan is far outside the offline data, the critic can still mis-score it. That is one of the method’s main weaknesses, and the paper is explicit about it. The system trades cheap pre-execution feedback for a dependence on good offline coverage.

What the paper is actually buying

The empirical story is straightforward. On DV-RoCoBench and adapted Overcooked-AI, both ReAd-S and ReAd-J improve success rate while reducing environment interactions and LLM query rounds relative to strong baselines. A concrete sanity check is the open-source run on Y2_G3: with Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct, ReAd-J reaches 0.9 success rate, 11.8 environment steps, and 13.6 LLM queries.

The robustness results tell the same story from a different angle. When the environment resets mid-task, ReAd-S can rescore the new state immediately and recover faster than methods that depend on physical verification. The advantage score stays relevant even when the history and the world have drifted apart.

Where it breaks

ReAd is not a free lunch.

  • Joint refinement scales poorly when the number of agents grows, because the joint action space explodes.
  • The critic needs offline data collection and extra compute before it can guide planning.
  • Out-of-distribution plans can still be scored incorrectly, especially when the planner wanders into unfamiliar territory.

Those limits are important because they show what ReAd really is: a learned pre-execution filter, not a universal coordination oracle. It is strongest when better feedback is more valuable than extra generation, and when the critic’s offline coverage is good enough to stand in for live negotiation.

What to remember

ReAd is easiest to remember as score first, act later. The critic grades candidate moves before the robots pay for them in the world. ReAd-S does that one agent at a time, ReAd-J does it for the whole bundle, and the advantage threshold decides whether the planner keeps the draft or rewrites it.

That is the durable idea: when coordination is the bottleneck, do not ask the environment to prove every move wrong. Learn a score that can reject weak moves early, then let only the useful plans reach execution.
