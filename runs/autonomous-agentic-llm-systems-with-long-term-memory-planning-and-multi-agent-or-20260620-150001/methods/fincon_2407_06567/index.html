FinCon

FinCon is easiest to understand as a trading desk built out of LLM agents. Instead of asking one model to digest news, filings, earnings calls, price history, and portfolio constraints in a single monologue, the paper splits the work into specialists. Each specialist compresses one stream of market evidence. A manager then combines those compressed notes into a trade. The important idea is not just “more agents.” It is a cleaner division between reading the market and deciding what to do with it.

What to notice in this picture is the shape of the pipeline. Data does not flow straight into a final answer. It first gets filtered into role-specific summaries, then the manager turns those summaries into an action, and then the risk-control loop feeds the outcome back into the next round of prompts. That is what makes FinCon feel like a desk with memory instead of a one-shot chatbot.

The paper frames this as a partially observable decision problem, which is the right mental model. The manager never sees the whole market state directly. It sees pieces of it, plus the consequences of its own previous actions. So the job is not to predict everything. The job is to keep refining beliefs fast enough to keep the next trade sane.

Why the split matters

Financial decision-making is noisy in a way that punishes overconfidence. News arrives quickly, filings arrive on a different schedule, earnings-call audio carries tone and nuance, and numeric indicators can point in yet another direction. A single generalist agent can technically read all of this, but in practice the context window gets crowded and the reasoning gets mushy.

FinCon’s answer is to separate reading from deciding. The analysts are responsible for compression. The manager is responsible for action. The risk system is responsible for correcting the next round of behavior. That division is why the method scales better than a flat peer-debate setup, where every agent keeps rehashing the same evidence and burns context on chatter.

The analysts do the reading, not the deciding

The analyst layer is where FinCon turns heterogeneous market data into compact, role-specific signals. The paper implements seven analyst roles, including text processors for daily news and financial reports, an audio agent for earnings-call recordings, a data-analysis agent for time-series indicators, and a stock-selection agent that helps build a diversified portfolio pool.

The exact split matters because different sources age differently. News can become stale in hours, filings usually matter on a slower cadence, and earnings calls sit somewhere in between. FinCon does not pretend those signals are interchangeable. It gives each source its own analyst, so the summary for that source can preserve what is timely and discard what is not.

That design also trims noise. Instead of moving raw articles, transcripts, and tables across the whole system, each analyst hands the manager a short investment insight. In practice, that means the manager sees denoised, source-specific evidence rather than a pile of duplicated context.

Think of it like this: the analysts are the people in the room who read all the reports. The manager is the one who has to place the order. The point is to keep the room from turning into a debate club.

The manager is a synthesizer, not a talk show host

Figure 2 makes the control flow much clearer.

What to notice here is that the manager sits above the analysts, not beside them. It does not need to negotiate with every other agent. It receives distilled outputs, considers its current belief state, listens for risk alerts, and then emits the actual trading action.

For single-stock trading, that action is the familiar buy, sell, or hold. The paper makes an important point here: “sell” can mean short-selling, so the manager is allowed to take a negative position when the signal is strong enough. For portfolio management, the decision gets one layer more structured. The manager still reasons in terms of buy, sell, and hold, but the final weights are produced through a mean-variance optimization step, which turns directional intent into an actual allocation.

So the manager is not just a classifier. It is a synthesis layer that turns multi-source evidence into a position choice.

That synthesis uses four kinds of information at once:

  • compressed outputs from the analyst agents,
  • live risk alerts from the control module,
  • updated beliefs about which sources matter for which target,
  • self-reflection over previous trading outcomes.

This is also where FinCon differs from a simple LLM wrapper. The manager is not only answering, it is revising how it should answer next time.

Risk control is the learning loop

This is the most important part of the paper. FinCon does not just chase return. It watches risk in two different time scales.

The first layer is within-episode risk control. It runs during a trading episode and also stays active at test time. If downside risk spikes, the manager becomes cautious immediately, even if the earlier part of the episode looked fine. The paper uses Conditional Value at Risk, or CVaR, as the trigger.

Formula Card: tail-risk trigger

Purpose: track downside tail risk instead of only looking at average return.

Intuition: if the worst part of the PnL distribution starts getting ugly, the system should tighten up before the episode gets worse.

Formula:

CVaRα(PnL)=E[PnL∣PnL≤VaRα(PnL)]\mathrm{CVaR}_{\alpha}(PnL)=\mathbb{E}\left[PnL \mid PnL\leq \mathrm{VaR}_{\alpha}(PnL)\right]CVaRα​(PnL)=E[PnL∣PnL≤VaRα​(PnL)]

Symbols:

  • \mathrm{CVaR}_{\alpha} is the conditional value at risk at confidence level \alpha.
  • PnL is profit and loss for the trading day or episode segment being measured.
  • \alpha is the confidence level that defines the tail cutoff.
  • \mathrm{VaR}_{\alpha} is the value at risk threshold at the same confidence level.
  • \mathbb{E} is the expectation operator.

Read-aloud meaning: average the outcomes that are worse than the VaR cutoff.

Why it matters: this is the signal that tells FinCon when recent trades have slipped into the downside tail, so the manager should switch into a more risk-averse stance.

In plain language, CVaR is the “something is going wrong” alarm. If recent PnL keeps landing in the worst tail, FinCon does not keep leaning harder into the same trade idea. It slows down and behaves more defensively.

The second layer is over-episode risk control. This one only runs during training. It compares consecutive episodes and turns the difference into a verbal update. The paper calls this Conceptual Verbal Reinforcement, or CVRF. The idea is simple: profitable episodes are not just better numbers, they are evidence about which market viewpoints, analyst summaries, and reasoning patterns should matter more next time.

This is where the method starts to feel like textual gradient descent. FinCon is not changing model weights. It is changing prompts and beliefs. The episode-to-episode comparison works like a stability knob for that update. If two action traces already overlap a lot, the prompt only needs a small nudge. If the trajectories diverge because the market changed, the update can be larger.

The paper then selectively propagates those updated beliefs. The manager receives the new conceptual guidance first, and only the relevant analyst agents get the part that applies to them. That keeps the system from broadcasting every lesson to every agent, which would waste context and blur responsibilities.

Memory turns past episodes into prompt changes

FinCon’s memory stack is not just a log. It is part of the control loop.

Working memory handles the current inquiry and the current round of reasoning. Procedural memory stores past decision fragments and ranks them by relevance, recency, and importance so that the most useful events come back first. Episodic memory belongs to the manager and stores whole-episode traces, PnL series, and the conceptual beliefs produced by CVRF.

That split matters because it matches the time scale of the information. A short-lived market signal should not sit in memory forever. A durable belief about a stock’s behavior should survive longer. The paper even gives different analyst roles different procedural-memory decay rates, which is a practical way to reflect that some data sources go stale much faster than others.

There is also a small but important reinforcement trick. When an event keeps helping trading decisions, the access logic can promote that event instead of letting it fade. In other words, useful memories get repeated access, and repeated access makes them easier to retrieve next time. That is how FinCon keeps important lessons alive without dragging the whole history into every prompt.

If you want one concrete example, imagine this sequence:

  1. News looks bullish, but the latest PnL tail starts falling.
  2. CVaR says the episode is getting risky, so the manager becomes cautious immediately.
  3. The next training episode performs better only after the prompt is nudged to trust a different mix of sources.
  4. That updated belief is then stored and selectively reused later.

That is FinCon in miniature. Observe, compress, decide, check risk, and then rewrite the next round of prompts using what happened.

Portfolio mode adds one more optimization step

Single-stock trading is the cleaner case, but the paper also uses FinCon for portfolio management. Here the method adds an extra layer of structure.

First, the analyst agents help build a stock pool by looking at correlations and diversification pressure. Then the manager makes directional decisions for the stocks in that pool. Finally, an external mean-variance optimizer converts those decisions into actual portfolio weights. The paper then rebalances those weights daily and linearly scales them into target positions.

This is a smart division of labor. The LLM is good at synthesizing messy signals and making qualitative judgments about direction. The numerical optimizer is better at turning those judgments into a constrained allocation. FinCon uses each part for the job it is best at.

The tradeoff is that portfolio mode is harder to run well. The context gets longer, the number of assets goes up, and the model has more chances to confuse one memory event for another. The paper even notes that multi-asset reasoning can still hallucinate memory references. So the portfolio version is a stronger demonstration of the architecture, but also the place where the context-length limit starts to bite.

Why the paper thinks this works

The ablations are clean and they line up with the design story. Removing CVaR weakens the live risk shield. Removing CVRF weakens the episode-to-episode learning loop. The paper’s own takeaway is that the belief-update loop contributes the larger share of the gains, which makes sense if you think about how much damage repeated prompt mistakes can do in a sequential market.

The headline results matter mostly because they support that story. On COIN, FinCon reaches 57.045% cumulative return and 0.825 Sharpe ratio, and it also leads the small-portfolio baselines. But the more interesting point is not the number itself. It is that the same hierarchy works in both single-stock trading and compact portfolio management, which means the design is not locked to one narrow benchmark setup.

So the paper’s claim is not “LLMs are magic traders.” It is more precise than that. If you split market reading across specialists, keep a manager above them, and use explicit risk signals to rewrite the next prompt, the system becomes much easier to steer than a single overloaded agent.

What to remember

FinCon is a funnel.

The specialists read the market, the manager makes the trade, CVaR catches immediate downside risk, and CVRF turns episode history into prompt edits for the next round. The memory system keeps useful lessons alive, while selective propagation keeps the communication cost under control. If you remember one sentence, remember this: separate reading from deciding, then use measured risk feedback to teach the next prompt what mattered.
