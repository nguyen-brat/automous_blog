SE-Agent: Self-Evolution Trajectory Optimization in Multi-Step Reasoning with LLM-Based Agents

SE-Agent starts from a simple but important shift: do not treat each agent rollout as a disposable answer. Treat the whole trajectory as editable search material. A pilot trajectory can be reflected on, revised, recombined with other trajectories, and finally refined by a reward that cares about correctness, reasoning quality, and cost. The output is not just a patch; it is the best evolved reasoning path that produced the patch.

That is the core idea of the paper. The search unit is the whole trajectory, not a single step and not a single sampled answer. Once you see it that way, the method reads less like a prompt trick and more like a small evolutionary system for agent reasoning.

The framework figure makes that loop easy to hold in your head.

What to notice is that SE-Agent does not ask the base agent to start over from scratch every time. It keeps previous attempts alive and lets them influence the next generation. That is why the paper calls the method self-evolution: the agent is not just sampling more. It is editing its own search history.

Why a pool beats one rollout

The problem SE-Agent is trying to solve is familiar in multi-step code repair. The first few rollouts often look different on the surface but not in substance. They inspect the same files, follow the same diagnosis path, and land on near-identical fixes. In that setting, more samples do not automatically mean more useful search. They can just mean more copies of the same mistake.

SE-Agent tries to break that pattern by starting with a small but deliberately diverse pool of pilot trajectories. The paper seeds that pool with multiple planning strategies and then mutates existing trajectories to push the search space wider. The implementation keeps the default candidate pool small enough to control API cost, but large enough to give the later stages something genuinely different to work with.

That design choice matters because diversity is not a side effect here. It is the raw material. If the pool is too homogeneous, revision has little to improve and recombination has nothing meaningful to mix. If the pool is too large but shallow, the cost grows without producing better trajectories. SE-Agent is trying to stay in the middle: enough diversity to matter, enough restraint to remain practical.

This is also where the method differs from a standard MCTS-style setup. MCTS can balance exploration and exploitation inside one search tree, but SE-Agent is not just searching a tree. It is evolving a population of whole reasoning traces. That lets one trajectory become useful evidence for another instead of remaining an isolated branch.

Revision: turn each draft into a better draft

The first improvement step is reflection. Each trajectory is inspected for weaknesses: where the reasoning is thin, where a step is redundant, where the path is circling the same idea, and where a better direction might exist.

Revision then rewrites the trajectory using that critique. The goal is not to polish the wording. It is to improve the path. A revised trajectory should remove useless motion, strengthen weak reasoning, and keep the tool-use sequence coherent.

In a code-repair task, that difference is easy to feel. One trajectory may keep staring at the symptom file because it looks nearby. Reflection can point out that the real bug is probably earlier in the data flow. Revision can then replace a local but misleading fix with a broader diagnostic move. The trajectory is still valid, but it is less trapped.

This is the step that makes the later stages possible. Without revision, the pool stays too similar. With revision, the system creates candidates that are different not only in wording but in route.

Recombination: let good ideas cross over

Revision improves one trajectory. Recombination improves trajectories together.

Crossover

The simplest recombination move is crossover. SE-Agent looks for useful segments in two trajectories and splices them into a hybrid. One path may have found the right place to inspect, while another may have the right order of edits. Crossover lets those strengths meet in a single candidate.

This is useful because different failed attempts often fail for different reasons. One rollout might localize the bug correctly but choose the wrong patch. Another might misunderstand the root cause but still discover an important constraint. Crossover can preserve the good part from each one instead of forcing the system to pick just one parent.

Transfer

Transfer is more directional. Here, a successful trajectory contributes knowledge or strategy to a weaker one. Think of it as borrowing a useful move from the path that already seems promising.

This is slightly different from crossover. Crossover mixes two parents on more equal footing. Transfer takes a stronger idea and adapts it to a path that still needs help. In practice, that can mean carrying over a good debugging tactic, a better ordering of checks, or a more sensible sub-plan.

Restructuring

Restructuring works at the level of the whole pool. Instead of mixing two trajectories locally, the method reorganizes the trajectory set using the information collected so far. This is the broadest form of recombination because it can change the shape of the search, not just the content of one branch.

That global pass matters when the pool has started to converge on a narrow family of ideas. Restructuring gives the method a way to step back, look at the entire candidate set, and build new trajectories from the best pieces that are still missing from the current shape of the search.

The important thing across all three recombination modes is that the method is not doing arbitrary cut-and-paste. Any mixed candidate still has to preserve task constraints, state/action coherence, and valid tool-use steps. Duplicate branches and circular reasoning are fair game to discard. The point is to keep the trajectory executable, not just novel.

Refinement: score what survives

Once the pool has been revised and recombined, SE-Agent needs a way to decide what deserves another round. That is the job of refinement. The paper does not rank candidates by a single signal. It uses a multi-objective reward that blends task success, reasoning quality, and efficiency.

Formula Card: score a trajectory before keeping it

Purpose: rank each candidate trajectory so the system keeps the ones that are both correct and practical.

Intuition: a good trajectory should solve the task, reason clearly, and avoid wasting steps or compute. A candidate that only looks clever but never completes the repair should not survive just because it sounds plausible.

Formula:

Reward(τ,t)=w1⋅TaskCompletion(τ,t)+w2⋅ReasoningQuality(τ)+w3⋅Efficiency(τ)\text{Reward}(\tau, t) = w_1 \cdot \text{TaskCompletion}(\tau, t) + w_2 \cdot \text{ReasoningQuality}(\tau) + w_3 \cdot \text{Efficiency}(\tau)Reward(τ,t)=w1​⋅TaskCompletion(τ,t)+w2​⋅ReasoningQuality(τ)+w3​⋅Efficiency(τ)

Symbols:

  • \tau is one candidate reasoning trajectory.
  • t is the task being solved.
  • w_1, w_2, and w_3 are the weights that trade off success, reasoning quality, and efficiency.
  • TaskCompletion(\tau, t) measures whether the trajectory actually solves the task.
  • ReasoningQuality(\tau) measures how coherent, deep, and robust the reasoning is.
  • Efficiency(\tau) measures how many steps and resources the trajectory used.

Read-aloud meaning: keep the trajectory that best balances solving the task, reasoning well, and staying efficient.

Why it matters: this is the selection rule that turns the pool into an evolutionary loop instead of a random set of edits. It also makes the method honest about cost. A longer or more elaborate trajectory is not automatically better if it is wasteful or logically weak.

The paper further splits task completion into automatic validation and expert-style evaluation. That detail matters because some trajectories are easy to check mechanically, while others need a softer judgment about whether the repair is genuinely good. The result is a reward that can reject shallow but valid-looking candidates before they become the final answer.

Selection then keeps the elite trajectories while preserving some diversity in the pool. That balance is important. If the system only keeps the top scorer, the population can collapse into clones. If it keeps too many weak variants, the search never converges. SE-Agent tries to keep a small set of strong but distinct candidates, then repeats the loop until the best reward stops improving or the iteration budget is exhausted.

Why the ablation matters

The ablation study is the cleanest proof that the loop is doing real work. It compares the full method with variants that remove revision, remove recombination, or remove all trajectory optimization.

What to notice is straightforward: taking away any of the three operations hurts Pass@1. The full loop wins because the pieces are complementary, not redundant. Revision matters because it creates diverse candidates for later mixing. Recombination matters because it lets useful ideas cross trajectory boundaries. Refinement matters because diversity alone does not guarantee correctness or efficiency.

That last point is especially important. Diversity is only half the story. A diverse pool can still be wrong in many different ways. Refinement is what keeps the evolutionary process pointed at actual task completion instead of just novelty.

What the results say

The headline result is that SE-Agent improves the same base agent across multiple models on SWE-bench Verified. With Claude-3.7-Sonnet, the paper reports 61.2% Pass@1 and 63.6% Pass@5. Across the five evaluated LLMs, the framework delivers up to 55% relative improvement over the baseline agent setups.

The more useful lesson is not the exact leaderboard number. It is that the wrapper is modular. SE-Agent improves the reasoning process without requiring a new base model architecture. That makes the method more like a search upgrade than a model redesign.

The paper’s comparison with SWE-Agent and SWE-Search is also telling. SWE-Agent gives you a code-act style agent, and SWE-Search gives you tree search. SE-Agent does something slightly different: it keeps trajectories as editable objects and uses them to guide later trajectories. That cross-trajectory reuse is the distinctive move.

Where it still costs compute

The tradeoff is easy to state. SE-Agent gets better trajectories by spending more compute on generating candidates, reflecting on them, recombining them, and scoring them. The method is therefore stronger than a one-pass or single-rollout agent, but it is not free.

The paper is also mostly evaluated on SWE-bench Verified, so the strongest evidence is in code repair rather than in every possible agent domain. And because the reward and selection logic are heuristic, the system depends on those heuristics being good enough. If the reward is poorly tuned, the search can keep the wrong candidates alive or overvalue trajectories that look efficient but miss the real fix.

Those are the natural open questions the paper leaves behind: can the same evolutionary loop transfer to non-code tasks, can the reward be learned instead of hand-set, and can the pool stay diverse without becoming expensive?

What to remember

SE-Agent is easiest to remember as evolution over reasoning traces. Keep a pool of pilot trajectories, critique each one, splice useful pieces together, and keep the strongest evolved path.

The big idea is simple: when first-pass solutions are too similar, better search comes from editing whole trajectories, not just sampling more of the same.
