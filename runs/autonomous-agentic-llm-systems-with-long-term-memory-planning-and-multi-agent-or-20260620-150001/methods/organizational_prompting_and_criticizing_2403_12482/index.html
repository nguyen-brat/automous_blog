Organized LLM Agents: Hierarchical Organizational Structures Emerge in Multi-Agent Systems

This paper is about a simple but important shift in how we think about multi-agent LLM systems. The problem is not only that each model can make mistakes. The deeper problem is that a team of agents can become a noisy room with no working organization. Everyone talks, everyone reacts, and nobody clearly owns the next move. The paper’s answer is to treat organization itself as a promptable object: give each agent a split between communication and action, encode the team structure in natural language, and then let a critic rewrite that structure after each episode.

That is a useful way to read the whole method. It is not trying to make one model smarter in isolation. It is trying to make the team behave like a small organization instead of a free-for-all chat room.

When the team has no shared script

The failure mode is easiest to see when the team has no designated leader. In the paper’s embodied setting, agents start from partial observations, closed containers, and random positions in a virtual home. Nobody sees the whole house at once. That means the chat itself becomes part of the sensing system: agents have to report what they found, coordinate who should move where, and keep track of what has already been done.

Without a shared organization, that communication becomes messy fast.

What to notice in Figure 1 is not just that the agents talk too much. It is that the talk is not organized around responsibility. Messages are repeated, decisions overlap, and action gets interrupted by more chatter. In other words, the team is spending part of its budget on coordination noise instead of task progress.

Split talking from doing

The cleanest idea in the method is that communication and action are handled separately. One LLM decides what to say and to whom. Another decides what to do in the environment. That split matters because it gives the team a real communication phase before anyone commits to the next physical move.

Figure 2 is easiest to read as a two-step rhythm: talk under team rules, then act. The important detail is that communication is not treated as just another action. It gets its own phase, which means the team can negotiate, report, or correct one another before the action module takes over.

That flow is the core of the method. The team does not just emit words and hope for the best. It alternates between structured conversation and embodied action, then uses the episode outcome to decide how the next round of conversation should be organized.

The paper uses this setup in VirtualHome-Social, where tasks look like preparing afternoon tea, washing dishes, setting up a dinner table, or moving objects between rooms. Those are useful test cases because the agents need to share partial discoveries, coordinate roles, and work with incomplete state rather than a tidy text-only prompt.

The organization prompt is the team constitution

The next key idea is that the team structure lives inside the prompt. The paper calls this the organizational prompt, and it plays the role of a small constitution for the agents. It tells them how information should flow, who has authority, and which behaviors are encouraged or discouraged.

That prompt has three pieces:

  • Topology: the shape of information flow, such as centralized, decentralized, chain-like, pyramid-like, or dynamic.
  • Role assignment: which agent is leader, supporter, or follower, and what each role should prioritize.
  • Rules: extra behavior guidance, such as when to broadcast, when to target one recipient, when to stay silent, and when a follower is allowed to correct the leader.

This is why the same base agents can behave like very different teams. A prompt can make them look like a flat chat group, a leader-centered team, a chain of handoffs, or a hierarchy with more than one coordinator.

What to notice in Figure 9 is that the communication shape changes even though the underlying agents do not. The prompt is doing the organizational work. That is the real conceptual move in the paper: leadership and hierarchy are not treated as vague social labels. They become promptable design choices that control who speaks, who listens, and how information is routed.

The communication rules are also more flexible than simple broadcast-only cooperation. During the communication phase, an agent can broadcast a message, send a targeted message to one recipient, send distinct messages to multiple recipients, or remain silent. Silence matters here. Sometimes the best coordination move is not to add another message, but to avoid adding noise.

Leadership is the coordination lever

Once the team has a constitution, the next question is who gets to coordinate. The paper compares several leadership modes, and that comparison is one of its most useful lessons.

Figure 4 is the headline result: a clear leader usually makes the team finish faster, and the communication cost does not rise much in the fixed-leader setting. The big picture is that structure itself is a performance primitive. If everybody is allowed to drift in parallel, the team burns time on negotiation. If one agent is clearly responsible for coordination, the team spends less effort re-litigating the same decision.

The paper compares four leadership modes:

  • Fixed leadership: one agent is assigned as leader from the start.
  • Elected leadership: the agents vote and may rotate who leads.
  • Human leadership: a person replaces the leader and directs the team.
  • Dynamic leadership: leadership can shift as the episode unfolds or as a new prompt discovers a different structure.

The fixed leader is the cleanest baseline. It reduces ambiguity and usually shortens the task. The paper reports that this can improve three-agent performance by up to 30% with almost no extra communication cost.

Elected leadership is more adaptive. The team can promote whoever currently has the best information. That makes sense in embodied tasks, because the person with the freshest local observation is often the best coordinator for a few turns. The tradeoff is that voting itself costs dialogue, so the team may become more chatty even when completion improves.

Human leadership is the strongest coordination signal in the paper. In the human-AI experiments, a human leader coordinates GPT-4 agents better than a GPT-4 leader does. The paper does not prove the exact cause, but the most plausible reading is that the human gives clearer global instructions, catches contradictions faster, and spends the team’s communication budget more carefully.

Dynamic leadership is the most interesting version because it shows that leadership does not have to be frozen at the start of the task. The team can discover, through interaction, that a different agent should take over for a while or that a more centralized structure is needed. That idea becomes important again in the Criticize-Reflect loop.

The paper also tests open communication by allowing followers to correct the leader when the leader is wrong. This helps stronger teams, especially GPT-4 teams, because they can use the correction channel without turning it into chaos. With weaker GPT-3.5 teams, the same rule tends to add more noise than value. That is a nice reminder that more freedom is not always better. A correction channel only helps when the team can use it well.

Criticize-Reflect makes the constitution editable

The most original part of the paper is not the hand-designed leadership comparison. It is the idea that the organization prompt itself can be improved after each episode.

Figure 3 shows the control loop. Run one episode. Critique what happened. Rewrite the organization prompt. Try again. That is the whole idea, but it changes the method a lot. Instead of treating team structure as fixed, the paper treats it as something the system can learn to redesign.

The loop has two LLM roles:

  • The Critic reads the dialogue and action history from an episode, summarizes what happened, points out the problems, and ranks the agents’ leadership qualities.
  • The Coordinator takes that critique plus episode costs such as time to completion and communication cost, then writes a new organization prompt for the next episode.

The critic is important because it does not just score the team with one number. It gives structured feedback about how the episode unfolded and which agent looked like the strongest leader. That is a better fit here than a single scalar score, because leadership quality is episode-dependent and the useful signal is often the pattern of behavior, not just the final metric.

The coordinator then turns that diagnosis into new prompt text. In practice, the prompt can change topology, role assignment, and behavioral rules. So the output of Criticize-Reflect is not just better task performance. It is a different organizational design.

That is how the paper discovers novel structures such as chain, dual-leader, and dynamic hierarchies. These are not hand-coded from the start. They emerge from the rewrite loop.

What the team learns when the rules are right

Once the organization is explicit, the agents start acting more like members of a team and less like isolated generators. They report observations to the leader, ask for guidance, make task-specific suggestions, and occasionally correct bad instructions. The disorganized baseline mostly produces repetition and interruption. The organized team produces more specialized behavior.

The leadership patterns also become easier to read. A good leader does not just speak more. It speaks more usefully. Stronger models tend to issue clearer and more holistic instructions, while weaker ones may produce vaguer coordination. That is one reason the GPT-4 leader outperforms the GPT-3.5 leader in mixed teams.

Criticize-Reflect also has a practical effect on the communication graph. In the stronger-model settings, it can reduce communication cost while keeping or improving task efficiency. In other words, the system is not simply trying to maximize chatter quality. It is trying to make the team cheaper to coordinate.

The paper’s generalization check is a good sanity test. The dynamic prompt discovered on one task transfers better to harder tasks than a fixed leader does, while its advantage is smaller on easier tasks. That matches the intuition: more complex tasks benefit more from flexible organization, while simple tasks do not need as much structural sophistication.

What still breaks

The method is effective, but it is not a universal fix for team coordination.

  • The paper studies one embodied environment, so transfer to other domains is still uncertain.
  • Larger teams can become crowded and slower, even with explicit organization.
  • Hallucination and counting mistakes can still break coordination.

That last point matters. Organization helps the team communicate better, but it does not magically remove model errors. If an agent confidently miscounts objects or misremembers what a teammate said, the team can still derail. The paper’s contribution is that it gives you a way to reduce coordination noise, not a way to eliminate all failure.

What to remember

The paper is easiest to remember as team design for LLM agents.

The base idea is simple: split communication from action, encode the team structure in a prompt, and make leadership explicit. Once that is in place, you can compare fixed, elected, human, and dynamic leadership as different ways to route information and authority. Then, with Criticize-Reflect, you can rewrite the team constitution after each episode and search for a better organization automatically.

So the main lesson is not just that leaders help. It is that structure itself is a performance primitive. When the prompt tells agents how to organize, coordination becomes cheaper, clearer, and more reliable.
