Mixture-of-Agents Enhances Large Language Model Capabilities

Mixture-of-Agents, or MoA, starts from a very practical observation: one strong language model can still leave useful ideas on the table. A single draft might be fluent, but it may miss a fact, skip a useful angle, or stop short of a better synthesis. MoA turns that weakness into a workflow. Several models write independent proposals first, later models read the whole bundle of proposals, and the final layer rewrites the best parts into one answer.

That is the core move. The paper is not trying to make one model magically smarter in isolation. It is trying to make a prompt-only collaboration loop that lets models improve each other.

What to notice here is the collaborativeness effect. The paper shows that a model can often do better when it sees other models’ answers, even when those extra answers are not obviously better than the model’s own standalone response. In other words, the auxiliary draft does not need to be the best draft in the room. It just needs to give the next model something useful to react to.

Think of MoA like a small editorial desk. One round of writers produces rough drafts, the next round of editors reads all of them, and the final editor publishes the piece. The important part is that the editor is not choosing one draft and throwing away the others. The editor is rewriting from the whole pile.

The method in one pass

The whole pipeline is easy to picture once you strip it down to the flow of information:

What to notice in this figure is the repeated synthesis. Every layer gets a bundle of candidate answers, not just one answer to polish. That means the system can accumulate complementary strengths across layers. One model may surface a key fact, another may provide structure, and a later layer can merge them into one response that reads more coherently than any single proposal.

The paper also allows the same model to be reused across positions or layers. If a layer is filled with identical models, the system becomes a kind of single-proposer setup where the diversity comes from sampling rather than from the model roster itself. That is a useful control, but it is not the main punchline. The stronger results come when the layer actually contains diverse models.

Formula Card: One MoA layer

Purpose: show how a layer turns many proposals into the next input bundle.

Intuition: each layer asks several agents for independent drafts, then uses a prompt to synthesize those drafts into one new response that can be passed forward.

Formula:

yi=⊕({Ai,j(xi)}j=1n),xi+1=yiy_i = \oplus \bigl(\{A_{i,j}(x_i)\}_{j=1}^{n}\bigr), \qquad x_{i+1} = y_iyi​=⊕({Ai,j​(xi​)}j=1n​),xi+1​=yi​

Symbols:

  • x_i is the input available to layer i.
  • A_{i,j} is agent j in layer i.
  • n is the number of agents in that layer.
  • A_{i,j}(x_i) is the response produced by that agent when it sees the current layer input.
  • \oplus is the aggregate-and-synthesize prompt that rewrites the set of responses into one answer.
  • y_i is the synthesized output of layer i.
  • x_{i+1} is the input passed to the next layer.

Read-aloud meaning: each layer asks several agents for proposals, then rewrites those proposals into the next layer’s context.

Why it matters: MoA is not a voting scheme. The final answer is refined by repeated synthesis, which is why the system can combine useful pieces instead of simply picking one winner.

Why the aggregator matters more than a vote

It is tempting to think MoA is just reranking with extra steps. The paper shows that is too shallow. A reranker looks at the candidate answers and selects one. MoA asks the final model to write a new answer from the whole set of candidates.

That difference matters because the best final response is often not already present as one of the candidates. One proposer may get the framing right but miss a detail. Another may have the key fact but bury it in clutter. A judge can only choose between those imperfect drafts. A synthesizer can keep the useful fact from one draft and the clean structure from another.

That is why the paper frames the final layer as an aggregator and synthesizer, not as a referee. The aggregator is acting more like an editor than like a scorer.

Why width and diversity help

The paper’s ablations are important because they explain when the loop actually improves quality.

More proposers help. As the number of proposer models grows, performance improves. That makes sense once you think about coverage: more models means more candidate phrasings, more partial facts, and more chances that at least one model will surface the right angle.

Diversity helps even more. A layer built from different models performs better than a layer built from several copies of the same model. That tells you MoA is not just exploiting randomness from sampling. It is exploiting model-specific strengths and failure modes.

There is also role specialization. Some models are good at both proposing and aggregating. Others are good proposers but weaker aggregators. That is a useful reminder that “good at answering” and “good at synthesizing other answers” are not the same skill. MoA works best when the layer design respects that difference.

This is where the Mixture-of-Experts analogy becomes useful. In classic Mixture-of-Experts, a gate routes inputs to different expert sub-networks inside one model. In MoA, the “experts” are full LLMs, and the gate is the prompt that asks the next layer to synthesize. The analogy is not exact, but it captures the key idea: use specialization, then combine the results.

The difference is that MoA stays entirely at the prompt level. There is no fine-tuning, no learned router, and no internal activation surgery. That makes the method surprisingly easy to apply to existing models.

Why it beats simple selection

The paper compares MoA with an LLM-ranker baseline, where the aggregator only chooses one of the proposer answers instead of writing a new answer. MoA does better.

That result tells you something important about the mechanism. The final layer is not merely selecting the best candidate. It is integrating the best pieces. If the answer quality came only from picking the strongest draft, ranking should have been enough. The fact that it is not enough means the aggregator is doing real synthesis work.

The paper also checks whether the aggregator tends to incorporate the strongest proposed answer, and the answer is yes. Similarity between a proposed answer and the final answer correlates with performance, which fits the picture above: the synthesizer often borrows the best parts of the strongest draft while still rewriting around them.

What the numbers say

The headline result is easy to remember. An open-source MoA setup reaches a 65.1% length-controlled win rate on AlpacaEval 2.0, beating GPT-4o’s 57.5% on that benchmark. The lighter MoA-Lite variant reaches 59.3% while staying close to GPT-4o on cost. The paper also reports leadership on MT-Bench and improvements on FLASK, especially for correctness, factuality, completeness, and metacognition.

The pattern is more interesting than the leaderboard position. MoA helps most when the task benefits from complementary drafts. It is less about producing a single brilliant first answer and more about repeatedly tightening a good-enough answer until the best pieces survive.

The tradeoff you pay for

MoA is not free.

  • Iterative aggregation delays time to first token, because the system has to finish the earlier layers before it can emit the final answer.
  • The quality gains depend on having diverse, reasonably strong base models.
  • Some tasks become a little more verbose, not more concise.

That is why the paper includes a lighter configuration. MoA-Lite reduces the depth of the stack and keeps the cost much closer to a single strong model. The deeper version buys more quality, but the lighter version is useful when latency and budget matter more.

The open questions follow naturally from that tradeoff. How many layers are enough? How wide should each layer be? Which model should occupy which role? Could chunk-wise aggregation cut latency without giving up much quality? The paper leaves those as design questions rather than pretending the current setup is already optimal.

What to remember

MoA is easiest to remember as proposal, synthesis, proposal, synthesis, final answer. The first layer creates options. Later layers read the whole set of options. The last layer writes the response after preserving the useful facts and discarding redundancy.

The deeper lesson is that some of the best gains from multi-agent systems do not come from debate or voting. They come from letting models see one another’s drafts and then making the final model behave like a careful editor. In MoA, the winning move is not to choose one answer early. It is to keep synthesizing until the answer is better than any single draft that came before it.
