Richelieu: Self-Evolving LLM-based Diplomat

Richelieu is easiest to understand as a diplomat with a notebook. It does not try to solve Diplomacy with one giant prompt. Instead, it turns every turn into a loop: read the board, infer who wants what, choose a smaller goal, negotiate with a truth check, move units, and store the episode for later.

That matters because Diplomacy punishes short memory. A move that looks good now can create a future threat, and a friendly message can be a trap. The paper’s main idea is that an LLM agent becomes much more useful when social reasoning, planning, dialogue, action, and memory all keep correcting one another instead of living in separate silos.

The whole machine in one glance

Figure 2 is the right place to start because it shows the whole loop rather than a single module.

What to notice here is that Richelieu is not one monolithic generator. It begins with map details, game rules, domain knowledge, and the long-term goal of winning the game, then it passes through distinct stages. First it builds a social belief about the current turn. Then it chooses a sub-goal that is smaller than victory but still aligned with victory. Then it negotiates. Then it acts. Finally, it writes the whole episode back into memory so the next turn can reuse what happened.

That design is the real departure from a plain chat-based LLM agent. The system does not only ask, “What should I say?” It asks, “Who am I talking to, what are they likely after, what should I try to achieve this turn, and what should I remember if this worked or failed?”

Compared with methods that split negotiation and planning into separately trained pieces, Richelieu keeps the whole diplomatic loop coupled. That makes the system easier to evolve, because the memory of a good or bad episode can influence the belief model, the planner, the negotiator, and the actor at once.

The first job is to understand the social map

Richelieu starts from a simple but important assumption: there are no permanent allies and no permanent enemies. In Diplomacy, the same neighbor can be a useful partner one phase and a serious threat the next. So the first question is not “what move is best?” but “who is this player right now?”

The paper splits that question into two parts. One part is the relationship view: is the other player a likely ally, a likely enemy, a temporary partner, or a future danger? The other part is the intention view: what is that player probably trying to do in this moment? Those are related, but they are not the same. A player can look friendly while secretly trying to grow stronger, and that distinction matters when you choose both words and troop orders.

Figure 3 shows the kind of social questions Richelieu asks before it replies.

This figure is easy to read as a checklist, but the deeper point is that the agent is not treating dialogue as literal text matching. It is checking whether the message fits the current board state, whether it fits the opponent’s inferred goal, and whether it fits the player’s past behavior. That is what makes the negotiation stage tactical instead of decorative.

The appendix gives a good example of why this matters. Before self-play memory was available, the agent could tunnel on the current war and miss a more dangerous rival that was quietly becoming stronger elsewhere. After self-play, the same kind of board position led to a more forward-looking response. Instead of focusing only on the visible fight, Richelieu recognized the future threat, looked for a ceasefire where one was useful, and used alliances to redirect pressure.

That is the role of social reasoning in this paper. It is not there to sound human. It is there because a diplomatic agent that cannot estimate trust, threat, and intent will keep getting blindsided by exactly the kinds of moves Diplomacy is built around.

Planning means choosing a sub-goal, then challenging it

Once Richelieu has a social belief, it does not jump directly to unit orders. It first chooses a sub-goal. That sub-goal sits between the long-term win condition and the immediate move. In plain language, it is the answer to, “What should I try to make true this turn so the larger plan still makes sense later?”

That matters because LLMs often lean toward the nearest visible gain. They are good at “what can I do now?” but weaker at “what will this decision do to the board two or three turns from now?” Richelieu tries to fix that by forcing the planner to work at a shorter horizon than victory but a longer horizon than a single order. So the sub-goal might be something like contain a fast-rising rival, buy time with a ceasefire, or set up a partnership that prevents a future encirclement.

The second half of the planner is reflection. This is where memory becomes more than storage. Richelieu retrieves past episodes that look similar to the current board position and the current sub-goal. The retrieval is similarity-based, using both the shape of the state and the kind of goal being pursued. That means the model is not just reading old text for nostalgia. It is looking for episodes that can challenge the current plan.

The important intuition is simple. If a similar state previously led to success, the planner gets a reason to keep the idea. If a similar state previously ended badly, the planner gets a reason to revise it. That turns memory into a kind of lived criticism.

You can think of this as the agent asking itself, “Have I seen a situation like this before, and did this plan actually work?” Because the context window is limited, Richelieu cannot pour all of its history into the model. It must choose a few especially relevant memories. That selectivity is one of the paper’s real engineering constraints, and it becomes harder as the archive grows.

This is also why the planner feels more like a working diplomat than a static policy. It does not just pick a plan and move on. It tests the plan against experience before it commits.

Negotiation is not just conversation

Negotiation is where Richelieu becomes clearly social. The negotiator is not there to echo whatever the other player said. It is there to update the social belief and to try to shape the other player’s future behavior.

That means the negotiation stage has to answer questions that are easy to miss if you only look at the text. Is the message consistent with the opponent’s likely sub-goal? Does the message fit the current board? Has this player been honest in similar situations before? Is the best response a cooperative offer, a delay, or a deception?

The paper adds a discrete truth check for exactly this reason. Words in Diplomacy are not binding. A promise can be genuine, but it can also be bait. Richelieu therefore mixes the received message with the inferred relationship, the opponent’s likely intention, a credibility score, and retrieved similar experiences. That is what lets it judge whether a statement is likely sincere or whether it is better to treat it as tactical noise.

One of the appendix cases makes the logic very concrete. England proposes a ceasefire and asks Germany to cooperate against France. Richelieu suspects that the offer is really meant to pull German forces away from a vulnerable front. Instead of blindly accepting, it appears cooperative in the conversation while also seeking a safer arrangement with France. In the action phase, the opponent’s later move confirms the suspicion.

The point of the example is not “be paranoid.” The point is that a good negotiator should not confuse a pleasant sentence with a reliable plan. Richelieu uses the social belief to decide when to trust, when to stall, and when to deceive back.

After negotiation, the actor turns the updated social state into unit orders. This split between negotiator and actor is more important than it may look at first. It keeps persuasion and movement separate. A message can be strategically useful without being a legal or tactically sound order, and a great order can still be badly explained. Richelieu treats them as related but distinct steps.

Memory is the engine of self-evolution

The memory module is where the paper’s “self-evolving” claim comes from. Richelieu stores the current state, the negotiation messages, the actions taken by every player, and the sub-goal it was trying to satisfy. Later, those raw episodes are summarized into shorter experiences with evaluations.

There are two memory layers worth keeping apart. One is a short-term credibility estimate for the current turn. That tells Richelieu how much to trust each player’s current message. The other is the longer-term history of episodes. That is where the planner and negotiator go looking for lessons.

The evaluation of a sub-goal is also updated over time. The paper’s idea is that you should not judge a decision only at the instant it was made. You should see how the board actually moved afterward and then decide whether the old sub-goal still looks sensible. If the original objective was clearly wrong in hindsight, the memory should remember that. If later events show the plan was actually clever, the memory should preserve that too.

That retrospective scoring is the difference between a log and a learning memory. The log records what happened. The learning memory records what the episode means for future turns.

Self-play is what fills that memory without human demonstrations. Instead of depending on pre-collected expert games, the system lets its own agents control the countries and generate fresh episodes. That gives Richelieu a much wider range of alliances, betrayals, retreats, and recoveries than a narrow supervised dataset would.

Figure 5 shows the effect clearly.

The curve tells a familiar but important story. More self-play generally helps, and then the gains flatten. That plateau is not a failure of the idea. It is a sign that memory growth has diminishing returns. At some point, the archive contains so many similar episodes that finding the truly useful ones becomes the harder problem.

So the self-evolving part of Richelieu is not “keep adding memory forever.” It is “keep adding experience until the memory is rich enough to change the policy, then improve how you retrieve from it.”

What the experiments actually show

The experimental story is strongest when you read it as evidence for the loop, not as a leaderboard.

In no-press Diplomacy, Richelieu stays ahead of six baselines on relative score. That matters because it shows the planning and memory stack is useful even when negotiation is removed. The model is not only winning because it can talk. It is already stronger on board management before conversation enters the picture.

In press play against Cicero, the win-rate gap is modest, around 0.7 percent, but the score gap is closer to 10 percent. That sounds more subtle than a simple win/loss headline, but it actually makes sense. When several countries are controlled by a similarly strong model, games can end in draws, so score is often a better indicator of who managed the board more effectively over time.

Against AutoGPT, the difference is much larger. Richelieu averages about 9.10 percent win, 18.33 percent most-supply-center, 38.27 percent survived, and 34.30 percent defeated, while AutoGPT is much worse on the same metrics. That comparison is useful because it shows Richelieu is not just a generic LLM wrapper. It is much more structured about social inference, goal formation, and action selection.

The ablation study sharpens the same lesson. Each module helps, but removing the framework leaves even a strong base model behind. In other words, the paper is not claiming that GPT-4.0 by itself is enough for AI diplomacy. It is claiming that the loop around the model matters. The model’s raw language ability gives the system a foundation, but the social reasoning, reflection, and memory layers are what unlock the diplomacy behavior.

The cross-backbone result says something similar. GPT-4.0, Llama 3, ERNIE Bot, and Spark Desk all improve with self-play. The stronger backbones start better, but the same loop helps all of them. That is a nice sign that the method is not narrowly tied to one model family. It is a reusable control structure for LLM agents.

The social reasoning test is also informative. The paper reports strong accuracy for relationship inference and lower, but still useful, accuracy for intention inference. That gap makes sense. It is usually easier to guess whether someone is friend or foe than to infer the exact sub-goal they are currently pursuing. Richelieu is doing both, and the harder part is naturally the less accurate one.

Where the method stops

The paper is clear about its limits. Diplomacy is a useful testbed, but it is still a bounded game with a public board state. Real-world diplomacy has a much larger action space, more hidden information, and messier incentives. So Richelieu is best read as a strong demonstration of social planning under partial information, not as a solved model of geopolitical reasoning.

Memory also brings a tradeoff. As the archive grows, retrieval becomes harder. More episodes are not automatically better if many of them look nearly identical. The system needs better selection, not just more storage.

The base LLM still matters too. The framework helps multiple backbones, but a stronger model starts from a better place. And even when Richelieu improves the score, direct head-to-head win-rate gains can stay modest because the game often produces draws.

Those limits do not weaken the core idea. They actually make it clearer. Richelieu is not a magic policy. It is a loop that keeps updating what it believes, what it wants, what it says, what it does, and what it remembers. That is why it gets better with experience.

What to remember

If you want one mental model from the paper, keep this one: Richelieu is a feedback loop for social action. It does not treat conversation, planning, execution, and memory as separate problems. It lets each stage revise the others.

That is the trick that makes the system feel more like a diplomat than a chatbot. The agent learns who to trust, what to aim for next, how to talk, how to move, and what lesson the last episode left behind. Then it uses the next turn to test that lesson again.

That is the real contribution of the paper. Richelieu shows that for hard multi-agent social tasks, you often do not need a bigger one-shot prompt. You need a better loop.
