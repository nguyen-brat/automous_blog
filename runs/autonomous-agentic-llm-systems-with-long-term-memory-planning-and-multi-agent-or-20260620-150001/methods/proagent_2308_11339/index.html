ProAgent: Building Proactive Cooperative Agents with Large Language Models

ProAgent is best understood as a cooperation loop, not as a single model that magically “knows” how to play well with others. The paper starts from a simple but important observation: in a team task, the hard part is often not producing a fluent plan. The hard part is keeping track of what your partner is probably doing, what the scene currently allows, and whether your chosen move is actually executable right now.

That is why ProAgent is modular. It first translates the environment into language, then uses memory and reasoning to infer teammate intent, then checks whether the chosen skill is legal, then corrects its belief when reality disagrees, and finally hands the validated skill to a controller that turns it into low-level actions.

Figure 1 is the cleanest way to read the method.

What to notice in the diagram is the direction of control. ProAgent does not jump straight from observation to action. It moves left to right through a small pipeline: scene translation, planning, belief correction, skill verification, and then execution. The verifier sits in the middle for a reason. If the plan is wrong, the system should not quietly act on the mistake. It should explain the failure and re-plan.

Turning the board into language

The first problem ProAgent has to solve is almost embarrassingly practical: raw environment state is not the best input for a language model. A tensor-like board state may be precise, but it is not the form in which an LLM reasons best. So the paper inserts a translation step before planning begins.

This translation is supported by a knowledge library with three pieces:

  • Task: what the job is and who the other agents are.
  • Rules: which skills are legal and how the model should format its reasoning.
  • Demos: a few example scenes showing the expected pattern of analysis and action.

The point of the library is not to stuff the prompt with more text for its own sake. It is to make the planning space explicit. The model needs to know the objective, the vocabulary of allowed skills, and the style of response the system expects from it. That is especially important because ProAgent is doing zero-shot coordination: it does not get to adapt through fine-tuning for the exact teammate it meets.

Once the knowledge library is loaded, the raw scene is rewritten into a language-based state. In practice, this is what lets the planner read the situation as a small story rather than as a grid of symbols. Instead of staring at tensors, the model sees something closer to: the partner is near one station, a pot is cooking, one ingredient is missing, and a useful delivery opportunity is coming up.

That kind of wording matters because coordination is about relative timing. Language is a good interface for that timing problem.

Memory keeps the recent story alive

ProAgent’s memory is split into two roles, and the split is useful.

  • Knowledge Library is the persistent side. It keeps task rules, layout knowledge, and demonstrations around across the whole run.
  • Trajectory is the short-term side. It is a FIFO buffer that stores the latest language-based state, the planner’s analysis, the current belief about the teammate, and the skill that was used.

The short-term side is the more interesting one. A teammate’s likely intent is often not visible in the current frame alone. It depends on what happened a few steps ago, what was just predicted, and whether that prediction has already been challenged by later behavior. That is why ProAgent does not just keep the latest state. It keeps the recent interaction trail.

The paper also describes a recent-K retrieval strategy, where the agent only pulls back the most recent K trajectories when it needs context. That is a sensible compromise. It keeps the prompt focused on what actually matters for the next decision without dragging in a long history that may no longer be relevant.

In other words, ProAgent treats memory like working notes, not like an archive dump. The goal is to keep enough of the recent story to reason about the teammate, but not so much that the planner gets buried.

The planner reasons before it commits

Once the scene has been translated and the useful history has been loaded, the Planner module takes over. The paper explicitly frames this as a chain-of-thought style process: do not jump directly to the final skill. First analyze the scene, then infer what the teammate is likely trying to do, then choose the agent’s own next high-level move.

This is a small but important design choice. In a coordination task, a high-level action is only good if it fits the partner’s expected behavior. If your teammate is likely to fetch a plate, then your best move is different from the move you would choose if the teammate is about to cook. The planner has to reason over that uncertainty rather than pretend the uncertainty does not exist.

That is why the paper treats “analysis” as more than a verbose preface. The analysis is the bridge between the language-based state and the final skill choice. It is the place where the model writes down its current story about the scene and the partner before it commits to action.

If you want a simple intuition, think of the planner as a teammate who says to itself, “Here is what I think the other person is about to do, here is the current constraint set, and here is the skill that makes the most sense if that guess is right.” That is the right level of abstraction for coordination under uncertainty.

The verificator stops a bad plan before it becomes a bad move

Planning is not enough if the chosen skill is impossible in the current state. That is where the Verificator module comes in.

The paper presents the verificator as a multi-round prompt-and-response loop. One useful way to read it is as a small internal audit. First, the system checks whether the current skill is actually legal. Then it double-checks the diagnosis. Finally, it explains why the skill failed and what should happen next.

The first round is the most concrete: Preconditions Check. The model is asked to examine the scene and the planned skill step by step and identify which precondition is violated. That is valuable because many coordination failures are not deep reasoning errors. They are simple feasibility errors. The agent wants to do something that looks sensible in the abstract, but the object is not in the right place, the route is blocked, or the state is otherwise inconsistent with the skill’s requirements.

In a tightly coupled task, that kind of mistake is expensive. If one agent acts on a bad assumption, it can waste several steps of the other agent’s time too. The verificator prevents that from turning into a cascade.

So the verificator is not just a passive checker. It is the system’s local reality check. If the skill does not pass, the agent does not execute and hope for the best. It re-plans.

Belief correction keeps the teammate model honest

This is the part that makes ProAgent genuinely proactive rather than merely reactive. The planner does not just guess what the teammate will do and then forget the guess. It stores that belief and later compares it with what the teammate actually did.

If the observed behavior does not match the predicted intention, ProAgent corrects the belief in one of two ways:

  • replace the wrong predicted intention with the observed behavior, or
  • keep the original prediction but annotate it as wrong.

Those two choices serve slightly different purposes. Replacement is the strict version: update the belief to ground truth. Annotation is softer: it preserves the old guess but marks it as unreliable so the model can remember why it was wrong.

There is also an indirect correction mechanism built into the re-plan loop. Every time the verificator pushes the agent back into another planning round, the model gets another chance to revise its story about the teammate. That matters because belief errors in cooperative tasks tend to repeat if they are not explicitly corrected. The system has to learn not only the world state, but also the partner’s tendencies.

This is the core insight behind the paper. Coordination is not just plan generation. It is planning under an uncertain partner, which means belief maintenance is part of the job.

The controller turns skills into motion

At the end of the loop, ProAgent still has a gap to cross. A high-level skill is not the same thing as an environment action. The controller is the bridge between those two levels.

The controller takes the validated skill and decomposes it into low-level actions that can actually be executed. The paper notes that this controller can be rule-based or learned with language-grounded reinforcement learning, but in the reported Overcooked setup the authors use the built-in best-first-search controller. They also point out that a better controller could likely improve performance further.

That is an honest and important limitation. ProAgent is strongest as a reasoning and coordination framework. Its final performance still depends on how well the chosen skill can be grounded into motion. If the controller is weak, even a good plan can stumble.

The upside is that the split makes the system much easier to reason about. The planner handles intent and strategy. The controller handles movement and local execution. Each module stays in its lane.

Why the loop works

The method only makes sense if all of these pieces are allowed to interact.

The memory module helps the planner remember what just happened. The planner proposes a high-level move based on the current scene and its current belief about the teammate. The verificator catches plans that are illegal or internally inconsistent. Belief correction updates the partner model when later behavior contradicts the earlier guess. The controller then executes the accepted skill as a sequence of actual actions.

That is a surprisingly compact way to handle cooperative uncertainty.

If you strip any one of those pieces away, the system becomes less robust. Without memory, the planner forgets the recent interaction. Without belief correction, it clings to stale assumptions about the teammate. Without verification, it acts on plans that are not feasible. Without a controller, the high-level skill never becomes a real move.

The paper’s experimental story lines up with that design. ProAgent beats five self-play and population-based baselines across the tested cooking layouts when paired with AI agents. With human proxy partners, it reports an average improvement of more than 10% over the previous state of the art. That is the headline result, but the ablations are the more interesting clue about why it works.

Figure 2 makes the human-proxy result easier to digest.

What to notice here is not the exact height of every bar. The point is that the same reasoning loop still helps when the teammate is a behavior-cloning proxy for a human partner. That is a stronger claim than just “it works against other learned policies.” It suggests the system is not memorizing one partner style. It is trying to infer intent.

The ablation numbers tell the same story from a different angle. In one setting, analysis plus belief scores 204. Removing belief drops the score to 184. Removing both analysis and belief and planning directly drops it all the way to 100. And when the verificator is removed, the 100-step success rate falls to 20%.

Those numbers are worth remembering because they separate the value of the components. Analysis helps because it gives the planner a scratchpad for the current situation. Belief helps because the teammate is not fully observable. Verification helps because a fluent plan can still be illegal. The gains are not coming from one giant prompt. They are coming from the structure around it.

Where the method is strong, and where it is brittle

ProAgent is a good example of what modular prompting can do when the task really benefits from explicit teammate modeling. It gives a clear story for how to build a cooperative agent without task-specific fine-tuning, and it makes that story interpretable enough for humans to inspect.

But the tradeoff is also clear. The method depends on hand-designed prompts, a hand-built skill set, and a controller that knows how to ground those skills. It is also evaluated mainly in Overcooked-AI-style coordination, and the human study uses behavior-cloning proxies rather than direct human participants.

So the method is best read as a design pattern, not as a universal solution. The pattern is useful: keep a short memory, infer teammate intent, verify feasibility, revise beliefs when reality disagrees, and only then execute.

What to remember

ProAgent’s central lesson is simple: coordination is planning under uncertainty about another agent.

The paper shows that a cooperative agent gets much better when it does four things explicitly. It remembers the recent interaction, it predicts what the teammate is trying to do, it checks whether the chosen skill is currently feasible, and it updates its belief when later observations contradict the guess. The controller then grounds the validated skill into low-level actions.

That is the whole idea. Not a single clever prompt, but a belief-aware loop that keeps the plan tied to reality.
