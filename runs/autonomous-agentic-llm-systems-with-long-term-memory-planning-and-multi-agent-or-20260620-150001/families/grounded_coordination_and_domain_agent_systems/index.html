How Agent Teams Stay Grounded: Beliefs, Specialists, and Verified Handoffs

Multi-agent LLM systems usually fail for a boring reason before they fail for a glamorous one. The hard part is often not raw reasoning. It is keeping the right state in the right place while several agents, tools, or dialogue steps are trying to cooperate. One agent misreads a teammate, another lacks the needed expertise, a long discussion buries the important fact, or a fluent answer slips past a basic feasibility check. The common theme is not a single algorithm. It is a shared interface problem: how to keep planning connected to reality.

That is why grounded coordination matters. These systems try to make handoffs explicit. They translate state into a usable representation, plan with the right scope, correct beliefs when evidence changes, verify that an intended step is actually possible, and only then act. Different methods emphasize different bottlenecks, but they all treat coordination as something that must be engineered, not assumed.

The recurring pipeline

The compact input supports a real recurring flow:

This pipeline should be read as a control loop, not a rigid architecture diagram. “State grounding” means turning the current world, conversation, or market snapshot into something the planner can reason over. “Planner” means choosing a high-level action, report, or next move. “Belief correction” updates assumptions about partners, users, or the environment when new evidence conflicts with earlier guesses. “Skill verification” checks preconditions before committing. “Action execution” hands the validated choice to a controller, dialogue module, or execution layer.

In some systems the loop is literal, as in ProAgent. In others the same structure appears in disguised form: expert gathering and report revision in MedAgents, central critic suggestions plus actor confirmation in LLaMAC, or candidate act selection plus reflection memory in MACRS. The surface changes, but the engineering question stays the same: what must be preserved across handoffs so the final action still reflects the real task?

Why coordination breaks so easily

The main bottlenecks in this subtopic are complementary rather than identical.

The first is teammate intent uncertainty. In cooperative settings, the state you need is partly inside someone else’s head. If your agent cannot estimate what the partner is about to do, even a technically valid plan can be badly timed.

The second is the specialized knowledge gap. One agent may reason well in general but still miss the domain-specific distinction that decides the case. In those settings, the problem is not just planning. It is getting the right expertise into the shared artifact before the decision is made.

The third is coordination and token cost. As more agents join, asking everyone to reason deeply at every step becomes expensive and unstable. Long free-form exchanges increase both latency and drift.

The fourth is uncontrolled dialogue flow. In interactive systems, the question is not only what answer to give, but what kind of move to make next. Ask, clarify, recommend, negotiate, wait, or reject are different control actions, and collapsing them into one generic generation step often hurts performance.

The first missing state is usually belief

Belief-aware coordination starts from a simple observation: your agent does not act in a vacuum. It acts next to a partner whose intentions are only partially observable. That makes recent interaction history more important than a long undifferentiated memory.

This is where ProAgent is useful as a teaching example. Its contribution is not merely “use an LLM for cooperation.” The key lesson is that cooperation improves when the system explicitly stores recent trajectory, infers teammate intent, checks whether a chosen skill is currently feasible, and revises its belief when the teammate behaves unexpectedly. In other words, the planner should not be trusted as a one-shot oracle.

The supporting evidence fits that lesson. In ProAgent’s ablations, analysis plus belief modeling outperforms versions that remove belief reasoning, and direct planning without analysis or belief drops much further. Another ablation shows how severe the validation problem is: removing the verificator drops the 100-step success rate to 20%. The headline result points in the same direction. With human proxy partners, ProAgent reports an average improvement of more than 10% over the prior state of the art.

The durable idea is broader than Overcooked-style tasks: if coordination depends on what another agent is likely to do next, belief update and feasibility checks are not optional polish. They are part of the state.

When no single agent knows enough

Some tasks fail because the system has the wrong social model. Others fail because no single pass surfaces enough domain knowledge. In those cases, the fix is often to split the work by perspective before recombining it.

MedAgents illustrates this clearly. Instead of asking one model to produce a polished medical answer end to end, it separates question-focused analysis from option-focused analysis, merges them into a shared report, and lets the report be revised through consultation until the panel reaches consensus or runs out of attempts. The central artifact is not the final answer. It is the report that carries the right distinctions forward.

This teaches an important design axis: consensus is useful when it operates over a shared intermediate object. If agents only exchange unconstrained rationales, the discussion tends to become expensive without reliably improving the decision. MedAgents’ evidence is modest but instructive: GPT-4 zero-shot MedAgents averages 86.7 accuracy, higher than zero-shot CoT+SC at 83.0 and slightly above few-shot CoT+SC at 85.4. The number matters less than the mechanism. Specialist tasks often benefit from perspective-specific analysis followed by report revision, not just more verbose single-agent reasoning.

There is also a caution here. Consensus does not guarantee correctness. If the underlying knowledge is stale or missing, a group can converge on a polished mistake. Grounded coordination improves handoffs; it does not magically create facts that were never present.

Scaling teams without drowning in chat

Once the number of agents grows, a different bottleneck takes over: planning every step through unrestricted multi-agent discussion costs too many tokens and creates too many chances for drift. The system needs a narrower coordination interface.

LLaMAC shows one answer to that problem. It separates central proposal from local confirmation. A shared critic proposes suggestions, decentralized actors compare those suggestions against local observations, and only send external feedback when the proposal looks wrong or infeasible. That design keeps a planner in the loop without forcing every actor to replan from scratch on every turn.

The underlying design axes here are worth remembering. One axis is controller style: should grounding be rule-based, RL-based, or some hybrid controller that translates a validated plan into action? Another is memory scope: should the system keep only a short FIFO trajectory, or also maintain longer-lived summaries? A third is validation strategy: do actors merely execute suggestions, or are they allowed to reject and escalate them? LLaMAC is useful because it makes these choices explicit and ties them to token efficiency instead of treating communication as free.

This same lesson reappears in larger domain systems. FinCon uses specialist analysts plus a manager so each component sees a narrower slice of the market. TradingAgents relies on structured reports, short debate loops, and a final risk gate to stop long agent chains from drifting away from current evidence. The pattern is consistent: narrow roles, structured handoffs, and a final check before execution.

Dialogue is a control problem

Interactive systems have an extra failure mode: they can say sensible things while taking the wrong conversational action. A recommender that recommends too early, or keeps chatting without gathering enough preference information, is making a control mistake, not just a wording mistake.

MACRS makes that visible by splitting the next-turn decision into dialogue acts. Separate responder agents generate “ask,” “chat,” and “recommend” candidates. A planner chooses among them using dialogue history, prior acts, and memory derived from user feedback. Reflection then updates both the user profile and strategy guidance for later turns.

This is a useful design axis because it reframes dialogue from free-form generation into action selection under uncertainty. The important question becomes: what information must survive from one turn to the next? In MACRS, that includes weak preference signals, recent act history, and corrective feedback from earlier failures. The compact evidence supports the value of that separation: removing both reflection modules drops MACRS-C to a 0.51 success rate, and strategy reflection matters more than information reflection alone.

The more general lesson is that a conversational multi-agent system should often choose the type of move explicitly before it chooses the wording.

Grounding noisy domains with hierarchy and memory

When tasks are long-horizon, noisy, or strategic, grounded coordination often grows into hierarchy plus memory. The issue is no longer a single bad handoff. It is a sequence of decisions in which early simplifications can distort everything downstream.

FinCon is a clean example of hierarchy under noisy inputs. Specialists compress different market signals, a manager synthesizes them, and risk feedback updates future behavior. Its most teachable mechanism is the risk trigger:

Purpose: Measure downside tail risk so the system can switch into a more cautious mode.

Intuition: Average return can look fine even when the worst outcomes are getting dangerous. The agent needs a signal that focuses on the bad tail, not the mean.

Formula:

CVaRα(PnL)=E[PnL∣PnL≤VaRα(PnL)]\mathrm{CVaR}_{\alpha}(PnL)=\mathbb{E}\left[PnL \mid PnL\leq \mathrm{VaR}_{\alpha}(PnL)\right]CVaRα​(PnL)=E[PnL∣PnL≤VaRα​(PnL)]

Symbols: \mathrm{CVaR}_{\alpha} is conditional value at risk at confidence level \alpha; PnL is profit and loss; \mathrm{VaR}_{\alpha} is the value-at-risk cutoff at the same confidence level; \mathbb{E} means the average over those tail outcomes.

Read-aloud meaning: Take the average of the profit-and-loss outcomes that are worse than the VaR threshold.

Why it matters: In FinCon, this gives the manager a concrete trigger for risk-aware control instead of relying on vague verbal caution.

Richelieu extends the same grounded-control instinct into social planning. It separates relationship inference, sub-goal setting, negotiation, action, and memory writing so the agent can reason about trust and deception over time instead of reacting myopically. TradingAgents pushes the idea toward institution-style pipelines, where structured reports and risk approval keep a long chain of analyst and trader interactions from collapsing into untraceable chat.

What the evidence actually supports

The compact input does not justify a sweeping claim that one architecture dominates all multi-agent settings. What it does support is a narrower and more useful claim: explicit grounding, verification, and structured handoffs matter.

Three anchors stand out. ProAgent’s large drop when belief modeling or verification is removed shows that teammate modeling and legality checks are not cosmetic. MedAgents’ gain over strong chain-of-thought baselines shows that specialist consensus can beat a lone reasoner on domain-heavy tasks. LLaMAC’s reported gains at larger team sizes show that central proposal plus local validation is a plausible way to manage coordination cost. Beyond that, the evidence is promising but domain-specific.

What to remember

Grounded coordination is not about making every agent smarter in isolation. It is about making the interfaces between agents, memories, and controllers harder to corrupt.

If you remember one design rule, use this one: preserve only the state that future decisions truly need, but preserve it in a form that can be checked. That usually means short task-relevant memory, explicit intermediate artifacts, narrow specialist roles, belief revision when reality disagrees, and a validation gate before execution.

The shared mechanism here is not a single algorithm. It is a shared interface discipline.
